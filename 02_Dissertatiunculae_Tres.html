I. Rosenmuller's Prolegomena to Deuteronomy

The Hebrews call the fifth book of the Pentateuch from the words with which it begins, eleh ha-devarim ("These are the words"), and mishneh ha-torah ("repetition of the law") from chapter XVII, 18, or simply mishneh. Similarly, the Alexandrians entitled it Deuteronomy, not because it contains a second and new law, but because a second time, the law already given before is repeated and explained in this book.

In the first part of the book (I -- IV, 40), Moses, in a solemn assembly of the people, reviews what had been done and accomplished from the departure from Egypt up to the encampment in the plains of Moab, after recounting the victories over the king of Bashan, the Amorites, and the Midianites, and exhorts the Israelites to observe the divine laws. In the second part (IV, 45 -- XXX, 20), the precepts scattered through the three preceding books that were necessary for the entire people to know -- excluding those pertaining to priests alone -- are collected, with some also added entirely anew, interspersed with weighty exhortations, to which weight is added both by the promises of rewards proposed to those who observe the divine laws and by the threats of punishments that await those who neglect them. There follows in chapter XXXI an Epilogue, in which Moses explains that, being advanced in age at 120 years, he is unequal to bearing the burden of the state and the army, and therefore he had resolved to abdicate his leadership and appoint Joshua, given to him by God, as his successor; and the code of laws collected by him and deposited in the hands of the Levites and Elders of the people he ordered to be read every seven years at the Feast of Tabernacles in a solemn assembly of the people. There follows a double canticle: one (chapter XXXII), which, deeply implanted in the minds of the Israelites, would remain as a lasting monument of God's benefits and, in turn, of the duty owed to Him by the Hebrews; the other (chapter XXXIII), in which he made prayers for each of the tribes. In the last section of the book (chapter XXXIV), the death and burial of Moses are described.

That this book was composed by Moses at least up to XXXII, 47 is expressly stated in XXXI, 9, 19, 24, where Moses himself testifies about himself that he committed to writing the canticle that constitutes part of the book (XXXII), and adds moreover that he likewise put into writing all the laws he had set forth, the context of which laws is so connected with the remaining parts of the entire book that it can scarcely be separated. Very many passages of the book lend credibility to the testimony about Moses as the book's author -- passages that indicate the land of Canaan had not yet been conquered but was soon to be occupied by the Hebrews, such as chapter VI, 1: "These are the precepts, statutes, and judgments which the Lord gave me in command, that I might teach you to observe them in the land into which you are crossing to possess." Verse 10: "When the Lord your God shall have brought you into the land which He swore to your fathers He would give you, with great and prosperous cities which you did not build, houses full of all good things which you did not fill, cisterns hewn out which you did not dig, and vineyards and olive groves which you did not plant," etc. Verses 18, 19: "You shall do what is right and pleasing to the Lord, that it may be well with you, and that you may enter and possess the good land which the Lord swore to your fathers He would give you, and that you may drive out all your enemies from before you." In chapters VII, 1--5, 16--26, and XX, 16, 17, Moses commands the Canaanites, whose lands the Hebrews were about to occupy, to be utterly destroyed. Chapter XI, 10, 11: "The land which you are entering to possess is not like the land of Egypt from which you came out, where you sowed your seed and watered it with your foot like a garden of herbs; the land into which you are crossing to possess is a land of mountains and valleys, where you shall drink water from the rain of heaven." For other entirely similar passages, see chapters VIII, 1, 7, 12; IX, 1--6; XI, 23--25, 29--31; XII, 8--10, 14; XVI, 6; XVII, 14; XVIII, 14; XIX, 1; XXIII, 20; XXVII, 1; XXVIII, 2, 4; XXX, 18; XXXI, 2, 3, 6. Someone might say that such things were cleverly fabricated by a later writer of the book to persuade readers that the book was committed to writing by Moses. But if this reasoning holds, what book could there ultimately be about whose author and date a certain judgment could be formed from internal evidence? Moreover, what we read in chapters II, 20--23 and III, 8--10 about the ancient peoples of Canaan and the boundaries of their territories could scarcely have been transmitted so accurately in any age other than that of Moses. And if you counter that these too were fabricated, we ask: to what end would anyone have invented such things?

Nevertheless, Vater, de Wette, Gesenius, and Bleek maintain that to believe Deuteronomy was written by Moses does not allow for the different and peculiar character of this book as compared with the earlier books, the diversity of style, narratives, and laws, and the many indications in it of an age later than Moses. And as regards the manner of expression, they say that the ornate, copious, and verbose eloquence of this fifth book greatly differs from the brief and concise speech of the earlier books. But since this fifth book, as a kind of epitome of the three preceding books intended for the use of the entire people -- as we noted above -- was to be publicly read every seventh year (chapter XXXI, 10--13), it was the mark of a prudent writer to employ in such a work a copious and oratorical style of expression, and throughout the entire book to admonish readers and hearers again and again with a grave and passionate discourse to observe most scrupulously the laws set before them. From the use of the book that we have indicated, the reason also seems to be found for certain formulaic expressions and individual words that are peculiar to this book alone, namely that they were, as is credible, adapted to the common and popular understanding and usage. Moreover, that Deuteronomy has a great similarity with the earlier books in style and in the entire manner of expression, and that many passages agree so well with passages in those books that they appear to have been written by the same author recalling from memory what he had previously written -- not even de Wette can deny this; but he considers the cause of so great an agreement to be that the writer of Deuteronomy appears not only to have known and read those earlier books but to have carried them, as it were, in his bosom, to have embraced them in memory, and to have set them before himself as a model in writing his own book. This, however, will not seem equally plausible to everyone. As for those passages that either truly differ from the earlier books in the narrative of events, or seem to differ, or in which the learned men we have cited thought they detected traces of an age later than Moses -- we shall consider these in their interpretation. Moreover, just as the two last chapters of the book, so also certain other passages were augmented or slightly altered by later hands -- this we readily concede. Nevertheless, the number of such passages is not so great as to compel us to deny the entire book to Moses.

The chronology of the book, to be drawn from chapters I, 3, 4 and Joshua IV, 19, V, 10, encompasses the last two months of the fortieth year after the departure.

II. On the Prophecy of Moses (Deut. XVIII, 15-18)

1. In the prophecy of Moses, the words of the Vulgate translation differ hardly at all from the Hebrew original, just as the words of the other translations do not differ either. Yet diverse explanations of the entire oracle are to be found, which, after we have weighed them, we shall set forth our own opinion and confirm it with direct and indirect arguments, being prepared then to resolve the objections that are raised.

Section I. The Various Interpretations

2. The first interpretation is that the oracle was spoken simply about Joshua, which is indeed an ancient view; for the Fathers refute it; and from certain words of Augustine it seems possible to gather that the Jews of his time had embraced this view, Contra Faust. bk. XVI, ch. xix. Gregory of Nyssa refutes this on the grounds that "the son of Nave (Joshua) had long before been designated by the hand of Moses, as we read in Leviticus," in Euthymius Zigabenus, Panopl. dogm. P. I, tit. 8, init.; he meant to say in the Book of Numbers (xxviii, 18-23). Isidore of Pelusium, moreover, bk. III, ep. 94, adduces other reasons besides this, and they are: that Joshua was not like Moses, but far inferior to him; that according to the reasoning brought by Gregory of Nyssa one should have said "He raised up," not "He will raise up"; and that Joshua added nothing to the law, but merely followed it. Furthermore, the words of Deuteronomy stand in the way: "There has not arisen again a prophet in Israel like Moses," ch. xxxiv, 10, which we shall explain more fully below (12). From these words Lachais, La Sainte Bible, on this passage, after Sherlock, L'usage et les fins de la Prophetie, Disc. 6, rightly argues that, since these words, as many believe, were added to Deuteronomy by Joshua, then by Joshua's own admission the prophet foretold by this oracle cannot be Joshua.

3. The second interpretation is that the oracle pertains to Joshua, inasmuch as he was a type and figure of Christ, and at the same time to Christ, as the one who was prefigured by Joshua -- or what amounts to the same thing, to Joshua according to the literal sense and to Christ according to the spiritual sense. This interpretation belongs to Clement of Alexandria, who wrote concerning this oracle: "Signifying through Jesus the son of Nave, Jesus the Son of God," Paedag. bk. I, ch. vi, near the end. But the arguments we have brought against the first explanation prove that the literal sense of the oracle cannot pertain to Joshua; hence this second interpretation must also be rejected.

4. The third interpretation is that prophets in general, or a series of prophets, are foretold. So hold the more recent Jews, following Solomon Jarchi, on this passage, and Moses Maimonides, Jad Chazakah, P. I, bk. I, tract. 5, ch. x, section 5. But what is more significant, a single authority of the greatest weight seems to favor this opinion -- I mean Jerome (on Isa. viii, 19). However, since from Jerome's words it in no way follows that Christ is to be excluded from the meaning of this oracle, no one can prevent Jerome's endorsement, withdrawn from this opinion, from being assigned by us to the one we shall expound after this. That the older Jews opposed the authority of Jarchi and Maimonides we shall demonstrate below. Then the words of Deuteronomy already cited also stand in the way of this interpretation, whether they were added to this book by Joshua prophetically, or historically by Ezra. Indeed, if they were added by Ezra, it is already clear, as we just said, that the older Jews opposed this interpretation. For if the oracle according to the opinion of the older Jews pertained to prophets in general, the prophets, again according to the opinion of those same Jews, would have to be said to have been like Moses. How then would Ezra have contradicted this oracle by inserting those words into Deuteronomy?

5. The fourth interpretation is that both prophets and Christ are foretold simultaneously. This is the opinion of many interpreters of great renown, although not all propose it in the same way. For either certain things from this oracle they affirm apply to Christ, but certain other things only to the prophets -- to Christ, I say, verse eighteen, but to the prophets verse fifteen; so hold some writers cited by Tostatus (on this passage, Quest. VI). Or they take everything as said of the prophets, but principally of Christ, as do Oleaster, Lyra, Tostatus, Cornelius, Bonfrerius, Tirinus, Frassen, and among the Sacred Critics, Fagius. To the patrons of this opinion must be reckoned those who take the oracle as spoken now of Christ, now as if of prophets, as Origen did (hom. 12 on Exod., 1 on Jer., on Matt., vol. XVII, section 14; cf. Contr. Cels. bk. I). But if you say that this oracle also concerns the prophets, I do not know how solidly you will be able to prove that it pertains principally to Christ, especially if you are dealing with Jews. If, however, we say what some cited by Tostatus say, we all but oppose Moses himself, who teaches us that what is read in verse fifteen, and was uttered by him, is the very same thing that God had already said, and which we read in verse eighteen. Finally, how did it come about that this promise of God vanished in the age when the second temple stood?

6. Furthermore, all these interpretations are opposed by the arguments with which we shall confirm our own, which is as follows.

Section II. The Proposition

7. This oracle pertains to the one Messiah alone; that is, Moses speaks of the Messiah only in this oracle. One should read Cajetan (on this passage), Sherlock, and Lachais (loc. cit.). We shall confirm this proposition first with direct and then with indirect arguments.

Section III. The Direct Proof

8. And first it is proved by an argument drawn from the fitness of things. All the foremost among the prophets foretold Christ in express words. Is it then credible to anyone that Moses alone, the greatest of all the prophets, should have expressly and openly predicted nothing about the greatest of all things that concerned the Israelites? Yet unless it is here, you will find nowhere else that Moses himself accomplished this, although he reported the predictions of others.

9. Secondly, it is proved by authorities. From Tirinus and other patrons of the fourth opinion, indeed even from some who agree with us, you may see that from all antiquity only Eusebius is cited, as if he alone among the Fathers supported our proposition; whereas the Fathers who hold that this oracle was spoken simply of the one Messiah are so numerous that within the first five centuries of the Christian era at least twenty are counted. They are: Tertullian, Adv. Marc. bk. IV, ch. xxii; the author of the Recognitions, bk. I, ch. xxxvi; Cyprian, Testim. adv. Jud. bk. I, ch. xviii; Novatian, De Trinit. ch. ix; Archelaus, Disput. cum Man. sections 41, 43; Methodius, De Symeone et Anna, section 11; Eusebius, Dem. Evang. bks. III and IX; Lactantius, Instit. div. bk. IV, ch. xvii; Athanasius, Orat. 1 contr. Arian. section 54; Cyril of Jerusalem, Catech. 12, section 57; Titus of Bostra, Contr. Manich. bk. III, ch. vi; Gregory of Nyssa, Testim. adv. Jud. 11; Epiphanius, Haeres. 42, schol. 27 from the Gospel of Luke and schol. 8 from the Epistle to the Galatians; Philastrius, Haeres. de Deuter. 416, al. 68; Gaudentius of Brescia, Serm. 9; Augustine, Contr. Faust. bk. XVI, ch. xv; Chrysostom, Contr. Anomaeos, hom. 12 on II Cor., hom. 7 on Gal. II; Cyril of Alexandria, De Ador. in spir. et verit. bk. II, on Isa. bk. I, orat. 1; on John i, 21; v, 46; vii, 40, 49; viii, 24; ix, 30; xii, 49; the author of the Consultatio of Zacchaeus and Apollonius, bk. II, ch. xiv; the author of the Questions to the Orthodox, among the works of Justin, Quest. CI.

10. Now we affirm that all these understood the oracle in this way, namely as spoken simply of the one Messiah. For although not all of them expressly and openly exclude other prophets, yet since they draw an argument from the oracle to prove things that pertain to the one Messiah, they must be said to exclude all others from the meaning of the oracle. For otherwise the argument would prove nothing: for someone could respond that the oracle, even according to the Fathers' own opinion, pertains to all the prophets.

11. Thirdly, it is proved by an argument drawn from the very words of the oracle, even without regard to the antecedents and consequents. For you have first the noun navi, prophet, in the singular number; and throughout the entire oracle this prophet is spoken of in the singular number. Nor do I deny that in the Hebrew language, as in others, the singular number is sometimes used for the plural, examples of which are found everywhere, such as the saying: "You shall love your neighbor," Matt. v, 43. Indeed, I know that the noun navi is used in the singular for the plural in the famous oracle of Daniel (Dan. ix, 24), where, although some more recent Protestants think that Jeremiah is designated by this noun because they believe the entire oracle was spoken about the captivity of the Israelites soon to be ended, yet for us who contend that the prophecy was written about Christ, the noun navi must necessarily be taken as a collective. Nevertheless, as regards the oracle of Moses, everyone must accept the following points, since they are manifest. The first is that the noun is used in that number in which it would necessarily have had to be used if the discourse were truly about the one Messiah. The second is that the noun must be taken and interpreted in the singular number if nothing stands in the way; and we shall soon see that nothing does stand in the way. The third is that one who simply reads that chapter does not even suspect that several prophets rather than one unique and singular prophet are promised by Moses. The fourth is that the Messiah was foretold under the name of "the prophet," as is evident from the Gospel (John vi, 14, etc.); yet you will nowhere find Him foretold as a prophet unless you hold that this oracle was spoken of the one Messiah. The fifth and last is that where the Scriptures have a collective noun or a singular used for the plural, very frequently the pronouns and verbs that depend on it are plural, but in this oracle they are consistently singular.

12. Next you have the word kamoni, like me. From this predicted likeness of that prophet to Moses, that he could be none other than the Messiah, argue Archelaus, Eusebius, Gregory of Nyssa, Augustine, and Cyril of Alexandria. The reasoning is drawn from these words of Deuteronomy: "There has not arisen again a prophet in Israel like Moses," ch. xxxiv, 10; and that none ever did arise, all Jews have always most firmly maintained, and this very thing is confirmed by what is read in the Book of Numbers, xii, 2, 6-8. Furthermore, that the ancient Jews understood the word kamoni as spoken not of any likeness whatsoever, but of such a likeness as would make that prophet in no way inferior to Moses, and therefore of that very likeness which the words just cited from Deuteronomy deny to the other prophets -- of this we have as witness Pseudo-Jonathan, who interprets that word thus: like me in the Holy Spirit. And lest anyone confidently maintain that the words "in the Holy Spirit" should not be joined with the preceding words "like me," but rather with the following words "will raise up for you," a comparison is at hand with verse eighteen, in which this same oracle, uttered long before by God, is reported in God's own words, which words this same Pseudo-Jonathan paraphrases thus: "I will raise up a prophet for them, ... in whom the Holy Spirit is as in you." In what manner the likeness between Moses and Christ is said to exist may be seen especially in Archelaus, Eusebius, Augustine, and Cyril of Alexandria, at the places cited. Sherlock and Lachais should also be consulted. Moreover, the principal titles by which Moses and Christ can be called similar are mediator, redeemer, and lawgiver.

13. Finally, the words "to him you shall listen" also aid us. What can be concluded from them in support of our position will shortly be evident, when we bring forth arguments from other passages of Scripture. In the meantime, note that the verb shama often means the same as "to obey," and always does so when, as occurs here, it is construed with the preposition el. Therefore, not only to hear that prophet but also to obey him were the Israelites commanded by this oracle. Cyril of Alexandria beautifully reasons from these words, in John ix, 29, arguing on the human level against the Pharisees who were uttering this: "We are disciples of Moses; we know that God spoke to Moses," John ix, 28, 29. Chrysostom moreover, on the Epistle to the Galatians II, draws from these same words the reason for explaining that saying of Paul: "For I through the law died to the law," Gal. ii, 19, inasmuch, namely, as the law of Moses commands obedience to Christ, who was going to abrogate it.

14. Fourthly, it is proved by other testimonies of Scripture. Christ thus reproached the Jews: "For if you believed Moses, you would perhaps believe Me also; for he wrote of Me," John v, 46. From these words of Christ, that this oracle pertains to Him, argue Cyprian, Archelaus, Epiphanius, Chrysostom, Cyril of Alexandria, and the author of the Consultatio of Zacchaeus and Apollonius. And they argue rightly. For as Tostatus observes, this is the only passage in which it can be said that Moses spoke of the Messiah in such a way that the words are Moses' own. For elsewhere in the Pentateuch there is indeed discourse about Christ, but nevertheless either under the veils of types or in the words of others rather than of Moses. But what is the chief point, and what adds every kind of force to this argument drawn from Christ's words, is the occasion on which Christ uttered them -- namely, when He was reproaching the Jews for refusing to believe in Him; for then He appealed, as it were, to Moses by what He immediately added: "Do not think that I shall accuse you before the Father; there is one who accuses you -- Moses," John v, 45. But unless Moses had established something in his laws concerning the faith to be given to the Messiah, there would be no ground for the appeal. Now where or when did Moses speak words about this? Unless you acknowledge that it is contained in that "him you shall hear, him you shall obey," you would search in vain throughout all of Moses.

15. Nor should the comparison be neglected that can be made between these words of Moses, "him you shall hear," and the words uttered by the Eternal Father when Christ was transfigured on the mountain, "hear Him," Matt. xvii, 5, Luke ix, 35. For Moses, in uttering that oracle, did nothing other than repeat what God had declared forty years earlier on Mount Horeb (cf. Deut. xviii, 15 with what follows); therefore those words "him you shall hear" are the very same words that God had spoken long before. And just as God had spoken them on Mount Horeb when He promised that His Son would come, so He spoke them again on that other mountain when He manifested that His Son had now arrived -- and in both cases Moses was present.

16. The Jews, astonished at seeing Christ's miracles, exclaimed: "This is truly the prophet, who is to come into the world," John vi, 14; vii, 40. Now one must attend especially to the force of the Greek article in these passages, from which Isidore of Pelusium argues that some singular prophet was being spoken of by those Jews; for this syntax has no place in the New Testament, nor perhaps in the Old, except when one particular prophet is mentioned. It is therefore evident that those Jews were expecting at that time some singular prophet; consequently, they knew that he had been foretold by God. Now that they were using the name "the prophet" as one of the names by which the Messiah had been foretold, everyone will easily persuade himself to believe: for they were not expecting anyone else, since no one else had been foretold. If this is so, we draw from it yet another argument. For if the Jews were expecting the Messiah foretold under this appellation of "the prophet," we must ask which of the ancient prophets called the Messiah by this name. None, assuredly, except Moses when he uttered this oracle.

17. Furthermore, the proof devised by Isidore of Pelusium is elegant and ingenious, with an argument drawn from another passage of Scripture. It is narrated in the Gospel that those who had been sent from Jerusalem to John the Baptist asked him whether he was "the prophet," John i, 21, and that John replied that he was not. But had not Zechariah, John's father, prophesied of him thus: "And you, child, shall be called prophet of the Most High," Luke i, 76? Isidore deftly resolves this knot: he was a prophet, but he was not the prophet. What objections could be raised against Isidore we shall address elsewhere. Origen had already written similar things, and Eusebius, Demonst. Evang. bk. IX, ch. ix.

18. Finally, the writers of the New Testament plainly teach us that the Jews of that age believed this oracle was spoken of the Messiah. For Peter, Acts iii, 22, and Stephen, ibid. vii, 37, when they wished to prove that Jesus was the Messiah, drew their argument from that oracle.

Section IV. The Indirect Proof

19. The matter seems capable of being settled by this argument as well. The prophet whom Moses foretells is none other than the one whom God foretold on Mount Horeb. But the prophet foretold by God is the Messiah. Therefore the prophet whom Moses foretold is none other than the Messiah.

20. The first proposition is entirely evident. Moses immediately gives the reason for his promise, Deut. xviii, 16, namely that this same thing had been promised by God at Horeb, in almost the same words with which he himself had just before foretold it (cf. ibid. 15 with 18, 19). For, as Cajetan observes on this passage, who handles this question admirably, surely the fact that Moses recalls the minds of his hearers to what the people had asked from God at Horeb can point to nothing other than this -- which God had promised the people on that very day when they assembled to hear the law (Exod. 20:1; 18, 19 cf. Deut. 5:2, 23-28), namely that those who were then present as Moses uttered this prophecy, and who heard that prophet proclaimed, might know well that Moses was promising no other prophet than the one whom God had already long ago promised at Horeb.

21. Moreover, not a few arguments confirm the second proposition, from among those by which we have directly proved our thesis; but there are also others besides. For the prophet whom God promised at Horeb cannot be any of the other prophets, nor the entire series of prophets taken together. For when He had then said that that prophet would be like Moses, roughly a year later He said that the other prophets were far unlike Moses because of the different manner in which He Himself was accustomed to deal with Moses as compared with them (Num. 12:2, 6-8). Lachais had already observed this after Sherlock. But if that prophet is none of the other prophets, who can he be but the Messiah?

22. Among the Fathers listed above, some -- whom it would perhaps have been better to enumerate here -- do not cite the words of verse fifteen, which are Moses' own, but those of verses eighteen and nineteen: that is, the words which God Himself uttered at Horeb. They understood, therefore, that the prophet whom God promised at Horeb is the Messiah.

23. But what proves it most powerfully are the circumstances of the events and the time at which that promise was made. Cajetan presses this argument wonderfully above all others, and Sherlock and Lachais also treat it well. It is worth quoting Cajetan's own words: "Weigh carefully, prudent reader, this circumstance concerning this prophet of whom there is question, namely that he is the prophet who was promised by God when He gave the law with His own voice on Mount Sinai, with all the people listening (for the whole commemoration of what the people said on the day when the people assembled to hear the law from the mouth of God tends toward this), so that you may draw from this that the discourse is not about a prophet in general, but about a prophet who is a bearer of law in the place of God. For they feared to hear God proceeding to further matters of the law, and therefore most fittingly... from that time God Himself is promised, who will address the people in human nature most gently, not in majesty... It would have been beside the point for God to reveal to Moses at that time that a prophet like Moses himself would come, unless He were speaking of a prophet who is a lawgiver. For this reason, namely the giving of law, fits all the things commemorated here by Moses. For it fits the petition of the people who dreaded further legislation from the mouth of God in majesty. It fits the subject matter that was being treated at that time. It fits the likeness to Moses. It fits the penalty attached for those who do not hear the voice of that prophet. It fits the imperfection of the old law that was to be perfected through the Messiah. It fits also the singularity and excellence of that prophet, revealed to Moses with such great ceremony, at such a time, and described in this text."

This interpretation is favored by Lactantius, Gregory of Nyssa, who writes: "For the phrase 'like you' indicates likeness chiefly in the establishing of laws"; Chrysostom; and Isidore of Pelusium, all of whom maintain, or even expressly teach, that Christ is foretold in this chapter of Deuteronomy as a new lawgiver. Moreover, Cyril of Alexandria declares: "When, therefore, the people who were being instructed at that time could not bear the spectacle of those things that prefigured the glory of God, at last the manner of the incarnation of the Only-Begotten was necessarily foretold," Against Julian, book VIII. Indeed, Chrysostom uses this oracle to prove that the old law was to be abrogated by Christ. It is, in truth, abundantly clear that this prophet is not foretold as one who predicts the future or reveals hidden things, but as one who delivers laws given by God.

24. We must therefore conclude that the prophet foretold by Moses is none other than the Messiah. Meanwhile, let us shrewdly consider that when Moses made this prediction, he was by no means issuing a new oracle, but was recalling to the minds of the Israelites what God Himself had already uttered. We ought not, therefore, to examine and explain this oracle as if it were connected with the circumstances that existed when Moses was recalling it, but as having been delivered by God at Horeb, as if it were actually read in that chapter of Exodus in which God is narrated to have first promulgated the law (Exod. 20 cf. Deut. 5:23, 31). From this it will further follow that many -- or at least the chief -- of the arguments heaped up against our view will collapse.

Section V. Objections Are Refuted

25. Diodorus of Tarsus raised this objection: "God the Word is not from among the brethren"; and since it belongs to a prophet to minister to the spirit, "to whom did God the Word render ministry?" But since these arguments accomplish nothing except to betray the errors of Diodorus in dividing Christ, we dismiss them without concern.

26. Faustus argued that the Messiah could not be called like Moses, or said to be as Moses, since Jesus, whom we believe to be the Messiah, was far more excellent than Moses. Hence the oracle does not pertain to the Messiah. Bonfrerius honestly revived this argument of Faustus. But Augustine already suggested what to reply to Faustus, instituting a comparison between Christ and Moses and drawing out their likeness from those very things on account of which Faustus said they were entirely unlike (Against Faustus, book XVI, ch. 15 ff.). To Bonfrerius, however, we reply that the excellence of one over another is indeed opposed to equality, but not to likeness between the two. And did not God Himself say that man was made in the likeness of God? Gen. 1:26.

27. The words kamoni, "like me," and kamokha, "like you" (Deut. 18:15, 18), should be understood as if it were said that the prophets who were being foretold would arise from the Israelite nation itself, just as Moses had arisen from it -- which is the interpretation of Solomon Jarchi (Rashi); or that they would be true prophets, just as Moses was a true prophet -- which is the view of Tostatus. Therefore, from these words we can draw no argument. Indeed, I do not deny that these words can be understood in this way, if we look at them in isolation from the whole discourse and from the circumstances of this prophecy. But this is precisely what must not be done -- they must not be considered in isolation, but quite the contrary. For we have already discussed that the meaning of what Moses said at the fords of the Jordan depends on the meaning of what God had said at Horeb. Therefore the meaning of the former word kamoni depends on the meaning of the latter kamokha. Let us therefore weigh this second word, about which the following points are to be made. First, the meaning that we find in this word is more obvious, inasmuch as it extends to many things that were the cause of Moses' great excellence, and not to some one particular thing that we must strive to guess by conjecture. Certainly, anyone reading verse eighteen for the first time would understand by that word a full likeness being signified, rather than some single one. Second, this word would seem entirely superfluous if it were to be taken in the sense of Solomon Jarchi or Tostatus. For God had already said that that prophet would arise from the same nation as Moses; and that he would be a true prophet -- who would even have thought of doubting it? Third, the opinion of the ancient Jews stands against both of those interpretations. Fourth, and finally, the circumstances of the events and the time in which God uttered the oracle also stand against both -- circumstances which, on the contrary, are most favorable to our view.

28. Since Christ is foretold many times in the Pentateuch, they say it is not necessary to take Christ's words, "for he wrote about Me," as referring solely to this oracle. But we have already dealt with this above, where we drew arguments from these words of Christ.

29. The argument that Isidore of Pelusium draws from the narrative of the delegation of priests and Levites to John seems to carry no weight, since in that narrative Christ is plainly distinguished from the prophet, so that there are two, not one (John 1:20, 21, 25). But consider this: that different questions, posed by different people, are gathered together in that narrative; for many had been sent by the Jews as delegates to John. What is surprising, then, if different people called the Messiah by different names (see also John 7:40, 41)? Moreover, it is possible that some among the common people, less skilled in the Sacred Scriptures, just as they erred in expecting Elijah himself, so also erred in thinking that the prophet and the Messiah were two different persons, rather than two names for one and the same person. Yet one would be wrong to conclude from this that such an error was common to all the Jews or to the greater part of them, and that therefore whatever we have brought forward in support of our case from the opinion of the Jews of that age carries less weight. You will say that those who interrogated John were priests and Levites, and that it would be proper to think they were well versed in the interpretation of oracles. But knowledge of the Scriptures belonged to the scribes, not to the priests and Levites, even though scribes could come from among them; indeed, it is clear enough that priests and Levites suffered from ignorance of the law even from this passage, as also from what they replied to Nicodemus when he spoke in Christ's defense (John 7:52 cf. 45, 50). Perhaps also those who interrogated John preferred to speak according to the opinion of certain uneducated people among the common folk. Finally, the argument that Isidore of Pelusium presses, drawn from the Greek article, is unanswerable, since from it a particular prophet was manifestly being commemorated at that time; and that this could have been no one other than the Messiah is equally apparent from the other arguments we have brought forward.

30. Tostatus also argued that the oracle pertains to the prophets, and more recently Rosenmuller argued it pertains to the prophets only, from its connection with what precedes and follows, that is, from the contrast with soothsayers and diviners, of whom there was question before, and the false and fictitious prophet, of whom there is question afterward. But against these interpretations stands what we have already urged should be kept in mind, namely that Moses was not promising something new, but repeating what God had promised at Horeb. But at Horeb there was no talk either of soothsayers or diviners, or of a false and fictitious prophet. We, however, interpret and press this oracle not as issued by Moses at the fords of the Jordan, but as uttered by God at Horeb. But, you will say, from the very fact that Moses recalled this promise of God and repeated it with reference to soothsayers and the false prophet, we must conclude that Moses himself understood that God had by no means promised the Messiah, or at least not him alone, but that a succession of prophets would not be lacking. But I ask: is it certain and established that that prophet was therefore promised because he would take the place of soothsayers for the Israelites? For what was to serve in their stead among that people was indicated by the preceding words, which in the Hebrew text read thus: "And you, the Lord your God has not so given to you," Deut. 18:14, from which, if we may draw any conclusion, God must be said not to be about to give something in the future, but to have already given it, when Moses was speaking these words. Now what He had given up to that time was the office of the high priest, whose duty it was to consult God and to report His responses. Although indeed, after prophets began to exist, God also gave responses through them, nevertheless, since the prophets acted by an extraordinary commission, it was not necessary that there be laws regarding this manner of consulting God, but the situation is different with respect to the high priest, who stood in the place of God by ordinary authority.

But if the Messiah had been foretold by Moses in this prophecy, Rosenmuller asks, "how would these things cohere: 'Do not go to soothsayers, for God will raise up for you the prophet Messiah'?" But first, let Rosenmuller allow us to delete that "for," which he himself has inserted; with that done, the necessity of that coherence disappears. But I in turn will ask him how the matters in the first part of this chapter concerning the sustenance of priests and Levites (Deut. 18:1-8) cohere with what is said about soothsayers in the second part (ibid. 9-14). If we see no coherence between these, why should such coherence be thought necessarily required between that second part and the third, in which the Messiah is discussed (ibid. 15-19), and between the third and the fourth, in which the fictitious prophet is discussed (ibid. 20-22)? But we can even grant Rosenmuller what he demands -- indeed, we can even retain that "for" -- since Lachais has already suggested what more we might reply to him, connecting the oracle with what precedes it thus: "It would be shameful for you to resort to soothsayers -- you, I say, for whom God is so ready to respond that He has also promised the greatest prophet," etc. Note moreover that Rosenmuller concedes what is established by other testimonies of the sacred writers (Acts 3:22; 7:37; John 6:14), namely that in Christ's time this oracle was understood as pertaining to the Messiah.

Wherefore we conclude that, since on the one hand there is nothing that truly compels us to assent to the interpretations of Tostatus or Rosenmuller, and on the other hand we have seen that there are very many things that compel us to recognize the Messiah alone as foretold in that oracle, this latter view is certainly to be preferred by us.

31. They say that the argument drawn from the fact that the people at Horeb had asked God not to speak to them Himself has no force; that on the contrary, this proves rather that God promised He would in the future address the Israelites through prophets, and not by Himself in person. But first, note that the people had by no means simply asked that God not speak to them, but only that He not do so in that manner and fashion -- that is, from the midst of fire, amid lightning and thunder, with such great terror for the listeners (see Deut. 5:23-27). Then, the people's request pertained only to that particular time and that promulgation of the law, not to a future age; for they were asking that Moses speak to them (ibid. 27; Exod. 20:19). Finally, God approved this wish of the people (Deut. 5:28; 18:17) because it proceeded from fear, and He certainly would not have approved it if the Israelites had been refusing any and every address from God Himself. When these points are carefully considered, the opposing arguments are easily refuted.

Section VI. What in This Oracle Would Portend That Jesus Would Be the Messiah

32. We have demonstrated that the Messiah, and no one other than He, was foretold in this oracle. But in order that an argument may be drawn from this same oracle to prove that this Messiah is Jesus, it is necessary that it furnish us some distinguishing mark that fits Jesus. This you have in the fact that that prophet is foretold as another Moses and a lawgiver like him. And that this was Jesus, who, having established a new law, abrogated the Mosaic law for the most part -- one must be blind not to see it.

III. Weights and Measures Among the Hebrews

The Bible provides us with some data for establishing the relative value of the different weights and measures; but their absolute evaluation is subject to great difficulties. Josephus is the only ancient author who can serve as our guide; for he often indicates the relationship of the Hebrew measures to those of the Greeks, which are better known to us. The New Testament and the Talmud also furnish us with some useful information. It might seem, in truth, that all these works are too modern for their data to be applied without reservation to ancient times; but if one reflects that weights and measures among the Hebrews were considered in some sense a sacred thing, because of their application to the dimensions of the sanctuary and its utensils, as well as to the offerings, libations, and sacred taxes, one will not find it implausible that the Jews, after the exile, sought to know exactly the ancient measures in order to conform strictly to the prescriptions of the Mosaic law. The sacred character attributed to weights and measures also follows from this verse of Proverbs (16:11): "The scales and just balances belong to the Lord: all the stones of the bag (the weights) are His work." For the evaluation of weights in particular, we have a nearly reliable basis in some Maccabean coins that still remain to us, and whose intrinsic value is known with fair precision, as will be seen below. We cannot engage here in a detailed discussion of this matter, and we shall content ourselves with summarizing the data drawn from the Bible, from the works of Josephus, and from the combined information of some other ancient documents. We shall speak successively of measures of length, distance, and capacity, of weights, and of coins.

The measures of length, called middoth, are generally borrowed from the hand and the arm; the following are mentioned: 1st, etsba (Jer. 52:21), the finger, that is, the width of the finger or thumb; 2nd, tephach (1 Kings 7:26), or tophach (Exod. 25:25), the palm, that is, the width of four fingers or the breadth of the palm; 3rd, zereth (ibid. 28:16), the distance from the tip of the thumb to that of the little finger, or the span; 4th, ammah, the whole length of the forearm, or the cubit. The relative value of these measures is nowhere indicated in the Bible; to fix it, one must consult Josephus and the rabbinical tradition. In Exodus (25:10), the Ark of the Covenant is given two and a half cubits in length, one and a half in width, and one and a half in height; Josephus, in his Antiquities (III, 6, 5), renders the two and a half cubits as five spans, and for one and a half cubits he puts three spans, from which it follows that the span was half a cubit. The rabbis agree with Josephus: according to them too, the zereth is a half-cubit; the standard cubit, they say, was six palms, and each palm was four fingers. We have every reason to believe these figures are exact, since we find the same proportions in other ancient systems; thus, for example, the Greeks had cubits of one and a half feet, which makes six palms, or twenty-four fingers; Herodotus (II, 149) also speaks of a cubit of six palms, in use among the Egyptians. We would therefore have the following table for the relative value of Hebrew measures:

Ammah 1. / Zereth 2. 1. / Tephach 6. 3. 1. / Etsba 24. 12. 4. 1.

It would suffice to know the absolute value of one of these measures to know the others; but since we find, in this regard, no positive data either in the writings of Josephus or in those of the rabbis, we must content ourselves with an approximate estimation, aided by the Egyptian measures, which modern discoveries have allowed us to determine with sufficient precision. It is very probable that the Hebrew system was borrowed from the Egyptians. The rabbis determine measures of length by the width of barley grains placed side by side, as the Arabs and other peoples of the East also do. Maimonides, who devoted himself to meticulous calculations on this subject, found that the etsba of the Bible was equal to the width of seven average barley grains, which gives 168 for the ammah. Now, it has been found, by fairly exact calculations, that the Arab cubit, estimated at 144 barley grains, reduced to lines and decimals of a line, came to 213.58, which would give for the Hebrew ammah of 168 barley grains approximately 560 millimeters. The ancient Mosaic cubit was undoubtedly the Egyptian royal cubit, and the various scales that remain of the latter give an average of approximately 525 millimeters. This being granted, we find for the common cubit 450 millimeters, or else 433.5, depending on whether one accepts the Egyptian ratio (7:6) or the Talmudic ratio (6:5). Each of the two cubits was divided proportionally into two spans, six palms, and twenty-four digits.

As for the measure called gomed, which is mentioned only once, in the book of Judges (III, 16), to specify the length of the weapon with which Ehud killed the king of Moab, it is entirely unknown; but since it is said that Ehud hid the weapon under his garments, and that it entered with the handle into the body of Eglon, one can hardly accept that the gomed was a longer cubit, and one will prefer, with the Greek version, to see in it a span, so that the weapon, which had two edges, would have been a kind of dagger. The kaneh (reed, rod) was a long staff of six cubits, serving to measure buildings (Ezek. XL, 5; XLI, 8), which was also done with a cord (ibid. XL, 3; Amos VII, 17).

To the measures of length are attached those of distances, or itinerary measures; but the ancient Hebrews measured roads in a very vague manner. We find in the Old Testament two itinerary measures, one of which is called Kibrath arets (space of land), the other derekh yom (a day's journey). It would appear that the first was a fixed measure; at least the Septuagint must have considered it so, for they preserve in their translation the Hebrew word, which they pronounce chabratha. The Syriac version always renders it as parasang, which would make approximately one French league. The derekh yom, that is to say the distance one can travel on foot in one day, is an equally vague measure; but it is found among several peoples of antiquity, and it is often used by Arab geographers of the Middle Ages. By comparing the various data from ancient authors and from the Arabs, we find for an average day's journey approximately seven leagues. Later, in the Greco-Roman period, the Jews counted by stadia and by miles; these measures are found in the New Testament and in the Talmud, as well as the sabbath day's journey (Acts 1:12), which was two thousand cubits. For land surveying, the ordinary measure was the tsemed, whose exact value cannot be determined.

II. The measures of capacity, called Mesuroth, were of two kinds.

a) For liquids three different measures are mentioned: 1st, the bath contained, according to Josephus, 72 xestes (sextarii), which make one Attic metretes, or 38 liters and 843 milliliters; 2nd, the hin, which Josephus compares to two Attic congii; now, since the congius is one-twelfth of the metretes, the hin would be one-sixth of the bath, or 6 liters 474 milliliters; 3rd, the log, which is not determined anywhere by Josephus, amounts, according to the rabbis, to the volume of six eggs, or one-twelfth of the hin, which makes exactly one xestes, or approximately 539 milliliters.

b) For dry goods we find the following measures: 1st, the homer (Lev. XXVII, 16), according to Ezekiel (XLV, 11, 14) equal to 10 baths. The same measure is also called cor; and its half lethech (Hosea III, 2). 2nd, The ephah, one-tenth of the homer, and consequently equal to the bath (Ezek. XLV, 11). 3rd, The seah, according to the rabbis, one-third of an ephah; likewise according to Josephus, who says that the saton (seah) is equal to one and a half Italian modii, which makes 21 xestes or one-third of the bath and the ephah. 4th, The omer (gomor) is the tenth part of the ephah (Exod. XVI, 36); this is why it is also called issaron, tenth. 5th, The kab, according to the rabbis, one-sixth of the seah, or one-eighteenth of the ephah, that is, four Attic xestes.

To show at a glance the relative value of all the measures of capacity, both for liquids as for dry goods, we represent them in the following table, taking the smallest, the log, as the common unit of measure:

Homer 1. / Bath/Ephah 10. 1. / Seah 30. 3. 1. / Hin 60. 6. 2. 1. / Omer 100. 10. 3 1/3. 1 2/3. 1. / Kab 180. 18. 6. 1 4/5. 1. / Log 720. 72. 24. 12. 7 1/5. 4. 1.

Since we know that the value of the bath is exactly that of an Attic metretes (38.843 liters), we can easily calculate that of all the other measures. The identity of the bath and the Attic metretes is not coincidental; both are nothing other than the ancient artaba of the Egyptians, which also contained 72 xestes.

This table shows in the Hebrew measures the mixture of two different systems; the ephah is in a decimal ratio with the homer and the omer, while it forms a duodecimal system with the other measures. The latter appears to be the primitive system, for we find it also in the measures of length.

III. Weight, called mishkal, was determined as among us, by means of balances (moznaim) or a steelyard (peles). The weights were made of stone, and merchants carried them in a pouch attached to the belt, which is still done in the East today. Here are the different weights of the Hebrews: 1st, the kikkar, talent; 2nd, the maneh, mine; 3rd, the shekel, sicle; 4th, the beka; and 5th, the gerah. The relative value of all these weights, with the exception of the maneh, is clearly indicated in the Pentateuch. It follows from a passage in Exodus (XXXVIII, 25, 26) that 100 kikkars and 1,775 shekels made 603,550 bekas, and that the beka was a half-shekel, from which it follows that the kikkar had 3,000 shekels. The shekel was divided into twenty gerahs (ibid. XXX, 13). On the maneh, opinions are divided: some give the maneh one hundred shekels; others reduce the maneh to 60 shekels, invoking the authority of Ezekiel (XLV, 12); others finally have recourse to the Alexandrian version, according to which the value of the mine would be reduced to 50 shekels. But the Greek version of this verse being itself very corrupt and uncertain in the manuscripts and editions, we do not think that its authority can prevail over that of the text, for which we believe we should adopt the interpretation of the Vulgate. Josephus, who fixes the mine at two and a half Roman pounds, agrees well with those who attribute to it the weight of 60 shekels.

The relative value of the different weights is summarized as follows:

Kikkar 1. / Maneh 50. 1. / Shekel 3,000. 60. 1. / Beka 6,000. 120. 2. 1. / Gerah 60,000. 1,200. 20. 10. 1.

IV. Coins are originally nothing other than pieces of metal of a determined weight, marked with a sign generally recognized in commerce. The question of whether the Hebrews, before the Babylonian exile, had coins in the sense we attach to this word, cannot be completely resolved. The silence maintained by the books of the ancient Hebrews about minted money is not sufficient to deny its existence. It is true that no trace of Egyptian coins belonging to the time of the Pharaohs has been found, nor of Phoenician coins dating back beyond the Persian invasion; but, on the other hand, it is reported that Phidon, tyrant of Argos, around the year 750 before the Christian era, was the first to strike coins in Greece, according to a system of weights and measures borrowed from the Phoenicians. Is it not therefore very probable that the Phoenicians, at that time, knew of coinage and made use of it in their commerce? However that may be, it is certain that the Hebrews had, from the most ancient times, if not coins properly so called, at least pieces of silver marked with a sign and current among merchants (Gen. XXIII, 16). Mention is made not only of pieces of one shekel, but also of half-shekels (Exod. XXX, 13), quarter-shekels (I Sam. IX, 8), and small pieces of silver called agora (ibid. II, 36), which were probably of the weight of a gerah.

But what is the absolute value of these pieces? Several scholars have made the entirely gratuitous assumption that the gerah was the same thing as the keration of the Greeks and the siliqua of the Romans, that is, a carob seed. Eighteen of these seeds weighed, according to Eisenschmid, approximately 87 to 88 French grains, from which it was concluded that the shekel, which had 20 gerahs, weighed approximately 96 grains. This result, whose complete falseness we are about to recognize, has been generally accepted. We have, however, a much more reliable basis in the shekels that remain to us from the era of the Maccabees, notably from Prince Simon, who, from the first year of his reign, had national currency minted. That this prince and high priest adopted for his coins the system of Mosaic weights is beyond doubt; his shekels, on which one reads on one side the words shekel of Israel, and on the other Holy Jerusalem, must have corresponded exactly to the sacred shekel. Now the weight of the surviving shekels of Simon varies from 256 grains to 271.75; most have 266 to 268 grains. It is now demonstrated that the Aeginetan system, that is, the one that Phidon had borrowed from the Phoenicians, was also that of the Babylonians, the Syrians, and the Hebrews. The Attic talent, which weighs 493,200 French grains, is to the Aeginetan talent as 3 to 5, and the latter, consequently, weighs 822,000 grains. This talent was divided into 3,000 didrachms, just as the Hebrew talent into 3,000 shekels; the normal weight of the Aeginetan didrachm was therefore, like that of the shekel, 274 grains, which agrees perfectly with the actual weight of the shekels of Simon.

It remains to see how this result accords with the data of Josephus and the New Testament. Josephus says that the shekel was worth four Attic drachms. In the Gospel of Saint Matthew (XVII, 24), the half-shekel tax is assessed at two drachms, and the stater, which was worth four drachms, was sufficient for Jesus and Saint Peter to pay their tax; now, four Attic drachms weigh 328.8 grains, while the normal weight of the shekel is only 274 grains. But the difficulty disappears if one reflects that at a time when Jewish coins had become very rare, the value of the shekel must have risen considerably, and that moreover one always paid the half-shekel tax in whatever currency was current, provided its real value was not below that of the ancient shekel.

What we have just said is entirely confirmed by the rabbis. Maimonides states positively that the shekel of the Second Temple weighed 384 barley grains, while the ancient Mosaic shekel weighed only 320. What results positively from Maimonides' datum is that the ancient shekel was to the new one as 5 to 6, and this ratio is exactly the same as that which we find between the Aeginetan didrachm, or the Hebrew shekel (274 gr.), and the Attic tetradrachm (328.8 gr.). We will further observe that Saint Jerome also makes the shekel equal to the stater or tetradrachm, which, according to him, is equivalent to half a Roman ounce; now, since the Roman pound has twelve ounces, the two and a half pounds that Josephus gives to the Hebrew mine make thirty ounces; from which it follows that Josephus, like the rabbis, gives the mine sixty shekels.

According to all that we have set forth, the absolute value of Hebrew weights can be fixed as follows:

1 gerah weighed 13.7 grains. / 1 beka 137. / 1 shekel 274. / 1 maneh 16,440. / 1 kikkar 822,000.

The shekel in coinage could have been worth approximately 3 francs 10 centimes.

Such is, in summary, the system of measures, weights, and coinage of the Hebrews, which, as demonstrated by the learned researches of Boeckh, is but a branch of the general system that passed from Babylonia into southwestern Asia, into Egypt, and into Greece, and rests entirely upon the cubic foot of the Babylonians.
