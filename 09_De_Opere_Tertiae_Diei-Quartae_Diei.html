ON THE WORK OF THE THIRD DAY

(Verses 9, 10, 11, 12)

XLIV. Gathering of Waters; Appearance of Dry Land

Moses now sets before us a twofold work of God: for he relates that the waters were gathered together into one place, that part of the earth was laid bare and a dwelling-place prepared for the habitation of future living creatures; and then that the earth was clothed with an abundance and profusion of plants.

God commands: Let the waters under the heaven be gathered together into one place, verse 9 in Hebrew, which the Vulgate renders as those which are under the heaven, as also the Septuagint (and the Targum of Jonathan: Let the lower waters which were left below under the heavens be gathered). Mazzocchi thinks that the Hebrew word which is rendered "let them be gathered" could more faithfully be rendered, though in poor Latin, "let them be hollowed out" (that is, let them be stored in a hollow) the waters... in one place. Let the dry land appear. Land not covered by waters is called by the Latins also "dry ground." Virgil: "Sea-birds play on dry ground." Pliny: "Neither in the sea nor on dry ground." The Latin Church, in Psalm 94, provides the reading indiscriminately: "And his hands formed the dry land. And his hands founded the arid ground."

Many believe, such as Petavius and Mazzocchi, that the phrase into one place, as the Vulgate says with the Hebrew, or into one collection (synagogen), as the Septuagint has, should be understood distributively -- that is, each collection of waters should flow into some one place; and if there are several collections, all water nonetheless should settle in a definite and predetermined place. Basil taught that, since there are many collections of waters, Moses speaks of the principal and greatest of all; he adds that any lakes and pools are not properly collections of waters, but those which are greater, including those which, like the Caspian, are called seas, flow together into the Ocean through open or hidden and subterranean channels, and mingle with it. So also Theodoret, Procopius, and Bede. Nevertheless, it is very probable that in that first time when the dry land appeared -- that is, when certain lands were uncovered and stripped of waters -- it is very probable, I say, that all the great collections of waters communicated with one another and thus were gathered into one place and one congregation, with perhaps some small pool excepted.

To verse 9 the Septuagint adds: And the water which was under the heaven was gathered together into its congregations, and the dry land appeared; which reading the Greeks accept, and with them Ambrose. But this appendix is absent from the Hebrew text and the Samaritan, as from the Vulgate and other versions; and it is only implicitly asserted in those words, and it was so.

That God called the collection of waters "seas" is found in the Hebrew of verse 10. The Vulgate renders it in the plural as "congregations": so also the Septuagint as systemata, collections or confluences of waters. The Hebrews also called lakes "seas," whether the water was fresh or salt, such as the Dead Sea and the Sea of Galilee; which other nations sometimes also did. (Footnote: Mazzocchi, Spicileg. biblic., vol. I, pp. 22-24.)

XLV. What Caused the Waters to Recede

Let us now inquire what was the cause or occasion by which the waters receded and the dry land appeared. Was the cause merely a single command of God, "at the voice of whose thunder the waters trembled and fled," as the Psalmist poetically says (Psalm 104:7-8), while "the mountains ascended and the plains descended to the place which he had established for them"? Certainly those effects had no other first cause. But that this command of God was entirely distinct from the laws by which he governs the world (which would mean it was a miracle) -- this does not seem necessary, and therefore according to the accepted rules of the wise should not be admitted. Some say that the sacred text indicates as the sole instrument of the works of creation the divine commands: "Let there be, let them be gathered"; and they add that God, unlike a human being, does not need external means for acting. It should be noted first that we are not dealing here with creation: for nothing in this work is said to have been created. While Moses indicates the first cause, which alone was suited to the end he had in view, he certainly does not deny that within some law of God (namely within some uniform and permanent volition of God, by which he produced many other effects afterwards and indeed still produces them now) this particular volition was included. God certainly does not need external means; but we should not be asking "what the divine power could accomplish as a miracle of its might," but rather what he who "disposes all things sweetly" is accustomed to do. It surely seems more worthy of God's wisdom that he, through laws established by himself in the beginning and co-created with matter, first brought it about that the newly-born earth was covered with waters, so that while it lay hidden beneath them it received great increases from the sediments of the water; and that afterwards, now these, now those parts of the earth emerged from the waters, so that they could sustain plants and terrestrial animals. When I conceive things in this way, I am compelled to exclaim: "In wisdom you have made all things" (Psalm 104:24). See how many effects proceed from few and simple laws!

Behold how, by one volition of God, all things proceed toward their goal. "This view" (I use the words and teaching of Suarez, On the Work of the Six Days, II, ch. VII) "is more philosophical and more conformable to reason, and it should be adhered to more firmly, when Scripture does not compel otherwise." The same author teaches: "One should not have recourse to the first cause when effects can be reduced to secondary causes" (On Angels, book I, no. 8).

On the other hand, if I were to say that only by some special divine command was the earth covered with waters, and that afterwards by another divine command contrary to the first the earth itself emerged from the waters, without any cause or any useful effect of this washing appearing -- I would indeed fear that I might expose Scripture to the mockeries of the wise men of this world, which we have already heard must be especially guarded against; but it is useful to hear more.

Scripture attributes natural effects to God: truly indeed and philosophically. For all effects which do not arise from created will must necessarily arise from the will of the Creator alone. And this doctrine suffices abundantly for most people: for those who have learned and hold it are far superior both to those who formerly, "attending to the works, did not recognize who was the artisan; but thought either fire, or spirit, or swift air, or the circuit of the stars, or excessive water, or the sun and moon to be the gods ruling the world" (Wisdom 13:2); and also to more recent pseudo-philosophers, who, having returned through the paths of the sciences to the old ignorance, think fire, water, the sun, universal attraction, affinity, and other laws of nature to be the rulers of the world and the universe -- laws which, when they reject the Creator, are effects without a cause and laws without a lawgiver. However, what suffices for faith does not also suffice for science, whose task is to endeavor to refer those effects which certainly did not arise from any particular volition of God (such as creation and miracles) to some general law established by him. Timaeus in Cicero teaches (Cicero, Timaeus, ch. XIV) that "he who professes himself a lover of intelligence and wisdom" must necessarily seek out both the primary causes of nature and then the secondary ones. And indeed of this science, wherever some connection exists between natural and supernatural things, the Theologian has need, so that he may be capable of refuting those who contradict and resist the truth; so that he may show that even in the sacred sciences (although the dogmas are immutable, because they are truth) there is "progress; so that what was formerly believed more obscurely may be understood more clearly through his exposition; so that posterity through him may rejoice in understanding what antiquity, not understanding it, venerated; yet teaching the same doctrines it has learned, so that when it speaks in a new way, it does not say new things" (see Vincent of Lerins, Commonitorium, chapters 27-28).

XLVI. Geologists Agree with Moses

We return to the recession of the waters, in which matter it happily occurs that geologists generally agree with Moses. For from their observations they deduce that the earth was once covered with waters, and that afterwards certain disturbances occurred on account of which "mountains emerged from the straits, and the world leaped forth through the waves" (Manilius); and the strata formed from the sediments of water, which had originally been almost horizontal, were for the most part elevated and tilted, and received an oblique and even vertical direction. De Luc supposed that there had been immense caverns in the earth, and that when the rocks forming the upper vaults that supported them broke, those vaults fell below the water level; then the waters, seeking the lowest points, descended into the cavities, and the higher parts of the ground that had been under water raised their head above it: "Places grow as the waves diminish" (Ovid, Metamorphoses I).

More probable and more widely accepted now is the opinion that mountains and other land masses ascended from below by the force of subterranean fire, whose very great power is most certain. There are many examples from historical times, including recent ones, in which lands are observed rising from below, sometimes rapidly, sometimes gradually, and not infrequently through water. To recount these and at the same time set forth the foundations of this doctrine (by which the phenomena indicated in section 18 are easily explained, as no one fails to see), and to investigate the physical cause by which subterranean heat is stimulated to produce these effects, is not our task. I acknowledge that geologists teach from observations that such elevations occurred not just once but many times, and that those which are more firmly established by observations were subsequent to the creation of certain living things (section 18). But Scripture does not say that all the earth which is now dry land emerged from the waters on the third day. Moses, who is not writing a history of the vicissitudes of the globe, mentions only that event which constitutes a single epoch of the greatest importance in the history of the earth, as one which greatly advanced the earth toward its proper perfection.

For it was then that dry land properly so called had its beginning: it was then that for the first time a place was prepared for plants, which are so necessary for animals and for man himself. The later elevations, with which geologists are concerned, do not pertain to the third day.

Before this elevation recorded by Moses, was there a similar elevation of strata? If there was any, it was small, and did not raise the earth above the waters. Moses speaks of the one in which the dry land appeared and in which it could first be said:

And the sea contracts, and there is a field of dry sand / Where just now was the deep; and the mountains stand forth / Which the deep sea had covered. (Ovid, Metamorphoses II.)

That mountains existed under the waters before the earth raised its head above the waves seems to be indicated by Psalm 104: "The waters stood (that is, were standing) above the mountains. At your rebuke they fled; at the voice of your thunder they trembled." But to explain these things I do not think it necessary to have recourse to elevations of the earth caused by the force of fire. Even before these occurred, in that part of the globe which had already acquired a solid state, one may suppose that certain tracts were more elevated than the rest (as also happens in fruits): and these tracts could rightly be called mountains. More probably, however, it seems that those mountains standing under the waters were nothing other than lands that were to be elevated into mountains.

XLVII. Evening of the Third Day; Volcanic Hypothesis

The third day was probably illuminated by a feeble light, as I said of the second day (section 39): it is probable that this light was somewhat less feeble, since many opaque or semi-opaque particles had been precipitated from the atmosphere.

If it is necessary to find a darkness that could be called the evening of this day, we shall indicate it by a not absurd hypothesis. A great force from below, acting over a sufficiently extended area of earth and capable of elevating it above the waters, if it operated at one and approximately the same time in many parts of the earth widely separated from each other, the material ejected from below into the atmosphere could have produced a certain obscurity, as often happens in some part of the air when the mouths of volcanoes erupt. Besides the ejected material, we may also reckon a great force of vapor then raised up, and with the vapor innumerable heterogeneous particles which were in the water at that time. Nor is it necessary to suppose complete darkness: for as I have already said, darkness or night is not mentioned here, but only evening.

Nor does it lack probability that at the very beginning of the Genesiac fourth, fifth, and sixth days, there were three revolutions in the earth, and from these new elevations proceeded, still greater than those which this third period brought about. Geologists have observed that these disturbances of the earth, as they are less ancient, produced greater effects; and also that either before these disturbances, or as a result of them, certain changes of great importance occurred in the water and in the atmosphere, by which these media were made suitable for the existence and nourishment of new classes of living things. It certainly seems very probable that some change occurred in the atmosphere before the fourth period, by which the upper part of the air was made transparent and permitted the sight of the stars: "And he showed the lands to the sky and the heavens to the lands" (Ovid, Metamorphoses I); likewise that the fifth and sixth periods began with not inconsiderable changes: for we see that in these periods both the lands and the waters were arranged for the preservation of various classes of animals. It is therefore not absurd to suppose that the works of each of these three days were preceded by an obscurity produced by a great quantity of condensed vapors, sand, and other particles erupting from below: I always mean obscurity, not night, of which there is no mention in the history of these days. The dispersion of this haze and the restoration of light can be called morning; and thus perhaps the phrase "and there was evening, and there was morning" in these days can be explained. I am not unaware that from geological observations, after living things began to exist on the earth, not only four but more elevations of land seem to need to be admitted; indeed it is probable that during the fifth and sixth periods the elevations were greater than the one in which the dry land appeared, or the other which we may conjecture occurred before the stars appeared. But nothing prevents three of the revolutions following the first (that of the third day) from having determined epochs in the history of the earth (on account of the effects of the greatest importance which preceded them) worthy of record; and although they may not have elevated greater mountains than other intermediate or subsequent ones, yet they may have been more universal, as having occurred at the same time in many places, and therefore having generated a more widespread obscurity, perhaps also more intense from the quantity and nature of the exhalations. Let it suffice to have indicated these things in this place, nor shall I add anything more about the obscurity of the following days.

XLVIII. The Earth Brings Forth Plants

When the earth had emerged from the waters, at God's command it produces grasses and trees: "Let the earth bring forth grass.... and the fruit tree.... And the earth brought forth grass... and the tree yielding fruit, and having each its seed according to its kind" (verses 11, 12). Life begins on the earth: plants present themselves to us in the first place among living things, if we ascend in the scale of existing beings from the less perfect to the more perfect; and they are the first recorded in Scripture to have inhabited the earth and to have given it, so to speak, the appearance and aspect of a living thing. Moses, who does not set forth a nature history complete in every detail, makes no mention of aquatic plants nor of terrestrial ones which lack at least a conspicuous seed. It is credible that the first existence or origin of many of those species should be referred to this third period. Indeed, that class of the plant kingdom which is constituted by the so-called vascular cryptogams appears from the observations of scholars to be more ancient than the rest. Plants of this kind entirely lack flowers and exhibit simpler organs in place of fruit. Moreover, marine plants have never been found in those more ancient formations which contain terrestrial plants. In the calcareous rock underlying fossil coal, Adolphe Brongniart found more species of terrestrial plants than marine ones, and the latter from only one genus.

This same scholar deduces from his investigations that the first living things on earth were plants: from this it seems to be deduced that in that first period of vegetation there was a much greater amount of carbonic acid in the atmosphere than there is now; and that therefore the air, while most suitable for promoting plant growth, was at that time quite unfit for the respiration of animals. Thus the newly-born earth was clothed with plants, and the plants found in the air so prepared a rich nourishment. In this way it is explained why the creation of the plant kingdom preceded, and why plants are older than animals. Indeed, divine Providence, "disposing all things sweetly" and acting in its proper time, is to be believed to have deferred the formation of animals, especially those which breathe free air, until that time when the atmosphere became more suitable for respiration. And since God willed that things be brought to completion successively, it is easily believed that plants, on which animals live and grow, were brought to light and sufficiently propagated before the origin of the latter. I add that those first cryptogamous plants seem less suited to the nourishment of terrestrial animals, on account of the rigidity of their leaves and the lack of succulent fruits and grains yielding flour.

Noteworthy is what the distinguished Gaudichaud read in 1842 at the Paris Academy of Sciences: "God, having created the earth, willed to make it fruitful..... The most powerful intellects of the old and more recent age agree on this, that plants preceded animals, and that the earth was entirely covered with them before the latter appeared -- the same thing which Theology transmitted from age to age in the history of the seven epochs or divine days of creation. Certain philosophers of our time, demonstrating that man has not left traces of his existence in the more ancient formations; others, that the simpler things among plants preceded those which have a more complicated constitution, have added the calculation of science to the great epochs of former times. Every century brings its own advances, and every progress of the human spirit brings a new argument for the confirmation of eternal truth."

Marcellus de Serres writes (Cosmogony of Moses, 1838, p. 69): "According to Moses, as well as according to geological observations, life on earth began with plants and first with herbaceous plants. At least that great historian consistently names grass before trees, although trees attract the eyes far more than a small blade of grass. This truth, therefore, which has now been demonstrated only after many centuries of observations, he held -- namely, that living things preceded one another in the inverse ratio of the complexity of their structure. This succession of plants, bearing a relation to this complexity, is very noteworthy. It is remarkable to find it in a book as ancient as Genesis; for otherwise it has not been known for more than half a century at most."

We shall speak of a certain doubt concerning the earlier existence of plants when we come to treat of the origin of animals.

XLIX. Fossil Evidence for the First Vegetation

From consideration of the fossils of the plant kingdom which are found in the more ancient strata, it seems to be gathered that the land then left by the waters formed only certain islands, sometimes joined together in an archipelago; and that the temperature was higher than it now is in the warmest parts of the earth, and more uniform across various regions, as the uniformity of vegetation shows. Ferns, arborescent horsetails, and arborescent club-mosses then abounded, to which hardly anything similar is now found, and only in the warmer zones. These plants are more abundant and larger where the climate is warmer and wetter. At that time, however, the heat of the earth's surface was chiefly to be attributed to the internal temperature of the globe, and was almost independent of the sun, and therefore scarcely subject to daily variations. Hot springs and exhalations of warm vapor, probably more frequent, together with mists, must have moderated the nighttime cooling radiation of the earth. It is well established that plants thrive best in a moist, warm, and constant temperature. "In the earliest times after the creation of organic beings, the earth, which consisted of not very elevated islands, was covered with an immense abundance of plants: the variety among them was not great: no mammal, no bird; in a word, no animal enlivened these dense forests." So Adolphe Brongniart (Considerations on the Nature of the Plants which Covered the Surface of the Earth at Various Epochs of its Formation, Institute of the Academy of Sciences, Paris, vol. XVI, p. 423).

Some object that plants need sunlight to gain strength, and especially for their leaves to turn green. I note in passing that no one among us has seen those green leaves. In the Vulgate indeed we read: Let the earth bring forth green grass; but the word "green" does not appear in the Septuagint, which renders it "grass for pasture," nor in the Syriac, Arabic, Samaritan, or Persian versions, nor in Onkelos and Jonathan. Learned men think the Hebrew should rather be interpreted as tender or small grass, that is, a little herb.

For a fuller response, much could be adduced from physics. It will suffice, however, to point out that I do not exclude sunlight from the third day onward (sections 39 and 48), although I deny with Moses that the face of the sun appeared to the earth before the fourth day. Now feeble rays of the sun passing through clouds have sufficient power to make leaves turn green and to serve for vegetation: indeed they accomplish this better than the rays of fire, certainly brighter, which we use for heating or illumination. In fact, in the densest forests and in their center, where the direct rays of the sun never penetrate, we see green trees. And in those regions where the face of the sun is almost always veiled by cloud, the leaves nonetheless turn green.

It seems superfluous to inquire whether all plants of the same species arise from one individual, or from two (with respect to those species in which the organs of reproduction are not in one and the same individual, as for example in palms). The same question, as far as brute animals are concerned, can be asked. Linnaeus indeed maintained that "in the beginning of things, from every species of living things a single pair of sexes was created" (Philosophia Botanica). But this cannot easily be demonstrated, although it does not seem improbable; for God does not customarily do directly by himself what he can obtain through laws established by himself -- such as the increase and diffusion of living things. In Genesis certainly we read nothing regarding these matters. (Footnote: Romano, The Science of the Interior Man, vol. III, Theology. Naples, 1849, p. 385.)

L. The Limits of Science at the Origin of Life

The divine book teaches only that God commanded: "Let the earth bring forth grass (more precisely, in the literal sense, let the earth grow green with little herbs) making seed and the fruit tree," etc. And, with nature obeying the Creator, he adds that grasses and trees were produced from the earth. Nor does human science teach us more regarding the origin of plants. Science itself, unless it wishes to admit effects without their reason, must seek in the command and will of the Creator the first cause of life and of plants. By its own investigations it confirms that the earth before the origin of animals was clothed with plants and was luxuriant: it endeavors to determine something about the relative age of certain families of the plant kingdom: but when we demand of it that it explain to us the manner in which the first plants arose, it falls silent, and, eloquent in its very silence, as though pointing a finger toward heaven, it proclaims that this must be sought from another source.

And though it is talkative enough or more than enough when inquiry is made into the formation of celestial bodies, or of the sea, or of mountains -- if it is questioned about a mustard seed, or a little flower of the meadow, or a tiny blade of grass, its pride is humbled, as if it heard these small but wonderful creatures saying to it: "Thus far you shall come and no farther, and here you shall break your swelling waves" (Job 38:11). Now, as Cicero says (On Old Age), "the earth, having warmed the seed in its embrace, spreads it forth and draws out of it a sprouting greenness, which, supported by the fibers of the stems, gradually grows." But whence is the seed, if not from a plant? And whence does the plant spring, if not from a seed? What then came first, the seed or the plant? This is that old question, at once serious and humorous: whether the chicken or the egg existed first. "So also regarding animals," says Augustine (On the Literal Meaning of Genesis, book V, ch. 23), "it may be uncertain whether the seeds came from the animals or the animals from the seeds: whichever of these came first, however, it is most certain that it came from the earth."

Therefore up to this point, aided by the analogy of what we see and of what men accomplish in physical experiments on a far smaller scale, and also by conjecturing that those very great and most ancient phenomena may have been certain consequences of the laws of nature and, as it were, particular cases of the present order and course of things -- aided, I say, by these helps, we have tried to understand something of the first history of the world. But now analogy and experiments fail, the laws of nature fall silent; nothing at all is given us to comprehend about the beginning of life by these means. We do not now see life generated except by living things: and therefore regarding its first origin, from the sciences which rely on the observation of things, we can hope for absolutely nothing. To transform brute matter into organic matter, except through some living body, we simply do not know how. Therefore natural science can conclude nothing else except that God willed plants and seeds, or seeds and plants, to be made from created matter; and so it was done.

Augustine thought, as we have already seen in section 7, that on this day the earth germinated "only causally and potentially grass and trees," that is, "it received the power of producing." What are usually called forces or powers implanted in nature by God are themselves "numerous principles incorporeally interwoven with corporeal things" (De Genes. ad litt., book IV, ch. XXXIII), or intimate principles "which, as if seminally, God scattered in the act of creation, when He spoke and they were made" -- a doctrine that is rather obscure to me. For if these principles are the laws by which the Creator even now produces natural effects (when, for example, He produces new plants, or multiplies species through seeds), as Augustine's second definition seems to suggest, it is not understood why these laws no longer produce those first effects, namely either making plants without seed produce seed, or seeds without plants. Nor does that merely causal and invisible germination seem sufficiently conformable to the sacred text: for after the divine command it immediately says: "And the earth brought forth," etc.

But, if I am not mistaken, some small modification of this opinion reconciles it beautifully both with the text of Genesis and with geological observations, which are to be regarded as a kind of natural commentary on that text. Let us therefore establish the matter thus. God in His eternity decreed the creation of plants, as of all other things: but He promulgated this decree, so to speak, on the third day, with His own words, that is, by an efficacious command, when He produced the first plants from the earth. The vegetable kingdom therefore arose at that time, and the earth first brought forth those plants for which the climate of that time, those terrestrial conditions, those atmospheric conditions that then existed, were suitable, and it brought them forth by the power of the command it had received from the Creator, perhaps also through some secondary cause which He Himself had prepared, if by some means unknown to us He disposed the earth for this effect. While these things were happening, can we not say that the remaining species were simultaneously created in their cause and produced potentially and seminally, for which germination itself was reserved until some other period from the following ones had arrived and simultaneously brought with it those circumstances that would be more favorable to their life and future propagation? Among those very species that were actually established on the third day, some individual plants existed actually, while all the others existed only causally, the production and germination of them being deferred to another time. Therefore the effects of the divine command extend themselves beyond this period; nor is this to be said only of the second command in verses 11 and 12, but also of that in verse 9, by which the uncovering of the dry land is commanded: for we cannot doubt that even after the third period, lands emerged from the waters; and it is highly probable that this happened through those very causes by which the first lands were uncovered, and by the force of those laws which, having been ordained by the Creator from eternity, were made manifest on this day. Therefore, in the same manner as regarding plants, it will be permissible for us to affirm regarding the lands that some were actually elevated above the water on this day, while the rest were elevated only causally and invisibly on this very day.

I know that Augustine does not approve (De Genes. ad litt., book VI, ch. IV), "if perhaps someone should say that not every kind of tree was created on the third day, but that something was deferred which would be created on the sixth, when man was made and established in paradise." "In vain," he says, "were all things so carefully distinguished through each day, if any suspicion of mixing of days can remain, so that we should believe some trees were also created on the sixth day, though grass and trees had been assigned to the third day, which Scripture was silent about on the sixth day itself." Nor would I praise the one who would posit a double creation of plants, because speech about plants established by God occurs again in chapter II, 4 ff. But neither, I believe, would Augustine disapprove of one who posited a single creation of living things, and that wholly on the third day, indeed potentially made, but also actually begun, and continued and developed through the following days; this, I say, Augustine would not disapprove, if he could see with us that this doctrine is deduced from the observation of nature. Those who properly understand these things and follow the Augustinian interpretation of the Hexaemeron must necessarily distribute the formation of plants over a very long period of time. They, however, learned from Augustine himself that on the third day all were made causally; but afterward "visibly in the work pertaining to the course of time, just as He continues to work until now."

Anyone can easily see that in the works of this day there was evening after morning, in the force of the Hebrew words (section 11) carefully considered. The mixing (erev) was of water with earth, when the earth did not have its own distinct place, and the waters covered and saturated (as Sirach XXXIX, 28 says: "the cataclysm saturated the dry land") the dry land; likewise the mixing between the matter that was to be formed into plants and that which was to remain in the state of brute matter: therefore no distinction between organic and inorganic matter. This mixing was followed by a double division (boqer), an opening and a birth of firstborn things. The sea was divided from the dry land: the sea was opened in many places, and it appeared to give birth to the first lands: again the earth was opened in very many places, and brought forth the firstborn of plants into the light, and for the first time organic matter was distinguished from inorganic matter in the earth.

LI. Genesis II, 4-6: Recapitulation of the Third Day

In order that we may fully grasp Moses's teaching about the work of the third day and about the formation of plants, it remains for us to investigate a notable passage from Genesis II, and indeed a difficult one. After Moses described the work of six days in chapter I, immediately in the following chapter, gathering everything together in summary, he says in verse 4: "These are the generations of heaven and earth (that is, the birth or formation of heaven and earth), when they were created, in the day when the Lord God made heaven and earth." These words do not designate the first day, as some have thought: for the creation of heaven and earth was before any day, and there is no mention of light here; but they seem to be understood of that time when God, after the creation of universal matter from nothing, fashioned and perfected heaven and earth (down to the creation of man, to which he descends in verse 7): for the word "made" seems to signify this, according to the Hebrew interpreters themselves. Moses adds, verse 5: "And every shrub of the field, before it was in the earth, and every herb of the region before it germinated. For the Lord God had not rained upon the earth: and there was no man to work the earth. Verse 6: but a spring (ed) was ascending from the earth, irrigating the whole surface of the earth."

So the Vulgate reads. Now a manifold question arises from this passage. Why, in a general and very brief recapitulation of the divine works, is there particular mention of grass and shrubs? Is it certain that rain had not yet fallen? What kind of spring was that? For many, along with the Arabic and Persian versions, put "vapor" instead of "spring"; or "cloud," as it is in the Samaritan version, in the Targum Onkelos, and in other Chaldean paraphrases. Furthermore, should we read with the Vulgate and other interpreters, "and ... was ascending"; or rather with the Arabic, "nor was vapor ascending"? Some of the Hebrews, such as Kimchi, think that the negation from the preceding clause should be carried over here. A certain answer to these questions is not easy.

To the first doubt Petavius responds (De Opif., book I, ch. XIV, 1, 2): "It seems to me that Moses wished to inculcate more carefully that marvelous and stupendous thing which happened on the third day, that plants and seeds germinated from dry soil not irrigated by any waters or rains, and were nourished by a covering cloud that served in place of rain and irrigation." He himself thinks "that cloud and vapor to be the remains of the primeval water, which, attenuated into mist with intervening air, were separated from the water below; which cloud then nourished the plants and herbs without any rains, by brooding warmth alone; by a great miracle, and therefore to be more carefully commended to posterity." But if truly without the aid of water the herbs and trees germinated and grew, this was from the sole will of the Almighty, not from mist, and no indication of this exists in the words of Moses. Why Moses mentioned plants here, I do not know. Perhaps because mention of the trees of Paradise, in which God placed man, had to be made immediately in verses 8, 9; and because those trees are exceedingly useful to us, and they strike us with greater admiration among the works of God, if you except the animals whose formation is indicated after the description of Paradise in verse 19. Some more recent scholars deny our supposition and contend that a period should be placed after verse four, and that verse five has nothing in common with it; so that it would read: "The Lord God made heaven and earth. And every shrub of the field had not yet germinated..."

LII. The Question of Rain; The Meaning of "Ed"

That rain fell at that time, Moses seems openly to deny. Nevertheless, we read in Maimonides (More Nevochim, part II, ch. XXX): "Our sages observe that God produced herbs and trees from the earth only after rain had watered it: that what is said, 'and vapor ascended from the earth,' contains a narration of a prior event, preceding the statement: 'let the earth bring forth grass.'" Hence Onkelos translated: "And a cloud had ascended from the earth," etc. Moreover, it is possible that the primitive state of that atmosphere, not sufficiently known to us, prohibited rains; or at least that at that time it rained quite rarely (as still happens in some regions); or finally, that rains never fell upon the dry land, that is, upon the lands from which the sea had receded, which perhaps did not extend very far after the first emergence (for Moses speaks of these first lands in verse 5: "It had not rained upon the earth"), until, with many plants already existing and having propagated, the fourth period succeeded the third.

The lack of rain seems to be posited as the cause why plants had not arisen or germinated: "for it had not rained." But this cannot be the case; for there were no plants before the recession of the waters, and during that time the lack of rain certainly did not hinder their germination; but after the waters receded, we are taught that abundant vapor took the place of rain. Nor should we be greatly concerned about the particle ki ("for"), which is omitted in the Arabic version, where there is only "it had not rained." That particle, moreover, has multiple meanings. Sometimes it signifies "for" (Genesis II, 17; III, 5, 19), or "because" (Genesis III, 14, 17; XXX, 14); and often it is rendered "that" (Genesis XVIII, 15; XXIV, 4; Exodus XVI); often also it indicates "when" (Genesis IV, 12); and not rarely it is to be interpreted as "although, even if" (Psalm XLVIII, 18; Genesis VIII, 21); and sometimes "certainly, indeed, altogether," etc. (see Stockius, Clavis linguae sanctae, p. 523).

The word ed the Vulgate, together with the Septuagint and the Syriac, renders as "spring." Augustine suspects (De Genes. ad litt., book V, ch. IX-X) that this spring was many springs, which irrigated their respective regions. More commonly, those skilled in the Hebrew language and the Eastern versions render it "vapor" (as Aquila, Pererius, Molina, Del Rio), or "cloud," which is a collection of visible vapors. And indeed, where evaporation is abundant, the air is very moist, and such a quantity of dew moistens the plants as to compensate well for the lack of rain. I would certainly not believe that there was less evaporation and abundant dew on the lands first uncovered than there is now on the shores of Egypt, or the Red Sea and the Persian Gulf, or on the Chilean or Brazilian coast, places which are now celebrated for their abundance of dew. The land of that first time is called "dry" in comparison with the lands that the water still covered, but the universal ocean had saturated that dry land, and the heat at that time was blazing hot; everything, moreover, as it seems, was either coastline or near the coast: therefore the evaporation must have been altogether great. For those regions, other things being equal, are said to receive more dew which the sea washes, or which are situated near lakes or rivers; likewise those places where winds blowing from the direction of the sea prevail, as well as regions closer to the equator. Perhaps at that time a smaller difference than now exists between daytime and nighttime temperature diminished the quantity of dew properly so called: but the less dew fell, the more the elastic and condensed vapor grew in the air, which could serve in place of rain and, like a spring, irrigate the lands. The Vulgate followed the Septuagint, for whom it is common, as also for the New Testament writers, to use pegen ("spring") for any eruption of moisture (see Grotius on this passage, and on Mark V, 29).

Cornelius a Lapide says that ed is a flood or inundation, namely that primeval abyss of waters, and thus the passage can be rendered: "On the day (the first of the world) when God made heaven and earth, every shrub of the field (for this is what terem means, as is clear from Exodus IX, 30) was not yet in the earth, and every herb of the region had not yet germinated, but an inundation," namely, "the abyss of waters, which seemed to emerge or ascend from the earth, irrigated and covered the whole earth." The Syriac version also, the Targum Pseudo-Jonathan, and the Jerusalem Targum render "not yet" where the Vulgate has "before," together with the Septuagint translators as well as the Samaritan, Arabic, and Persian. But to substitute for "was ascending," which all translators have, "seemed to emerge or ascend" is forced, if I am not mistaken, and poorly fitting. Certainly the waters of the sea which now occupy such a great part of the terrestrial sphere do not seem to "emerge or ascend" from the earth. Petavius, in defense of the opinion which we reported in section 51, says that "to ascend" here means the same as "to stand out, to be elevated above" -- but he brings forward no example or witness for himself. The main objections raised against the interpretation of a flood, virtually unknown to all interpreters, are two. First, because it is not proved that this meaning suits the Hebrew word. In Job XXXVI, 27, this word is indeed rendered by the Vulgate as "flood," but by others it is explained as "cloud" or "vapor" or "small drops," which hardly differ from condensed vapor. Second, and most importantly, because it seems altogether contrary to reason: for rain is posited there as something that could have been useful to plants. It is furthermore implied that man, if he had been present, could have worked the earth. These things are indeed absurd if it is that time which is being described when the whole earth was covered with water. Would rain have fertilized an abyss of water? Could a man have plowed, sown, and harvested there? Why, indeed, and not in few words, is it said that shrubs of the field and herbs of the region were lacking at that time? -- which, in that explanation, is a physical necessity that everyone sees.

LIII. Against the Forced Negation in Genesis II, 6

But now what shall I say about that negative particle which some add, reading "nor was the spring (vapor) ascending"? If someone contends that the negation was in the original text, I will oppose to the single ancient codex which they offer us, and which they say was read by Saadia, all the codices that survive, including Samaritan ones and ancient codices which were used by both the Septuagint and the others who compiled versions of the Bible, such as Jerome and other ancients. If they say the negation is to be understood, and is understood elsewhere by the Hebrews, I respond that the Hebrews understand a negation implicitly either when it is manifestly clear from the context of the discourse itself, as also happens in our languages, that it must be supplied (otherwise the expression would be intolerably ambiguous, and it would be better to remain silent than to speak thus); or when, according to the custom of Hebrew poetry, what was said in the first part of the clause is repeated in the second part, with the negation omitted, as is also done with other words to be supplied from the first part. So in Psalm XLIII, 19: "And our heart did not turn back: and (nor) did you turn our paths from your way." But that the negation should evidently be understood in the passage we are treating, which nearly all interpreters and commentators have not even suspected could be understood, no one, I think, will affirm, unless perhaps from a preconceived judgment of mind, by which someone has persuaded himself that a great miracle is being narrated here by Moses: namely that herbs and shrubs, without any aid of moisture, when the earth was parched with the greatest dryness, germinated and grew.

If I were to say, for example, that on this third day there was no fish in the waters, nor did any animal walk upon the earth, or no bird was flying through the sky, it is easily understood that in this third member the negation is to be implied. But if I say: on this day there was no animal in the waters, none on the lands, and plants were germinating upon the earth -- who but a fool would say that in this third member the negation must be understood, and that this is certain and evident? Moreover, if anyone wishes to find in these verses a poetic parallelism, as they call it, these verses must be separated from the preceding ones, and should be thus translated and arranged from the Hebrew:

And every shrub of the field was not yet in the earth,
And every herb of the region had not yet germinated:
For the Lord God had not rained upon the earth, erets
And there was no man to work the ground, ha-adamah
And vapor was ascending from the earth, ha-arets
And was irrigating the whole face of the ground. ha-adamah

Namely, it was ascending from the earth (to irrigate), and (descending) was irrigating the ground. Sometimes from a prior clause or member the negation is supplied in the following one, as we have noted: but never from another verse. (Footnote: "I doubt whether a negation can be repeated from one verse to another. There is no example of this." Drusius. See Malvenda in Poole's Synopsis.) But just as that negation accords poorly with philological reasoning, so it is altogether contrary to philosophical reasoning: for, from what was said shortly before, the exhalation of vapor must have been not merely some, but great, and must have abundantly irrigated the face of the earth.

To this add that, in order for this negation to be introduced, it is necessary to establish that the surface of the earth was parched with dryness at that time (which could not have happened without a miracle, at least according to the common opinion of 24-hour days); moreover, that the evaporation from the surrounding waters was also continually prevented by God: which miracles (of which no one is a witness) were adverse to the germination, growth, and life of the seeds or plants that God had made: thus two miracles were directed to this end, that a third further miracle would become necessary, namely that plants would grow without moisture physically necessary for them. If someone does not hesitate to admit such things without any adequate witness, can it not be said that he has declared war on philosophy? Does not such a wise person seem to be striving to oppose Scripture, which he professes to follow, to the study of nature, and to alienate the cultivators of these studies from the faith, while he himself is mocked by them? "What is not so grievous," I will say with Augustine, "is that the erring man is mocked, but that our authors are believed by those outside to have held such views, and with great peril to those for whose salvation we are concerned, they are criticized and rejected as unlearned" (see De Genes. ad litt., book I, ch. XVIII-XIX). (Footnote: Augustine says elsewhere: "When I hear some Christian brother ignorant of these natural matters and holding one thing for another, I patiently observe the man forming opinions, nor do I see it harming him, since he does not believe anything unworthy of you, Lord Creator of all, if perhaps he is ignorant of the position and nature of a corporeal creature. But it is harmful if he thinks this pertains to the very form of the doctrine of piety, and more stubbornly dares to affirm what he does not know." Confessions V, ch. V.)

They say that Moses by these words is emphasizing that plants were made and grew by the will of God alone: why then does he not say so openly? Was it not enough, to demonstrate the immediate work of God, that there were no seeds before this day? Do men, however uneducated, not know that plants, at least those which are either more useful or larger, arise from seed? And if the third day was an ordinary day, did this not sufficiently declare (and far better than the lack of rain upon land recently brought forth from the waters) the immediate work of the Creator? Does the common people not know that plants, and especially trees, even if irrigated by the most abundant rain, cannot grow so quickly without a miracle?

Those who force that negation, just as those who prefer a flood and inundation to springs or vapor, are motivated by thinking that these verses indicate the reasons why plants had not germinated. I rather think that the instrumental cause of the first germination is being revealed, when there were no rains falling and no artificial irrigation. But besides, it seems ridiculous to seek the cause why terrestrial plants did not exist before the dry land appeared, just as to seek the cause of the absence of eggs when hens and other birds did not exist, or of milk when cows and other dairy animals had not yet been created.

But of those who completely separate verses 5 and 6 from what precedes, what shall we say? About this division I determine nothing: I only establish this: if those verses are separated from the preceding ones, they certainly must express some meaning in themselves; for they cannot be joined with verse 7: "The Lord God therefore formed (and formed, in Hebrew) man from the clay of the earth." For when man was made, he certainly found the earth clothed with herbs, shrubs, and trees. In this hypothesis, therefore, that negation must be more evidently excluded. For what, if it is posited, do those verses narrate? Namely, that there were not (but when?) shrubs or herbs, nor had rain descended, nor had vapor (or a spring) ascended: they are mere negations that teach us nothing; and they can lead the reader into error: for since the narrative of the origin of man follows afterward, we would be led to believe that this very state of things presented itself to the first man, when he became a living soul (1 Corinthians XV, 45). Therefore if verses 5 and 6 are separated from what precedes, this kind of negation must be altogether rejected; and the sense will be: "Before shrubs and herbs had germinated"; or: "Shrubs had not yet germinated... But vapor was ascending from the earth to irrigate... and by its aid, serving in place of rain, the plants made by God grew"; or: "Although it did not rain... nevertheless vapor... irrigated." For the connective particle vav, "and" (which Heinsius calls the Proteus of particles), sometimes it can be rendered "but," as the Vulgate here renders it, sometimes "moreover, yet, truly," or "when" or "although" or "nevertheless, nonetheless," or "for, indeed, because," etc.

Ludovicus de Dieu wishes this passage to be understood only in the sense that plants cultivated by agriculture did not exist in the earliest times. "We point out," he says, "that there are three words here requiring attention, none of which occurs in chapter I. The first is siach, which signifies a shrub, that is, a multitude of shoots sprouting from an established root. The second is sadeh, which does not denote the earth absolutely, but cultivated land, adapted either for sowing or for the pasture of flocks. The third is tsamach, which signifies to germinate, to sprout, and is therefore said of shoots and stolons that spring up from an established seed or root. Nothing of this sort existed in the first creation, for which rain and cultivation are required. The herbs and trees spoken of in chapter I did not spring from seed and roots, but were themselves the first seeds and roots of all plants." So he says, and Hassius and Gussetius agree with him. But all of them are refuted by Ernst Friedrich Rosenmuller, who argues thus. "Nor does the claim that the words used in this passage pertain to agriculture rest on a solid argument. For siach is usually taken to mean a shrub or small bush (see Job XII, 8; ch. XXX, 4, 7), and it seems all the more to be used thus here, in order to signify that not even a wild or uncultivated shrub, let alone a cultivated or garden one, or any tall tree, had existed when God created the earth. Furthermore, that sadeh does not at all denote land apt for cultivation, but has a broader range, is clear from the use of this word observed below in verses 19, 20, chapter III, 1, where chayyath ha-sadeh undoubtedly denotes wild beasts distinct from domestic cattle; tsamach, finally, is known to denote arising in general, and to be used of things that come about by natural means. The fact, therefore, that the writer uses in this passage some different words than above (chapter I, 11, 12), dealing with the same subject, apart from deshe (grass) which also occurs there, is to be regarded as mere variation of style. But since, in the course of the narrative about the origin of the whole world within a span of six days, he had briefly mentioned the origin of plants on the third day, now proceeding to the most ancient history of the earth and of mankind, he first explains more precisely how the plants were created, in verse 6, and premises this in verse 5: that when God had first created heaven and earth, plants did not simultaneously exist on dry ground, which although it is evident from the preceding narrative, he here expressly states, in order to make a transition to what follows--"

LIV. Consequences: The Third Day Must Have Been Long

It will be helpful to point out some consequences that seem to me of some importance. Rain is indicated here as something that would have been suitable for the plants of that period. But if those plants, according to the common opinion, all germinated,

When God adorned the earth, recently snatched from the ocean waves,
With trees and verdant grass (Leon, presbyter of Paris, History of the Old Testament, book I);

if the earth produced plants barely a few hours after it had been elevated above the sea, if it brought them forth while still entirely saturated with water, rain was certainly altogether useless; indeed it would rather have been harmful to those plants that prefer drier rather than moist terrain. For it was more expedient for them to be freed from excessive moisture than for moisture to increase still further from rains. Therefore, if plants, as happens now, needed irrigation, it is necessary for us to say that this day (more truly the second part of this day) was extended far beyond a span of 24 hours, indeed even beyond the time when that great quantity of water vanished by evaporation, so that new lands, now dried out and arid, would need either rainfall or artificial or some other irrigation.

Furthermore, if on one ordinary day or in the first hours of the third day all plants grew by the power of the Creator to such a size that on the fifth and sixth day they could be available for the use of all animals and of man himself (chapter I, 29; chapter II, 16): was it necessary for us to be reminded that in such a brief interval of time rain had not fallen upon the earth? Certainly the power of rain is unequal to such a marvelous thing; this was known to the Hebrews as well. But if God was doing everything immediately, what would rain have accomplished? It remains therefore to be said that God indeed made the seeds or shoots of plants, but willed at the same time that they should grow and bear their fruits by the ordinary means He had established, that is, water: but for plants to grow by this means, a time longer than 24 hours is clearly needed. I have already cited St. Ephrem teaching that God ordained that water should lend its aid to the formation of the first plants (section 34, near the end).

This consequence is invincibly confirmed by verse 6 (unless that negation which we have rejected is intruded), where the source of moisture is indicated, namely vapor, which supplied the lack of rain by irrigating "the whole surface of the earth" -- which would certainly have been useless and not worth mentioning according to the common opinion. I note that even according to the opinion of Buckland (section 5), this day must be posited as far longer than an ordinary day: for here we are dealing with the Mosaic days, not with phenomena that can be assigned to the time preceding those days.

And I will note this: The dry land was named by God in chapter I, 10. Now in this second chapter we read that for a certain time neither herb nor shrub germinated upon erets, nor had God rained upon erets, and a spring or vapor then ascended from erets. Therefore for some span of time, between the first and second work of the third day, the dry land was without plants. If we set this time at one or a few hours, was it worth the trouble to write so carefully about such small and useless matters?

Moses did not write of such a kind as to have presented us with a system in which the infancy of every physical and astronomical discipline betrays itself. I would not indeed assert that the most versatile Moses was an outstanding physicist or an eminent astronomer: but if he could not or would not set forth anything for us other than the common opinions of the uneducated populace, why, I ask, did he introduce light, which by its splendor creates day, two days before the sun? The reason why Moses establishes this paradox, we, now illuminated by the light of more recent science, can explain (section 34). But certainly this was not so obvious and common a thought that it could easily occur by itself to the mind of a man ignorant of physical matters, or to the rude common people, without the aid of heavenly light. Why did plants, according to this same author, spring up before the stars appeared? Is this the kind of teaching that could spontaneously sprout in an uneducated man's head, unless the Lord God should rain down from heaven upon his barren mind and water it? Moses indeed uses those words and those images which were suited to the understanding of the common people, so that the populace could at least grasp and taste the outer shell of his narrative: but the kernel that lies hidden beneath that shell is as far from being drawn from the understanding of the rude populace as it diverges from the doctrines of the philosophers of that age.

In passing, let me here observe that it is not so easy in our own times for learned men to maintain a middle position, neither readily admitting any path of reconciliation between the physical sciences and the Mosaic narrative, nor despising it as though it smelled of the ignorance of ancient times.

Let us return to chapter 1, and pursue the works of the three days that remain.

ON THE WORK OF THE FOURTH DAY

(Verses 14, 15, 18, 19)

LV. The Luminaries in the Expanse of Heaven

God commands that there be luminaries in the expanse of heaven (or of the heavens), to divide between day and night, and to be for signs of seasons, days and years (or for signs and seasons)..., and to give light upon the earth. And so it was. The expanse or firmament of verses 6 and 7, dividing the waters from the waters, is occupied by air; above this expanse the stars appear: but the expanse of heaven, or the heaven of heavens, where the stars are, is filled only with ether.

If the heavenly bodies had been created on the fourth day from nothing, it would not be true that in the beginning of time, before any day, the heaven (or heavens) and the earth, that is, the totality of things, had been created by God: for the smallest portion of the world and of heavenly matter would have been created in the beginning. And indeed the verb bara is not used in this place, but God says let there be, as it is in the Vulgate, or, as in the Hebrew, let them be (just as when the matter of light was treated he said: let light be). Likewise in verse 16 it does not say he created, but God made two great luminaries (or greater ones), and placed them in the expanse of heaven, to give light upon the earth. This manner of speaking not only does not prevent us from saying that the matter of these stars was already created from the beginning, but also seems to be able to be understood in such a way that even before the fourth day the stars themselves had already been formed and perfected, but had not appeared in the firmament before this day, nor had they begun to perform the duty assigned to them by the Creator, namely to be luminaries for the earth. If this explanation is accepted, we would have to say that "made" is used for "had made"; which is not absurd, since the Hebrews do not have several forms of the past tense in their language.

The matter of the stars, created from the beginning, condensed into globes and became luminous (section 34). That the heavenly luminaries existed from the very first day, and that the sun was not without light, many have believed, both Christians (section 33) and Hebrews. Maimonides says (More Nevochim, part II, ch. XXX): "Our sages write in Bereshit-bara concerning the light of the first day: These are the luminaries which were created on the first day, but He did not suspend them until the fourth day." The luminaries in the expanse of heaven, with a dense vault of cloud covering the earth all around, the earth had not seen until this day. Accordingly they were not yet luminaries of the earth, about which alone Moses speaks here. Imagine for yourself a dining hall prepared for a banquet, whose height is divided in two by a horizontal partition. If a lamp is hung in its upper part, it will certainly not be visible to the lower part, where the guests are or are expected; nor, until the partition is removed, can the lamp be said to shine in the ceiling of the hall. Say exactly the same about the luminaries of the earth. They existed indeed, but before the cloud was dispersed they were not luminaries of the earth. Nor, if from the sun, as I conjectured (sections 39, 41, 47, 49), during the second and third days, through the mediation of clouds, some feeble light illuminated the earth somewhat, is it therefore absurd that afterward it is commanded: Let there be luminaries in the expanse of heaven. For if into the room where you sit, a little light penetrates through the door from a lamp that shines in the next room; does this light, which is not direct nor reflected from a mirror, but diffused from an illuminated wall, prevent you from reasonably ordering your servant: bring a light? St. Thomas advises, and more than once, that "Moses set forth only those things which are manifest to appearance" (I part., Quest. LXIX, art. 2, ad 3): thus he was silent about the sun until on this fourth day it manifestly appeared, perhaps also because, since he was eager to turn the people of Israel away from the worship of the stars, it was opportune for him to mention the chief of the stars, the sun, after the origin of herbs and trees: "Lest perhaps, lifting up your eyes to heaven, you see the sun and the moon and all the stars of heaven, and being deceived by error you adore them and worship them, which the Lord your God created for the service of all the nations that are under heaven." So Moses, Deuteronomy IV, 19. (Footnotes: Petavius, De Opif. book I, ch. xv, no. 2. Maimonides, More Nevochim, part II, ch. xxx.)

LVI. The Gradual Appearance of the Stars

That the stars proceeded gradually, as happened with the earth, from a primitive imperfect state to that which they now have, this indeed can be believed with probability. I would not, however, easily believe that the sun was without light throughout the entire first day, while the earth was already luminous (section 33); but perhaps at that time the sun was not separated from our earth, and indeed was joined with several other great globes. It is probable, however, that at the first time when it constituted a globe by itself, the sun was encircled by a great luminous atmosphere, and its light was then indeed more diffused, but perhaps less intense in its shining. Nevertheless this luminous atmosphere did not reach the orbit of the earth, at least after the end of that light which is mentioned on the first day. For as long as the earth was enveloped in that ocean of light, the light of the first day did not cease, nor did that first day have an end. Whatever, however, the state of the sun may have been in the earlier periods, it did not show itself to the earth; and its direct rays, much less those of the other stars, did not reach the earth, as long as a denser darkness, or at least an immense mass of condensed vapors enveloping the earth on every side, wrapped it around. Afterward came the time when this cloudy vault was broken through, opened, and at last dissipated; and this either gradually through natural causes slowly and constantly operating, or, which is perhaps more probable, through some great catastrophe which brought a notable change to the terrestrial atmosphere, and dispersing those continuous vapors, made the air permeable to the rays of the stars. Whether also in the sun and in the stars some notable change occurred in this period, I do not venture to determine.

Likewise, when this change in our atmosphere took place, on account of which the path was no longer blocked to the rays of the stars, this we cannot know from physical science as it now stands: but neither from this can anything of any importance be objected to Moses narrating that the sun and stars appeared to the earth between the period of the first vegetation and the creation of animals belonging to those classes which are mentioned in the work of the fifth day.

This fourth day was probably preceded by some great disturbance, from which darkness was made over the face of the earth for some time (section 47), and new lands emerged from the sea; which also, whether on account of some change in the air, or on account of the decreasing heat proper to the earth, or on account of increasing solar influence, while plants were propagating, were perhaps somewhat modified, the earth sprouting new shoots when suitable conditions had arrived for them.

And in this period a certain commingling (section 11) preceded, and a division, opening, and a new and greatest birth followed. Whatever the state of the heavenly luminaries had been before, certainly as far as the earth is concerned, they were as though commingled and confused, and by that commingling of condensed vapors their sight was prevented from reaching the earth. Afterward the cloudy vault was divided and the heaven was opened, and for the first time, as regards our earth, the stars arose.

LVII. The Sun Presides over the Day; Signs and Seasons

Some distinction between the natural day and night, if our hypothesis is true (section 39 etc.), was already being made in the second and third periods, mediately indeed by the light of the sun still unknown to the earth, but immediately by its own luminous rays, not reflected specularly from the cloudy vault, but diffused. (Footnote: Between true or specular reflection and diffusion of light from illuminated bodies, Euler and other more careful Physicists rightly distinguish.)

But now it begins to happen immediately from the sun, which presides over the day and during the day pours its rays directly upon the earth. The moon also and the stars serve for measuring times, which men, with the help of the stars, divide into days and months and parts of months, and also into years and seasons of the year. They are called instruments of time by Plato, and Claudian sang:

That Father of things, who divides the seasons by the stars.

Hence God says through Moses: "That they may divide between day and night, and be for signs of seasons (months, or the four seasons of the year), days and years." This, as it seems to me, is a probable interpretation that does not contradict the Hebrew, nor the Vulgate, which has, in agreement with it, for signs and seasons and days and years. For it is well known that the particle "and," both among the Hebrews and among the Latins, when it joins two nouns, sometimes indicates that the latter noun stands in place of a genitive or adjective. Thus Genesis III, 16: "I will multiply your sorrow and your conception," that is, the sorrow of your conception. Thus Acts XXIII: "Concerning the hope and resurrection of the dead I am judged," that is, concerning the hope of the resurrection. Ibid. XIV, 11: "The priest bringing bulls and garlands," which you will probably interpret as, garlanded bulls. Thus Romans I, 5: "Through whom we have received grace and apostleship," which is, the grace of apostleship. And 1 Corinthians XI, 7: "Because he is the image and glory of God," that is, the glorious image of God, or the image of the glory of God. Such are those expressions of Virgil: "And he placed the mass and high mountains upon them" (Aeneid I), and "We pour libations from cups and gold" (Georgics II); and in Lucan, "He bit the steel and bridles." In the sacred Liturgy we daily pray that the reception of the Lord's body may not be for us unto judgment and condemnation, that is, unto a judgment of condemnation.

Many, however, in this verse 14, prefer to separate entirely the word "signs" from the other word "seasons": and they interpret "signs" either as the months from the signs of the Zodiac (although the most ancient distinction of months came from the moon, and even now ruder peoples, or those more tenacious of antiquity, use the lunar month), or they think them to be indications which we receive from the stars for sowing, planting, harvesting, navigating, etc., or signs of rain, etc. St. Thomas says (I part., Quest. LXX, art. 2, in the body): "From the luminaries of heaven is perceived the indication of rainy weather or fair, which are suited to various occupations, and as regards this he says: that they may be for signs." That this too is indicated in this passage is not improbable; and also that men were going to use the stars for determining feast days.

"The moon, a display of the time and a sign of the age. From the moon comes the sign of the feast day" (Sirach XLIII, 7). Nearly the same things as Aquinas taught Basil, Ambrose, Theodoret, and others, who also refute the inventions of astrologers and casters of nativities who think that future and chance events are written in the heavenly bodies as in certain signs. God condemns superstitious or vain signs through Jeremiah (ch. X, v. 2) saying: "Thus says the Lord: do not learn according to the ways of the nations; and do not fear the signs of heaven, which the nations fear."

LVIII. The Sun and the Length of the Fourth Day

The sun measured days in this third period and those following; but it does not follow from this that the fourth, fifth, and sixth days in chapter 1 were ordinary days of 24 hours. Nothing prevents those days from having been long periods, just as the first and the following two were; and perhaps even longer. For the sun, illuminating more than half of the terrestrial sphere, produced in various parts of the globe indeed alternations of days and nights, but in the globe itself a long and continuous day (that is, a time of light), to which in the fourth and following period, only that evening (which we conjectured was the darkness produced by subterranean emanations and disturbances of the earth, section 47) put an end. Moreover, even the day itself which the sun produces in some part of the world is not always contained within the span of twenty-four hours. For the same sun which in the greater part of the world measures the day at twenty-four hours, in polar regions makes a day equal to an entire year. No one therefore compels us to believe that, after the sun appeared to the earth, the three Genesis days that follow must be considered different in nature from the three preceding ones.

Otherwise it would have to be supposed that the solar day of the earth until man was created consisted not indeed of twenty-four hours or a slightly longer interval of time, but of many years and indeed centuries: which someone proposed, but only as a possible hypothesis, which runs as follows. Since the moon always shows the same face to the earth, the time of its rotation around its own axis is equal to its periodic time in which it is carried through its orbit around the earth. If the earth also, while being carried in its orbit around the sun, were perpetually to turn the same hemisphere toward the sun, there would certainly be no succession of days and nights for us. But imagine that the time of rotation given to the earth is not entirely equal to the time in which it completes its orbit around the sun, but that the one is faster than the other by the smallest quantity: there will be some place on earth where a long continuous midday will be experienced, and then after many years or many centuries, the sun will appear moved by some measurable distance from its first place, until at last, after a twilight of many centuries, the sun will set, abandoning that hemisphere plunged in a very long night. In this hypothesis, therefore, this would indeed be a solar day, but it would have absolutely no proportion to these present days of ours of twenty-four hours. If you say that this could have happened in the Mosaic days, you say a thing indeed that involves no contradiction; yet since nothing of this is even obscurely hinted at in Scripture, since no astronomical science yet provides any reason by which this very great difference between the velocity of the earth's rotation, which now exists, and that which is imagined to have existed then, can be proved to us: certainly this hypothesis cannot even be called probable.

Augustine teaches that "the power of reasonable fictions" has been granted to poets, but not a wider one (De Ordine, I, 11, 14): much more is it granted to Philosophers and Theologians, where the light of certainty is lacking, to have recourse to hypotheses, but these must be reasonable, that is, resting on probable reason: those, however, which are only possible must never be accepted; and can only be brought forward when no other reason is available, to show that some matter, which is deduced either from human science or from revelation, should not be rejected as entirely impossible.

Why the sun is said to preside over the day, and in some manner to rule during the day, no one fails to see. As soon as that powerful King of the day (Thomson, Seasons, "Summer") arrives at the horizon, all the other stars grow pale, and are hidden by its overpowering light. The sun alone among all the stars is for us "a vessel (instrument) of wonder of the Most High, scorching the mountains, breathing out fiery rays" (Sirach XLIII, 3), and a great fountain of light and life, of beauty and joy; although by itself it does not produce such great things, but insofar as it has been appointed as a minister of the Creator. The moon also, while it shines in the sky, not a few stars are obscured, so that it seems to hold a certain primacy and as it were a dominion over the night. The eyes of peoples are directed especially toward it, to such a degree that pagan peoples superstitiously worshipped it as the queen of heaven, as is found in Jeremiah (ch. VII, 18; XLIV, 17-19, 25). The sun and moon are called great luminaries, because they appear far larger than other stars, and because they are truly the greater (for it can also be translated this way) luminaries of the earth, for they illuminate it much more than the stars do. But the moon, which compared with the stars is called the great or greater luminary, is said in comparison with the sun to be the small or lesser luminary: for the Hebrew word can be translated either way, since the Hebrews lack comparatives. Let us hear St. Thomas (I part., Quest. LXX, art. 1, ad 4): "As Chrysostom says, they are called two great luminaries, not so much in quantity as in efficacy and power. Because even if other stars are greater in quantity than the moon, nevertheless the effect of the moon is felt more in these lower regions, and also according to sense it appears greater."

LIX. The Moon and Stars Illuminate the Earth

These luminaries, Moses says in verses 15 and 17, were made by God to shine in the firmament of heaven and to illuminate the earth. Just as in these words he does not intend to say that the sun shines in the firmament and illuminates the earth during the night, and the moon during the day, so neither does he mean to suggest that the moon was placed in the sky for this purpose, that it should illuminate the earth on every single night and for the entire duration of the night. He predicates of the moon only that one thing which it actually does, and affirms that nights are illuminated by the moon only in the manner in which everyone sees with their own eyes that it provides this service. And since the uses of things, especially those which are natural, are the purposes established by God for the things themselves, therefore he says that those two luminaries were made by God for this purpose, to illuminate the earth. The philosopher Secundus said that the moon was the rival of the sun, the enemy of sorceries, the comfort of travelers, and Epictetus called it the helper of the day and the eye of the night. St. Thomas should be heard again (I part., Quest. LXX, art. 2): "Moses, in order to recall the people from idolatry, touched upon that single cause, namely that they were made for our benefit."

Laplace wrote that certain advocates of final causes had devised the idea that the moon was given to the earth to illuminate it by night, and he objects to them that there are many nights that lack this light; he himself indicates how the moon could have been arranged so as to perpetually show its entire illuminated face to us. But with the moon placed as he himself positions it, namely outside the cone of the earth's shadow, lest the solar rays directed toward the moon be intercepted by the earth's shadow, the moon itself would appear to us six times smaller, and so would never furnish us with sufficiently abundant light. Furthermore, learned geometers have demonstrated that, given the laws of nature as they have been established, not even in that hypothesis could the moon always show its entire illuminated portion to the earth: from which it would ultimately result that the moon for a long interval of time would entirely withdraw its light from the earth. But it was not fitting for God to change his laws so that the earth might sometimes be better illuminated, especially at that time which is destined for the rest of men and gentler animals. God made the moon also for this purpose, namely to illuminate the earth, but not through all nights nor in the best way possible, but only in the manner in which we perceive it to illuminate.

Moses mentions the stars only because, although they constitute the greatest part of material creation, they nevertheless have the least connection with the earth and with men. If we attend only to those things which Moses expressly mentions, the work of this fourth day certainly seems to have been able to be completed in a quite brief time. Yet we have shown above in section 16 that from this day a not weak argument can be drawn to confirm that long periods lie hidden in these days.

LX. The Debate on Animate Stars: Patristic Testimony

That the stars are animate, many have thought; an opinion derived from the Academy and profane philosophy, which Philo Judaeus embraced, as did also Maimonides, who judged it most consonant with the law, that is, with the teaching of Moses. This opinion is moreover taught in several books of Origen; and in the commentaries on the Gospel of John, this author suspects that Christ suffered on behalf of the stars themselves. However, in the books On Principles, Origen writes that this matter is not clearly handed down. The pseudonymous Clement, in book V of the Recognitions, holds the same opinion. But generally the Fathers contradict this invention: Eusebius of Caesarea (although an admirer and supporter of Origen), Basil, Chrysostom, Theodoret, Dionysius (or the author of the book On the Divine Names), and his commentator Maximus the Martyr, Cyril of Alexandria, John Damascene, Procopius, Lactantius, and Peter Chrysologus, whose passages Petavius collected in De Opif. sex dier., book I, ch. XII.

To these add Didymus, On the Trinity, book II, ch. VII, sermon 87, and Rufinus, or the author of the book On the Faith, which is ascribed to Rufinus. He says of the stars: "Some, deceived by an error of the mind, have said these are rational animals, whose madness even the wicked Origen followed." And Orosius places this among the errors of the Origenists: "They said that the creature subjected to corruption unwillingly was to be understood as the sun, moon, and stars, and that these were not elemental lights but rational powers." (Footnote: Orosius, Admonition to St. Augustine, On the Error of the Priscillianists and Origenists.)

Ambrose and Jerome seem to incline toward the teaching of Origen, each in only one passage. But the words of Ambrose (Epistle to Horatian, 38) are to be piously interpreted and referred to a figure of speech; which Paul also seems to have used in the passage that Ambrose imitated and expanded, namely Romans chapter VIII, 19: "The expectation of the creature waits for the revelation of the sons of God. For the creature was made subject to vanity, not willingly, but on account of him who subjected it in hope." Where most of the ancients consider that by the name "creature" the entire universe is signified. But Chrysostom notes that there is a personification there, like that by which the Prophets attribute sense and intelligence to inanimate things. Moreover, does not Ambrose himself seem to refute that opinion in Hexaemeron II, 4? Jerome's words also admit a benign interpretation, and he moreover shows himself opposed to that view in several places, and calls the doctrine that considers all things animate an error of heretics.

Augustine once doubted about the soul of the stars; but afterward, both elsewhere and in II Retract. ch. VII, he showed that he was confirmed in the view that the stars entirely lack soul and sense. The Emperor Justinian, in his Epistle to the Fifth Synod against the errors of Origen, accuses Origen himself of madness for having said that heaven, the sun, the moon, and the stars, and the waters that are above the heavens, are animate: and among the anathemas appended to the letter, the sixth is: "If anyone says that heaven and the sun and moon and stars, and also the waters that are above the heavens, are animate and rational beings, let him be anathema." Nicephorus Callistus reports these anathemas (Book XVII, ch. XXVII-XXVIII), and writes that they were pronounced by the Synod itself. That is, the bishops took them from Justinian's letter and ratified them with synodal authority, as Cedrenus attests.

Cajetan asserted that the heavens are animate (see against him Catharinus on ch. 1 of Genesis), relying on the authority of the Church, which sings in the sacred Liturgy: "The heavens and the powers of the heavens, and the blessed Seraphim." Unworthy, says Petavius, of a theologian of keen intellect, and a common type of argument, which if it has any force, by the same reasoning you would conclude that the earth and all the elements, and in short nothing at all is not animate: for Scripture sometimes speaks of all these things as though it attributes soul and spirit and reason to them; which must be understood tropically and figuratively, something not even children may be ignorant of.

LXI. Aquinas on the Animation of the Stars; Moving Intelligences

St. Thomas, in Contra Gentes II, seems to favor the animation of the stars: but in chapter LXX, he openly declares that what he said about that question as though affirming it, he did "not say as though asserting it according to the doctrine of faith, to which it does not pertain whether it is said one way or the other." "Wherefore," says Petavius, "he published his opinion only to the extent of saying that the Church had not pronounced on either side, since the acts and decrees of the Fifth Synod concerning Origen do not survive. Yet I think that doubt can no longer be cast on its authority and judgment: and this is sufficiently established both from Justinian's letter and the anathemas set forth therein, and from the testimony of Nicephorus and others: although not to the extent that one who asserts that opinion should be considered a heretic."

The same St. Thomas, in Part I of the Summa, treats that question more extensively, Quest. LXX, art. 3, and dissolves the arguments of those who hold that the luminaries of heaven are animate, and finally concludes "that the heavenly bodies are not animate in the way that plants and animals are, but equivocally." That is: they are not truly animate, because no soul is their form, or is joined to them so as to make one composite; but there are joined to them moving intelligences "through contact of power, as a mover is united to the movable." The Holy Doctor adds: "Hence between those who hold them to be animate and those who hold them to be inanimate, little or no difference is found in reality, but only in words." This is explained from what precedes: "The Platonists also did not hold that souls were united to bodies except through contact of power, as a mover to the movable. And so through the fact that Plato holds the heavenly bodies to be animate, nothing else is given to understand than that spiritual substances are united to heavenly bodies as movers to the movable."

And indeed, if this opinion, which teaches that the stars are animate, is so declared, it closely approaches that doctrine of heavenly movers which the Church has always tolerated; and which Philosophy, which now rejects it, at that time not only tolerated but even considered necessary. Nevertheless the Platonists, although they did not correctly explain the combination of soul with body, were well aware that the mind is affected by pain when the body is struck; and also that the very powers of the mind grow, age, and become weakened together with the body; which certainly does not happen to a mover: for the horse does not suffer pain if the vehicle is struck, nor does it die or fall ill while the vehicle itself is utterly destroyed. Now if they felt as Aquinas interprets them, they certainly spoke worse than they felt. For who would call a chariot "endowed with reason" and "illuminated by the light of knowledge from wisdom" -- which Origen says of the stars (book IV Against Celsus) -- just because its mover, namely the charioteer, is rational? Nor does Ovid imagine the golden chariot of the sun to be animate or rational, since only Phoebus drove it, but rather the horses drawing it were animate and breathing fire and sated with the juice of ambrosia.

Certainly in our own time, it was not moving intelligences that philosopher (Schelling) was thinking of who called the stars rational animals, blessed animals, and immortal Gods -- he showed himself a friend of foolishness, not indeed of wisdom.

It is not necessary for us to point out that those moving intelligences have no foundation in the Scriptures, but were accepted by many theologians only because Philosophy, in those times, considered them necessary for producing the heavenly motions; in which matter I will not say praise, but certainly pardon is deserved. But since now the motions of the stars are perfectly explained by the simplest laws imposed by God upon nature, those motions are no more to be referred to moving intelligences than the fall of heavy bodies, or the descent of waters down a slope, or any other motion derived from natural causes, such as from heat or from electrical force or from chemical affinity.
