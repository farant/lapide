ON THE WORK OF THE FIFTH DAY

(Verses 20, 21, 22, 23)

LXII. Living Creatures of the Fifth Day; The Verb Bara and Animal Souls

Moses speaks of living creatures not on the fourth day where he treats of the stars, but on this fifth day. This day appears to have been a very long period, preceded by some great terrestrial disturbance that probably induced darkness for some time, and rendered the condition of either the waters or the air more suitable for animal life.

God commanded the waters to swarm with creeping things (sherets), and flying creatures to fly over the earth, over the face of the expanse of heaven (the firmament). So reads the Hebrew text and the Samaritan, and likewise the Syriac and Samaritan versions. The Vulgate, attending less to the words than to their sense, renders it thus: Under the firmament of heaven. But the meaning is the same: for flying creatures fly both through the sky which is called air, and often over its lower part. And God created (bara) great sea creatures (thanninim) and every living animal that creeps (or swims), which the waters had brought forth, and every winged flying creature. Now for the first time after the creation of heaven and earth in verse 1, Moses uses the verb bara, "created." Why this, if the matter of animal bodies had been created in the beginning? The waters brought forth (verse 21) the bodies of aquatic animals, which cannot be said to have been created from nothing any more than the plants of the third day. This verb seems, unless I am mistaken, to indicate here (and more clearly in verse 27 where the subject is man) that the Creator, when He made animals, not only fashioned from already-created matter certain bodies equipped with organs -- which He also did when He formed plants -- but also added to them something not previously existing, which He created from nothing. It seems, namely, that this word indicates here, or presupposes, that there is some principle in living creatures distinct from matter -- that is, an immaterial soul created immediately by God. Yet I do not deny that Moses may have had another reason for speaking thus. For the Egyptians honored animals with divine worship, and the Hebrews were inclined to imitate them, as the single story of the golden calf abundantly shows. He therefore, in order to turn his people away from this danger, beginning to speak of animals, had to declare in the most explicit words possible that they were entirely the work of God. Yet he refrained from using this word when he spoke of the stars on the fourth day.

Moses does not use this verb in verse 25, where he treats of the formation of the remaining animals; but it does not follow from this that Moses could not have used this word in that place as well, nor that he employed it without just cause in verse 21. On the sixth day (to which verse 25 pertains), when he speaks of the creature bearing the image of God -- that is, when the subject is the creation of the human soul -- then indeed the word is reserved for a nobler use. Moreover, the creation of the souls of brute animals was not a new thing on the sixth day, nor was it being narrated by Moses for the first time: the law had already been promulgated by which it had been decreed that a soul was to be joined to an animal body properly equipped with organs, and Moses, for whose purpose this was of little or no use, did not need to teach this again. In like manner God, having created living creatures on this fifth day, blessed them, saying: Be fruitful and multiply and fill the waters of the seas, and let the birds multiply upon the earth.

Verse 22. But we do not read that this blessing was repeated over the animals of the higher order on the sixth day: it is pronounced only over man. Yet the animals produced on the sixth day also had to grow, that is, to generate offspring and multiply and fill the earth. But already on the fifth day the blessing had been bestowed on the entire family of brute animals; because when it was given to certain classes and orders, it extended to all other orders and classes as well, just as what was given to the first individuals of a certain species comprehends the entire species. So says St. Thomas: "The blessing of God gives the power of multiplying through generation. And therefore what was stated for birds and fish, which occur first, did not need to be repeated for land animals, but is understood." He teaches likewise that the individuals which "are now generated pre-existed in the works of the six days not only materially (as regards the matter of bodies), but also causally, in the first individuals of their species."

LXIII. Why God Blessed Animals but Not Plants

Why does God say to living creatures: "Be fruitful and multiply..." which He had not said to plants, although they too had to reproduce, multiply, and fill the earth? This seems to be the reason. Nothing needed to be commanded to plants; for since they are destitute of all cognition and of the faculty of willing, even instinctive willing, they cannot actively cooperate in their own preservation or in the propagation of the species. The material molecules of plants obey the laws of the Creator, through which both individuals live and grow and species are propagated; but the plants themselves, in which these molecular operations are carried out, cannot cooperate with them. St. Thomas says: "Plants have no desire to propagate offspring, and they generate without any sensation; hence they were deemed unworthy of the words of blessing." But the divine command was fitting for brute animals; which, although they do not know God and lack reason and reflective concepts, nonetheless feel the laws imposed on their volitional faculty by divine Providence (just as they feel the warmth of the sun and see bodies illuminated by the sun, even those who do not see the sun or do not know that the sun exists), and are driven by internal stimulus, hunger, thirst, and other such impulses to fulfill those laws; and by instinctive will and by the motion of body or limbs, they actively cooperate in the execution of the divine command -- something that no machines, however fashioned with the most exquisite and truly divine craftsmanship, will ever accomplish. Moreover, humans command domesticated animals and often succeed in getting them to accommodate their own will to received commands, something that no one would dare expect from plants. I would not wish to use the words of Sacred Scripture to settle questions which divine wisdom has left to human debate. Nevertheless, let it be permitted to observe that these verses 21 and 22 favor more the opinion, which reason also seems to support, namely that the souls of brute animals are immaterial substances created by God, rather than the opinion of those who think either that animals are machines without sensation, or that their soul, although sentient and instinctively willing, is matter, or some mode or substantial form of matter -- which is neither a substance nor a mode of substance. What is most remarkable, however, is that living creatures diligently obey this command: "Be fruitful and multiply" -- they strive with all effort to preserve their life and safety, and to obtain what is necessary for life; nor is it said of them what is said of many humans, especially among more civilized peoples, namely that, weary of life and hating the light, they have cast away their souls. No less wonderful is that instinct which manifests itself in living creatures at fixed times for reproduction, and the love for offspring, from which flows such solicitude and care even for the eggs themselves, in those species which do not need to brood over them, as well as in those in which the mothers will never see the offspring for whom they so carefully prepare a dwelling and food -- which happens in many insects. Truly, instincts of this kind, so wisely adapted to the conservation and propagation of the animal kingdom, are a most brilliant testimony to the power, wisdom, and providence of God.

LXIV. "According to Its Kind": God Created Both Sexes

Those words in verse 21, according to its kind (or into their kinds, as the Vulgate has it), seem to emphasize that God created species -- that is, everything that was necessary for the generation and propagation of animals -- and therefore produced both sexes. For when He made living things, He was making things that would die; and without both sexes neither would a true species have existed, nor could individuals have multiplied upon the earth or filled the waters of the sea. Only one of the two sexes in this ordering of things is not fruitful by itself; it was therefore not fitting for the end intended by the Creator, nor was it good, for that one to be alone. "He made mention of genus and species in order to designate the generation of like from like." So says St. Thomas. This observation applies also to what is found in verses 24 and 25.

When it is said (verse 20): Let the waters bring forth (or let the waters swarm), as below in verse 24, let the earth bring forth, and verse 11, let the earth sprout, no one rightly thinks that the earth or the waters, even by God's command, were the artisans of animals or plants, or their true parents. These words are to be understood as referring to the place where these things began to exist, as if He said: "Let them be brought forth in the waters, let them be brought forth on the earth." Nevertheless, although neither of the waters in verse 20 nor of the earth in verse 24 is it expressly said that this was the material from which the bodies of living creatures were assembled, yet it must be believed that they were fashioned from the material existing there -- namely, aquatic creatures from water and from materials existing in the water itself, or in the mud or earth underlying the waters; while terrestrial creatures of the sixth day were made from earth (from which we also know the human body was made) -- not indeed from that pure and elementary earth which was the invention of the ancients, nor from any of the earths which chemistry catalogs, such as calcium; but from ordinary and common earth, which is always a mixture of several heterogeneous and composite substances. But what shall we say about the flying creatures made on this day? "Let the waters bring forth creeping things ... and flying creatures over the earth under the firmament of heaven." So the Vulgate, verse 20, with the Septuagint. And Onkelos: "And the flying creature that flies ..." They say that "which" is understood in the Hebrew, as elsewhere. But there is no indication of this in the original text, nor in other versions, such as the Syriac or the Samaritan. Nor does it appear why flying creatures -- even those which have no relation to the waters -- why, in short, the birds of heaven were made in the waters and came forth from the waters (with wet wings, no less) to fly over the earth. In the Vulgate itself we read in chapter II, verse 19: "The Lord God having formed from the ground all the animals of the earth and all the birds of heaven..." Although the last words may be referred to "having formed" and not to "from the ground," it must be admitted nonetheless that this passage gives little support to those who want flying creatures to have originated from water. The apocryphal book IV Esdras, chapter VI, verse 47 favors them, but testimony of this kind proves only the antiquity of the opinion.

Augustine did not doubt that Scripture teaches that flying creatures were produced from the waters just as aquatic creatures were; yet he explains it thus: "Whatever of the waters is either in flowing, undulating, and fluid form, or has been thinned and suspended in vapor -- so that the former appears distributed to creeping creatures of living souls, and the latter to flying creatures." Rupertus and St. Thomas hold the same view. This amounts to saying that flying creatures were formed not in the sea or in water properly so called, but in the moist air.

LXV. Hebrew Classification of Animals into Four Classes

In order to better understand the work of this day, or at least to distinguish what is certain from what is doubtful, certain things must be observed more carefully. And first, in what manner animals were distributed into classes among the Hebrews must be clear to us. The simplest of all divisions, and therefore the most ancient, assigned all living creatures to four classes in total, passing over the lower families which lack locomotion: first, walking creatures, or quadrupeds (behemah); second, flying creatures, or winged ones (hoph); third, creeping creatures, or reptiles (remesh); fourth, fish, or swimming creatures (dag). This distribution is indicated by Moses in verse 26 of this chapter and in chapter IX, 2, as well as Leviticus XI, 46 and Deuteronomy IV, 47-48. Hence we read of Solomon, the most ancient of zoologists, in III Kings IV, 33, that he discoursed on beasts (quadrupeds), and birds, and reptiles, and fish. In reality, man first distinguishes animals from plants by the faculty of locomotion which brute animals enjoy, and much later observes that certain creatures exist which, although they lack locomotion, nonetheless seem endowed with some spontaneous movement and sensation. But at first glance one notices, as I may say in the words of Cicero, that "some animals" reach their food by walking, others by crawling, others by flying, others by swimming. Hence that simple classification.

Lithophytes (which among more recent naturalists constitute the class they call polyps, showing neither the forms nor the locomotion of animals, were never classed among animals by the ancients; nor when the ancients speak of animals are they to be understood as speaking of these. In reality, if by the name "animal" they understood a creature endowed with the faculty of locomotion, equipped with organs fitted for exercising it, and having a form more or less approaching that of better-known animals, then surely lithophytes were not to be placed among animals. Nor should they now be placed in the animal kingdom, nor the other living things contained in the last division of this kingdom which they call radiate animals or zoophytes, if it were true what Lamarck teaches -- that there is no sensitive faculty in creatures of this kind, but that they exist entirely without feeling. Animals are not, according to St. Thomas, those which "lack local motion and sensation, by which the animate is most distinguished from the inanimate," and in which "life itself is hidden." Moses does not speak of these, nor can zoophytes be placed among reptiles or sea creatures or among flying creatures. The same St. Thomas says that Moses does not speak of minerals, because these "do not have a manifest distinction from the earth." So he would now say of zoophytes, because they do not have a manifest distinction from plants. Therefore, if it were demonstrated by geologists that certain species from this class, or rather province, had been produced before this day, nothing could thereby be deduced against sacred history.

Moses never mentions an animal in general in this narrative. Therefore we can say that he passed over entirely in silence the formation of those animals which are not classified under the classes indicated for the fifth and sixth day -- namely those which are neither flying creatures, nor creeping things, nor swimming things (a living and movable soul), nor things walking on feet.

Furthermore, as we heard from St. Thomas in section 55, "Moses set forth only those things which are manifestly apparent." Therefore someone would not absurdly say that he is silent about those innumerable microscopic organisms which are found as fossils, just as he is silent about those mineral bodies of which these organisms constitute no small part. The same can perhaps be said of trilobites, which, although they once constituted one family in the class of crustaceans, are certainly not among those things "which are manifestly apparent." For their ancient existence was unknown not only to the Hebrews, but also to students of natural history both old and recent, right up to these most recent times, in which they have been dug out of the earth already petrified and carefully examined; nor was it discovered, except after much inquiry about them, that they belonged to the crustaceans and did not lack feet, although these are almost never found. If by some chance any of these petrified organisms presented themselves to the eyes of the ancients, they supposed them, I think, to be inorganic bodies and the work of nature at play, as the saying goes. Therefore if someone were to prove that these trilobites, or certain microscopic organisms, existed before the emergence of dry land and thus before the fifth and even before the fourth period, this would not be a valid objection against the history of Moses. It would rather be a commentary demonstrating that among the animals of this day those creatures are not included which are in fact neither reptiles, at least in the proper sense, nor fish, nor sea creatures, nor flying things. The same can perhaps be said of certain shells entirely or almost entirely lacking in local motion, which are found among the more ancient fossils together with those crustaceans.

Fish are not mentioned here by their proper name dag, although Hebrew writers and Moses himself often use this word (Genesis IX, 2; Exodus VII, 18). Indeed even in this very chapter, verses 26 and 28, he most clearly presupposes that fish were created by God. But in this passage he preferred to use the word sherets, which is translated as "creeping thing," perhaps because it has a more extensive meaning; and this word, which is said to derive from abundance and fruitful propagation, seems more suitable for denoting aquatic creatures than the other word remesh, which is derived from treading the earth, and by which Moses indicates terrestrial reptiles in verses 24-25. The word sherets in this passage indicates fish and aquatic reptiles or amphibians, and probably other aquatic creatures which are not of the smallest size and enjoy local motion. To this class sherets Moses also assigns in Leviticus both insects equipped with wings, such as locusts, flies, and butterflies, and the smallest quadrupeds which rise little from the earth, such as moles, weasels, mice, etc., although they are viviparous animals and mammals (for oviparous quadrupeds are also counted among reptiles, such as the frog, the tortoise, the lizard, etc.). Therefore from all these animals, those which are either aquatic or amphibious can be thought to be comprehended in this passage by the word sherets. Among these are also the thanninim, great sea creatures, by which name are comprehended not only the class of cetaceans (which are not fish in the zoological sense, but are so in the common and ancient sense), but also other larger aquatic creatures, whether they are fish properly so called, or aquatic or amphibious reptiles. The other name hoph, from the similar verb houph ("it flew"), designates not only birds but all flying creatures. In this passage are to be understood, it seems, not only birds, but also flying reptiles, and perhaps flying insects of no small size. Seven or eight species from the genus pterodactyl (these were flying reptiles) have already been discovered in ancient fossiliferous strata.

LXVI. The Paleontological Sequence of Animal Life

That animal life began in the sea is deduced from observation in section 18. Scripture seems to confirm this; for it speaks first of aquatic creatures and of living things produced in water, and of the rest afterward. But which among aquatic creatures came first, Scripture is silent. That zoophytes preceded other animals seems probable by analogy, though observation has not yet confirmed this, although it is now held as certain that they were among the first. It seems that the ancient sea was inhabited for some time only by zoophytes and certain species of testaceans, and by trilobites which at that time, it appears, constituted the most perfect family of the animal kingdom and surpassed all other inhabitants of the water in the power of locomotion during that period. Afterward certain fish seem to have appeared, which were perhaps all of those now called sauroids, on account of the resemblance they bore to lizards and other reptiles of this kind. After these came marine reptiles, called saurians, and certain microscopic shells believed to have been inhabitants of fresh water. After these we find the first signs of flying creatures, namely flying reptiles and certain insects -- these two families, I mean flying reptiles and the most ancient insects, were probably the first flying creatures. There also belong to this period certain fish which seem to have inhabited fresh water, as well as certain saurians not entirely aquatic but probably amphibious, and tortoises both marine and fluvial, etc. Toward the end of this period, however, the remains of birds are less rare; and the most ancient of all seem to be those families which frequent waters and riverbanks. Perhaps also some very rare and small mammal should be assigned to this period, but probably an aquatic one (section 18). Reptiles are to be placed earlier. It is credible that the first cetaceans belong to the end of this period: we may also suppose that the word thanninim indicates those enormous saurians, in which combined characteristics and similarities of cetaceans, reptiles, and fish are perceived.

This day, as I have indicated, must be considered from geological teachings to have been a very long period. That some catastrophe of the earth preceded it, and another greater one followed, is entirely probable. Both were spread through vast parts of the earth, and could in many places cast darkness upon the sky, and bring obscurity over the face of the whole earth, or nearly the whole (Section 18).

In addition, between the first and last catastrophes, several others appear to have existed, though perhaps contained within a smaller span. Therefore the face of the earth and sea was changed many times; lands were often elevated, and dry ground was made from the sea; and conversely, waters occupied those places where the most solid earth had been. Meanwhile muddy eruptions from the bottom of the sea widely disturbed the waters, and destroyed not only innumerable individuals, but many species perhaps, and even entire genera of living things: an immense mass of vegetable substances was buried deep in the sea, and reserved for the future uses of the human race yet to be born: finally those ancient disturbances of the earth prepared for us springs of water and metals, and so arranged and placed the very coal that it could easily be found and extracted. And amidst all this, the execution of the divine command of verse 20 proceeded, and new species of aquatic and flying creatures appeared day by day; the waters swarmed with living things, and winged creatures flew over the face of the earth and sea. Those disturbances of the earth which we have mentioned could have introduced small modifications into the bodies of living things; but by successive natural changes of this kind, those abrupt, sudden, and great differences which we perceive in the organization of many successive animals are in no way explained; rather, we are compelled to admit the creation of entirely new species. Otherwise some indications of these transformations -- namely transitions between the earlier and later forms of the same animal -- would be found in terrestrial strata.

LXVII. Whether the Animal Kingdom is More Ancient than the Vegetable

Some have thought the animal kingdom to be more ancient than the vegetable. Others say that the testimony of nature does not determine whether one preceded the other -- the vegetable kingdom or the animal. As to this last objection, certainly nothing follows from it against our explanation, and much less against the truth of Scripture. For it is sufficient that nature does not demonstrate the contrary; nor should we demand that physical science demonstrate everything that Moses indicated -- especially a science which can scarcely be called mature, as is certainly the case with positive geology. The contrary, indeed, it has not demonstrated. But those who thought animals more ancient cited in their support certain strata more ancient than the carboniferous formation, in which some animal remains are found. But in some of these strata, vegetable remains have also been discovered; and in the others, since they are very similar to those first ones, especially with regard to animal remains, it is highly probable that, with more diligent study applied, remains of plants will also be detected. Moreover, it is by no means certain that these strata are always more recent than the carboniferous formation: indeed the contrary has sometimes been observed. If we attend to physical reasoning, that great quantity of carbonic acid which, as is very probable, widely mixed itself with the air in the first period of vegetation, would have been a great impediment to the life of animals -- at least those which breathe free air, such as not only birds and mammals, but also reptiles, cetaceans, and dragonflies and similar insects. From analogy also, the vegetable kingdom should be considered more ancient, inasmuch as it serves the other kingdom of living things, and is simpler. Furthermore, the more ancient the fossiliferous strata are, the simpler the forms of organic nature they display. Would it not then be contrary to analogy if the vegetable kingdom were posterior to the animal, when it has so much simpler forms? At least it is evident that those animals which receive their nourishment directly or indirectly from plants could not have been brought into the light before the plants which were destined as their food existed, and had sufficiently grown and been multiplied. If we reason from what we observe, surely that immense quantity of vegetable remains, with which the more ancient fossiliferous strata abound and which increase the wealth of more opulent nations -- if compared with the relative scarcity of fossil animals -- almost compels us to conclude that plants had already existed for a long interval of time, and had been greatly multiplied upon the earth, when animals were brought into the light and began to multiply; especially if we remember that there was indeed a very ample space for aquatic animals in the sea at that time, but not a very extensive one for land plants, whose territory was confined to the surface of small islands. But most of all, I think it should be noted, first, that those strata which are objected to us were formed in the sea, which is why they more easily display marine offspring but necessarily contain fewer terrestrial ones, especially in those places which, being then far distant from islands, received marine sediments; second, that a great quantity of vegetable substances is only accidentally buried under water, and moreover most of these substances, unless quickly covered by earth, easily decompose in water.

LXVIII. Augustine's Doctrine of Causal and Seminal Creation

But those who assert that "the vegetable nature and the animal appeared at one time" neither contradict us nor Scripture, if they understand that when the more imperfect plants were appearing, the lowest creatures of the animal kingdom were also produced, namely zoophytes (Section 65). Nor would one perhaps be opposing Moses who believed that a few now-extinct species of testacea, and the likewise extinct family of Trilobites, were not less ancient than plants. For Moses does not indicate the history of individual species or genera one by one, but rather the highest classes of living things, in a general manner, and "puts forward only those things which appear openly." On the third day God made the vegetable kingdom; on the fifth, the class of aquatic reptiles, which includes fish, and of flying creatures: that is, swimming and flying things; on the sixth day, finally, creeping and walking things upon the earth. About certain exceptions, if there was room for them, he is not concerned, nor was he pressed by any necessity to be so. And so if before the fifth or before the fourth day a few species of more imperfect animals arose (which ceased to live long ago), yet with no species from the principal division -- namely from the four classes of vertebrates -- accompanying them; would it be any less true that those classes of which Moses speaks were created on the fifth and sixth days? Would the statement of Marcel de Serres be false: "From physical facts, as well as from the history of Genesis, it appears that life upon earth began with the simplest of living things, and that plants not only originally abounded more than animals, but that those species of plants which lived on dry land appeared long before the greatest part of animals that live there"? Does Moses lie and not speak the truth when he attributes either the formation of the vegetable kingdom to the third day, or of aquatic and flying creatures to the fifth, if certain species and genera of plants were not brought into the light until three days later (Section 50), or if certain aquatic and flying creatures not until the sixth day? This seems more tolerable; because if we partly embrace Augustine's doctrine of causal, potential, and invisible creation, or rather, if we proceed by a certain middle way between his doctrine and the common one, we may rightly say that when God visibly created the classes of swimming and flying things and actually produced their firstborn, He also created at the same time invisibly, seminally, and according to potency the genera and species of those classes which were to exist in the following period. Concerning the words of God to the first parents in verses 28-29: "Increase..." Augustine says: "His very word, before all sound of the air, before all voice of flesh and cloud, in that supreme wisdom of His, through which all things were made, did not as it were sound in human ears, but was inserting into things made the causes of things to be made, and by omnipotent power was making future things, and was laying up man -- to be formed in his own time -- as it were in the seed or root of the ages, when He was establishing everything from which the ages, founded by Him who exists before the ages, were to begin." These things I neither defend nor censure; but certainly he would seem less deserving of censure who thought the words of God to the animals in verse 22 -- "Increase," etc. -- to be indeed a law imposed on those classes of brutes which were visibly being created, but that, just as that law was imposed not only on the first individuals already existing, but also on those which were to come in their own time, and on each species of which those individuals bore, as it were, the individual person and office: so too it was imposed on entire classes, to which the first animals actually created belonged, through which the universal classes were displayed before the eyes of the Creator: but that the species to be formed in their own time were then established, as it were in seed or in root.

Indeed, if God, while calling the vegetable kingdom into existence on the third day, and many classes of the animal kingdom on the fifth, willed that the formation of certain families, genera, or species be deferred to another time, was Moses bound to report all these exceptions and deferrals? This would have been to write a kind of treatise on paleontology. But he had not been sent by God to satisfy our curiosity, but to free the Israelites from the captivity of Egypt and to teach them the law and worship of God.

LXIX. The Meaning of "Every" (Kol) in Scripture

Nor should it cause us difficulty, if I am not mistaken, that we read in verse 21 that God created every living and creeping animal, and every winged creature. The Hebrew word kol is indeed rendered "every, all"; but interpreters have noted that it sometimes means only "many." For example, in Genesis chapter 41, verses 54 and 56, and chapter 43, verse 1, we read that "famine prevailed in the whole (kol) world, and famine increased in every (kol) land," which is certainly not to be understood of the entire world then known; for in Egypt there was no scarcity of grain -- indeed there was an enormous abundance. In Exodus 9:3 it is predicted: "The hand of the Lord shall be upon your cattle which are in the field, upon the horses, and asses, and camels, and oxen, and sheep." And in verse 6 we read: "And all (kol) the cattle of Egypt died," or, as the Vulgate has it, all the animals of the Egyptians. And yet afterward it is predicted, in verse 9: "There shall be boils and blistering sores upon men and beasts throughout all the land of Egypt," which is narrated as having been fulfilled in verse 10. And afterward, in verses 19 and following, Moses says to Pharaoh: "Gather your cattle, and all that you have in the field: for men and cattle, and whatever is found outside and not gathered from the fields, and the hail falls upon them, they shall die. He who feared the word of the Lord among the servants of Pharaoh made his servants and cattle flee into the houses... and the hail struck throughout all the land of Egypt, from man to beast." We read in Genesis 24:10 (Hebrew), that Abraham's servant carried with him all the goods of his master, which means, as the Vulgate well renders, "from all his goods."

If absolutely every sherets (swarming creature) was made on the fifth day, then moles, mice, shrews, weasels, hedgehogs, and other such creatures also existed; for these animals are also enumerated among the sherets by Moses in Leviticus 11, and they would also have to be said to have been made again on the sixth day; for on that day (verse 25) God made both chayya, that is wild beasts, and behema, by which word any terrestrial viviparous quadruped can be signified, and finally remes, which is interpreted as (from treading the earth) a terrestrial reptile. But this word extends to any terrestrial animal that is neither a quadruped of considerable size nor a bird. Therefore, to avoid the absurdity of saying that something was created twice, we must not interpret the words of Moses in the broadest possible signification. It seems therefore that it cannot be established from Moses whether the formation of those animals should be assigned to the fifth day or to the sixth; and so whether geologists find that they existed in the fifth period, or not before the sixth, or whether they can finally prove that some of them appeared in the fifth period and some in the sixth, these findings will never be against Genesis. When we read that man was to have dominion over "every reptile that moves upon the earth," verse 26, "all living things that move upon the earth," verse 28, that God gave us every herb bearing seed upon the earth, and all trees that have seed, to be our food, verse 29, and "to all animals of the earth, and to every bird," verse 30, I do not think we are bound to always take the words "every," "all," "each" in the broadest signification.

LXX. Animals Generated from Putrefaction; Seminal Reasons

St. Thomas teaches that "animals which are generated from the corruption of other animals could not then (on the fifth and sixth days) have been produced except only potentially." And in the following Question he adds: "New species, if any appear, pre-existed (in the works of the six days) in certain active powers, just as animals generated from putrefaction are produced from the powers of the stars and elements, which they received from the beginning, even if new species of such animals are produced... but some preceded according to likeness, just as souls which are now created." Nor were these doctrines peculiar to him, but were common to the Peripatetic school, which flourished for many centuries. Thus Peter Lombard: It can be said that those things which are born "especially from the bodies of dead animals were not created with the animals except potentially and materially." Also St. Bonaventure says that those things were "produced in their principles and seminal reasons." And Cornelius a Lapide: "Small animals which are born from sweat, exhalation, or putrefaction, such as fleas, mice, and other small worms, were not created formally on this sixth day, but potentially, and as it were by seminal reason; because, namely, those animals were created on this day from whose certain condition these were naturally going to arise... Certainly that fleas and similar worms which are in human beings should then be born would be contrary to the most happy state of innocence." No one is unaware that this kind of doctrine is obsolete, which maintained that so many animals, and some even of a higher class, such as mice, arise from putrefaction: nevertheless I have brought these forward to show that it did not seem absurd to pious and learned men that certain animals should be said to have been first produced outside of these days. And indeed, if certain lowly animals, such as certain intestinal worms, arise from some modification of animal matter according to the laws of God, then certainly this doctrine is to be applied to them. Likewise, if there are eggs which do not develop and produce a self-moving animal except in the body of another animal, and in peculiar and morbid circumstances, and perhaps in animals modified by a state of servitude and domestication, or in those substances which are extracted from plants and sometimes worked by human skill; these tiny animals could have been made on those days as it were by seminal reason, that is, only in the state of seed or egg.

Regarding intestinal worms or entozoa, note the following. In most of them there are organs of reproduction: these are lacking in those called vesicular worms. The distinguished Louvain professor Van Beneden demonstrated by many and most careful observations that worms of this kind are not animals in a perfect state, but rather the first stage of an entozoon which has not yet arrived at its final and perfect state, to which it often cannot arrive except in the body of an animal of another species; this translocation occurs when the animal that had received this parasite as its guest, so to speak, is devoured by another -- for example, a fish by an aquatic bird or by a larger fish: then the parasite, if it remains alive, acquires new organs and a perfect state in which alone it is capable of producing offspring. The experiments of the distinguished Kuchenmeister confirm these findings. Therefore those worms no more arise from spontaneous generation than does a caterpillar or larva which arises from the egg of a butterfly.

It is almost superfluous to point out that also on the fifth day (as on the sixth) a certain mixing preceded -- namely, of the matter destined for the formation of animal bodies together with other inanimate matter, which was followed by division, opening, and the birth of the firstborn, as the waters and lands were opened, and the firstborn of various species of the animal kingdom were brought into the light. It is probable that this period began with a great catastrophe, raising certain lands from the sea and darkening the sky, and was ended by a similar but even greater disturbance of lands and seas, which extinguished very many living things, changed the face of the earth, and likewise could have darkened the sky for some time.

Now let us proceed to examine the greatest birth of the earth, by which the voice of the commanding Creator ennobled the earth itself, not only with the appearance of more perfect brutes, but also with the presence of the firstborn of the human family.

ON THE WORK OF THE SIXTH DAY AND ON THE SEVENTH DAY

(From Verse 24 to the End of the Chapter)

LXXI. Quadrupeds, Wild Beasts, and Terrestrial Reptiles

There are two works of the sixth day. The first is the formation of quadrupeds, or more perfect brutes, and of terrestrial reptiles: the other, and far more excellent, is the creation of man. And concerning the first, little remains for us to bring forward, after what we have discussed in Sections 50, 62, 63, 64, 66, 68, 69, 70.

God said: Let the earth bring forth the living soul according to its kind, cattle (behema -- beast of burden, domestic animal) and reptiles (remes -- reptile) and beasts (chajja -- wild animal) of the earth according to their kinds. And it was so. The word behema is sometimes used generally for a terrestrial quadruped, sometimes for a terrestrial quadruped of not the smallest size; but when it is set in opposition to the name chajja, as is done here, it is usually interpreted as a beast of burden, by which name we understand a domestic animal: chajja, however, is interpreted as a wild beast or forest animal. Some prefer to render the first name as a herbivorous or plant-eating animal, and the second as carnivorous. And indeed, before the creation of man, there were no domestic animals; moreover this distinction between domestic and wild animal is, in great part, accidental. For even now a species that is entirely the same -- for example, the pig -- belongs to both classes. And the African species of the horse genus would be, I believe, domestic and useful, as the horse and donkey are, if men had applied effort to subjugating those also. On the other hand, man's most faithful companion, the dog, is reckoned in the order of wild beasts; and left to itself, at least after some generations, it returns to the nature of wild beasts and shows itself to be a congener of the wolf, as indeed it is. St. Thomas thinks that here "by beasts are understood fierce animals, such as bears and lions," but not gentle ones, such as "deer and goats." He adds, "by reptiles, however, animals which either do not have feet by which they are raised from the ground, such as serpents, or have short ones by which they are little raised, such as lizards and ants." In fact, as I have said, remes among the Hebrews seems to denote any animal which is neither a quadruped of considerable size, nor a bird, nor a fish. Here are excluded those which were made on the fifth day.

The first part of this period, in which only brute animals are said to have been made, appears to have been comprised within a sufficiently long time. Both kingdoms -- the vegetable, I mean, and the animal -- approached and eventually arrived at that state which they now hold during this time. To speak only of viviparous quadrupeds, certain genera appear to have been produced earlier (Section 18), which no longer appear on the earth; afterward other genera very similar to the genera now living, or the same as them, but whose species differ somewhat from the species living at this time; and finally this class arrived at its present state and condition. Marcel de Serres writes that the law of succession of living things, whose appearance is in inverse ratio to the complexity with which their organization is endowed, appears most clearly in mammals. Moreover the two orders, namely wild beasts and quadrumanes (primates), appear to have been produced late, for (which is not explained in the common understanding of the six days) they are found rarely, and only in the upper strata. But the body of these quadrumanes approaches most closely to the structure of the human body. And in the divine command (verse 24), wild beasts are mentioned last. And in truth, it seemed to belong to divine Providence that wild beasts, which live by the slaughter of other animals, should not arrive except late, and not before those others had sufficiently grown and multiplied upon the earth; especially if in the first ages of animal life, all the habitable land was contained in islands of no great extent, where animals of such diverse character could not long dwell together, because the defenseless species would quickly be destroyed.

LXXII. Were All Animals Originally Herbivorous?

Many interpreters of Scripture thought that all animals were herbivorous from the beginning, and that it came about through man's disobedience that some are now food for others. Not a few, however, held the opposite view, such as A Lapide and especially St. Thomas, who says this assertion is "entirely irrational." I think no one now takes pleasure in that opinion who is even slightly acquainted with the study of nature. That carnivores were formed by the Creator for prey and for animal food is evident from the structure of their teeth, articulated digits, and entire body: wherefore if a herbivorous animal were turned into a carnivore, its entire form would be changed, and a true metamorphosis would occur. Certainly a lion or a cat, if their teeth, claws, instinct, and habits were entirely changed, and especially if their neck and legs also became longer, would be entirely different animals. Moreover, it is established by certain observations that even in the preceding period there were both aquatic reptiles and flying creatures which sustained their life by hunting and killing other animals, even of their own kind. Therefore nothing prevents terrestrial carnivores from also having existed before the rise of man and his sin. Two things, I think, gave occasion to that doctrine. First, the brevity, as it was supposed, of those days; and indeed if these days are concluded within the space of 24 hours, it was absolutely necessary for God to create very many individuals of many species, not so that they might grow and multiply, but only so that they might immediately satisfy the ravenous hunger of lions, tigers, panthers, wolves, dogs, and other such beasts from those very first times: which is certainly not probable. We rather, from the nature of wild beasts, which were always wild, and which not a single witness testifies to have undergone any change of form, can construct a not-weak argument against the brevity of those days. The other and principal foundation of that opinion are the words of God to the first parents in verses 29-30: "Behold I have given (or give) you every herb... and all trees... to be food for you: and to all the animals of the earth, and to every bird (flying creature) of the sky, and to all things that move (or creep) upon the earth, and in which there is a living soul, that they may have for food." But we have just pointed out (Section 69) that words of this kind -- "all," "every," "each" -- are not always to be taken in the strictest sense: here it is evident that they cannot be taken in the strictest sense, unless one were to say that absolutely all herbs and all trees are suitable food for all animals. But all these things can be conveniently explained if we say that the totality of plants, the vegetable kingdom, is given by God as food to these great classes of quadrupeds, flying creatures, and reptiles. St. Thomas says most aptly: "For the nature of animals was not changed by the sin of man, so that those for which it is now natural to eat the flesh of other animals would then have lived on herbs, like lions and falcons. Nor does the gloss of Bede on Genesis 1 say that trees and herbs were given to all animals and birds as food, but to some." Furthermore, to pass over the fact that carnivores sometimes eat plants, as we see in dogs, plants provide food to the entire animal kingdom either immediately or mediately: for wild beasts are nourished by animals which are fed by plants.

LXXIII. The Earth Prepared for Man; Fossils Confirm Moses

The terrestrial globe now teems with animal life: no region is deprived of its own living creatures:

The waves yielded to the bright fish to be their dwelling;
The earth received the wild beasts, the restless air the birds.
A holier animal, more capable of lofty mind,
Was still lacking, one that could rule over the rest:
Man was born.

"Just as hosts do not call guests to dinner before they have prepared everything necessary for the feast, and those who put on athletic games and theatrical shows, before they assemble the spectators in theaters or stadiums, provide an abundance of competitors and of those things which pertain to the delight of the eyes or ears: so the prince of the whole world, as the giver of a contest or feast, about to call man to the banquet and spectacle, prepared everything that pertained to both: so that upon entering the world, he would immediately find both a feast and a most sacred theater." Thus Philo Judaeus in his book On the Creation of the World: Gregory of Nyssa says similar things. No less elegantly, Chrysostom and Fulgentius draw a comparison from an Emperor, whom the palace guards and household retainers are accustomed to precede when he is about to enter a city, to prepare the palace: just as rays precede the rising sun, as Synesius says.

That man was not made before the earth and seas teemed with living animals and plants is excellently confirmed by the natural distribution and succession of fossils; and all geologists of our age, whatever they may think about religion, readily agree with Moses on this point. For they find no remains of man or of human arts in the ancient fossiliferous strata, amid so many remains of both kingdoms of nature. Therefore what in the common exposition of the days of Genesis is an objection against Moses' history, and one not easily resolved, is on the contrary in our view a powerful confirmation of the same.

Since Theologians treat of man in a special treatise, it will be permitted for us to treat this chief work of God -- which is said to have been made in the image of God Himself -- with a somewhat hastening pen. This was also observed by the pagans, as by Plato and by Eurysus the Pythagorean in his book On Fortune: man --

While other animals look down upon the earth with lowered gaze,
He gave man a lofty face, and bade him gaze upon the sky,
And lift his upright countenance to the stars.
Thus the earth, which had lately been rough and without form,
Put on, being changed, the unknown shapes of men.

Many others looked to this same idea. Seneca says: "He not only raised man upright, but also, having made him for contemplation so that he might follow the stars gliding from their rising to their setting, and cast his gaze about with the whole heavens, He made his head sublime." Silius, XV, 84:

Do you not see how God raised the lofty faces of men to the stars,
And fashioned their countenances to look on high?

Aristotle writes: "This alone among all animals is upright, because its nature and substance is divine. Moreover its work, which is the most divine of all, is to understand and to reason. But this would not be easy if a vast bodily mass pressed down from above. For heaviness makes the mind slower and dulls the common sense."

Gregory of Nyssa writes that the upright form of man, tending heavenward, is a sign of dominion and authority, and of a certain royal dignity. While on the contrary, the other animals, stooping and looking toward the earth, testify by that bodily posture to their servitude and subjection.

"The body of man," says Augustine, "because alone among the bodies of earthly animals it is not cast down prone upon its belly, but is visible and raised upright for gazing upon the sky, which is the source of visible things -- although it is known to live not by its own power but by the presence of the soul -- yet not only because it exists and insofar as it exists is it certainly good; but also because it is such as to be more fitted for contemplating the sky, it can rightly be judged to be made more to the image and likeness of God than the bodies of other animals." Although Augustine here concedes that the living body of man is in some way made to the likeness of God, yet elsewhere he rejects a material likeness of our body to God as ridiculous, indeed impious invention, and teaches that the image of God, according to which man is said to be made, properly consists not in the exterior man, but in the interior. Nevertheless, the upright stature and the countenance raised toward heaven indicate that the soul, ruler of the body, sprung from a heavenly origin and similar to God, capable of eternity and divinity, ought to look upon and aspire to things above. For indeed, if glass is worth so much, how much more the pearl? If the body is such, what must the soul be? So Augustine and Bernard.

LXXIV. "Let Us Make Man": The Holy Trinity and the Image of God

"Let us make man"... and further: "Let us make him a helper like unto himself." "In a somewhat tumultuous manner," says Gregory of Nyssa, "the rest of creation is produced by divine power, subsisting by command alone. But deliberation precedes the fashioning of man: and by the Artificer himself, a certain verbal portrait foreshadows what was to be." Chrysostom likewise observes this, as do others throughout. Catholic theologians hold that the mystery of the most Holy Trinity is here indicated. God does not address angels with those words, as though they were equals, as if He were committing to them the construction of the human body, while reserving to Himself the creation of the rational soul. This view, which the Platonist Philo and other Jews believed, is entirely rejected by the Holy Fathers, and specifically by Saints Basil, Chrysostom, Cyril, Augustine, and Thomas. For He would not have said "in our image" if He were addressing creatures. The Fathers therefore teach -- Chrysostom, Basil, Rupert, and others -- that "this is said to signify the plurality of the divine Persons, whose image is found more expressly in man" (these are the words of Aquinas); and that God the Father addresses His Son and the Holy Spirit. Indeed the Council of Sirmium (found in Hilary of Poitiers' book On Synods) pronounced anathema against all who explain this passage otherwise. Irenaeus says: "He was fashioned by His hands: that is, by the Son and the Holy Spirit, to whom He also said: Let us make man."

"And God created man in His own image," verse 27. The word vayyibra ("and He created") and the image of God indicate sufficiently that here it is not about the body alone, but also about the rational soul, created from non-existence. On the word bara see sections 23 and 24. Furthermore, in the following chapter II, verse 7, the twofold formation of man is expressed more clearly. "The Lord God formed man from the dust of the earth, and breathed into his face the breath of life, and man became a living soul." In this passage, the breath signifies the soul (which is not explicitly named in chapter I); which the Hebrews interpret as the rational and intelligent soul. But it is probable that by that word the soul in general is designated -- the soul that gives form to the animal body -- which in man is indeed rational. God thus joined and bound together in a truly marvelous way two such diverse natures -- the rational mind and the dust (as the Hebrew reads) or clay of the earth -- into one person.

The author of Ecclesiastes was looking to this passage when he wrote, in the last chapter, verse 7: "Let the dust return to the earth, whence it came (behold man made from the dust of the earth), and let the spirit return to God who gave it (there you have the breath inspired by God)." The Hebrew reads "dust." But this must be understood as mixed with moisture, that is, clay: for the verb yatsar properly pertains to the potter's art, and means the same as "fashioned." In Wisdom VII, 1, Adam is called "first-formed" and "earth-born"; and in I Corinthians XV, 47, the first man is called "of the earth, earthly."

Just as God made the bodies of the beasts of the earth, so also He made the body of man from pre-existing earth, as Moses teaches, chapter I, 24 and II, 7, 19, and thus he reminds us of our lowliness: but the sublimity of those words by which the formation of God's image is narrated sufficiently demonstrates the excellence of our nature. "Your hands have made me and fashioned me entirely round about," Job said to God. "Remember, I pray, that You made me like clay." Nor does Elihu contradict him in this: for he says: "God made me just as He made you, and I too was formed from the same clay." Furthermore, the hands of God are frequently mentioned among the ancients when they speak of the formation of man. We have heard Irenaeus; let us hear Tertullian: "Who, after all, is worthy to inhabit the works of God, other than His own image and likeness? That too, goodness -- and indeed a more excellent work it is -- fashioned, not by an imperial word, but by a familiar hand, with even a coaxing word sent ahead: 'Let us make man in our image and likeness.' Goodness fashioned man from clay." Prosper in his Poem on Providence: "And while He would create all things by His word, this one He deigns to form with His own hands, that he might have more of his Creator." And Prudentius:

So great the love of earth, so great the affection for us!
He deigns to grasp in divine fingers the soft clod
Of rich soil; nor does He consider foul
The contact of the clinging mass. He had commanded that light
Be made; it was made, as He had commanded: all things
Drew forth new forms at His commanding word.
Man alone earned to receive a face fashioned
By the Lord's right hand, and to be born by the breath of the Godhead's creative act.

That the hands and fingers of God are to be understood figuratively is sufficiently clear. Irenaeus says that the Son is signified, and the Holy Spirit, who are sometimes called the hands of God the Father. By this tropical expression, from which the author of Genesis abstained, there can simply be signified a certain special care and diligence of God the Artificer, bending to the work of fashioning man. Nevertheless, setting aside the anthropomorphites, there were those who believed that God assumed some kind of bodily form in order to fashion man from clay, indeed even in order to create the visible world: which view seems to me to show either an excessive adherence to the literal sense, or a mind enslaved to fancy.

LXXV. Male and Female; Dominion over the Animals

"Male and female He created them." So in the same verse 27 of chapter I, and in chapter V, 2. Unless the formation of the woman were treated elsewhere, we would suppose that female and male were made at one and the same time. Yet chapter II teaches us that the matter turned out far otherwise. And here I cannot restrain myself from noting in passing: If, just as in chapter II the formation of our first parents is set forth at somewhat greater length, so in another chapter any other divine operation indicated in chapter I were described more fully -- for instance, the formation of plants, or of birds, or of land animals -- how many things that are described in chapter I as having been accomplished at the same or nearly the same time, we would discover to have been produced at altogether different and widely separated times!

We read that man was created by God in chapter I, 26, to preside over the animals as His vicegerent and image: for to our first parents it is not only said in verse 28: "Increase and multiply and fill the earth." But it is added: "And subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea, and the birds of the air, and all living things that move upon the earth." As a sign of this dominion (section 36), man is given the task of imposing names upon the animals (chapter II, 19, 20).

A remarkable thing! This imposition of names has a certain connection with the formation of the woman. For see how the succession of events is set forth in chapter II. Verse 18: "The Lord God said: It is not good for man (Adam) to be alone (it is not good for Adam, it is not good for the male sex -- I do not say for the human species, for the species without the other sex is nothing; it is not good, but after the sufficient propagation of mankind, it will be good for many who will choose the better part): let us make him a helper like unto himself (or suitable for him)." Verse 19: "The Lord God, having formed out of the ground all the animals of the earth and all the birds of the sky, brought them to Adam, to see what he would call them. For whatever Adam called (or whatever man might call) a living creature, that was (or would be) its name." Does Moses wish to indicate that it belongs to the man whom God set over the brutes to impose names upon those brutes? Why then is it not recorded that man was commissioned to give names to the plants as well, although he was to devote himself to their cultivation (chapter II, 15)? Is it because this kind of name-giving indicates that dominion which is held over servants? Plants certainly are not subject to man as king, as the brutes are, but were given to him for food (chapter I, 29).

Verse 20: "And Adam called by their names all the animals (domestic or herbivorous) and all the birds of the sky, and all the beasts of the earth (wild animals of the field). But for Adam there was not found a helper like unto him (or suitable for him)." Verse 21: "The Lord God therefore sent (and He sent) a deep sleep upon Adam: and when he had fallen asleep, He took one of his ribs (or one of his parts, one portion of him), and filled up flesh in its place." Verse 22: "And the Lord God built the rib (or part) which He had taken from Adam into a woman; and He brought her to Adam." Verse 23: "And Adam said: This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh." As if he were saying: the other animals do not suit me, because they are dissimilar in kind, with heads bent down toward the earth, lacking speech and reason; this one is most like me, and is part of my flesh and my bones: "She shall be called 'virago' ('vira'), because she was taken from man." When Adam imposes a name on the woman, he demonstrates that he has some authority over her. But the other name, which remained peculiar to the first woman, Adam gave to his wife (chapter III, verse 20) after God's sentence, in which among other things Eve had heard it said to her: "You shall be under the power of your husband, and he shall rule over you" (chapter III, 16). Many questions arise from this account. Setting aside those that do not concern us at present, I shall say something about only a few.

LXXVI. The Animals Brought to Adam; Formation of Eve

You may ask first: Whether what we read about the animals being brought to Adam, and also about the formation of Eve from the body of Adam himself, should be taken literally or spiritually? Second, did all these things happen on the sixth day? Third, did Adam truly give each and every animal of the earth and every bird its own name? Even those living in the waters, as Chrysostom believed? Fourth, how can these events be understood so that we neither abandon the literal sense nor expose sacred history to the ridicule of the wise men of this world?

To the first I respond: Cajetan interprets spiritually what is written about the animals being brought into Adam's sight. "He brought them," he says, "not by local motion, but by intellectual approach... so that he might see with the eyes of the mind the nature of terrestrial creatures," etc. Thus he denies the history of Eve's formation and thinks it should be interpreted according to a mystery or as a parable. Catharinus sharply, and not without cause, rebukes him. But we know that Cajetan annotated Scripture too boldly in many matters, and not cautiously enough (Petavius). Interpretations of this kind have the Holy Fathers and Doctors of the Church opposed to them, and smack of something of Origenian hermeneutics (section 20); and therefore they are to be entirely rejected. Who would ever tolerate a person interpreting the more difficult passages of Herodotus or Livy in such a manner? Although some difficulties may be found in this account, they are nevertheless not of such a kind that we must abandon the historical sense. Yet we are not thereby compelled to rush to the opposite extreme, and to take each and every word, whether of the Hebrew text or of the Vulgate, in the stricter grammatical sense that they present, even when a less fitting and less plausible meaning results from it.

To the second question I respond affirmatively: Philastrius, Bishop of Brescia, is not to be heeded in this matter. He denounces as heretics those who say that the body of Adam was fashioned from the earth before the soul was created. Against them he asserts that his soul was created on the sixth day, but his body on the seventh day, when the woman was also taken from his side; and that the breath of life is not the soul, but a certain grace of the divine Spirit. There is no heresy -- not even in the broadest sense -- in that first, quite common position, which corresponds more closely to the letter of verse 7, chapter II. But that Adam was made on the sixth day is openly stated in chapter I, 26 and following ("Let us make man... and God created man"). The discourse in chapter I is not about souls alone, nor is the soul the whole man, nor do separated souls rule over "fish, birds, and beasts, and the whole earth," nor is it said to them: "Increase and multiply, and fill the earth;" nor are herbs and "the fruits of trees for food" given to the soul. Although some have denied that Eve was made on the sixth day (perhaps because one solar day seemed too short for so many operations, not only of God but also of Adam himself), nevertheless the contrary is proven by entirely certain arguments. And first, in verse 27 of chapter I, we read on the sixth day: "And God created man... Male and female He created them. And God blessed them, and said: Increase," etc. Second, God had completed His work by the seventh day, "and He rested on the seventh day from all the work He had done" (chapter II, 2); therefore on the seventh day He made neither the first woman nor anything else new. Finally, on the sixth day "God saw all that He had made, and it was very good" (chapter I, 31): therefore man was not then alone without a woman; for this God would not have seen as very good, but as not good, chapter II, 18. Since this is the case, neither can what we read about the brutes being called by their proper names be deferred beyond the sixth day: for the imposition of names preceded the formation of the woman. For since God had destined a woman as companion and helper for Adam for the procreation of offspring, in order that Adam himself might understand the need for this help and desire the companionship of a creature like himself, God willed that he should review the animals and call them by their proper names. That this was the very reason why God brought the land animals and birds to Adam, Moses clearly indicates; for he adds: "But for Adam there was not found a helper like unto him," plainly as if to say: When he saw that each of the animals had been given its own mate, differing only in sex, he found no suitable companion for himself among that number: therefore he desired what was lacking and hoped for it from God. Ambrose too notes this. God did not wish to bind to marriage one who was unaware and therefore not at all desirous, but rather to provide a companion and helper to one who desired and asked.

LXXVII. Did Adam Name All Animals?

To the third question I say that there is no mention of aquatic creatures in this passage; nor is there any reason to assert that Adam gave a name to all fish and sea creatures, or even to one of them, at that first time. It would have been an endless task, especially if we include shellfish and all the other small creatures that live in the waters. Augustine excepts fish from that naming, and believes their names were given gradually over time.

But are we bound to believe that Adam gave fitting names to all birds and land animals, after inspecting them all and noting each one individually? I think not: because, as interpreters have observed (sections 69, 72), "all," "every," "the whole" must sometimes be interpreted as "many." Recall (section 72) the gloss of Bede praised by St. Thomas on verse 30 of chapter I, where "all" is explained as "some." Certainly Scripture no more compels us to say that Adam gave names to absolutely every land beast and bird on the sixth day, than it does to say that absolutely every animal of the earth and every bird and everything that moves is now herbivorous, or was so in the earliest times. That innumerable animals from diverse and widely separated regions, inhabitants of different climates and hostile to one another, should have gathered in one place to present themselves before Adam and receive from him a name that no one would use, as soon as those animals had returned to their native lands still inhabited by no human beings -- this exceeds all plausibility whatsoever. Some, like Calmet, have tried to make the matter more credible by supposing that all land animals were created near paradise, where God placed man. Augustine and Gregory of Nyssa had by no means approved this opinion, and after fossil remains of animals have been discovered everywhere on earth, no one, I believe, accepts it in our age. Augustine believed that the animals were brought to Adam by the ministry of Angels. Ambrose thought that wild beasts and birds were gathered by divine operation, but that the tamer land animals could have been collected by man himself. Whether there is here or not a necessity for us to have recourse to miracle, let others judge. If the ancients had known that many animal species are found only in America or Australia, perhaps they would not have brought them to Adam, and that by means of a great miracle, just so that they might hear him utter a single word whose memory would immediately have been lost, since those animals enjoyed the company of no human being. Indeed I fear it may seem ridiculous to imagine the individual species of animals, two by two, in order and as if in a marching column, passing before the man, while the man himself, as they file past, rapidly recites over them a large part of the Linnaean System of Nature. It seems much more plausible that all classes of land animals, with many species standing for their respective groups, were brought before Adam's eyes -- species that either dwelt in the place where the first man had been set, or approached him, divine Providence gently arranging this without any supernatural cause or effect. Nor, I think, was it necessary that he should give a particular name to every single species that presented itself before Adam, except to those that were either larger, or more useful, or distinguished by some other notable quality. To small animals, it seems plausible to me, only the names of genera or families were given, embracing several species not very different from one another. For example, if Adam gave a name to winged insects, is it not simpler to believe that the whole order, say the Lepidoptera, was called by a single word, which the first humans afterward used, much as the Latins use the word "butterfly"; and that a single word equivalent to the Latin "fly" encompassed not just one species, but as many as the Linnaean genus of flies contains, and similar dipterous insects, and perhaps others, such as bees? Likewise, larger beasts, wherever their species are very similar to one another -- as for example the tiger, panther, and leopard -- could originally have been designated by a single name. Observations of this sort ought to seem more appropriate to those who compress the works of this day into the space of twenty-four hours.

LXXVIII. What Happened between the Creation of the First Parents

As for the fourth question, I would be rash to presume to describe accurately what happened between the creation of our two first parents. I think, however, that without doing violence to the text, the matter can be conceived roughly as follows.

Adam, as soon as he was brought into the light, if he immediately used reason, as I suppose, I believe he would have thought about two things above all else -- first indeed with wonder and great astonishment, and afterward with a calmer gaze of the mind -- namely, himself, and God, from whom together with creation he must be believed to have received the knowledge of creation and of the Creator. He recognized himself and said: "I"; and thus, in the likeness of God, he generated his own word, that is, the idea of himself: "I, who lately did not exist, have been created by God." Then, struck by this great thought, he must have poured forth the sweetest sentiments of admiration, gratitude, and love, and given praise to the Lord and extolled the Most High -- though probably not with Psalm XCI: "It is good to give thanks to the Lord," which certain Rabbis fancifully claim that the newly created Adam sang. Anyone will readily suppose that our first parent lingered for some time in this sweetness of contemplation and affections, not to mention the supernatural knowledge and awareness of supernatural gifts that the Creator had abundantly poured into him, "creating his nature and bestowing grace at the same time." "God placed the man whom He had formed in the paradise of pleasure, to work it and to keep it," that is, the garden, which Scripture presents to us as planted by the divine hand and overflowing with delights. God had produced in paradise "every tree beautiful to behold and (or) pleasant to eat." To Adam therefore "He commanded, saying: From every tree of paradise you may eat," etc. (chapter II, 8, 9, 15, 16). As for dominion over animals, in the formula: "Have dominion over the fish and birds," chapter I, 28, this seems to have been granted to man after the creation of Eve; for to both it is said, "have dominion" ("He blessed them and said: Increase... have dominion"). Yet we can believe that this was also said to Adam before Eve's formation, whether by an internal or an external and audible voice. But I do not think Adam was occupied solely with the animals, giving them names; although God, in order to move him to desire and ask for a companion and wife, arranged that very many animals of both sexes should pass before his eyes. And he meanwhile, free as he was from cares, carefully observed the appearance, forms, voices, and habits of the animals: he saw that none was alone; he saw that all their species were complete, namely that in each one there was found everything necessary for preserving and multiplying the species itself. But among so many animals clothed in feathers or fur, he found none like himself or a suitable companion: hence the desire for fitting companionship, and for the means by which, according to the divine command, the human race might increase and multiply, must have arisen.

He would multiply, fill the earth, and subdue it. While he observed the animals and heard the various sounds they made, perhaps he first used the faculty of speech given to him by God, and imposed names on one animal after another. Yet other things also occupied man at that time, and first of all, that he should often raise his mind to the Creator, whom he "saw through created effects," who "delighted him in His handiwork," and so that "he might exult in the works of His hands." We may also imagine that he often raised his eyes to the luminary of the day, to the most beautiful of the works of God's hands, and to the azure firmament itself, and often also bent them down to the herbs and flowers with which the earth was clothed. Nor did he believe the trees were prepared for him in vain -- those beautiful to behold, or those which bore fruits pleasant to eat -- nor was he forgetful of those words: "Of every tree of paradise you may eat." The very beasts, constantly intent on their pasture, would have shaken off this forgetfulness in him. Nor did he to whom it had been entrusted to work and keep that paradise, and who while he was alone could more usefully exercise the faculty of walking than of speaking, remain motionless like a tree in one spot forever; rather, as anyone easily understands, he either walked through that garden of innocent delight, or at least surveyed some parts of it, and did not always keep his hand from flowers and fruits. And who would begrudge him the sweetness of some gentle rest upon the grass and of sleep? If night overtook him while he was absorbed in these various and delightful occupations (and the more delightful because they were more varied), how much do we think the setting of the sun, the twilight glow, the moon, the planets, and that innumerable host of stars struck his mind and eyes? Could brute animals entirely claim for themselves a man made in the image of God, if he possessed such great knowledge as theologians teach was in him? If "he knew God more clearly through the intelligible effects of God than we now know Him," as St. Thomas teaches? If, as Pope Gregory said, in "paradise man enjoyed the word of God, and shared in the company of the blessed Angels' Spirits, through purity of heart and loftiness of vision"? If "the soul of the first man had a more excellent mode of knowledge concerning the Angels than we have, because his knowledge was more certain and fixed concerning interior intelligible things than our knowledge is," as the same Aquinas says? If Adam, in some sense, had knowledge of all things, and "was so constituted by God that he would have knowledge of all things in which man is naturally apt to be instructed; and these are all those things which virtually exist in first principles known in themselves, namely all things that men can naturally know; and concerning supernatural things he received such knowledge as was necessary for the governance of human life according to that state"? If his knowledge of natural knowable things was so great that in it "he would not have progressed as to the number of things known, but as to the mode of knowing; because what he knew intellectually, he would afterwards have known through experience" (St. Thomas)? And even if his knowledge was not so great, should he who was deemed worthy of converse with God or Angels be believed to have dwelt in thought solely among mute animals? A fine science indeed is zoology, and worthy of man; but far nobler and more worthy of man is theology, and also anthropology, and the knowledge of Angels. He also knew agriculture to be more useful than zoology, nor was the knowledge of the stars, of atmospheric phenomena, and of plants altogether to be despised by him. It therefore seems more befitting the first man, and the dignity of that innocent and happy life, if we imagine him alternating the study of animals and their naming with other pursuits -- either more sacred, or more necessary, or no less agreeable -- than if we suppose him perpetually occupied with a single task of naming. This, I think, was more pleasing to him if it happened gradually, so that Adam would not assign each species or family its own name until he had observed the characteristics of each for some time.

LXXIX. The Deep Sleep of Adam; The Command in Paradise

After the first man had conceived, from this inspection of the animals, a desire for the thing he lacked, and had begun to wish from God a helper like himself -- probably also so that he might have someone with whom to exercise the faculty of speech, and who might respond to him -- then indeed the Lord cast a deep sleep upon Adam, section 73. This deep sleep I believe to have been that ecstatic state in which a person does not feel pains inflicted on the body and endures all things without being awakened from sleep; a state which is induced either supernaturally, or by nature itself, and sometimes also by the art of physicians, as has now been discovered.

To Adam, as soon as he was placed in paradise, God gave a commandment by which He forbade him to taste the fruit of a certain tree. That this command was given to Adam alone, before the woman had been formed, Scripture seems to indicate; for it says in chapter II, 16: "He commanded him: of every tree of paradise you may eat" -- which was said in the singular number, and before the birth of Eve is narrated. Augustine thought the woman had already been made when God enacted this law. Here are his words: "'Of every tree that is in paradise you shall eat for food'; He did not say: 'you shall eat' [plural]. Then He adds: 'But of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, you shall not eat of it.' Here now, as if to both, He ends the precept in the plural, saying: 'On whatever day you eat of it, you shall surely die.'" This variation in number occurs in the Greek manuscripts, while in the Hebrew and in Symmachus both forms are singular. God could most fittingly give the commandment to Adam alone, so that it would pass from the husband to the wife, as Chrysostom and Ambrose hold.

It is fitting here to note what I indicated (in section 75), which seems to me to have no small bearing on our subject. In chapter II of Genesis, there is again (from verse 4) a discourse on the first fashioning of things; but in this chapter, scarcely the works of the third or sixth day are mentioned; and the few things that are here added about each of those days immediately furnish you with arguments against the common opinion and those accepted twenty-four-hour days (section 54). Shall we not therefore be permitted to argue that the same would happen concerning the other days as well, if, as the sacred writer does with two of them, he were to discuss each of the others at somewhat greater length in some passage?

With the first chapter, the sixth day is concluded with these words: "And God saw all things that He had made, and they were very good. And there was evening and morning, the sixth day." Hear Augustine discussing this: "When He was treating of individual things, He said only: 'God saw that it was good'; but when it was said of all things together, it was not enough to say 'good' unless 'very' were added. For if individual works of God, when considered by the wise, are found to have praiseworthy measures and numbers and orders, each established in its own kind, how much more all things together, that is, the universe itself, which is completed by these individual things gathered into one? For all beauty that consists of parts is far more praiseworthy in the whole than in the parts."

From these words of God praising the perfected totality of the things He had made, some thinkers, among them Wolff, supposed that the system of optimism was confirmed -- namely, that this world is the best, and the most perfect among all possible worlds. But in vain: for no basis for comparison appears here; therefore it should be understood simply that the entire work of God is very good, and even optimal in a certain sense -- both because every divine operation is optimal and infinitely perfect ("the works of God are perfect," Deut. XXXII); and because it most excellently achieves the end which God intended, which we must believe and also in part know; and finally because it corresponds most perfectly to the eternal archetype existing in the mind of God -- about which we may not doubt. For it does not happen to God, as it does to man, that He sometimes fails to achieve in His work the idea preformed in His mind. Furthermore, in the question of optimism, the discussion is chiefly about moral evil. Now Moses speaks of the world as it was before man's sin, and does not speak of the works of men, but of all things that God had made. Among these, man himself was also good -- not in the sense that he was foreseen as going to sin, but because he had been made good and without sin, established in a state of justice and holiness, possessing an integral free will by which he could resist temptation and remain in innocence. However, "simply speaking, God can make something better than anything He has made," as St. Thomas teaches.

The works of the sixth day indeed seem excessive if this was a solar and ordinary day; for the first part of the day was occupied by the formation of terrestrial brute animals, which according to geological doctrines was accomplished over a long period of time. But even if such doctrines and the facts on which they rest were to be dismissed entirely, or if Buckland's view (section 5) were admitted, so that only a few hours or even one were allotted to that first work, nevertheless the total amount of time remaining in this solar day would certainly seem too narrow to contain the other events that took place in it -- as anyone must agree. This observation carries much more weight if the words of verse 19, chapter II -- "to all the animals of the earth and to all the birds" -- are to be taken strictly, or if we wish to join the aquatic animals with the terrestrial ones, or if we attribute to this same sixth day the disastrous transgression of our first parents, the just sentence of the supreme Judge, and the bitter expulsion from paradise. This last point, however, seems to me to have no likelihood; for God would not have "seen all things that He had made to be very good" -- and this when the sixth day was about to end (ch. I, final verse) -- if He had perceived that the chief work among the things He had made had already gone bad. Yet many assert this, among whom are Irenaeus, Epiphanius, and Ephrem, who have the first parent expelled from paradise on the very day on which he was created.

LXXX. The Seventh Day: God's Sabbath Rest Without End

So much for the work of the six days. Now very briefly about the seventh day, which is described thus in Genesis chapter II. Verse 1: "Therefore the heavens and the earth were completed, and all their adornment" (or "their host," as others render from the Hebrew); verse 2: "And God completed (had completed) on the seventh day His work which He had made" (the Septuagint and the Samaritan text have "on the sixth day," which may seem more precise, but the meaning is the same); "and He rested (ceased) on the seventh day from all His work which He had accomplished"; verse 3: "And He blessed the seventh day, and (that is) sanctified it, because in it He had ceased from all His work which God created to make." He sanctified the seventh day of the week, which is the sabbath, by decree and designation; that is, He designated the sabbath as sacred to Himself, so that in its time it would be observed as a feast day by the people of God: or He sanctified it, that is, He instituted a festival, and willed that it be celebrated by the first parents and their posterity with sacred leisure and worship of God. See section 10.

As for the seventh day, we read nothing further about it: neither its beginning nor its end is indicated. It certainly had a beginning, which we can place at that point of time when God, having formed Eve, ceased from the production of new things. But when was the end of this day? Was there ever one?

I seek this end, but I confess I do not find it. The terminus of the sabbath day, or of the period in which God ceases from new works, I for my part do not see. Once twenty-four hours after the formation of the woman had passed, that divine rest certainly did not come to an end, nor did that cessation cease. For the Lord did not undertake new works after a rest of one solar day, in the way that after the rest of the Lord's Day, when Monday morning comes, our craftsmen return to their workshops and their labor. Even now it seems to me that this day of industrious cessation continues, in which the heavenly Father "works even until now" (to say nothing of spiritual operations), preserving the created works and the created laws; and He simultaneously ceases, because He does not produce new classes of substances, at least not on our earth, nor does He bring about those great changes of things placed outside the present order, of the kind that occurred in those earlier periods, when the way was being prepared for creatures to be produced in some of the following periods, and especially in the sixth. Truly the great seventh day is without evening, and has no sunset (Augustine). This day endures still, as long as it pleases God, as long as the present order of things persists on earth, it will endure. And indeed I do not know (unless we go beyond the limits of natural things) what new period is to be expected.

The first day was the period of light, which can also be called the chemical period. This was followed by the period of atmosphere, firmament, and division of waters, at which time, if our conjectures are correct, solid inorganic substances began to exist; therefore this period can be called the period of solidity, or crystallization. The third period was one of vegetation. The fourth, in which the purification of the air was completed, can be called the sidereal period or period of the stars, since the stars then arose, at least as regards the earth. The fifth followed as the period of animal life; and finally the sixth arrived, which is the period of intelligence. Thus the work of the Creator and supreme Artificer was completed: the sabbath succeeded it, and the period of conservation endures. If the seventh day is of such great length, will the time of the six days that preceded this seventh be as brief as is commonly believed?

This alone remains, which must be earnestly sought from God: that the kingdom of God the Father and of our Lord Jesus Christ may come upon the earth, that all nations may serve Him, and that the works of His creature who was made in the image of God may truly reflect His likeness; so that God, who saw all things that He Himself had made to be very good, may no longer see -- as He has seen from the sin of Adam to our own times -- innumerable very great evils among the things perpetrated by Adam's posterity. Amen! Come, Lord JESUS.

COROLLARY

LXXXI. Ten Conclusions Moses Taught

From what we have discussed so far, it follows that Moses taught: I. That the corporeal world is not eternal, but was created by God in the beginning of time. II. That the works of creation were not all accomplished in a single moment, but gradually and successively. III. That the first state of the earth was as it were chaotic. IV. That the first phenomenon in the world, or at least on our earth, was a great light or fire. V. That in fashioning nature, God proposed this law for Himself: that the simpler and more imperfect things should be brought forth first, and afterwards the more perfect and more complex should emerge by degrees. VI. That the earth, which later appeared dry and began to be clothed with plants, had emerged from the waters. VII. That for some time there was no life on earth, either vegetable or animal. VIII. That terrestrial plants germinated before animals enlivened the waters, air, and lands by swimming, creeping, flying, and walking. IX. That terrestrial mammals were brought forth after other less perfect animals, and especially when the waters were already teeming with living things. X. That the creation of man at last put an end to the formation of the animal kingdom. These ten conclusions philosophy, made more astute by the inspection and observation of natural things, invincibly demonstrates in our own age.

I pass over other conclusions of the philosophers about which Moses is silent, although concerning that conclusion which is of great importance for our subject, it seems that we should not be entirely silent: namely, that a great interval of time, and one far longer than is commonly supposed, intervened between the first creation of corporeal things and the formation of man. For it has been shown above that Moses indicates this in chapter II neither very obscurely nor only once. I now ask: in that remotest antiquity of times, in that infancy of human learning, before the natural sciences arose which have received such great advances only in our own age -- I ask, I say, by what reasoning, by what means did those ten truths become known to Moses? Since he did not learn them through human sciences, it remains that he received them through divine revelation; and therefore the history of creation, which we read at the beginning of the divine books, sufficiently proves that some revelation was made by God to men in ancient times.

APPENDIX

ON THE CREATION OF ANGELS

LXXXII. That Angels Were Created, Not Eternal

It seems very fitting to me, and I hope it will seem so to the reader as well, if I add a few things in this place concerning the creation of Angels. That there exist certain intelligent substances, different both from men and from men's souls and from other sensible things -- whom Scripture and the Church call holy Angels, or wicked spirits -- and at the same time that such substances are not eternal: this the sacred Scriptures, the Fathers of the Church, and Doctors presuppose, teach, and insist upon. Let us hear St. Thomas: "It is said in Proverbs VIII, in the person of Wisdom (eternally) begotten: 'The Lord possessed me at the beginning of His ways, before He made anything from the beginning.' But the Angels were made by God; therefore the Angels once did not exist. God alone -- Father, Son, and Holy Spirit -- exists from eternity. For the Catholic faith holds this without doubt, and every contrary position is to be rejected as heretical. For God so produced creatures that He made them out of nothing, that is, after they had been nothing." The Apostle Paul, Colossians I, 16, teaches that "in Him (the Son of God) were created all things in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether Thrones, or Dominations, or Principalities, or Powers. All things were created through Him and in Him; and He is before all, and all things hold together in Him." In the canticle of the three youths, Angels are counted first among the creatures of God: "Bless the Lord, all you works of the Lord... Bless the Lord, you Angels of the Lord." But if the Angels were created, they are not eternal; for creation from eternity is a contradiction, as we have already demonstrated.

That the Angels were created by God is professed not only by the Christian faith, but also by the opinion of philosophers, especially the Platonists, whose master Plato reports that the lesser gods were created by the supreme one, and that the rest was then made and perfected by them.

It has been debated whether the Angels were created together with the corporeal world. St. Basil wrote: "For there was, as it appears, something even before this universe, which we can indeed contemplate with our mind; yet it has not been committed to any writings, since it seemed by no means suitable for those who were still rude and in the infancy of knowledge. There was indeed, before the production of the world, a state more ancient, fitting for those virtues superior to the world -- prior in time, everlasting, perpetual; and the creator and maker of all things produced creatures in it... namely rational and invisible natures, and the entire order of intelligible beings, whatever surpasses our mind, whose very names cannot even be investigated. For these fill up the substance of the invisible world, as Paul teaches us in these words: 'because in Him were created all things, whether visible or invisible, whether Thrones, or Dominations, or Principalities, or Powers, or Virtues, or hosts of Angels, or Archangels.'" This state -- prior in time and eternal, fitting for substances superior to the world -- is not easily understood, and seems to be interpreted as the aevum, or indeed eternity. By these words Vasquez thinks a certain imaginary interval is signified, prior to all time. Suarez believed that that eternal state "is to be understood not of eternity a parte ante, but a parte post." To Petavius it seems that the divinity itself, or rather the Word of God, is indicated -- by whom the heavenly spirits were created and in whom, as in a place and space, they were contained before this world and this time existed. What is certain is that Basil believed nothing to be truly everlasting except God, and he held with Paul, in the very passage he cites, that the Son of God "was before all Thrones, Dominations, Principalities." Therefore when he says that the Angels were made in the eternity of God and before time (namely before this time of ours, subject to changes, which we measure by the motion of bodies), he nevertheless does not teach that they were created from eternity.

Ambrose, in the Hexaemeron, where he usually follows Basil and sometimes translates him word for word: "The Angels," he says, "and Dominations and Powers, although they had a beginning, nevertheless already existed when this world was made."

Jerome, on chapter I of the Epistle to Titus: "Not yet are six thousand years of our world completed; and how many prior eternities, how many times, how many origins of ages must we reckon, in which Angels, Thrones, Dominations, and the other Virtues served God, and without the changes and measures of time subsisted at God's command?" These things are said on the occasion of Paul's words, verse 2: "In hope of eternal life, which God who does not lie promised before the ages of time." Jerome and Augustine read "before eternal times," and therefore Jerome uses the expression "eternities" in this passage. But these eternal times were not co-eternal with God, as Augustine says, who writes elsewhere that Paul calls "eternal" those times which have no time before them. The Apostle's words, here and in Romans XVI, 25, seem to signify simply all past times. Moreover, Jerome well knew that the Scriptures teach there is one eternal Creator, and he did not attribute eternity to the Angels; in the same passage he teaches that only "immortality was given to the Angels by the bounty of God."

LXXXIII. When Were the Angels Created?

When, then, were the Angels created? Together with the corporeal world? Or indeed before this visible world, as we have seen some Fathers of the Church teaching? Concerning this, a twofold opinion of the Saints is found, says St. Thomas; and this is a very ancient and celebrated question. The most common opinion of theologians, however, holds that the Angels were created simultaneously with the corporeal creature.

Concerning this controversy, I say first that neither side can be demonstrated by natural reason; since it does not depend on the nature of things -- I use the words of Vasquez -- but on the will of God alone, that the Angels be produced at this or that point of time. But we can investigate the will of God by no reasoning except from those things which we see to have been done. Since, therefore, it is not established from those things which we see to have been done whether the Angels were produced simultaneously in duration with the corporeal creature, or before it, we can understand nothing about the will of God concerning the point of their production from the things themselves.

Nor can we derive a sufficiently firm argument for settling this question from the tradition of the Fathers, if we prescind from the authority of the Church. A certain Greek writer in a manuscript codex cited by Petavius says: "Almost all the teachers of the Church throughout the world teach that the entire intelligible and angelic nature existed out of nothing before this world." But this consensus of the Doctors is false -- unless it is understood that the Angels existed before the world was perfected and adorned, that is, before the creation of animals. That the Angels were created before all bodies is taught, along with Basil, by Origen, who nevertheless acknowledges that it was not made sufficiently clear when the Angels were created; then by Gregory of Nazianzus, Zacharias of Mytilene, and others. The most common opinion, however, is that they were created together with the corporeal world, and this is the position most widely held among theologians.
