# Thai

Use formal scholarly/theological Thai (ภาษาเขียน / ภาษาทางการ). File suffix `_th`. Thai script, LTR (no `dir` attribute needed). Thai is classified as **hard-tier** for LLM translation due to the absence of word boundaries in Thai script, tonal complexity, and combining character placement.

## Biblical proper names

## Saint names

Use นักบุญ (naksabun) as the prefix for all saints. This is the standard Thai Catholic term. It does not change form. It precedes the given name directly: นักบุญออกัสติน, นักบุญเยโรม.

For Church Fathers with a city/epithet, Thai uses "แห่ง" (of) to connect the name and place: นักบุญออกัสตินแห่งฮิปโป (Augustine of Hippo), นักบุญฟรังซิสแห่งอัสซีซี (Francis of Assisi).

Full list:

- นักบุญออกัสติน (St. Augustine) — or นักบุญออกัสตินแห่งฮิปโป for disambiguation
- นักบุญเจอโรม (St. Jerome) — also spelled นักบุญเยโรม in some Thai Catholic sources; prefer เจอโรม per Thai Wikipedia standard
- นักบุญบาซิล (St. Basil) — นักบุญบาซิลแห่งซีซาเรีย (Basil of Caesarea), also called บาซิลมหาราช (Basil the Great)
- นักบุญแอมโบรส (St. Ambrose) — นักบุญแอมโบรสแห่งมิลาน (Ambrose of Milan)

- นักบุญเกรกอรี (St. Gregory) — specify: นักบุญเกรกอรีมหาราช (Gregory the Great), นักบุญเกรกอรีแห่งนาเซียนซ์ (Gregory of Nazianzus), นักบุญเกรกอรีแห่งนิสซา (Gregory of Nyssa)

## Quotation marks

Thai formal publishing uses standard double quotation marks "..." (อัญประกาศคู่). For nested quotations, use single quotation marks '...' (อัญประกาศเดี่ยว). A space should precede the opening mark and follow the closing mark when embedded in running text.

Do NOT use guillemets or any other quotation mark convention. Thai always uses "..." and '...' for primary and secondary quotations respectively.

## Psalm numbering

The Thai Catholic Bible (ฉบับสมบูรณ์ 2014) follows Hebrew/modern numbering as its primary system, consistent with post-Vatican II Catholic practice worldwide. Since Lapide's commentary uses Vulgate numbering, translations should convert to Hebrew/modern numbering as the primary reference: e.g., "สดุดี 90" (for Vulgate Psalm 89). Where the Vulgate number appears in the source, note it parenthetically for cross-reference: e.g., "สดุดี 90 (วัลเกต 89)".

## Thai script and writing conventions

### Word segmentation

Thai does not use spaces between words. Spaces in Thai mark clause or sentence boundaries, or separate items in lists. This is the single most important typographic feature for translation agents to understand:

- **Correct**: คอร์เนลิอุส อา ลาปีเด ได้เขียนอรรถาธิบายพระคัมภีร์
- **Wrong**: คอร์เนลิอุส อา ลาปีเด ได้ เขียน อรรถาธิบาย พระคัมภีร์ (spurious spaces between words)

Spaces should appear:
- Before/after parentheses and brackets
- Before opening quotation marks and after closing quotation marks
- Between Thai text and Latin-script text (foreign names, references)
- At natural clause/phrase boundaries in long sentences (Thai writing convention allows spaces at logical breaks)

### Tonal marks and vowels

Thai has 5 tones marked by 4 tone marks (ไม้เอก ่, ไม้โท ้, ไม้ตรี ๊, ไม้จัตวา ๋) plus a default tone determined by consonant class, vowel length, and final consonant.

Thai vowels can appear in four positions relative to their consonant:
- **Before** (left): เ-, แ-, โ-, ใ-, ไ-
- **After** (right): -ะ, -า, -ำ
- **Above**: -ิ, -ี, -ึ, -ื, -็
- **Below**: -ุ, -ู

Tone marks always appear above the consonant (or above the above-vowel if present). LLMs generally handle combining characters correctly in modern Thai text, but errors can occur with:
- Stacking order of above-vowels and tone marks
- Rare vowel combinations in transliterated foreign words
- Incorrect use of Thai characters that look similar (e.g., ฃ vs ข, ฅ vs ค)

### Transliteration of foreign names

Foreign names (Latin, Greek) should be transliterated into Thai script following established Thai Catholic conventions. For names not established in Thai Catholic tradition:
- Follow Royal Thai General System (RTGS) principles adapted for Thai phonology
- Use การันต์ (cancellation mark ์) to silence final consonants that Thai phonology does not support
- Use ทัณฑฆาต (the same ์ mark) on cluster-final consonants

Examples from Lapide's commentary: คอร์เนลิอุส อา ลาปีเด (Cornelius a Lapide), วัลเกต (Vulgate), เซปตัวจินต์ (Septuagint).

## Register

Use **ภาษาเขียน** (written/literary register) — the formal standard for academic and religious Thai. This is distinctly different from spoken Thai (ภาษาพูด).

Key register markers for formal Thai:
- Use ของ for possessive constructions rather than colloquial shortenings
- Use formal pronouns: ท่าน (he/she, respectful), พระองค์ (He, for God/royalty), เรา/ข้าพเจ้า (I/we, formal)
- Use Pali/Sanskrit-derived vocabulary where Thai Catholic convention has established it (e.g., ปิตาจารย์ not พ่อของคริสตจักร, ศีลศักดิ์สิทธิ์ not พิธี)
- Complex sentence structures using subordinate conjunctions: ซึ่ง (which), เนื่องจาก (because), แม้ว่า (although), อย่างไรก็ตาม (however)
- Ecclesiastical terms: พระเจ้า always referring to God; พระศาสนจักร (Church) for the institutional Church; พระสันตะปาปา (Pope)
- Avoid colloquial particles: ครับ/ค่ะ, นะ, เนอะ, จ้า — these belong to spoken register only
- Avoid English loanwords where Thai or Pali/Sanskrit equivalents exist in established Catholic usage
- Numbers: Use Thai numerals (๑, ๒, ๓...) for verse references in running text if the source does so, but Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3...) are also acceptable and widely used in modern Thai Catholic publications. Be consistent within a document.

## Agent pitfalls

### 1. Word boundary errors
The most critical Thai-specific error. Agents may insert spurious spaces within words, split compound words incorrectly, or fail to break at natural clause boundaries. Review all Thai output for unnatural spacing patterns. This is visually subtle and hard to catch programmatically.

### 2. Tone/vowel mark misplacement
Combining characters (vowels above/below, tone marks) must be placed on the correct consonant. Errors include placing a tone mark on the wrong consonant in a cluster, or separating a vowel from its consonant. Modern LLMs handle this reasonably well, but complex transliterations of foreign names can trigger errors.

### 3. Protestant vs. Catholic terminology
Thai has distinct Protestant and Catholic biblical vocabularies. Agents trained on general Thai text may default to Protestant terms:
- **Wrong**: ข่าวประเสริฐ (Protestant "Gospel") — **Correct**: พระวรสาร (Catholic)
- **Wrong**: กิจการของอัครทูต (Protestant "Acts") — **Correct**: กิจการอัครสาวก (Catholic)
- **Wrong**: พระคุณ (Protestant "grace") — **Correct**: พระหรรษทาน (Catholic)
Explicitly instruct agents to use Catholic terminology from the list above.

### 4. Mixing registers
Agents may mix colloquial spoken Thai with formal written Thai. Common slips include colloquial particles (ครับ, ค่ะ, นะ), informal pronouns (ผม, ฉัน instead of ข้าพเจ้า), and spoken contractions. All output must maintain consistent formal written register.

### 5. Incorrect transliteration of Latin/Greek names
Agents may produce inconsistent or phonetically incorrect Thai transliterations of the many Latin and Greek names in Lapide's commentary. Always cross-reference against the established Thai Catholic forms listed above. For names not on the list, maintain consistency throughout the document.

### 6. Leaving quotations untranslated
As with other languages, agents may leave Latin/Greek/Hebrew quotations untranslated. All quotations that the English source translates must be translated into Thai. Only preserve original-language text where the English source itself preserves it.

### 7. Missing or incorrect การันต์ (silencing mark)
Thai transliterations of foreign names require the การันต์ mark (์) to indicate silent final consonants. Agents may omit this mark or place it incorrectly, producing names that would be read with unintended pronunciation. Check all transliterated proper names.

### 8. Sentence-final particles in formal text
Thai spoken language uses sentence-final particles extensively (ครับ/ค่ะ for politeness, นะ for softening, etc.). These must NEVER appear in formal scholarly text. Agents sometimes add them to make text sound "more natural" in Thai — this is a register violation.

### 9. Thai numerals vs. Arabic numerals
Agents may inconsistently switch between Thai numerals (๑, ๒, ๓) and Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3) within the same document. Choose one system and maintain it consistently. For HTML chapter/verse references, Arabic numerals are standard. For running prose, either system is acceptable if consistent.

### 10. Classifier words
Thai uses classifier words (ลักษณนาม) when counting nouns. Agents may omit classifiers or use wrong ones. Common classifiers in this context: องค์ (for saints, monks, deities), เล่ม (for books), บท (for chapters), ข้อ (for verses), คน (for ordinary people). Using the wrong classifier sounds unnatural.
