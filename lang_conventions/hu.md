# Hungarian

Use formal scholarly/theological Hungarian (irodalmi nyelv / tudományos regiszter). File suffix `_hu`. Latin alphabet with Hungarian diacritics, LTR (no `dir` attribute needed).

## Biblical proper names

Follow Hungarian Catholic Bible tradition (Szent István Társulati Biblia / SZIT): Mózes (Moses), Ábrahám (Abraham), Izsák (Isaac), Jákob (Jacob), Ézsau (Esau), József (Joseph), Józsue (Joshua), Izajás (Isaiah), Jeremiás (Jeremiah), Ádám (Adam), Éva (Eve), Noé (Noah), Dávid (David), Salamon (Solomon), Illés (Elijah), Elizeus (Elisha), Áron (Aaron), Gedeon (Gideon), Sámson (Samson), Debóra (Deborah), Rut (Ruth), Sámuel (Samuel), Ezékiás (Hezekiah), Jónás (Jonah), Dániel (Daniel), Pál (Paul), Péter (Peter), János (John), Máté (Matthew), Márk (Mark), Lukács (Luke), Jakab (James).

Note: The SZIT (Catholic) uses different book names from the Protestant Károli tradition. The Catholic names derive from the Greek/Latin tradition (Teremtés, Kivonulás) while the Protestant tradition numbers them (1Móz, 2Móz). Always use the Catholic SZIT forms.

## Saint names

Use "Szent" (abbreviated "Sz." only in tight bibliographic references, never in running text). "Szent" is invariable — it does not decline or change form. It precedes the given name directly: Szent Ágoston, Szent Jeromos.

For Church Fathers known by a city or epithet, Hungarian uses the place-name or epithet as an adjective before "Szent": Hippói Szent Ágoston, Aranyszájú Szent János, Nazianzi Szent Gergely.

## Quotation marks

Hungarian uses the „lower-upper" double quotation marks: „..." (opening „ sits on the baseline, closing " sits at the top). For nested quotations, use inward-pointing guillemets without spaces: »...«. This is the standard convention in formal Hungarian publishing per the Magyar Tudományos Akadémia (Hungarian Academy of Sciences).

Example: „Amint az Apostol mondja: »Az igaz hitből fog élni.«"

Do NOT use English-style "..." or French-style «...» as primary quotation marks. Hungarian guillemets point inward (»...«), the opposite direction of French guillemets («...»).

## Psalm numbering

The SZIT (Szent István Társulati Biblia) has adopted Hebrew/modern numbering as its primary system, but also provides the Vulgate/Septuagint number in parentheses. Since Lapide's commentary uses Vulgate numbering, translations should convert to Hebrew/modern numbering as the primary reference and note the Vulgate number in parentheses where helpful: e.g., "Zsolt 90 (Vulg. 89)".

## Diacritics

Hungarian uses 14 vowel letters — 7 short and 7 long:

| Short | Long | Sound |
|-------|------|-------|
| a     | á    | /ɒ/ → /aː/ |
| e     | é    | /ɛ/ → /eː/ |
| i     | í    | /i/ → /iː/ |
| o     | ó    | /o/ → /oː/ |
| ö     | ő    | /ø/ → /øː/ |
| u     | ú    | /u/ → /uː/ |
| ü     | ű    | /y/ → /yː/ |

The **double acute accent** (hungarumlaut) on **ő** and **ű** is the most distinctive feature of Hungarian orthography. These are NOT interchangeable with ö/ü (which are short vowels) or ô/û (circumflex, not used in Hungarian).

### Critical diacritic distinctions
- **ö** (short rounded front) vs **ő** (long rounded front) — different words: örök (eternal) vs őrök (guards)
- **ü** (short rounded front) vs **ű** (long rounded front) — different words: tüz (pin/pierce) vs tűz (fire)
- **a** (short, rounded back /ɒ/) vs **á** (long, unrounded /aː/) — completely different vowel quality
- **e** (short /ɛ/) vs **é** (long /eː/) — different quality, not just length

### Common agent errors with diacritics
- Substituting ö for ő or ü for ű (dropping the double acute) — this is the single most common error
- Using circumflex (ô, û) instead of double acute (ő, ű)
- Omitting accents on á, é, í, ó, ú entirely
- Confusing a/á and e/é, which changes word meaning and grammatical function

## Grammar pitfalls for AI agents

### 1. Definite vs. indefinite conjugation
Hungarian verbs have two complete conjugation paradigms depending on whether the direct object is definite or indefinite. This is the hardest feature for non-native systems.

- **Indefinite** (alanyi ragozás): used with indefinite objects, no object, or 1st/2nd person object
  - „Olvasok egy könyvet" (I read a book — indefinite article)
- **Definite** (tárgyas ragozás): used with definite objects (definite article, demonstrative, proper name, possessive suffix, that-clause)
  - „Olvasom a könyvet" (I read the book — definite article)

Key triggers for definite conjugation: a/az (the), ezt/azt (this/that), proper nouns as objects, possessive-suffixed objects, 3rd person pronouns as objects, hogy-clauses.

**Agent error**: Using indefinite conjugation with definite objects (e.g., *olvasok a könyvet instead of olvasom a könyvet).

### 2. Vowel harmony in suffixes
Suffixes must harmonize with the vowels of the stem. Most suffixes have 2 or 3 forms:

- **Back-vowel words** (containing a, á, o, ó, u, ú): take back-vowel suffixes (-ban, -nak, -hoz)
- **Front unrounded words** (containing e, é, i, í): take front unrounded suffixes (-ben, -nek, -hez)
- **Front rounded words** (containing ö, ő, ü, ű): take front rounded suffixes (-ben, -nek, -höz)

For mixed-vowel words, the last vowel typically determines harmony.

**Agent error**: Applying the wrong vowel-harmony variant, especially with 3-way suffixes (-hoz/-hez/-höz, -szor/-szer/-ször).

### 3. Postpositions vs. case suffixes
Hungarian has no prepositions. Spatial and other relations are expressed through:
- **Case suffixes** (agglutinated to the noun): -ban/-ben (in), -ra/-re (onto), -ból/-ből (out of), -nak/-nek (dative)
- **Postpositions** (separate words after the noun): mellett (beside), szerint (according to), alatt (under), között (between), előtt (before)

**Agent error**: Using preposition-like word order (e.g., *a-ban ház instead of a házban) or confusing which relations use suffixes vs. postpositions.

### 4. Word order (topic-focus structure)
Hungarian has relatively free word order but it is NOT random — it follows a topic-focus structure. The position immediately before the finite verb is the **focus** position (for emphasis/new information). The default neutral order is SOV-like in subordinate clauses.

- Neutral: „Péter olvassa a könyvet." (Peter reads the book.)
- Focused subject: „PÉTER olvassa a könyvet." (It is PETER who reads the book.)
- Focused object: „A KÖNYVET olvassa Péter." (It is THE BOOK that Peter reads.)

**Agent error**: Using rigid SVO order borrowed from English, producing grammatically acceptable but unnaturally stiff sentences.

### 5. Possessive constructions
Hungarian marks possession on the possessed noun, not the possessor:
- „Péter könyve" (Peter's book — literally "Peter book-his")
- „az egyházatyák tanítása" (the teaching of the Church Fathers)

The possessor takes dative (-nak/-nek) optionally for clarity, and the possessed noun takes possessive suffixes (-a/-e/-ja/-je for 3rd person singular).

**Agent error**: Omitting possessive suffixes or using incorrect vowel-harmony variants (-a vs -e, -ja vs -je).

### 6. Definite article before possessives
When a possessive construction has a definite possessor, the possessed noun also gets the definite article: „a Szentírás szavai" (the words of Scripture), NOT *Szentírás szavai.

### 7. Verbal prefixes (igekötők)
Hungarian verbs frequently use separable prefixes (meg-, el-, ki-, be-, fel-, le-, etc.) that change meaning and aspect. In focus constructions, the prefix separates from the verb and moves after it:
- Neutral: „Megírta a levelet." (He wrote the letter.)
- Focused: „A LEVELET írta meg." (It was THE LETTER he wrote.)

**Agent error**: Leaving prefixes attached in focus constructions, or detaching them in neutral sentences.

## Register

Use **irodalmi nyelv** (literary language) — the formal written standard. Markers:
- Third-person narrative style throughout
- Complex subordinate clause structures with proper connectives (amely, amelyet, ahogyan — NOT ami, amit, ahogy, which are colloquial)
- Formal verbal forms: prefer -ván/-vén adverbial participles in scholarly prose
- No contractions or colloquial shortenings
- Latin-origin theological terms are acceptable and expected (exegézis, hermeneutika, teológia, patrisztika)
- Use the full form of demonstratives: „amely" (which, formal) not „ami" (which, colloquial); „azonban" (however, formal) not „de" alone
- Ecclesiastical register: „Isten" always capitalized; „Úr" (Lord) capitalized when referring to God; „Egyház" (Church) capitalized when referring to the Catholic Church as institution

## Agent pitfalls

- **English-style quotes**: Agents default to "..." instead of „...". Must be explicitly instructed to use Hungarian „lower-upper" marks with »...« for nested quotes. Even when agents use the correct opening „ (U+201E), they often use ASCII `"` (U+0022) for the closing mark instead of the correct `"` (U+201D). Explicitly instruct: closing quote is " (U+201D, right double quotation mark), NOT straight ASCII double quote.
- **Article before vowels**: Hungarian definite article "a" must become "az" before vowel-initial words. Agents sometimes miss this (e.g., *"a újabb" instead of "az újabb").
- **Dropping double acute accents**: The most common error. Agents write ö/ü where ő/ű is required. Explicitly instruct: „Use ő (o with double acute) and ű (u with double acute) — these are NOT the same as ö and ü."
- **Wrong conjugation paradigm**: Agents frequently use indefinite conjugation with definite objects. Review every verb+object pair.
- **Preposition word order**: Agents trained primarily on English/German may produce preposition-like constructions instead of using Hungarian postpositions or case suffixes.
- **Protestant Bible names**: Agents may use Protestant-tradition book names (1Móz, 2Móz) instead of Catholic SZIT names (Ter, Kiv). Explicitly specify Catholic convention.
- **Rigid SVO word order**: Agents produce syntactically correct but unnatural Hungarian by always placing the subject first. Review for natural topic-focus structure.
- **Colloquial relative pronouns**: Agents use „ami" / „amit" (colloquial) instead of „amely" / „amelyet" (formal/literary). The formal forms are mandatory in scholarly register.
- **Missing possessive suffixes**: Agents may translate "the grace of God" as *„Isten kegyelme" correctly but miss the suffix in more complex constructions, producing English-calque possessives.
- **Vowel harmony errors in suffixes**: Particularly with 3-way suffixes and mixed-vowel stems. Review all case suffixes.
- **Leaving quotations untranslated**: As with other languages, agents may leave Latin/Greek quotations untranslated. All quotations that the English source translates must be translated into Hungarian.
