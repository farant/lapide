Use formal standard Swahili (Kiswahili sanifu / lugha ya kitaaluma). File suffix `_sw`. Latin alphabet, LTR (no `dir` attribute needed).

## Quotation marks

Standard Swahili publishing uses double quotation marks "..." (alama ya nukuu). For nested quotations, use single quotation marks '...'. Swahili follows the same punctuation conventions as English in this regard.

Do NOT use guillemets. Swahili always uses "..." for primary quotations.

## Psalm numbering

The Swahili Bible (Biblia Takatifu / SUV) uses **Hebrew/modern numbering**, not Vulgate numbering. Since Lapide's commentary uses Vulgate numbering, translations must convert: e.g., Vulgate Psalm 89 becomes Zaburi 90. Where helpful, note the Vulgate number parenthetically: "Zaburi 90 (Vulg. 89)".

## Script and diacritics

Swahili uses the standard Latin alphabet with **no diacritics or special characters**. The alphabet includes all 26 English letters. There are no accent marks, tildes, or other diacritical marks in standard Swahili orthography. This eliminates an entire category of agent errors common in other languages.

Swahili has several digraphs that function as single phonemes: ch, dh, gh, kh, ng, ng', ny, sh, th. The apostrophe in ng' distinguishes the velar nasal + glottal stop from the ng digraph.

## Register

Use **Kiswahili sanifu** (standard Swahili) in formal scholarly/theological register. This is the variety used in academic publications, government documents, and religious texts in Tanzania and Kenya.

Key register markers for formal Swahili:

- **Avoid colloquial shortenings**: Use full verb forms, not contracted/slang variants. Write "hajui" not "hajuwi", "anajua" not "anajuwa".
- **Arabic/Islamic loanwords in Christian context**: Swahili absorbed many Arabic words due to historical contact. Some are used in Christian contexts too: Injili (Gospel, from Arabic), dhambi (sin), Zaburi (Psalms, from Arabic al-Zabur), toba (repentance), sala (prayer). These are standard in Swahili Christian usage and should not be avoided.
- **Bantu-derived vs. Arabic-derived alternatives**: Where both exist, prefer the form established in Swahili Catholic usage. Example: "Mungu" (God, Bantu origin) is standard, not "Allah" (which is used in Swahili Muslim contexts).
- **Formal sentence connectives**: Use "hata hivyo" (however), "kwa hiyo" (therefore), "kwa sababu" (because), "ingawa" (although), "zaidi ya hayo" (moreover) — not colloquial shortcuts.
- **Passive and relative constructions**: Formal Swahili uses complex relative clauses extensively. The relative marker can be infixed in the verb: "alilosema" (which he said), "walivyofanya" (how they did). Maintain these in scholarly prose.
- **Respectful language for God**: Always use "Mungu" or "Bwana" (Lord). When God speaks, use formal verb forms. "Yesu Kristo" in full, not abbreviated.
- **Noun class agreement must be maintained throughout**: This is the single most important grammatical feature. See pitfalls below.

## Grammar pitfalls for AI agents

### 1. Noun class agreement (ngeli)

Swahili has **15–18 noun classes** (depending on analysis), and every adjective, verb, pronoun, and demonstrative must agree with the noun class of its referent. This is the most pervasive source of errors in machine-generated Swahili.

Key classes relevant to theological translation:

| Class | Prefix (sg/pl) | Typical members | Subject prefix |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1/2 | m-/wa- | People: mtu/watu, mtakatifu/watakatifu | a-/wa- |
| 3/4 | m-/mi- | Trees, abstract: mti/miti, mwili/miili | u-/i- |
| 5/6 | (ji-)/ma- | Groups, augmentative: kanisa/makanisa, dhambi/madhambi | li-/ya- |
| 7/8 | ki-/vi- | Things, diminutive: kitabu/vitabu | ki-/vi- |
| 9/10 | N-/N- | Animals, loanwords: injili, sakramenti, neema | i-/zi- |
| 11 | u- | Abstract nouns: ubatizo, upendo, uzima | u- |
| 15 | ku- | Infinitives/gerunds: kusoma, kuomba | ku- |

**Agent error**: Using class 9/10 agreement for all loanwords and abstract nouns, when some belong to other classes. Example: "kanisa" (church) is class 5/6, not 9/10 — the correct plural is "makanisa", and adjectives/verbs must use class 5/6 concords.

### 2. Verb morphology

Swahili verbs are highly agglutinative. A single verb form encodes: subject prefix + tense marker + (relative marker) + (object infix) + verb root + (extension) + final vowel.

Example: "a-li-lo-ki-andik-i-a" = he-PAST-REL-it(cl.7)-write-APPL-FV = "which he wrote for it"

**Agent error**: Incorrect tense markers. Key tenses:
- -na- (present habitual/progressive)
- -li- (past)
- -ta- (future)
- -me- (perfect/completed)
- -ki- (conditional/simultaneous)
- -ka- (consecutive/narrative — used extensively in biblical narrative)

The **-ka-** tense (consecutive) is critical for biblical narrative style: "Mungu akasema... akaumba... akaona..." (And God said... created... saw...). Agents may incorrectly use -li- (simple past) where -ka- (narrative consecutive) is expected.

### 3. Object infixes

When the object is definite or animate, Swahili requires an object infix in the verb:
- "Ninampenda Mungu" (I love God) — NOT *"Ninapenda Mungu"
- "Aliwasaidia watu" (He helped the people) — NOT *"Alisaidia watu"

The object infix must match the noun class of the object. Agent errors include omitting the infix entirely or using the wrong class marker.

### 4. Possessive constructions

Possessive pronouns and the associative "of" construction (-a) must agree with the noun class of the possessed noun (not the possessor):
- "neno la Mungu" (word of God — class 5 "la")
- "maneno ya Mungu" (words of God — class 6 "ya")
- "kitabu cha Mwanzo" (book of Genesis — class 7 "cha")
- "vitabu vya Biblia" (books of the Bible — class 8 "vya")

**Agent error**: Using "ya" as a universal possessive connector regardless of noun class.

### 5. Adjective agreement

Adjectives take prefixes matching the noun class:
- "mtu mtakatifu" (holy person — class 1)
- "watu watakatifu" (holy people — class 2)
- "Roho Mtakatifu" (Holy Spirit — class 3)
- "Kanisa takatifu" (holy Church — class 5, adjective takes no prefix for monosyllabic roots in this class)

**Agent error**: Failing to adjust adjective prefixes for different noun classes, especially classes 5/6 and 9/10 where the prefix patterns are less intuitive.

### 6. Relative clauses

Swahili has three relative clause strategies: (1) -o- reference infix in the verb, (2) the invariable relative "ambaye/ambalo/ambacho" etc. (agreeing with noun class), and (3) the -o- of reference attached to a demonstrative. Formal written Swahili prefers strategy (1) for subject relatives and (2) for object/oblique relatives.

**Agent error**: Overusing "ambaye" for all relatives (including where the infixed form is more natural), or using "ambaye" (class 1, for persons) when the antecedent belongs to a different class (should be "ambalo" for class 5, "ambacho" for class 7, etc.).

### 7. Swahili-Arabic vs. English loanwords

For theological terms, prefer established Swahili forms (many of Arabic origin) over English borrowings:
- Use "Injili" not "Gospeli"
- Use "dhambi" not "sinni"
- Use "Zaburi" not "Psalms"
- Use "toba" not "repentance"

However, some terms are naturally borrowed from Latin/English in Catholic usage: "Ekaristi" (Eucharist), "Sakramenti" (Sacrament), "Katekisimu" (Catechism). Use these established forms.

### 8. Leaving quotations untranslated

As with other languages, agents may leave Latin/Greek/Hebrew quotations untranslated. All quotations that the English source translates must be translated into Swahili. Only preserve original-language text where the English source itself preserves it.

### 9. Protestant vs. Catholic terminology

Swahili has some terminological differences between Catholic and Protestant usage. Agents trained on general Swahili text may default to Protestant terms. Always use Catholic forms:
- **Padri** (Catholic priest), not "Mchungaji" (Protestant pastor)
- **Ekaristi** or **Komunyo** (Eucharist/Communion), not "Meza ya Bwana" (Lord's Table — Protestant)
- **Kitubio** (Catholic Confession/Penance), not "kuungama" alone
- **Bikira Maria** (Virgin Mary — Catholic), not "Mariamu" alone when referring to the Blessed Virgin in theological contexts

### 10. Consecutive -ka- tense in biblical narrative

Biblical Swahili extensively uses the -ka- consecutive tense to chain narrative events: "Mungu akasema, 'Na iwe nuru,' nuru ikawa" (And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light). Agents may break this narrative chain by switching to -li- (simple past) mid-paragraph, disrupting the biblical register. Maintain consistent -ka- chains in narrative passages.

### 11. Honorific and respectful forms

When referring to God, Jesus, saints, and Church authorities, maintain respectful Swahili forms:
- "Bwana wetu Yesu Kristo" (Our Lord Jesus Christ)
- "Baba Mtakatifu" (Holy Father — the Pope)
- "Mama yetu Bikira Maria" (Our Mother the Virgin Mary)

Agents may produce overly casual references. Maintain formal religious register throughout.
