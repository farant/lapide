# Kinyarwanda (Ikinyarwanda)

Use formal standard Kinyarwanda (Ikinyarwanda cy'amategeko / ururimi rwemewe). File suffix `_rw`. Latin alphabet, LTR (no `dir` attribute needed).

Reference Bible: **Bibiliya Ntagatifu (KBNT)** — the Catholic Kinyarwanda Bible published by Verbum Bible / Bible Society of Rwanda (2012). Available on YouVersion (version 430). This includes deuterocanonical books (Tobi, Yudita, Ubuhanga, Mwene Siraki, Baruki, 1–2 Abamakabe).

## Quotation marks

Kinyarwanda publishing follows French-influenced conventions and uses **guillemets** for primary quotations. For nested quotations, use double quotation marks "...". This reflects the historical French colonial and missionary influence on Rwandan publishing.

- Primary: « ... »
- Nested: "..."

Agents may default to English-style quotation marks — always convert to guillemets for primary quotes.

## Psalm numbering

The Kinyarwanda Catholic Bible (KBNT) uses **Hebrew/modern numbering**, not Vulgate numbering. Zaburi 23 is the Shepherd Psalm ("Uhoraho ni we mushumba wanjye"). Since Lapide's commentary uses Vulgate numbering, translations must convert: e.g., Vulgate Psalm 89 becomes Zaburi 90. Where helpful, note the Vulgate number parenthetically: "Zaburi 90 (Vulg. 89)".

## Script and diacritics

Kinyarwanda uses the standard Latin alphabet with **no diacritics or special characters in standard orthography**. The alphabet is: a, b, c, d, e, f, g, h, i, j, k, l, m, n, o, p, q, r, s, t, u, v, w, x, y, z.

Kinyarwanda is a **tonal language** (two-way contrast: high vs. low/toneless), but **tone is never marked in standard written Kinyarwanda**. Linguists may use acute accents for tone in academic contexts, but published Kinyarwanda text (including the Bible, newspapers, and government documents) does not mark tone. Do not add any diacritical marks.

The absence of diacritics simplifies one category of agent errors, but tone-related homography means that context is critical — agents must use the correct word form even when two words are spelled identically but differ in tone.

## Register

Use formal standard Kinyarwanda (Ikinyarwanda cy'amategeko) in scholarly/theological register. This is the variety used in government, academic publications, and religious texts in Rwanda.

Key register markers for formal Kinyarwanda:

- **Full verb forms**: Use complete conjugated forms, avoiding colloquial contractions or shortenings.
- **French-origin loanwords in Catholic context**: Rwandan Catholic usage has many French-origin terms: Kiliziya (eglise/Church), Umupadiri (pere/priest), Diyosezi (diocese), Paruwasi (paroisse), Liturujiya (liturgie), Ukaristiya (eucharistie), Isakramentu (sacrement). These are standard in Catholic Kinyarwanda and should be used, not replaced with calques.
- **Native Kinyarwanda vs. loanwords**: Where both a native term and a loanword exist, prefer the established Catholic usage. For example: "Iyobokamana" (native for theology) is formal, but "Teolojiya" is also accepted. "Igitambiro" (native for altar) coexists with "Altari."
- **Respectful forms for God**: Use "Imana" (God), "Uhoraho" (the LORD), "Nyagasani" (Lord). "Uhoraho Imana yacu" (the LORD our God) is the standard reverential form. "Nyagasani wacu Yezu Kristu" (Our Lord Jesus Christ).
- **Formal connectives**: Use "ariko" (however/but), "kuko" (because), "nuko rero" (therefore), "nubwo" (although), "ikindi kandi" (moreover).
- **Religious register**: The Rwandan Catholic Church's liturgical language has been standardized since the promulgation of the Roman Missal in Kinyarwanda (1992, "Igitabo cya Misa ya Kiliziya ya Roma"). Follow this established register.

## Grammar pitfalls for AI agents

### 1. Noun class agreement (amagambo)

Kinyarwanda has **16 noun classes** (grouped into ~8 singular/plural pairs plus locative classes). Every adjective, verb, pronoun, and demonstrative must agree with the noun class of its referent. This is the most pervasive source of errors in machine-generated Kinyarwanda.

Key classes relevant to theological translation:

| Class | Prefix (sg/pl) | Typical members | Subject prefix | Example |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|----------------|---------|
| 1/2 | umu-/aba- | People | a-/ba- | umuntu/abantu (person/people), umupadiri/abapadiri |
| 3/4 | umu-/imi- | Trees, abstracts, body parts | u-/i- | umutima/imitima (heart/s), umusaraba/imisaraba |
| 5/6 | i(ri)-/ama- | Groups, paired things | ri-/a- | ijambo/amagambo (word/s), isezerano/amasezerano |
| 7/8 | iki-/ibi- | Things, diminutives | ki-/bi- | igitabo/ibitabo (book/s), icyaha/ibyaha (sin/s) |
| 9/10 | iN-/iN- | Animals, loanwords | i-/zi- | inka (cow), intumwa (apostle) |
| 11/10 | uru-/iN- | Long/thin things, abstracts | ru-/zi- | urukundo (love), ururimi (tongue/language) |
| 12/13 | aka-/utu- | Small things | ka-/tu- | agakiza (salvation), agatabo (booklet) |
| 14 | ubu- | Abstract nouns | bu- | ubugingo (life), ubuhanga (wisdom), ubusaserdoti |
| 15 | uku-/ugu- | Infinitives | ku-/gu- | kwizera (faith/believing), gusoma (reading) |
| 16 | aha- | Locative (place) | ha- | ahandi (elsewhere) |

**Agent error**: Using wrong class agreement. For example, "icyaha" (sin) is class 7 (iki-/ibi-), so its plural is "ibyaha" (not *amacyaha). "Agakiza" (salvation) is class 12 — adjectives and verbs must use ka- concords. Agents trained primarily on Swahili will use Swahili-style class markers, which often differ.

### 2. Verb morphology — agglutinative structure

Kinyarwanda verbs are highly agglutinative. A single verb form encodes: **subject prefix + tense marker + (object infix) + verb root + (extensions) + final vowel**.

Key tense markers:
- **-ra-** (present/habitual when sentence-final): "arakora" (he works/is working)
- **-a-** (present with object or complement): "akora umurimo" (he does work)
- **-za-** (future): "azakora" (he will work)
- **-a-...-ye** (near past/perfect): "yakoze" (he worked/has worked)
- **-ra-...-ye** (today past with sentence-final): "yarakoze" (he worked today)
- **-ka-** (narrative sequential — critical for biblical narrative): "Imana ikavuga... ikabona..." (And God spoke... and saw...)

**Agent error**: Using wrong tense markers. The **-ka-** (narrative sequential) tense is essential for biblical narrative style: "Imana ivuga iti... ikabona..." Agents may default to simple past forms instead of maintaining the narrative chain. Also, the -ra- marker is only used when the verb is sentence-final; agents may insert it everywhere.

### 3. Kinyarwanda vs. Swahili confusion

Kinyarwanda and Swahili are both Bantu languages but differ significantly. Agents trained on more Swahili data may produce Swahili-influenced forms. Key differences:

| Feature | Kinyarwanda | Swahili |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| God | Imana | Mungu |
| Lord | Nyagasani / Uhoraho | Bwana |
| Holy | -tagatifu | -takatifu |
| Church | Kiliziya | Kanisa |
| Saint | Mutagatifu | Mtakatifu |
| Person/people | umuntu/abantu | mtu/watu |
| Class 1 prefix | umu- | m(u)- |
| Class 2 prefix | aba- | wa- |
| Tonal | Yes (unmarked) | No |
| Noun class prefixes | Longer (umu-, aba-, iki-, ibi-) | Shorter (m-, wa-, ki-, vi-) |

**Agent error**: Producing Swahili forms like "Mungu" instead of "Imana", "Bwana" instead of "Nyagasani", "Kanisa" instead of "Kiliziya", "-takatifu" instead of "-tagatifu". These are completely wrong in Kinyarwanda and must be caught.

### 4. Possessive constructions

The associative marker (-a) must agree with the noun class of the possessed noun:
- Class 1: **wa** — "umwana w'Imana" (child of God)
- Class 2: **ba** — "abana b'Imana" (children of God)
- Class 3: **wa** — "umutima w'umuntu" (heart of a person)
- Class 5: **rya** — "ijambo ry'Imana" (word of God)
- Class 7: **cya** — "igitabo cy'Intangiriro" (book of Genesis)
- Class 8: **bya** — "ibitabo by'Abapadiri" (books of the Priests)
- Class 9: **ya** — "inzu y'Imana" (house of God)
- Class 11: **rwa** — "urukundo rw'Imana" (love of God)
- Class 14: **bwa** — "ubuhanga bw'Imana" (wisdom of God)

**Agent error**: Using "ya" as a universal possessive connector regardless of noun class (Swahili-influenced). Each class has its own form.

### 5. The augment (initial vowel)

Kinyarwanda nouns typically have an augment (initial vowel) before the class prefix: **u**-mu-ntu, **a**-ba-ntu, **i**-ki-ntu. This augment is dropped in certain grammatical contexts (after prepositions, in vocative, after certain conjunctions). Agents may incorrectly include or drop the augment.

### 6. Honorific forms

When referring to God, Jesus, saints, and Church authorities, maintain respectful Kinyarwanda forms:
- "Nyagasani wacu Yezu Kristu" (Our Lord Jesus Christ)
- "Imana Data wa twese" (God our Father — lit. "God Father of us all")
- "Bikira Mariya, Umubyeyi w'Imana" (Virgin Mary, Mother of God)
- "Papa wacu" (Our Holy Father — the Pope)

### 7. Leaving quotations untranslated

As with other languages, agents may leave Latin/Greek/Hebrew quotations untranslated. All quotations that the English source translates must be translated into Kinyarwanda. Only preserve original-language text where the English source itself preserves it.

### 8. Protestant vs. Catholic terminology

Kinyarwanda has terminological differences between Catholic and Protestant usage. Agents may default to Protestant terms if trained on general-purpose text. Always use Catholic forms:
- **Umupadiri** (Catholic priest), not "Umupasitori" (Protestant pastor)
- **Ukaristiya** or **Inzimano Ntagatifu** (Eucharist/Holy Communion), not Protestant alternatives
- **Kiliziya** (Church — Catholic standard), which is the standard in both traditions in Rwanda
- **Bikira Mariya** (Virgin Mary — Catholic reverential form), not just "Mariya" when referring to the Blessed Virgin in theological contexts
- **Isakramentu** (Sacrament — Catholic), maintains the sacramental theology framework

### 9. The narrative -ka- tense in biblical text

Biblical Kinyarwanda extensively uses the -ka- narrative sequential tense to chain events: "Imana ivuga iti: 'Habeho umucyo,' umucyo ukabaho" (And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light). Agents may break this chain by switching to simple past or present forms. Maintain consistent -ka- chains in narrative passages.

### 10. Vowel harmony and coalescence

When prefixes meet stems beginning with vowels, complex vowel coalescence rules apply. For example: umu- + -ana -> umwana (child), iki- + -aha -> icyaha (sin). Agents may produce uncoalesced forms (*ikiaha) or over-coalesce. Follow standard orthographic conventions.

### 11. Limited LLM training data

Kinyarwanda is significantly underrepresented in LLM training data compared to major world languages. Research (KinyaBERT, 2022; various AfricaNLP studies) confirms that:
- Sub-word tokenization (BPE) handles Kinyarwanda morphology poorly
- LLMs systematically underperform on Kinyarwanda across reasoning, generation, and classification tasks
- Cultural and historical domain translations are particularly error-prone
- Agents may produce Swahili-Kinyarwanda hybrids or overly simplified output

**Mitigation**: Review all agent output carefully. For large files, split translation into smaller sections. Provide explicit examples of correct Kinyarwanda in prompts. Cross-check proper names against the KBNT reference list.
