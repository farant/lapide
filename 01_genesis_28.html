Synopsis of the Chapter

Jacob fleeing from Esau goes into Mesopotamia, and sleeping on the way sees God leaning upon a ladder, by which angels were ascending and descending, comforting him, and from this he called the place Bethel; and finally in verse 20 he makes a vow to God.

Note: All these things happened in the 77th year of Jacob's age, as I showed at the beginning of chapter 27.

Vulgate Text: Genesis 28:1-22

1. Isaac therefore called Jacob, and blessed him, and commanded him saying: Do not take a wife from the race of Canaan; 2. but go and set out for Mesopotamia of Syria, to the house of Bethuel your mother's father, and take a wife from there from among the daughters of Laban your uncle. 3. And may God Almighty bless you, and make you grow, and multiply: that you may become a multitude of peoples. 4. And may He give you the blessings of Abraham, and to your seed after you: that you may possess the land of your sojourning, which He promised to your grandfather. 5. And when Isaac had sent him away, he set out and came into Mesopotamia of Syria to Laban, son of Bethuel the Syrian, brother of Rebecca his mother. 6. And Esau, seeing that his father had blessed Jacob and sent him into Mesopotamia of Syria to take a wife from there, and that after the blessing he had commanded him saying: You shall not take a wife from the daughters of Canaan; 7. and that Jacob, obeying his parents, had gone into Syria: 8. seeing also that his father did not look favorably upon the daughters of Canaan, 9. he went to Ishmael and married, in addition to those he already had, Mahalath, daughter of Ishmael son of Abraham, sister of Nebaioth. 10. Jacob therefore departed from Beersheba and set out for Haran. 11. And when he had come to a certain place and wished to rest there after sunset, he took some of the stones that lay there, and placing them under his head, he slept in that same place. 12. And he saw in a dream a ladder standing upon the earth, and the top of it touching heaven: and the angels of God ascending and descending by it, 13. and the Lord leaning upon the ladder saying to him: I am the Lord God of Abraham your father, and the God of Isaac. The land on which you sleep, I will give to you and to your seed. 14. And your seed shall be as the dust of the earth: you shall spread to the West and to the East, and to the North and to the South: and in you and in your seed all the tribes of the earth shall be blessed. 15. And I will be your guardian wherever you go, and I will bring you back into this land: nor will I leave you until I have accomplished all that I have said. 16. And when Jacob awoke from sleep, he said: Truly the Lord is in this place, and I did not know it. 17. And trembling, he said: How terrible is this place! This is none other than the house of God, and the gate of heaven. 18. Rising therefore in the morning, Jacob took the stone which he had placed under his head, and set it up as a monument, pouring oil upon it. 19. And he called the name of the city Bethel, which was formerly called Luz. 20. He also made a vow, saying: If God shall be with me, and shall keep me in the way by which I walk, and shall give me bread to eat and clothing to wear, 21. and I shall return prosperously to my father's house: the Lord shall be my God, 22. and this stone, which I have set up as a monument, shall be called the House of God: and of all things that You shall give me, I will offer tithes to You.

Verse 1: He Blessed Him

1. HE BLESSED HIM -- he confirmed the blessing given a little before. So St. Augustine.

Verse 2: Into Mesopotamia

2. INTO MESOPOTAMIA -- into Haran, or Carrhae, a city of Mesopotamia, which was about eight days' journey from Beersheba, where Isaac and Jacob were living.

THE HOUSE OF BETHUEL -- which had been Bethuel's, but since he had died, it now belonged to his son, namely Laban, Jacob's uncle.

Verse 3: May He Make You Grow and Multiply

3. MAY HE MAKE YOU GROW AND MULTIPLY, THAT YOU MAY BECOME A MULTITUDE OF PEOPLES -- may He make you grow with many offspring and a great family, so that many tribes and multitudes of peoples may be born from you. And indeed the twelve tribes descending from Jacob were in fact populous.

Verse 4: To Your Seed After You

4. TO YOUR SEED AFTER YOU. -- Hence St. Augustine, Book XVI of The City of God, chapter 38, and Rupert gather that the promises of God about possessing the land of Canaan, about numerous offspring and wealth, about Christ to be born from him, etc., made to Abraham and Isaac, were appropriated to Jacob and his descendants: and for this reason Esau is regarded as excluded and foreign from the seed and family of Abraham and Isaac, and as having become the father and founder of a foreign nation, namely the Edomites; whence consequently the condition and obligation included in the covenant and blessing of Abraham, namely the law of circumcision, did not bind Esau and the Edomites; although they voluntarily and freely accepted it, as is clear from Jeremiah 9:25 and 26.

Verse 5: He Came into Mesopotamia

5. HE CAME INTO MESOPOTAMIA -- by that journey which is narrated more fully in verse 10. This is therefore a prolepsis; for Moses wished to set before our eyes at once, as in a synopsis, and to contrast the deeds of both Esau and Jacob, and Jacob's flight from his brother and its destination, so that he might then resume and narrate it more fully in what follows. For Moses intends to recount at length the deeds of Jacob, as the father of the twelve Patriarchs and of all the Israelites; hence, so that he may devote himself entirely to these, he briefly mentions in passing and summarizes the deeds of Esau, which happened at the same time.

Morally, St. Ambrose, in Book II of On Jacob and the Happy Life, teaches that Jacob was happy even in flight and exile. "Was not Jacob happy," he says, "even when he was leaving his homeland? Indeed he was truly happy, who accepted the hardships of exile so as to soften his brother's anger. For if he is happy who avoids sin, surely it cannot be denied that he is happy who lightens another's guilt and averts a crime. And so he avoided a prepared fratricide by voluntary exile, and by doing so sought safety for himself and granted innocence to his brother. Rightly therefore divine grace accompanied him everywhere, so that even while he slept, he might acquire the reward of the happy life; for he was seeing the mysteries of future things, and hearing divine oracles."

For in his sleep he received magnificent consolations from God, visions, blessings, and promises, according to Song of Songs 5:2: "I sleep, but my heart watches." Hence Clement of Alexandria, Book II of the Pedagogue, chapter 9, thus compares those who sleep soberly to angels who are always watchful, since they receive the eternity of life from the meditation of their vigil. By which he signifies that the soul, as it were, dies if it ceases from meditation, but lives and is made eternal if it exercises itself in continuous meditation.

Verse 8: Of the Syrian

8. OF THE SYRIAN -- that is, the Mesopotamian. See what was said in chapter 25, verse 20.

SEEING ALSO -- that is, observing and perceiving. So the Hebrew. "Seeing" here, therefore, does not mean the same as investigating, but as if to say: when by proof and experience he had learned and discovered. It seems Esau wished to take this third wife who would please his parents, or at least displease them less than the two former ones, who were Canaanites. But he did not wish to take a wife from the house of Nahor, although he knew this would be more pleasing to his parents: and this out of a certain pride of spirit, because Jacob had gone there, and he did not wish to appear to be following and imitating his brother, especially since he himself was older.

Verse 9: He Went to Ishmael

9. HE WENT TO ISHMAEL -- that is, to the Ishmaelites: for Ishmael had already been dead for fourteen years; for these events, as I said at the beginning of chapter 27, happened in Jacob's 77th year; but Ishmael died at age 137, which was Isaac's year 123 and Jacob's 63. So Tostatus.

Verse 11: He Took Some of the Stones

11. HE TOOK SOME OF THE STONES. -- From these words, Rabbi Nehemiah, in Midrash Tehillim, on Psalm 90: "He has given His angels charge over you," and the book Rabbah at this passage, think that Jacob took three stones, and that they were turned into one, of which it is said in verse 18: "He took the stone which he had placed under his head, and set it up as a monument"; so that by this the mystery of the Holy Trinity might be signified, in which three Persons unite in one essence, and therefore Jacob exclaimed in verse 17: "How wonderful is this place!" But this seems to be a Rabbinic fiction and fable; for St. Jerome says nothing of the sort, nor does any ancient or modern interpreter. What is said therefore: "He took some of the stones," understand: one larger and more suitable stone, as he explains in verse 18, saying: "He took the stone which he had placed under his head."

Verse 11: Placing It Under His Head (Moral)

PLACING IT UNDER HIS HEAD. -- Morally: Note here that Jacob makes himself a hard bed and pillow, namely a stone; because a hard bed, hard food, and all hard things befit the courtiers of heaven. "See," says St. Chrysostom, "the boy's strength, who uses stones for a pillow; see his manly spirit: he sleeps on the pavement." But by this stone (which was a type of Christ) he is refreshed and strengthened. Hence St. Jerome on Psalm 133, calls this stone of Jacob the stone of help: "He who had such a pillow," he says, "in which he cooled the heat of persecution, sees a ladder by which, if need be, he would be received into heaven." Christ therefore is the pillow of those who labor, in which they sweetly rest and receive the refreshments of heaven. On the other hand, golden and soft beds are like tombs of sleep, softness, torpor, and sloth, while hard and stony ones are gymnasiums and training grounds of strength and virtue. Wherefore St. Ambrose, Book II of On Jacob: "Jacob," he says, "was a good worker even in his sleep, since he transacted more business with God while sleeping than while awake."

Verse 12: And He Saw in a Dream

12. AND HE SAW IN A DREAM. -- This was therefore an imaginary and symbolic vision.

A LADDER STANDING. -- The Septuagint reads "A ladder firmly set." Alcazar, commenting on Apocalypse chapter 4, verse 1, thinks that God stood at this ladder not in heaven but on earth, at the lowest steps of the ladder, to hold and support it; because He spoke with Jacob, who was sleeping on the earth at the ladder, and was therefore near him. But more fittingly, Josephus and Cajetan think that God stood in heaven and was leaning upon the topmost steps of the ladder. For the angels were ascending to God by this ladder, to carry His commands to earth; nor is it surprising that God spoke from heaven to Jacob on earth, because this speech, like the vision, was not sensible but imaginary or intellectual, which can take place between those who are very far apart, and often does, not only from God but also from angels.

What Does Jacob's Ladder Signify Literally?

You ask first: what does this ladder of Jacob signify literally?

Philo and Origen answer, as cited by St. Jerome, epistle 161, that this ladder represents metempsychosis, or the migration of the human soul from one body to another. For they hold that the soul of every human being existed before the body, and as many times as it sinned then and descended from heaven to earth, so many times it now changes bodies, migrating from one to another, until through them, as through certain degrees of penance (like steps of a ladder), it ascends back to heaven.

Furthermore, in this descent of the soul, Origen assigns these steps. First, he says, the soul is lowered into more subtle bodies; then, if it continues to sin, into grosser ones; and finally it is thrust down into earthly bodies. Moreover, Philo, in his book On the Planting of Noah, writes that through this ladder the descent of souls into bodies when they are born is signified, or actually takes place.

For, explaining these words, Origen says that Jacob saw a ladder and writes: "The air, like a populous city, has immortal citizens, namely souls, which are equal in number to the stars; some of these descend to be bound to mortal bodies," etc. Hence Origen also imagined that the souls of human beings were angels who, having been cast down for sins committed in heaven, descended through this ladder step by step into different and ever more vile bodies.

Hear St. Jerome writing to Pammachius against the errors of John of Jerusalem: "Origen teaches that through the ladder of Jacob, rational creatures descend step by step to the lowest level, that is, to flesh and blood, and that it is impossible for anyone to be precipitated suddenly from the number one hundred to the number one, without passing through each number, as through the steps of a ladder, all the way to the last, and that they change as many bodies as they have changed dwelling-places from heaven to earth." He gives an example: "Imagine someone of tribunitian rank degraded through his own fault, through each rank of the cavalry down to the title of recruit -- does a tribune immediately become a recruit? No, but first he becomes a chief officer, then a senator, then a centurion, then a squad-leader, then a patrol officer, then a horseman, then a recruit." But these are the delusions of Pythagoras and Origen.

The Ladder as Symbol of Divine Providence

I say therefore with Theodoret, Aben Ezra, the Hebrews, and Pererius, that this ladder is, first, a symbol of divine providence and governance; hence God leans upon it, as the first cause and prime mover of all things, who commands time to proceed from eternity, and remaining stable Himself gives all things their motion.

Second, the angels ascend and descend as ministers and executors of God's providence, to whom God individually assigns their duties to each.

Third, this ladder extends from heaven to earth, because God governs lower things through higher ones, and humans through angels.

Fourth, the two sides of the ladder are sweetness and strength; for God, governing the world by His wisdom, reaches from end to end mightily, and orders all things sweetly.

Fifth, the various steps of the ladder are the various modes of God's providence, and the various species and perfections of things flowing therefrom.

Thus Homer in Iliad Book 8 describes and represents divine providence by a golden chain, lowered by Jupiter from heaven to earth, by which Jupiter comprehends, binds, and draws all things back to himself.

God's Three Consolations for Jacob

Second, more properly and particularly, Diodorus of Tarsus teaches that the descending angels signify Jacob's happy departure into Mesopotamia, and the ascending ones his happy return to Palestine. For God wished by this vision to console and encourage Jacob, who, leaving his parents and hated by his brother, a fugitive, exile, and solitary man, sad and anxious, was sleeping roughly here on a rock, as if to say: Do not be sad, do not fear, O Jacob. I know three things weigh upon and trouble you: homeland, parents, brother; against these I set three things to console you -- the ladder, God, and angels.

First, you have left your homeland, and as a stranger you head for a foreign land: but see the ladder, which opens heaven to you, which shows you the way prepared for you into heaven; second, you have left your parents and as a fugitive you go to strangers in Mesopotamia: but know that God directs this journey of yours, is with you, leads you, protects you, and will likewise bless and enrich you with His help; third, you are hated by your brother and travel alone: but know that angels are your companions and guides, who will lead you safely to Mesopotamia and bring you back unharmed to your parents in Canaan. That this is the literal sense is clear from what follows, which narrates that these things happened to Jacob in exactly this way.

Moral: God's Care for His Own

Morally: Note here that God cares for His own, especially those distinguished in virtue and heroes like Jacob, with such care through Himself and through angels, as if He were wholly devoted to them and cared for nothing else in the whole world, according to Song of Songs 2:16: "My Beloved is mine, and I am His." Where St. Bernard, Sermon 68 on the Song of Songs, says: "Is that majesty so intent on this one, upon whom the governance of the universe rests, and is the care of the ages transferred to the sole affairs, or rather leisure, of love? Indeed so. For all things are for the sake of the elect." We do not therefore deny God's providence to other creatures, but the bride alone claims God's special care for herself.

Such was the Blessed Virgin, who in this ladder is the highest step, upon whom God leans, as I shall presently say. Hence St. Thomas, II-II, Question 103, article 4, reply to objection 2, teaches that she is to be venerated above other saints with hyperdulia, because, he says, by her cooperation she more closely approached the bounds of divinity; for in the Incarnation of Christ she did everything to which the power of nature could extend, and when that failed, divinity succeeded, so as to complete the very substance of the work by itself alone.

What Does the Ladder Signify Allegorically?

You ask second: what does this ladder of Jacob signify allegorically?

Eustathius answers that this ladder signifies the cross of Christ. So also St. Augustine, Sermon 79: The Lord, he says, leaning on the ladder is Christ hanging on the cross; from there He took a wife, that is, He united the Church to Himself. Aptly; for the cross is a ladder and a way by which Christ and all Christians have ascended and daily ascend into heaven.

So we read in the martyrdom of Saints Perpetua and Felicity, March 7, whose courage St. Augustine celebrates in Psalm 47 and often elsewhere, that they received an omen and sign of their martyrdom from God through a ladder. For when they were held captive in prison, St. Perpetua saw in a vision a golden ladder extending from earth to heaven, on whose steps many swords were fastened, and they were very sharp, so that it seemed no one could climb it without serious injury. Below was a horrible dragon, wanting to prevent anyone from ascending. She then saw Saturus (who was one of her four companions, who were all crowned with martyrdom together with her in the year of the Lord 205) climbing the ladder with great courage, and urging the others to follow him boldly and not to fear the dragon, because it could not stop them. She then awoke and told this vision to her companions; who all gave thanks to God. For they understood that they were being called to martyrdom; for that ladder, sharpened with so many knives and swords, was the means by which God wished to lead them gloriously into heaven, and the infernal dragon could not impede their journey and ascent.

The Ladder of the Incarnation

But more fittingly and genuinely, Diodorus, Vatablus, and Rupert think that the Holy Spirit represented through this ladder the Incarnation of the Word, namely the generation of Christ, who was to be born from Jacob and descend through various steps, that is, generations and ancestors, of whom the last is Joseph with the Blessed Virgin, and the highest is Adam, who was directly and immediately created by God.

Second, the two sides of the ladder are mercy and truth, or God's faithfulness concerning the promised Messiah; for these two caused the Word to descend to us and to take on our flesh.

Third, this ladder touches the earth, because the Word was incarnate on earth and blessed it by His touch of incarnation; and it touches heaven, because Christ, who was incarnate, is the Son of God, that is, God-man: for Christ joined heavenly things to earthly, the lowest to the highest, and thus God to man in Himself. Hence He Himself says: "No one ascends into heaven, except He who descended from heaven"; He therefore is our ladder, through whom we ascend to God: for no one comes to the Father except through Christ.

Fourth, the angels descend to announce this mystery of the Incarnation to mankind; the same ascend to carry the ardent desires and prayers of the Patriarchs upward to God. Hence Blessed Peter Chrysologus, Sermon 3 On the Annunciation, calls the Incarnation the business of all ages, namely because in it every age labored, and through the angels the matter was vigorously pressed with God concerning this common remedy for the world, until within the Virgin's house the heavenly business was completed.

Fifth, the steps of this ladder are Christ's various virtues, and especially four, namely: 1. humility at His birth; 2. poverty in the manger; 3. charity in the course of His life; 4. obedience in His passion: this is the way to heaven, walk in it.

Finally, the Blessed Virgin is called the Ladder of Jacob in her Litanies; and so St. Bernard (or whoever the author is) calls her in his Sermon On the Blessed Mary, page 394: "She," he says, "is the ladder, the bush, the threshing floor, the star, the rod, the fleece, the bridal chamber, the gate, the garden, the dawn. For she is the Ladder of Jacob, which has twelve steps between two sides. The right side is contempt of self, even to the love of God; the left is contempt of the world even to the love of the Kingdom. The ascents of this ladder are the twelve degrees of humility. The first is hatred of sin; the second, flight from transgression; the third, fear of hatred; the fourth, in all these things being subject to the Creator; the fifth, obeying one's superior; the sixth, obeying one's equal; the seventh, serving one's inferior; the eighth, being subject to oneself; the ninth, constantly meditating on one's end; the tenth, always fearing one's own works; the eleventh, humbly confessing one's thoughts; the twelfth, in all things being moved by the hand, the nod, and the will of the Lord. By these steps the angels ascend, and they raise up human beings. Thus are ascents arranged in the heart, by gradual progress and step-by-step ascent. Thus in the Father's house they attain luminous mansions. These are the twelve Apostles, who follow in the wilderness the footsteps of Jesus Christ."

The Ladder of Perfection

Hence second, St. Basil on Psalm 1 says: The ladder is the ascent to perfection; its summit is charity; its steps are the ten degrees of renunciation, of which the first is to renounce earthly things, so as to say with the Apostles: "Behold, we have left all things"; the second, to forget those same things, Psalm 44: "Hear, O daughter, etc., and forget your people"; the third, to hate and abhor the same things as dung; the fourth, to put off the love of parents and relatives; the fifth, to hate one's own soul for Christ's sake, so that one is not at all concerned about one's own life, even if it should have the sentence of death, says St. Basil; the sixth, to deny one's own judgment and will; the seventh, always to mortify one's desires, so as to fulfill that saying of Christ: "Let him deny himself and take up his cross daily"; the eighth, to follow Christ and learn from Him, because He is meek and humble of heart; the ninth, to love constantly and effectively one's neighbors, even one's enemies; the tenth, in which the Lord was seen, is to cling to God and to be united with Him in one spirit. So says Pererius.

What Does the Ladder Signify Symbolically?

You ask third: what does this ladder signify symbolically?

Philo answers in his book On Dreams: The ladder, he says, is the soul; its base is sense and the appetite for earthly things; its summit is the most pure mind, which ascends to God through the steps of contemplation, just as, conversely, the aforementioned base descends to earth and earthly things through the steps of desires. Let man therefore take care not to descend by following the base, but rather to ascend by looking to his summit.

Second, the same Philo says: The ladder is the inconstancy of this life, in which some are cast down from the highest to the lowest, and others are raised up from the lowest to the highest, and this at the nod and will of God, who, leaning upon this ladder, rules and directs it. So Pittacus, as cited by Aelian, Book 2, made the ladder an image of fortune and vicissitude, of happiness and unhappiness; for the fortunate ascend on the wheel of fortune, and the unfortunate descend. But these are philosophical notions, and do not pertain to the mind of the Holy Spirit in this passage.

What Does the Ladder Signify Tropologically?

You ask fourth: what does this ladder of Jacob signify tropologically?

Tertullian answers at the end of Book III Against Marcion: This ladder is the way by which the just arrange ascents in their heart toward heaven. The Holy Spirit suggests the same in Wisdom 10:10, where speaking of this Jacob of ours and the vision of this heavenly ladder, He says thus: "This (wisdom) led the just man, fleeing from his brother's anger, through right ways, and showed him the kingdom of God." Hence Barlaam said to Josaphat: "Virtues are, as it were, certain ladders of heaven," as Damascene attests in his History, chapter 20. The two sides of this ladder are faith and works; or the Word of God and the sacraments; or "endure and abstain" -- which two words, if anyone observes them, he will live a most tranquil and most holy life without sin, as Epictetus used to say.

The steps are the ascents of various laws and virtues; again these steps belong to beginners, those progressing, and the perfect, to whom God at the summit unites Himself, and in whom He delights and dwells. The angels ascending to God, says St. Gregory, Book V of the Moralia, and following him St. Thomas, II-II, Question 181, last article, signify the contemplative life; while those descending to human affairs signify the active life.

Hence Alcazar aptly takes these angels as the Apostles and other preachers of the Gospel, who pour out upon mankind through preaching the wisdom which they drew from God in meditation. Therefore, in order to preach properly, a preacher should first ascend to God in heaven through meditation, to draw from Him what he is to say. Jacob therefore saw in these angels a foreshadowing of his offspring and descendants, namely the heralds of the Gospel, to be born from Christ his descendant, who would teach mankind the knowledge of the Saints, which therefore God is said to have given and revealed to Jacob, Wisdom 10:10.

The Ladder as the Rule of Religious Life

Here also belongs that explanation of Zeno, Bishop of Verona, who holds that this ladder signifies the two Testaments, which through certain steps of observance lead man from earth to God. The descending angels are men who fall from spiritual things to worldly things, and who, once nourished in saffron robes, now embrace filth; but the ascending are just men, who dispose ascents in their heart, seeking the things that are in heaven, and not the things that are on earth.

But why does no one stand still here? St. Bernard responds, epistle 253, because in this way, between progress and decline, there is no middle ground; just as on a wheel that turns, he who sits upon it cannot stand still, but must either ascend or descend. O monk, you think you have labored enough, you do not wish to advance: you must necessarily fall back; what you neglect here, you will not be able to recover for all eternity. Like an ant, therefore, gather merits in this life, so that by them you may live, and live gloriously, in the eternal life which awaits you; "whatever your hand is able to do, work at it earnestly"; how you will rejoice in eternity over this little time well spent!

Finally, St. Bernard, in his sermon on the text, Behold, we have left all things: The ladder, he says, is the discipline of Religious life, or the rule of the Order, for example, by which the beloved of God, Benedict, ascended to heaven; the two sides are humility of mind and austerity of life; the steps are the various rules and acts of virtue. For the ladder, being narrow, signifies the narrow way of discipline which leads to heaven. For, as St. Augustine says in the Sentences, Sentence 19: "Narrow is the way that leads to life, and yet one does not run along it except with an enlarged heart; because the path of virtues by which the poor of Christ walk is broad for the hope of the faithful, even though it is narrow for the vanity of the faithless." St. Antoninus in the Summa Theologica, part III, title 26, chapter 10, section 11: The good of Religious life, he says, was signified by that noble ladder of Jacob, whose steps are none other than those of reading, meditation, mortification, and similar exercises of which Religious life consists. On this ladder angels ascend, to offer these works to God; and they descend, to bring in turn to religious souls the various gifts and benefits of the Spouse. And God rests upon it, because by His grace and by His assistance all our endeavors are sustained, which, with Him holding them up, cannot fall; and He Himself is the firm support of those ascending, and for those arriving at the end, He is the reward. Whence it is truly said of it: "This is none other than the house of God and the gate of heaven."

Thus St. Romuald, as is recorded in his Life, by a vision of a ladder reaching from the lowest earth to heaven at its summit, through which he beheld monks in white garments ascending and descending, marvelously recognized that the perfection of Religious life and its habit were being signified. Wherefore he requested and obtained that same place from a nobleman, its lord, whose name was Maldulus, and there he built the primary monastery of his Order, in the year of the Lord 1009, which was thenceforth called Camaldoli, as if "the field of Maldulus"; it is situated near Florence on the Apennine mountain, flourishing now for six hundred years with an abundance of holy hermits, that is to say, terrestrial angels.

Thus St. Antoninus, treating of the death of St. Dominic: The Prior of Brescia, he says, at the very hour when St. Dominic died, saw an opening in heaven, through which two most brilliant white ladders were let down: the Blessed Virgin held one, Christ held the other; and angels were ascending and descending; and at the top of each ladder a seat was placed, and one sitting upon it who resembled a Friar Preacher (this was St. Dominic), having his face veiled, as if going toward heaven; and Christ and His mother were drawing the ladders upward, together with the seat and the one sitting upon it, and then the opening was closed. By this vision was signified the way by which St. Dominic passed to the heavenly realm, namely that a sure sign of predestination, and a sure path to heaven, is both the rule and the Religious life, which St. Dominic and other founders of Orders established by the prompting of God; and also the root and source of this life, namely the earnest imitation, worship, and consequent assistance and patronage of the Blessed Virgin. Hence the Blessed Virgin is called by the Fathers, and in the Litany of Loreto, the gate and ladder of heaven.

What Does the Ladder Signify Anagogically?

You ask fifthly, what does this ladder signify anagogically? I answer: This ladder represents the various seats, ranks, and choirs of the Saints and angels in heaven. The angels descend when they are sent to guard men; they ascend when they return, and they place the souls of the just in the ranks of this ladder, that is, in the seats of the angels who fell and became demons. The Wise Man also alludes to this, chapter 10, verse 10, as I said above.

Hence, to the Saints struggling in this life, their place in heaven, their crown, has frequently been shown, as to St. Stephen, to the Forty Martyrs whose memory is celebrated on March 9th, to St. Nicholas of Tolentino, to St. Francis, to St. Vitalis. For Vitalis, when he was being compelled by his persecutors to deny Christ, confessed Him all the more boldly; wherefore he was tortured with every kind of torment, so that there was no place on his body without a wound. But the Martyr, bearing his sufferings with a brave spirit, pouring forth most fervent prayers, said: "Lord Jesus Christ, my Savior and my God, command that my spirit be received; for I now desire to receive the crown which Your holy angel has shown me." Having said these words, he flew away to heaven; the witnesses are St. Ambrose and St. Jerome, Exhortation to Virgins. Rightly therefore the same Jerome, in his epistle to Julian, volume I: "Jacob saw," he says, "the ladder, and the Lord leaning upon it from above, so that He might extend His hand to the weary, so that He might provoke those ascending to labor by His own appearance."

Verse 13: The Land in Which You Sleep

13. THE LAND IN WHICH YOU SLEEP -- the whole land of Canaan. I WILL GIVE TO YOU AND TO YOUR SEED. -- "To you," that is, to your seed: for the "and" here is exegetical, or a mark of explanation, and signifies "that is."

Verse 14: In You All Tribes Shall Be Blessed

14. IN YOU AND IN YOUR SEED ALL THE TRIBES OF THE EARTH SHALL BE BLESSED. -- "In you," as in the origin and parent; but "in your seed," namely through Christ who is to be born from you, they shall be proximately and immediately blessed, that is, endowed with justice, grace, and salvation -- all the tribes of the earth, namely those who will receive Christ, believe in Him, and obey Him.

Verse 15: Six Blessings Promised to Jacob

15. UNLESS I SHALL HAVE COMPLETED -- that is, until I complete. Note here six immense blessings which God promises to His servant Jacob, who is sorrowful and afflicted. The first is, "The land in which you sleep, I will give to you"; the second, "Your seed shall be innumerable, like the dust of the earth"; the third, "In you all the tribes of the earth shall be blessed"; the fourth, "I will be your guardian wherever you go"; the fifth, "I will bring you back to this land"; the sixth, "I will not leave you, until I have completed all that I have said."

Verse 17: How Terrible Is This Place

17. AND TREMBLING -- filled with sacred fear, awe, and reverence. HOW TERRIBLE! -- how sacred, with what reverence, trembling, and humility this place is to be venerated, on account of the presence of God and the angels ascending and descending by the ladder!

THIS IS NONE OTHER THAN THE HOUSE OF GOD -- where, namely, God rests upon the ladder, and dwells with His angels ascending and descending. The Chaldean renders it: How terrible is this place! It is not a common place, but a place in which there is good pleasure before God, and opposite this place is the gate of heaven.

See here how, from the time of Jacob and Abraham, God has distinguished certain places by His apparition, His benefits, and His miracles, and has willed to be worshipped and invoked there. Why then do the Innovators clamor against the Blessed Virgin of Loreto, of Halle, of Aspricollis?

Tertullian, in his book On Flight, thinks that Jacob in this vision saw Christ, who is the house of God and at the same time the gate through which we enter heaven, and that he understood and signified this by these words of his.

AND THE GATE OF HEAVEN -- because, namely, from there I saw angels going out, when they descend by the ladder; and entering in, when they ascend by it to God.

Allegorically, the Church is Bethel, that is, the house of God and the gate of heaven: because in it, as in His own house, God dwells by His presence, both spiritual and real and corporeal in the Eucharist; and because in the Church are the merits of Christ (whose forefather and type was Jacob), by which the gate of heaven was unsealed.

So says Rupert. See Cajetan. If therefore this place and this stone were so august and terrible, what will the Church of Christians be, in which not a shadow, that is, the ark of the covenant, is preserved, as was done in the tabernacle of Moses, but the Almighty Himself, the Creator of all things, truly dwells under the white veil of the most august Sacrament, as if in a cloud? Truly St. Chrysostom, homily 36 on 1 Corinthians: "The Church," he says, "is the place of Angels, the place of Archangels, the kingdom of God, heaven itself; and if you do not believe it, look at this table," that is, the altar.

Verse 18: He Erected as a Pillar

18. HE ERECTED AS A PILLAR -- that stone, or rock on which he had slept, Jacob lifted up and set upright, so that it might be a monument of the vision and apparition made to him.

Note: "Title" (titulus) is used in four senses and signifies four things. First, a title is an inscription of a thing, as the title of a book, the title of the cross; second, a title is a column or pyramid erected as a trophy of victory, or of a notable deed; third, a title is a statue, image, or idol erected for worship and adoration, such as the title forbidden in Leviticus 26:1; fourth, a title is a piece of wood, stone, or other thing, placed or erected as a memorial and indication of some event, for example, the angelic vision made to Jacob here. For Jacob erected this stone as a pillar, so that on his return from Haran to his homeland, at the same place he might recall and venerate this benefit of God, as he is known to have done in chapter 35, verse 5.

Hence he also consecrated the same stone as an altar, as will be clear in the last verse; whence this pillar signifies not only a monument, but also an altar. And hence the early Christians, following the example of Jacob, called their churches "titles" (tituli), from the title, that is, the sign of the cross, and from the title, that is, the name of some Saint, in whose honor they were entitled, that is, named, consecrated, and distinguished -- as the title of St. Praxedes is the church of St. Praxedes; the title of St. Lawrence is the church of St. Lawrence. This manner of speaking is frequent in the Lives of the early Popes. So says James Gretser, book II, On the Cross, chapter 7. And from these titles the Cardinals took their titles and surnames, as Jerome Platus teaches, in his book On the Dignity of Cardinals, chapter 2.

Verse 18: Pouring Oil Upon It

POURING OIL UPON IT -- as a sign of consecration, says Abulensis, because things that are consecrated are anointed with oil. This pouring of oil was not, therefore, a libation or sacrifice; for nowhere do we read that oil alone was offered as a libation or sacrifice to God. And so Jacob, awakening in the morning from this divine vision, brought oil from the nearby city of Luz, which was afterwards called Bethel by him, says Abulensis, and with it he anointed the stone on which so wonderful a vision had occurred to him while sleeping, and by anointing it he, as it were, consecrated it to God. Whence also afterwards he used it as a consecrated altar, and offered sacrifice on it, as is evident from chapter 35, verse 7.

Thus, following the example of Jacob, the Church dedicates and consecrates altars and churches to God with sacred anointing, the moral significance of which anointing see in St. Bernard, sermon On the Dedication of a Church. Furthermore, with a similar anointing, says Theodoret, pious women are accustomed to anoint the reliquaries of the Martyrs, to attest both to their sanctity and to their own devotion toward them. Whence also the devil, as the ape of God and the Saints, imitated this anointing in his own sacred rites, when he persuaded his followers to anoint and consecrate stones to Terminus. So says St. Augustine, book 16, On the City of God, chapter 38.

Allegorically, St. Augustine in the same place thinks that here is signified Christ and the Chrism of Christians: for Christ is the cornerstone of the Church, Ephesians 2:20, anointing and anointed with the oil of gladness above His companions.

Tropological Meaning of Oil

Tropologically, oil is a symbol of graces and virtues, on account of eight properties, analogies, and resemblances which it has. For first, oil has the power of illuminating: for it is the nourishment and fuel of light and lamps; second, oil has the power of seasoning foods, both usefully for health and pleasantly for taste; third, the power of oil is to float upon other liquids; fourth, to warm wounds and soothe pains: for hence in Luke chapter 10, that Samaritan bound up the wounds of him whom robbers had left half dead after inflicting very grievous blows, pouring in oil and wine; fifth, to gladden the face and to restore weary and languid limbs: whence that saying of Psalm 104: "That he may make his face cheerful with oil"; sixth, to lighten labors and diminish troubles, to which pertains that saying of Isaiah 10: "The yoke shall rot at the presence of the oil"; seventh, to invigorate and strengthen the body and make it fit for wrestling and combat, as used to be done among athletes; eighth, to soften and enrich, according to that saying of Psalm 22: "You have anointed my head with oil"; from which, on account of its softness and richness, oil is customarily a symbol of mercy. All these things are easy to apply to grace and the virtues.

Verse 19: He Called It Bethel

19. AND HE CALLED THE NAME OF THE CITY BETHEL, WHICH BEFORE WAS CALLED LUZ. -- The city which was previously called Luz or Luza, from the abundance of nut or almond trees (for luz in Hebrew means "nut"), says St. Jerome in his Hebrew Questions, was called Bethel by Jacob, that is, "house of God," because sleeping beside it, he had seen God resting upon the ladder.

This Bethel is not Jerusalem, nor Mount Moriah, as the Hebrews, Lyranus, and Cajetan would have it; but, as Abulensis, Adrichomius, and others rightly hold, it is a city distant more than eighteen miles from Jerusalem, situated in the territory of the tribe of Ephraim, near Shechem, in which, as also in Dan, as if at the farthest boundaries of his kingdom, Jeroboam set up his golden calves for the people to worship, abusing for this purpose the example of Jacob, who at the same place had erected this stone as a pillar; whence this Bethel is called by the Prophets, by antiphrasis, Beth-aven, that is, "House of the idol," or "of iniquity," as Theodotion translates it, Hosea 4:5 and 10.

Some think there were two Bethels, one here in the tribe of Ephraim, the other in the tribe of Benjamin, near Ai, of which Joshua 18:22 speaks. But Andrew Masius refutes this, and proves that there was one and the same Bethel, which was situated in the territory of Luz, so that it was at some distance from Luz itself, yet Luz itself was from time to time called Bethel. Which of these is more true, we shall discuss at Joshua 18 and Judges 1.

Verse 21: The Lord Shall Be My God

21. THE LORD SHALL BE MY GOD. -- The Lord was already and had been Jacob's God from birth. The sense therefore is, as if he said: If God shall have given me food, clothing, and a safe return to my homeland, I vow and promise to Him that henceforth I will worship Him with a special and greater worship than I have worshipped Him before, namely that I will give Him tithes, both for sacrifices and for any other worship of Him; and that after my return from Mesopotamia I will dedicate this place to God as an altar and temple or chapel: for thus Jacob himself explains this vow of his in what follows, as Cajetan rightly observes.

Morally, Rupert presses Jacob's words, If God shall be with me, and shall give me bread, and he says: "This he said as a poor man, and truly a beggar of God. Nor is it surprising, since the greatest king David says: But I am a beggar and poor. A good example therefore has been provided for us children from our fathers, so that however rich we may be, we may all say, as beggars before the door of divine grace: Give us this day our daily bread, etc., that we may acknowledge that it comes to us as a gift from Him, who alone was able to create the necessary nourishment of bread no less for a king on a shining throne than for a widow sitting at a millstone." Again, Jacob asks for bread, not meat, not partridges. For, as Gregory of Nyssa teaches in his book On Prayer: "We are commanded to seek what is sufficient for preserving the nature of the body: Give bread, we say to God, not luxury, not delicacies, not golden ornaments, not the splendor of gems, not fields, not governorships of nations, not silken garments, not musical entertainments, nor anything by which the soul is drawn away from divine and higher care; but bread." And further on: "What you owe to nature is small; why do you multiply tributes against yourself? The belly is a perpetual exactor of tributes, etc. Say to Him who brings forth bread from the earth, say to Him who feeds the ravens, who gives food to all flesh, who opens His hand and fills every living thing with blessing: From You I have life, from You also may the sustenance of life come to me. Give bread, that is, that I may obtain food from just labors. For if God is justice, he does not have bread from God who has food from a thing fraudulently and unjustly acquired."

Finally, St. Chrysostom reflects, in homily 54, on "and shall give me bread." For Jacob in fact anticipated the prayer which Christ afterwards taught and established, saying: "Give us this day our daily bread"; the nourishment of the day, he says: let us therefore not ask anything temporal from Him. For it is very unworthy to ask of one so generous and excelling in such great power those things which are dissolved in the present life and have great vicissitude. Of this kind are all human things, whether you speak of riches, or power, or human glory. But let us ask for things that are always enduring, sufficient, and free from change.

Verse 22: This Stone Shall Be Called the House of God

22. THIS STONE WHICH I HAVE ERECTED SHALL BE CALLED THE HOUSE OF GOD. -- It is a metonymy: for the thing located is put for the place, as if to say: The place in which this stone stands, by my application, designation, and as it were consecration, will be and will be called holy, and a house or dwelling of God, and upon this stone, as upon an altar, I will sacrifice to God. So say the Chaldean, Cajetan, Lipomanus, and others. That this is the sense is clear from chapter 35, verse 7; for there Jacob fulfills this vow of his, having returned from Haran, and upon this stone, as upon an altar, he offers sacrifice to God.

I WILL OFFER TITHES TO YOU. -- Hence it is clear, against Calvin, that a work can be piously and religiously vowed, even one that is not commanded by God; for such is the giving of tithes, which Jacob here vows.
