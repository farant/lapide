Genesis 1 (Latin Translation from the Hebrew)

1. In the beginning God created heaven (or the heavens, as elsewhere) and earth.

2. And the earth was desolate and empty (or formless, shapeless, confused, invisible) and darkness (obscurity) was upon the face of the deep, and the Spirit of God was moving upon the face of the waters.

3. And God said: let there be light (or fire), and light came to be.

4. And God saw the light, that it was good, and God divided between light and between darkness.

5. And God called the light day, and the darkness night. And there was evening, and there was morning, day one.

6. And God said: let there be an expanse (or firmament, as elsewhere) in the midst of the waters, and let it be separating between waters and waters.

7. And God made the expanse, and separated (or caused to be separated) between the waters which are under the expanse, and between the waters which are above the expanse. And so it was.

8. And God called the expanse heaven. And there was evening, and there was morning, a second day.

9. And God said: let the waters (which are) under heaven be gathered into one place, and let the dry land appear. And so it was.

10. And God called the dry land earth, and the gatherings of waters seas. And God saw that it was good.

11. And God said: let sprout (more precisely, let produce grass) the earth small plants, plants producing seed, and fruit-bearing trees producing fruit according to their kind, whose seed is in them upon the earth. And so it was.

12. And the earth brought forth small plants, plants producing seed according to their kind, and trees producing fruit, whose seed is in them, according to their kind. And God saw that it was good.

13. And there was evening, and there was morning, a third day.

14. And God said: let there be luminaries in the expanse of heaven, to divide between day and between night, and let them be for signs, and seasons, and days, and years.

15. And let them be as luminaries in the expanse of heaven, to give light upon the earth. And so it was.

16. And God made two great luminaries, the greater luminary to rule over the day, and the lesser (or small) luminary to rule over the night, and the stars.

17. And he placed them in the expanse of heaven, to illuminate the earth.

18. And to rule over day and night, and to separate between light and between darkness. And God saw that it was good.

19. And there was evening, and there was morning, a fourth day.

20. And God said: let the waters produce abundantly creeping things (or let the waters swarm with reptiles -- the Hebrew word signifies fish and aquatic reptiles), living creatures; and let winged creatures fly over the face of the expanse of heaven.

21. And God created great sea creatures (large aquatic animals) and every living creature that moves, which (creatures) the waters produced abundantly according to their kind, and every winged bird according to its kind. And God saw that it was good.

22. And God blessed them, saying: be fruitful and multiply, and fill the waters of the seas, and let the birds multiply upon the earth.

23. And there was evening, and there was morning, a fifth day.

24. And God said: let the earth bring forth living creatures according to their kind, cattle (or domestic animals, or herbivorous animals), and creeping things, and beasts of the earth according to their kind. And so it was.

25. And God made the beasts of the earth according to their kind, and the cattle according to their kind; and every creeping thing of the earth according to its kind. And God saw that it was good.

26. And God said: Let us make man in our image, according to our likeness, and let him have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the birds of heaven, and over the quadrupeds of the earth, and over all the earth, and over every creeping thing upon the earth.

27. And God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him, male and female.

28. And God blessed them, and God said to them: be fruitful, and multiply, and fill the earth, and subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the birds of heaven, and over every beast that moves upon the earth.

29. And God said: Behold, I have given you every plant producing seed, which is on the surface of the whole earth, and every tree in which is the fruit of a tree containing seed; it shall be food for you.

30. And to every beast of the earth, and to every bird of heaven, and to everything creeping upon the earth in which there is a living soul, every green plant for food. And so it was.

31. And God saw everything that he had made, and behold it was very good. And there was evening, and there was morning, the sixth day.

I. Introduction

St. Thomas, Summa part I, Question LXVI and following, and other theologians of the school have been accustomed to provide a theological, or theologico-philosophical commentary on chapter 1 of Genesis. It seems to me that this can be done more conveniently and usefully in our age, and I undertake to accomplish it. And first I think the entire chapter should be explained, not as it is read in the excellent Vulgate version -- which it would be superfluous to reproduce here, since it is in everyone's hands -- but by adhering more closely to the Hebrew text. I shall take care to render word for word as a faithful translator.

ON THE MOSAIC HEXAEMERON IN GENERAL

II. Principles of Interpretation: Scripture and Natural Science

Before I come to the exposition of the text, I think certain things must be stated in advance. And first I shall quote the most prudent opinion of Fr. Pererius, S.J., who illustrated Genesis with commentaries that were learned, laborious, and -- as those times afforded -- excellent: "This must be diligently guarded against, and altogether avoided: that in treating Moses' teaching, we should not state anything affirmatively and assertively that is repugnant to the manifest experiments and reasonings of philosophy or of other disciplines. For since every truth is always in agreement with truth, the truth of sacred letters cannot be contrary to the true reasonings and experiments of human disciplines." Augustine himself had taught the same in these words: "If an objection is raised against the authority of sacred letters from a manifest and certain reasoning, whoever does this does not understand, and he sets against the truth not the meaning of Scripture (which he has been unable to penetrate), but rather his own meaning; nor does he oppose what is found in it, but what he has found in himself as if on its behalf," Epistle 8, to Marcellinus. He has more to the same effect in his books On the Literal Meaning of Genesis. I shall cite one or two passages. "This must be held without any doubt: that whatever the wise men of this world can truly demonstrate about the nature of things, we should show not to be contrary to our books; but whatever others teach in their volumes contrary to sacred letters, we should believe without any hesitation to be completely false, and, as best we can, also show it to be so; and so let us hold the faith of our Lord, in whom are hidden all the treasures of wisdom, so that we be neither seduced by the loquacity of false philosophy, nor terrified by the superstition of feigned religion," Book I, Chapter 21. "But, someone says, how is it not contrary to those who attribute the figure of a sphere to the sky, what is written in our books: Who stretches out the heaven like a skin? Let it indeed be contrary, if what they say is false: for what divine authority says is true rather than what human weakness conjectures; but if perhaps they could prove that with such proofs that it cannot be doubted, it must be demonstrated that what we read in our Scriptures about the skin is not contrary to those true reasons," Book II, Chapter 9. See also Book I, Chapter 13, 19; Book II, near the end. St. Thomas has similar statements in more than one place (Summa, Part I, Question LXVIII, art. 1; and Opusculum X).

III. Mosaic Chronology and Geology

In the exposition of this chapter my chief aim is to demonstrate that no solid objection arises from the natural sciences against Moses' narrative; indeed I hope to show that certain things narrated by Moses agree accurately with the natural sciences, and indicate truths unknown to humanity for many centuries after Moses: so that a not inconsiderable argument may arise from this for the divine revelation of that history. In order to do this conveniently, I think we must first of all remove a difficulty that presents itself of its own accord, and which would disturb our commentary.

Mosaic chronology seems to be opposed to the doctrine of the more recent geologists, supported by many and accurate observations; for the interval of time which they posit as very long between the creation of the world and the creation of man seems to be compressed too much by Moses.

I observe in the first place that the more learned now profess that no other true and proper chronology is proposed by Moses besides that which alone is of no small importance, and which pertains to the earth already perfected in its final work, and therefore pertains to human beings; and that the divine writer did not wish to weave an accurate chronology of beasts, plants, or mountains.

Next I observe that there are two ways of satisfying this and similar difficulties. The first would be to deny the geologists' position, and show it to be false or at least scarcely probable; a path that not a few have taken, but with an outcome, I think, not very successful. For the most powerful difficulties arose which are founded not in some hypothesis or system, but in facts, both copious and diligently observed. Furthermore, it would be necessary to know much about the profane sciences and especially about natural history, as much as one can scarcely hope for in a man occupied with other studies and devoted to a holier ministry. Nor if anyone from among such men were to undertake this task, would he, I think, easily be understood by many students of theology. Finally, while the theologian laboriously strives to refute objections drawn from observations, behold, new objections arise from other observations, to which, since they were unknown, he himself could not respond, and so he would scarcely not have wasted his oil and labor.

I now see that the more prudent and learned theologians and defenders of religion are taking a different path, which is both more convenient and liberates the theologian from the troublesome task of scrutinizing the terrestrial crust, and inquiring into the formation of rocks and mountains, into the fossils of the organic kingdoms, into shells, into bones of extinct animals, and into other nearly innumerable things that pertain to the natural sciences. These men say that no length of time whatsoever is contrary to Moses' history, since here he defines absolutely nothing about the time from the creation of heaven and earth up to Adam's creation. Thus they, without further examining the geologists' views and the weight of their arguments, reconcile the long periods of time that the latter demand with the sacred text. Such a reconciliation, moreover, can be effected in three ways.

IV. Freedom of Opinion in Chronological Questions

But before I show these paths of reconciliation, let me be allowed to state in advance that if, in order to reconcile the truth of sacred letters with natural truth, we should need to interpret certain texts and words of Moses in a way that is indeed new, but not absurd -- since here the matter is not about faith or morals, but about chronology, since the ancient Fathers do not agree in their interpretation of these words and passages -- that new interpretation should not be rejected. For knowledge of natural things, growing daily, sometimes causes us to better grasp the meaning of certain passages from profane writers: therefore much more can it add some light to the word of God, when this deals with created things. I said "much more": because if the sayings of the profane ancients, to whom nature was less known, sometimes need more recent science to be understood, how much more the words of Him who infinitely surpasses our knowledge!

The Church has always granted freedom of opinion in chronological questions, although questions about man and about the progenitors of our Lord Jesus Christ were certainly of greater moment than those which deal with the chronology of plants and animals. Take an example of this. According to the Hebrew text of Genesis and the Vulgate which the Roman Church uses, from Adam to the Christian era approximately forty centuries intervene. Natalis Alexander places 4000 years exactly; Bellarmine and Petavius 3981; Sixtus of Siena and others 3960; A Lapide 3953; Scaliger 3950; St. Jerome in his Hebrew Questions 3944. The Church equally tolerates these and other opinions; but in the Roman Martyrology, where the Lord's nativity is announced, following the Greek version of the Seventy Interpreters, it records with Eusebius that there were 5199 years. But neither do those who follow the Seventy interpreters agree among themselves, for example Clement of Alexandria places 5624 years, and St. Julian, Bishop of Toledo (7th century) 6011.

V. First Mode of Reconciliation: Long Periods Before the First Day

Now in our matter perhaps there is no need for us to resort to new and virtually unheard-of explanations of passages and words; for observations already noted by interpreters are sufficient. I come now to the first of the three modes of reconciliation.

Those who follow this one will place the long geological periods after the creation of heaven and earth, which is indicated in verse 1, and before the rising of light, which was the work of the first day. Indeed what we read in the first two verses, or certainly what we read in the first, precedes the days in which we hear the divine words: Let there be light, etc. "You made heaven and earth before every day," says Augustine. And the Venerable Bede: "Two things God created before every day and before all time: the angelic creature and formless matter," On Genesis, chapter 1. Peter Lombard teaches that "in the beginning God created heaven, i.e. the angels, and the earth, namely the still confused and formless matter of the elements, which was called chaos by the Greeks, and this was before every day," II Sentences, dist. 12. It would be superfluous to add testimonies to the same effect from SS. Ambrose, Basil, Bonaventure, Theodoret, Albert the Great and others. We can therefore establish that the earth existed before the first day, in the state shown in verse 2, namely it was void and empty, etc. Pererius thinks the time between the creation and the first day was brief: but he does not dare say it was less than twenty-four hours. Prudently indeed, but more prudently Petavius writes of the time before the first day, "what interval this may have been, no conjecture can ascertain," On the Work of Six Days, Book I, Chapter 18, section 6. Gennadius of Marseilles (or the author of the book On Ecclesiastical Dogmas), to be cited below, thought that interval was very long.

Indeed we read in Exodus: "In six days the Lord made heaven and earth, and the sea, and all that is in them," chapter 20, verse 11, and chapter 31, verse 17. But the speech here is about the work of the six days, not about the first creation, or about the time intervening between this and that. We can interpret that passage in this way: "In six days the Lord made (from matter previously created by him)," or "completed," or "perfected heaven and earth," etc. For to these corresponds the Hebrew word asah, which does not include the idea of creation from nothing, and does not prevent one from believing that this creation preceded those six days by some time indeterminate as far as we are concerned. The supporters of the opinion I am speaking of think that this time was very long, and that cosmological and geological phenomena, which seem to require great intervals of time, occurred during that period. Some of them hold that the heavenly bodies were then formed from the created matter; indeed they believe that the earth itself was not without light during that entire time: that there was then dry land separated from waters; that there were plants and brute animals; both of which belonged to the same classes of living things that exist with us, and often to the same genera, though they should not be referred entirely to the same species as those. They also suppose that during that time many natural revolutions, floods, elevations of mountains, and other such things occurred; about which time, they say, Genesis narrates nothing to man, because that time did not pertain to man. They finally suppose that, on account of some great catastrophe, all life on earth perished, the earth remained covered with water, "became void and empty, and darkness was upon the face of the deep" -- produced perhaps from dense vapors and other particles; and finally that on the first day the renewal of the earth began, which was completed with the creation of man. (This is especially the view of Dr. Buckland, Oxford professor and noble geologist, and with him Dr. Chalmers; also the Catholic Parisian professor Desdouits. This opinion too was preferred above others by that most distinguished and learned man Nicholas Wiseman, now most eminent Cardinal of the Holy Roman Church and Metropolitan of England. But all of these seem to have been preceded by R. Abahu, cited by Moses Maimonides, who annotated at the beginning of Genesis: "Hence we learn that God built worlds and destroyed them again.")

In this opinion, which is tolerated by the Church, the disadvantage is that the second verse is too much separated from the first. "He created... the earth. The earth was void..." Here things seem to be described as following one upon another in close succession, with the first appearance of the earth being depicted, not a state into which the earth fell back after many ages and numerous changes. It seems somewhat harsh to me to hold that there was light before God's voice: "Let there be light"; plants and animals, before God said: "Let the earth bring forth grass... let the waters swarm with reptiles... let birds fly over the earth," etc. Furthermore, it seems less probable that God would have destroyed the earth he had created, so adorned and full of life -- even through secondary causes -- before making man, who could know him and contemplate his works. Nor does it appear, if they make the Mosaic days 24 hours long, why God would have completed that ancient world which was destroyed before man with such lengthy construction and the operation of the laws of nature; but remade this one, which he gave to us, so quickly and by his one omnipotent power alone. Nevertheless, if anyone thinks this system should be followed, he is free to do so. Victor De Bonald himself, although perhaps too hostile to theoretical geologists, nonetheless admits that the time between the beginning and the first day is not determined by Moses, and therefore it is permitted, if one wishes, to suppose cosmological changes, as long as one pleases, in that interval of time, and to multiply centuries at will, without divine Religion suffering any detriment from this (Moses and the Geologists, chapter III, p. 55).

VI. Second Mode of Reconciliation: Augustine's Instantaneous Creation

The second of the hypotheses which I indicated as having been devised to reconcile Genesis with geology, thinks the long geological periods should be placed after the six Mosaic days: a path which St. Augustine opened. When the holy Doctor had already attempted the explanation of those days a second time -- in On Genesis Against the Manichees, On the Literal Meaning of Genesis (unfinished) -- and could not be persuaded of the common interpretation, he composed, now as Bishop, the twelve books which he called On the Literal Meaning of Genesis. In these, accordingly, and later in the work On the City of God, Book XI, chapters 7, 29, 30, 31, he takes those six days as one and the same day, or as one moment; but that they are said to have had evening and morning, he refers to the knowledge of the Angels, by which they first knew each of God's works in the Word, then in the works themselves; and he calls the knowledge in the Word morning knowledge, and that in the created things themselves evening knowledge. See On the Literal Meaning of Genesis, Book IV, chapter 26 and following: "What need was there, then, he says, for six days to be narrated so distinctly and orderly? Because, of course, those who cannot see what is said -- that he created all things at once -- cannot, unless the discourse proceeds more slowly for them, arrive at that to which it leads them." This exposition, to tell the truth, seems to me indeed ingenious, but rather allegorical or mystical than literal. But now what must especially be noted is that Augustine in the cited passages did not teach that all the things recounted in Genesis chapter 1 actually existed in one day or one moment (which others taught with Philo the Jew and Origen), but that only the matter of many or all corporeal things was then produced (which Basil, Gregory the Great, and innumerable others teach), and that after those six days it was formed in the succession of time. What is peculiar to Augustine alone is that he contracts the six Mosaic days into one. His doctrine is reduced to these main points: first, all things were produced by God at once; second, since these things can be arranged so that elementary matter occupies the lowest grade, pure spirits the highest, and in between and in the middle ranks are mixtures, or chemical compounds, then physically compound bodies such as rocks, and especially organic bodies. Hence those things which pertain to the lowest and highest grades, and any others that are not now produced by the forces of nature, were then produced fully and perfectly; but those contained in the intermediate grades, which are now produced by natural forces, existed then by God's command only in potency and seminally or causally. Augustine's opinion, always held to be free from error, has pleased many theologians, among them Albert the Great, in Part II of the Summa, Tract. IV, Question XIV, article 3, part 2. St. Thomas, in the Summa, Part I, Question LXXIV, article 2, reveres it, and judges that neither it nor the common doctrine should be prejudged. But in an earlier work, in II Sentences, dist. XII, art. 2, he had written thus: "Augustine holds that at the very beginning of creation, certain things existed in their proper nature distinguished by their species, such as the elements, celestial bodies, and spiritual substances; but others existed only in seminal reasons, such as animals, plants, and human beings, all of which were afterward produced in their proper natures in that work by which, after the period of those six days, God administers the nature he had previously established, concerning which work it is said in John chapter 5: My Father works until now, and I work; nor should one attend to the order of time in the distinction of things, but to the order of nature and of teaching. Of nature, as sound precedes song by nature but not by time, and so those things which are naturally prior are recorded as having been made first, as the earth before animals, and water before fish, and so on. By order of teaching, as is clear in those who teach geometry: for although the parts of a figure constitute the figure without any order of time, nevertheless geometry teaches that the construction is made by drawing one line after another, and this was Plato's example. Thus also Moses, instructing an unlettered people about the creation of the world, divided into parts what was made simultaneously. Ambrose, however, and other Saints hold that the order of time was preserved in the distinction of things, and this position is indeed more common, and seems more consonant with the letter on the surface; but the former is more reasonable, and better defends Holy Scripture from the mockery of unbelievers, which Augustine teaches in On Genesis Book I should be carefully observed, so that the Scriptures may be explained in such a way that they are not mocked by unbelievers; and this opinion pleases me more."

It should be noted in passing that the Angelic Doctor saw, by the power of his intellect -- which was supreme in him -- long before the observations and discoveries of more recent times, that the Mosaic history of creation could not easily be entirely defended from the mockery of unbelievers if we adhere to the common understanding, and that he was above all persuaded that the ease of defending Scripture from such mockery should be kept before the eyes of the sacred interpreter, when he treats passages that are not sufficiently clear and have not been declared by the judgment of the Church. Perhaps the most acute man saw that profane men would easily mock a history set forth in God's name, which represents God as doing everything immediately and returning to his work repeatedly, like a human craftsman -- who, though he could splendidly show his omnipotence by making everything perfect in a moment of time, and though he could also show his wisdom if he left to be accomplished by those very laws the things that could be accomplished through the simple and fruitful laws of nature established and preserved by himself, nevertheless demonstrates neither his omnipotence by the unity of his act, nor his wisdom by the operation of his laws; a God who on each day works in one or a few moments, then rests for the whole day; who creates confusion and wants order to reign; who before the appearance of animals makes some sort of light, only for it almost immediately to vanish or flee to the stars; and covers the newly created earth broadly with waters, only to extract it from them as soon as possible.

VII. Conclusions from Augustine and Thomas

What do we conclude from all this? First, we can safely bid farewell to the common interpretation which maintains that the Mosaic days are natural days, or more precisely civil days of exactly twenty-four hours, with Augustine and Thomas and many other doctors leading the way. And since the Church permits us to shorten the Mosaic days and contract them into one day or into one moment of time; it will equally be permitted, where a sufficiently strong reason urges it, to make those days longer. Furthermore, from what has been said, and especially from a careful reading of the work On the Literal Meaning of Genesis, particularly Book VI, chapters 4, 5, 6, 7, it is clear that Augustine too does not deny all temporal succession in the works of creation (which would indeed be to deny the noonday sun), but he thinks that what is narrated in Genesis verse 5 truly occurred "according to intervals of time": and that those things which even now are generated from created matter -- such as seeds, plants, and animals -- were in the beginning of things made only causally, invisibly, potentially, in the way that future things not yet made come to be. "Causally, it is then said, the earth produced grass and wood, that is, it received the power of producing," he says in Book V, chapter 6. See also Book VI, chapter 6, and Book VIII, chapter 3. Thus when we dissolve common salt in water and allow the liquid to evaporate freely, we then make cubic salt crystals -- but future ones. This, however, is the difference: that the crystals form even though we think no more about the salt that remains dissolved: for they form by the law of nature, which is God's law. On the contrary: "The power of the Creator and the might of the Omnipotent and All-sustaining One is the cause of the subsistence of every creature: if this power should ever cease from governing the things that were created, at the same time their forms would cease, and all nature would collapse. For it is not as when a builder of houses departs after he has built, and his work stands while he ceases and departs; the world could not stand even for the blink of an eye if God should withdraw his governance from it," Augustine, On the Literal Meaning of Genesis, Book IV, chapter 12. Now those things which at the beginning of time had been created causally and invisibly, afterward began to exist outside their causes and visibly -- not indeed, according to Augustine, in those mystical days which are entirely different from ours, but "through temporal intervals, through those well-known days of corporeal light which come about by the circuit of the sun," Book VI, chapter 5. But how much time those temporal intervals occupied, how many solar days it took for those things which previously had existed only potentially to proceed to actuality -- this no one reveals to us, and so that time remains indeterminate for us, which each person will be free to extend as much as reason persuades him it should be done.

In truth this opinion is somewhat obscure and involved. Petavius writes: "For indeed, let them turn themselves this way and that as they please, those who think thus will be able to say nothing consistent and congruent with the narrative of Scripture," On the Work of the Six Days, Book V. Augustine himself wrote: "Whoever... seeks in the enumeration of those days another meaning which can be understood properly and more suitably, not in figurative prophecy but in this account of the creation of creatures, let him seek and, aided by God, discover it; for I do not so confirm this interpretation as to contend that no other, which might be preferred, can be found," On Genesis Literally, Book VI, ch. 25; Book V, Retractions II, 24.

Nevertheless it remains firmly established that it is open to everyone to hold, with Augustine, that those days of Moses were far different from ordinary days: that it is permitted to interpret these words -- days, evening and morning -- either with him or in another manner, if any shall seem more fitting to the letter; it will be permitted, finally, to assert that in that first and true creation of the visible world, God drew forth all matter from nothing and established for it the laws by which it would be governed; and that afterward, by the force of these laws imposed upon nature, the atmosphere was formed, likewise seas, lakes, rivers, rocky strata, islands and continents, so that with Augustine it may be said that from the beginning God "was inserting into things already made the causes of things yet to be made, and by His omnipotent power was making future things." As for plants and animals, that God likewise created only their matter at the beginning, and when He established the laws of nature, prepared from that very time everything necessary so that all organic substances could live, grow and multiply: and when, according to natural changes, favorable conditions arrived, then by the Creator's command various classes of living beings, various genera and species (without creating anew even a single atom of matter) were called forth to life. He who explains things thus reconciles not only observations already made, but also whatever future ones may be made, with Genesis, without departing from the path discovered by the Doctor of Hippo; indeed, as far as living things are concerned, he makes that path much better and more tolerable. This method of reconciliation was embraced by the distinguished Waterkeyn, a priest and professor of Louvain, in two short works, of which one was published at Louvain, the other at Liege (On Geology and its Relationship to Revealed Truths; Science and Faith on the Work of Creation, 1845).

VIII. Third Mode of Reconciliation: The Days as Long Periods

There remains a third mode of reconciliation, which places the lengthy periods neither before the Hexaemeron nor after it, but within the Mosaic days themselves -- indeed, it identifies those periods with these days; or, what is nearly the same thing, it grants that they were ordinary days, but that they did not immediately succeed one another, and that they were separated from each other by periods of unknown duration. This third mode, unless I am mistaken, is more suitable for our purpose, namely for the defense of revealed truth as well as natural truth.

This is founded on a principle long known to interpreters. That the word "day," yom, is often taken for "time" in the Scriptures (as also among profane authors), theologians and interpreters have long observed. The Arabs call the day from sunrise to sunset -- which properly for the Hebrews is yom -- nahur, which means "splendor." By the word jaum (whose affinity with the Hebrew yom is evident) they signify a period of time. What we read in Genesis II, 4: "In the day when God made heaven and earth, and every plant of the field..." most interpret as meaning in the time. Augustine, who in Book XX of The City of God, chapter 1, observes that "day" is customarily used for "time," wrote in Book II of On Genesis against the Manicheans, chapter III: "Above, seven days were numbered; now one day is spoken of, on which day God made heaven and earth and every green thing of the field, and every pasture -- by which name of day all time is well understood to be signified." Thus this passage is interpreted by the Arabic version, Bede, and many others both Catholic and heterodox: Natalis Alexander, Alphonsus Nicolai, Duguet, Clericus, etc. Petavius says: "That day is taken simply for time or a period, or for days, as most hold: for the singular is often placed for the plural in Scripture," On the Work of Six Days, Book I, chapter 5; and elsewhere: "In that chapter II, the day on which heaven and earth are said to have been created is used for the time and the whole span during which creation and ordering proceeded," chapter XIII. "The Doctors commonly say, says Molina, that Moses in that place took 'day' for 'time,' according to that passage in Deuteronomy XII, 'the day of perdition is near,' and elsewhere frequently in the Scriptures 'day' is taken for 'time,'" in Part I, On the Work of Six Days. "Bannes agrees with him (on this point). 'Day' can be taken for any duration and measure;" in Part I, Question LXXIII. Pererius writes: "So it is very frequently among the Prophets, who use the name of 'day' for 'time.'" Probably several of these writers would have accepted 'day' for 'time' also in Genesis I, where we read: "Day one, day second," etc., if they had seen what we see -- that against the common interpretation are opposed not empty fancies of opinion, but careful observations which are the verdicts of nature. "The passage of time destroys the inventions of opinion; it confirms the verdicts of nature," says Cicero. And not only in the plural number, as a certain theologian wrote, but also in the singular, "day" is taken in the Scriptures for an indeterminate time. In Amos, VIII, we read: "Behold, the days are coming, and I will send a famine... in that day (i.e. in that time, as the Targum called Jonathan's explains) the beautiful maidens will faint from thirst." You see that the singular "day" means the same as "days." Micah IV: "And it shall come to pass in the last of days: the mountain of the house of the Lord shall be prepared on the top of the mountains." In this passage, "the last of days" signifies the final or later times, and so the Seventy Interpreters, the Syriac, the Arabic, and the Targum of Jonathan translate it. So also Father De Carrieres and Clericus. Remarkable is the passage in Nahum III, 17, where "day" seems to express a cold time, especially nocturnal: "Your guards are like locusts, and your little ones like swarms of locusts that settle on the hedges in the day of cold. The sun rises, and they fly away, and the place where they were is not known." See Job chapter XXXVIII, verses 22-23; Hosea chapter II, verses 16, 21; Ezekiel chapter XXX, 2-3; Joel III, 18; Obadiah v, 11, 14; Zephaniah III, 11; Zechariah VIII, 11, etc. St. Irenaeus brings forward several interpretations of the threat made to Adam, "on whatever day you eat of it, you shall surely die," and this among others: the first parents "died on the very day on which they ate, because one day of the condition of the world," that is, as Petavius interprets, because "that entire span during which the world endures is like one day." Irenaeus adds (and Justin agrees in the Dialogue with Trypho) that Adam can be said to have died on the day he sinned, because he departed within the thousandth year: for a thousand years are one day, Psalm LXXXIX. The Rabbis distinguish between divine days and human days. R. Nechonja, in the book Bahir: "The days of God, blessed be He, are a thousand years," Psalm XC, verse 4, etc. Jalkut Rubeni: "God, blessed be He, said: Behold, I will leave man alive for one day; for thus I said to him: on whatever day you eat, you shall surely die; but you do not know whether divine days or human days are to be understood. But I will give him one day of mine, that is, a thousand years, so that he must live 930 years," in Schoetgen, Horae Hebraicae, volume I, page 1052. "The great day of God is bounded by a cycle of a thousand years," says Lactantius (Divine Institutions, Book VII, chapter XIV).

Those days were understood as long periods in the last century by Bertier, a priest of the Oratory (History of the Earliest Times of the World, p. 108), and more recently not a few other Catholics, including churchmen, have embraced this view; such as Bishop Becchetti, O.P., and more recently Augustinus Ghisi of the Barnabite family, and the distinguished Abbot Rohrbacher. Hermenegildus Pini, a Barnabite, had arrived at nearly the same opinion. Bossuet had written that God willed to make the world by six distinct stages, which He wished to call six days. The Abbot Flotier says the same. Earlier than Bossuet and contemporary with St. Bernard, St. Hildegard had written the same: "For the six days are six works: because the beginning and completion of each individual work is called a day." I see now that theologians cite this opinion as at least permissible and free from all censure. By this declaration, as if after a long war, peace has at last been established between Theology and Geology -- a peace that will surely be useful to both; wherefore not only cultivators of human science but even more so friends and ministers of Religion ought to rejoice in it.

Note that this opinion can stand together with the first one; and that we are free to assume both that some time, perhaps not brief, intervened between the first creation and the first day, and that those six Mosaic days are to be regarded as indeterminate periods.

IX. The Fathers and the Interpretation of the Days

But do the Fathers and expositors, while acknowledging that "day" is often taken in the Scriptures for "time," agree in taking those six days of Genesis chapter I as days of 24 hours? By no means; but it will be useful to observe that even if such a consensus existed, nevertheless the interpretation set forth, which rests on valid and compelling reasons, would by no means deserve condemnation. The most wise doctrine of the Angelic Doctor must be recalled: "The things that pertain to faith are distinguished in two ways: for some are per se of the substance of the faith, such as that God is three and one, and matters of this kind; in which it is permitted to no one to think otherwise. But certain things pertain to faith only per accidens, insofar as they are handed down in Scripture, which faith presupposes to have been promulgated at the dictation of the Holy Spirit -- things which can indeed be unknown without peril by those who are not bound to know Scripture, just as many historical matters, and in these the Saints also have held different views, expounding divine Scripture in different ways. So therefore concerning the beginning of the world, there is something that pertains to the substance of faith, namely, that the world had a beginning, created by God, and this all the Saints say in agreement: but in what manner and in what order it was made does not pertain to faith except per accidens, insofar as it is handed down in Scripture, the truth of which the Saints, preserving it through diverse exposition, have handed down diverse opinions," in II Sentences, loc. cit. In this question, therefore, about the manner and order in which the world was made -- a question not pertaining to faith per se, but only per accidens -- it will be permitted for us too to hold different views, as the Saints did, and, while preserving the truth of Scripture, to put forward a new exposition more congruent with the advances of the natural sciences.

But that the Fathers and interpreters agreed in the common interpretation of those days is false; indeed very learned and soundly orthodox men profess that it has in no way been ascertained and defined what is signified by the name of those days. What I have adduced from Augustine, Thomas, and others would suffice to show this -- to which I could have added Athanasius, Clement of Alexandria, Procopius, and not a few others. Indeed, Nicolaus Spedalieri wrote that a greater number of theologians think the world was created by God in an instant, since His power does not need time; which however I think was said less than truthfully. Among the theologians who seem to support this view, there is one who admits for the six days some very brief time, such as Father Serry.

Explaining the first day, or rather the one day, Augustine says: "Is this day the name of all time, and does it include all the ages of the centuries in this word, and was it therefore not called the first but one day?" On Genesis Literally, Book I, chapter XVII. The same words, nearly verbatim, are found in Bede. No geologist demands either more or so much. The same Augustine seems not always to have recoiled from this view -- namely, that those days are indeterminate periods; for in the books On Genesis against the Manicheans and On Genesis Literally (unfinished), he departs from the common understanding of those days, and yet seems to admit some succession of time, In On Genesis against the Manicheans, Book I, chapter XIV, he says: "How could the three earlier days have existed without light?... It remains that we understand, in the very delay of time itself, the distinctions of the works to have been so named -- 'evening' on account of the completion of the finished work, and 'morning' on account of the beginning of the future work -- from the likeness, that is, of human works, because they usually begin in the morning and end at evening." The same words, nearly verbatim, are found in the Commentary on Genesis commonly attributed to St. Eucherius, Bishop of Lyon, which seems to be attributed to him by St. Isidore of Seville. In the book On Genesis Literally (unfinished), chapter VI, Augustine writes: "It may be asked what day he means and what night. If he means that day to be understood which the rising of the sun begins and sunset closes, and that night which extends from sunset to sunrise, I cannot find how it could have existed before the luminaries of heaven were made." Truly singular is the passage from chapter XV of this book, where he speaks of the fifth day: "Here plainly even the slowest person should now be alert, to understand what sort of days are being enumerated. For since God gave to animals fixed numbers of seeds, maintaining a marvelous constancy in a fixed order, so that for a fixed number of days according to each kind they both carry their conceived young in the womb and warm their laid eggs -- an institution of nature preserved by God's wisdom, which disposed all things sweetly -- how could they in one day conceive and become heavy with young, and nurture and feed their offspring, and fill the waters of the sea, and multiply upon the earth? For thus it is added: And so it was done: before the coming of evening." To tell the truth, this reasoning rests on a false reading that deceived Augustine; for the words and so it was done are neither in the Hebrew nor in any known version. But it remains true that the holy Doctor in this book favors the opinion that posits long periods more than the common one.

I shall adduce a passage of St. Cyprian, rather obscure indeed, and to which the context lends no light: "So that the first seven days in the divine dispensation, containing seven thousand years," Letter to Fortunatus, On the Exhortation to Martyrdom. He probably indicates that those seven days are, as it were, a type of seven thousand years of the world, and this accords with the ancient letter circulated under the name of St. Barnabas, where we read: "Attend, children, to what he said: He completed all things in six days. This means that God will bring all things to completion in six thousand years. For a day with Him is a thousand years. He Himself is my witness, saying: Behold, today's day shall be as a thousand years." You will find similar statements in Irenaeus, Book V Against Heresies, chapter XXVIII, and in Victorinus, Bishop and Martyr, On the Creation of the World, number 3.

X. Objections Considered: The Sabbath Precept

I come to the objections that can be raised. I note in general that the objections are drawn from the words days, evening and morning, which are certainly explained less literally in the opinions of Origen, Augustine, Athanasius, and all who contracted those six days into a point of time or into the briefest time, or certainly into no more than an ordinary day. Therefore they increase these difficulties as much as we do, and indeed more. What they reply, we too can reply. We could therefore say: we respond what Augustine responded, and on his behalf Aquinas (at the cited places), what Augustine's more recent defenders Cardinal Henry Noris (Vindication of Augustine, chapter III) and Sylvester Maurus (Theology, volume I, Book IV, Question XI), what Cardinal Cajetan, Serry, Berti, and others respond.

I do not know whether it could cause difficulty for anyone that, according to this opinion, the name "day" in the creation account seems to be taken in a double sense -- namely, for the time of light in verse 3, and for an indeterminate time in verse 5 and following. There will be discussion of this below. For now I make just this one observation: those who adhere to the common opinion will not raise this objection; for they too find it necessary to do the same thing; indeed they customarily give three senses to that word: first, the time of light, chapter I, verse 3; second, a day of 24 hours, verse 5 ff.; third, time in general or six days, chapter II, verse 4.

They can rather object as follows. It is written in Exodus chapter XX, verses 9 ff.: "Six days you shall labor and do all your work. But the seventh day is a Sabbath to the Lord your God: you shall not do any work in it... For in six days the Lord made heaven and earth and the sea and all that is in them, and He rested on the seventh day; therefore the Lord blessed the Sabbath day and sanctified it." The reason for the precept is not understood -- indeed there is no such reason -- if those days are not ordinary days of 24 hours. I respond: the periods that go by the name of "day" can without absurdity be taken as the symbol and prototype both of the common days of labor and of the last day of the week destined for rest. For that this word is sometimes taken in different senses in one and the same context, they themselves must admit, as I was recently observing. Augustine openly says that the days of the week represent in some way the days of creation and are not similar to them, but very unequal (On Genesis Literally, Book IV, chapter 25). So immensely do the works of God differ from the works of men, and the necessary rest of men from the rest of God, which is merely a cessation from new works. The rest of the human sabbath was contained within twenty-four hours. But who will say that God's cessation was bounded by a similar limit? Did the seventh day of divine rest cease after 24 hours? The seventh day is without evening and has no sunset, says Augustine (Confessions, Book XIII, chapter 36); and similar expressions about this day are found among the Rabbis.

Finally, we should remember that a sabbath of the seventh year was also prescribed for the Hebrews. God commands, Leviticus chapter XXV, verse 2 ff.: "When you shall have entered the land which I will give to you, keep a sabbath rest for the Lord. For six years you shall sow your field, and for six years you shall prune your vineyard, and gather its fruits: but in the seventh year, the land shall have a sabbath of rest for the Lord." See also Exodus chapter XXIII, verse 11 ff. Now consider: these days of creation, or ordinary days of the week, serve as a type of seven years. Why then could not six or seven longer periods be the exemplar of six or seven solar days? It must be added that also in Leviticus, after the sabbatical year, the Hebrews are immediately prescribed to count also seven weeks of years, that is, seven times seven, which together make forty-nine years: after these the fiftieth year, or the year of jubilee, was to be sanctified. "You shall not sow, nor shall you reap what grows of itself in the field, nor gather the first fruits of the vintage, on account of the sanctification of the jubilee, but you shall eat what is offered immediately; in the year of jubilee all shall return to their possessions," etc., chapter XXV, verses 8-13. Finally, if through Irenaeus and others among the more ancient Fathers, six or seven days can signify six or seven thousand years (section 9), why on the other hand cannot six longer periods be types of six days?

XI. Objections Considered: Evening and Morning

The greatest objection drawn against this opinion comes from the words repeated six times in Genesis chapter I: "And there was evening and there was morning, one day... a second day," etc. Yet this objection presses with equal or greater force against the opinion of Augustine and others who take those days as a moment of time or as a single day. But I respond directly: first, if those words truly demonstrated that the six Mosaic days do not differ in duration from our solar days, one could still say that it is by no means necessary that those days succeeded one another without interval; but that they were only solemn days on which certain great events appeared -- the first light, the manifestation of the stars, the first dry land emerging from the waters, the first animals, etc. One could suppose that a long interval of time was interposed between the first and the second day, and likewise between the others; so that each of the mentioned days was the beginning of a new period and a new series of developments of works.

I observe secondly that even of the first three days it is said as of the remaining four: "And there was evening, and there was morning, a day..." But in the first three days the sun did not yet exist, which by approaching the horizon and receding from it would produce both morning and evening. Therefore I conclude generally: since the Mosaic days do not depend on the sun, since they are different from the solar day, it follows that the morning and evening predicated of them are not to be understood in the ordinary way. If, however, anyone should think that some darkness and beginning of light are absolutely designated by those words, we advise him that it suffices for us to find in each day, even if they are taken as indeterminate periods of time, some beginning of light after some preceding darkness. But more on this later.

I respond thirdly: we can accept the explanation of Augustine and Eucherius (sec. 9), namely that "evening is the completion of a finished work, and morning is the beginning of a future one." Also in Book I of De Genesi ad litteram, chapter XVII, Augustine writes: "Evening, in that entire three-day period before the luminaries were made, is perhaps not absurdly understood as the end-point of a completed work; and morning as the signification of a future operation."

Finally, fourthly: the Hebrew word for evening, erev, also signifies mixture and confusion, from the root erev, 'he mixed,' because at evening time light and darkness are in a certain way blended together and all visible things and colors are confused. Indeed, Aben-Ezra noted that erev is called 'evening' because the shapes of things are mixed together at evening time. From this word seems to be derived the Erebus of the Greeks, which they placed together with Chaos at the beginning of the world (see Hesiod, about whom Ovid says: "He invokes Erebus and Chaos"). And 'morning' can conveniently be derived from the Arabic root bachara, which means to divide, to extend, to open -- or from another word of the same language, bakaron, which means 'morning' as well as 'firstborn.' For morning, or Aurora, opens, as the poets say, the gates of day, generates light, seems to give birth to the sun, and reveals things previously obscure and makes them distinguishable. Also in Hebrew and Chaldee we have baqa, to cleave, to divide, and bakhar, to bear or rather to bear a firstborn. For the Hebrews, therefore, the word 'evening' indicated mixture and confusion; the word 'morning' indicated division, birth, arising. If we interpret those names according to the etymology of the word, they are indeed easily and aptly explained in each of the six days; for in each one there is a certain opening or unfolding or birth, with some mingling or confusion of things preceding -- but this will appear better below.

You will say that in this way those words are understood rather figuratively and poetically than according to the letter. But how many are the words and expressions among Orientals that are poetic and not to be understood according to the bark of the letter! This first chapter of Genesis, although I would not dare say it was composed in verse, nevertheless everywhere savors of poetry, which perhaps the subject matter itself almost necessarily required. And indeed God here speaks to creatures that have no sense, who nevertheless hear Him and obey His voice. "In what language," says Augustine, "did God call the light day, and the darkness night? Whether in Hebrew, or Greek, or Latin, or some other? In God there is, without noise and diversity of languages, a pure understanding" (De Genesi ad litteram I, ch. X). Does it not seem to smack of poetry that God sees the light and His other works and, like an artisan delighted with his work, approves them (He saw that it was good)? If we fear to call these things poetic, I shall not be troubled about the word. The color, certainly, so to speak, and the phraseology of this chapter seems placed so far outside common usage, differs so much from the customary style of histories and chronicles, as those events narrated there are altogether extraordinary and different from all other histories. It is therefore permissible to conclude, as Jerome wrote elsewhere, "take 'day' for 'time.'"

XII. The Expression "Evening and Morning" in Daniel and Genesis

Someone might further object: By these words 'evening and morning' the Hebrews designated the natural day, as is clear from Daniel ch. VIII, v. 14: "Until evening and morning, two thousand three hundred days, and the Sanctuary shall be cleansed;" and v. 26, "the vision of evening and morning." The Hebrew text does not have the word 'days' at v. 14 (nor does the Syriac and Arabic version), which word is added, for the sake of exposition, in the Greek version and in the Vulgate. The Hebrew codex has at v. 14: Until evening morning; and v. 26: the vision of evening and morning, or of the morning. Daniel therefore says: until evening-morning two thousand three hundred, when he meant to say: until two thousand three hundred days. So when we wish to say that someone stayed in a certain city for thirty days, we conveniently say that the man saw the noonday sun or sunset thirty times in that city; or we indicate that a man is seventy years old by affirming that he has counted seventy winters. For there are as many approaches of the sun to the meridian or to the horizon as there are days, and as many winters as years.

I respond: I do not know, first, whether this manner of speaking was in use among the Hebrews at the time of Moses, nor is it certain that it was customary in the age of Daniel, who perhaps, as interpreters have noted, used 'evening-morning' for 'day' because he was alluding to the perpetual sacrifice, which was offered evening and morning, and which during those days had to cease. However the matter may stand, it is certainly one thing to use 'evening-morning' in place of 'day,' and another to write: And there was evening, and there was morning, a day, etc. Furthermore, as we have already indicated, if the Genesis days are different from solar days, then the Genesis evening and morning must also differ from the ordinary ones.

Some think that Moses selected those words in his narrative which would better prepare the way for the Sabbath precept, so that men would be attracted to labor six days and devote the seventh to God, says Melchior Canus (cited by Bannes in Part I, Question LXXIV, art. 2), who nevertheless holds that those six days are only one. But this doctrine must be received cautiously. Moses did not in fact set out to teach a complete cosmogony; rather, first, wishing to turn his people away from the superstitious worship of creatures, he wanted to instill that God is distinct from the world and the creator of all things; and second, to establish an exemplary cause, so that the Hebrews would remember to sanctify the Sabbath. But he had no need of fiction for these purposes, nor did he affirm anything at all less than truly. He could, however, to indicate sufficiently long periods of time that were nonetheless determinate and bounded by two end-points -- though of a length unknown to us and probably unequal -- adopt the word 'day'; he could also introduce that usage of the word 'day' so as to prefer the words 'evening' and 'morning' over others that he might have used.

Finally, note that those words 'evening' and 'morning' always cause some trouble for interpreters of whatever opinion. For how was there morning without the sun? How evening? Some think the light appeared in the East, some in the West; but East and West refer to various regions, and yet in those first days there was no region at all. If after twelve hours of light -- serving in place of some unknown sun -- evening came in one place, then at the same time morning occurred in another, so that it was as much morning as evening. Augustine says (De Genesi ad litteram, Book I, ch. X): "If I say this, I fear I shall be laughed at both by those who know with certainty and by those who can very easily perceive that at the time when it is night among us, the presence of light illuminates those parts of the world through which the sun returns from its setting to its rising, and that through all twenty-four hours, throughout the circuit of the entire orbit, day is never absent somewhere, nor night somewhere else. Shall we then place God in some particular part, where evening would come when the light departed from that part to another?" Furthermore, the time between evening and morning is a time of darkness -- it is night, not day. Some, but improperly as Cornelius a Lapide says, want 'evening' to mean the whole night and 'morning' the whole day. Where in Scripture have they found an example of this meaning? Since Moses wrote in verse 5 that God called the light 'day' and the darkness 'night,' why immediately in the same verse and in the following ones does he not call the time of light and darkness 'day' and night, but rather called them evening and morning? Many think those days begin with evening; but the first day, others argue, seems to have begun when God said: Let there be light -- just as the other days seem to have begun with God's words at verses 6, 9, 14, 20, 24. Some join the time of the first light with the preceding darkness to make the first day; to others it seems absurd that universal darkness should be posited as part of a day in that very verse where we are taught that light was called 'day' by God. And furthermore, in the hemisphere opposite to the one where light appeared, the first day would have been perpetual night. Therefore, even in the common opinion, those words 'evening' and 'morning' are not without difficulty. Some have also thought it was written by enallage (substitution of tenses): 'there was evening and there was morning, one day,' putting the past tense for the future -- for the sun did not exist during the first three days. But I plainly do not see how this can be said either in the past or in the future about the first days, since their evening and morning were neither then first produced by the sun, nor were they ever to be produced in the future without the sun.

Amid so many difficulties, interpretations, and doubts, what better, what safer course can we follow than to tolerate what the Church tolerates, and not to reject as contrary to the word of God what observers of nature concordantly assert -- among whom no one would deny there are many who are most skilled in that science, many who are sincere lovers of truth, and not a few who are also reverent toward the divine word? Let us say with St. Thomas: "It seems to me safer that those things which the Philosophers have commonly held and which do not conflict with our faith should neither be asserted as dogmas of faith, even though they are sometimes introduced under the name of Philosophers, nor be denied as contrary to faith, lest an occasion of despising the doctrine of faith be given to the wise of this world" (Opusc. X). Let us therefore follow the opinion we have set forth; and if some insoluble difficulty, whether theological or physical, should command us to take leave of it, then it will be open to us to choose one or the other of the remaining two paths of reconciliation that we indicated before.

XIII. Why This Interpretation Is Necessary

To what end all this? someone will say. What reason compels us to take refuge in an explanation that is less obvious? Could not God have worked as He pleased? Why can it not be said that those six days were defined by our own days and by the revolution of the earth around its own axis? Why should we suppose that very long and perhaps unequal periods lie hidden in them? I respond: because Scripture itself sufficiently indicates this, as we shall see below; and because the common interpretation suffers no slight difficulties, as has already been partly shown; and because I cannot otherwise explain Moses's cosmogony in accordance with the present state of the sciences. Because when you accept this explanation, you will find Moses's narrative not only free from every difficulty, but its divine origin, in a wonderful way, confirmed in a remarkable way. Furthermore, because this is the certain opinion of all geologists, or at least of the majority and the most learned among them on this matter; and according to the prudent maxim of Aristotle, those things are to be approved which are taught either by all practitioners of a given science, or by the majority, or by its leading figures. Finally, because the weight of the arguments on which their doctrine rests seems to be of the greatest importance. Indeed, when we observe that so many students of nature agree on this -- the majority of whom, from an innate desire for petty glory, prefer nothing more than to bring forth novelties and refute others, who often disagree among themselves on theoretical questions, who often in many matters fight fiercely sometimes for water, sometimes for fire (whence arose the names Neptunists and Vulcanists or Plutonists), of whom some defend revealed truths with sincere religious devotion, others scarcely or not at all think about these things, and still others openly oppose them -- certainly such a consensus must be said to be produced by the strongest arguments, of the kind that flow spontaneously from facts perceived by the senses, facts that are great, remarkable, and constant. Certainly nature must speak clearly enough for its interpreters to agree so wonderfully among themselves (Brocchi, Physical State of the Soil of Rome, p. 195). From these considerations, even one ignorant of the natural sciences must conclude that their doctrine is at least highly probable, as Suarez teaches: "One should adhere more to the opinion that is more philosophical and more conformable to reason, when Scripture does not compel otherwise" (De Opere sex dierum, Book II, ch. VII).

XIV. Why God Worked in Six Days

The principal reason why God carried out His works in six days, when He could have done everything in a moment and all at once, seems to Petavius to be this: that He might accommodate the manner of His omnipotent working to human understanding. But no one, I think, among those who in our age are occupied in examining nature and its laws, will readily grant that the Creator set this as His goal in fashioning the universe -- that His handiwork should be accommodated to human understanding. We find, on the contrary, that what men first conjecture when investigating the causes of natural things is very often far removed from the truth; and that God has truly given the sons of men a lot full of toil and darkness, since "He delivered the world to their disputation" (Ecclesiastes 3:11). For although, stirred by that innate desire for knowledge, men have never ceased attempting to penetrate the hidden recesses of nature and to have a thorough knowledge of God's works, yet even the philosophers themselves, by meditating, observing, experimenting, and calculating, after many centuries and great errors, have been able to discover very little; so that it ought to be clear to an educated man above all that it has by no means been given to us to discover all "the works that God has wrought from the beginning to the end" (Ecclesiastes 1:13; 3:11). It would not be difficult to confirm this with many examples, and indeed very illustrious ones, but this is not the place for that discussion. Rather, I do not easily perceive how Moses's narrative, as it is commonly understood, is very well accommodated to human understanding, especially that of the uneducated. I think men would more easily have understood that the sun was made by God immediately at the beginning, than that unknown light of the first day, distinct from the sun, whose cause, use, and consequent status are equally unknown; or the three following days that are without the sun, yet have morning and evening. I think men would more easily have persuaded themselves that God made the dry land together with the sea at the beginning, than that the earth was first covered with waters and then on the third day emerged from the waves by God's command, with that entire mass of waters receding into the lower parts of the globe -- as though the waters had then and not before received the power of flowing downhill and tending downward by their own weight, which some have supposed.

XV. Natural Reasons Requiring Long Time Periods; Primordial Chaos

To set forth the natural reasons that seem to require long periods of time for the formation of the terrestrial globe before it was inhabited by man -- this would be to write a complete philosophical treatise, which is not the task of theologians. I, in order to satisfy the desire of some, have collected the principal points here with what brevity I could.

It has been the opinion of many that in the beginning the entire material of the earth, together with the material of the sun, moon, and all the stars, was gathered into one disordered mass, disordered and undigested, confused, and as it were rolled into a ball; of which kind is the Chaos of Hesiod and of Ovid. Very many of the ancients, says Petavius, held that at the beginning of things, and before the works of the six days, something formless and disordered was created by God, which they call hyle (matter). Augustine, in Book I of De Genesi contra Manichaeos, chapter 5, says: "The first matter was made confused and formless, from which all things that are distinct and formed would come to be: which I believe the Greeks call chaos. For thus also in another place we read it said in the praises of God: 'Who made the world from formless matter' (Wisdom 11:17) -- which some manuscripts have as 'from invisible matter.'" And in chapter 7: "That formless matter, which God made from nothing, was first called heaven and earth, and it was said: 'In the beginning God made heaven and earth' -- not because this already existed, but because it had the potential to exist. For heaven is written to have been made afterwards. Just as if, considering the seed of a tree, we should say that roots, trunk, branches, fruits, and leaves are there -- not because they already exist, but because they are going to come from it.... It was called heaven and earth because from it heaven and earth were going to be." He also thinks that by the names of the invisible and unformed earth, the abyss, and the water, the same matter is designated; but under all these names the matter was invisible and formless, from which God founded the world. He holds the same view in Book XII of the Confessions, chapters 4, 8, and 12. Following Augustine's example, Albinus Flaccus (Alcuin), Bede, Isidore, and others who customarily follow Augustine adopted this interpretation, as did Caesarius (Dialogue 1, question 51). Nevertheless, Augustine in his book Against the Adversary of the Law and the Prophets denies that God "first made formless matter" and then, with some interval of time interposed, "formed what He had previously made formless"; rather, he teaches that "He created it together with the world." He teaches the same in De Genesi ad litteram, Book I, chapter 15 -- which opinion seems different from the former one, unless we say with St. Thomas (Part I, Question 66, Article 1) that in these passages "Augustine understands the formlessness of matter as the absence of all form; and thus it is impossible to say that the formlessness of matter preceded in duration, because this was already in act -- for duration implies this... to say therefore that matter precedes without form is to say a being in act without act, which implies a contradiction." But in the earlier passages, Augustine did not exclude all form from that "confused and formless matter," and so, according to St. Thomas, Basil, Ambrose, and Chrysostom taught that formless matter preceded its formation in time. However that may be, those who hold that God in the beginning created only a certain formless matter, a kind of chaos from which He afterwards made all corporeal things, seem themselves also to hold that from the same mass both terrestrial bodies and celestial bodies were formed. And these confirm more and more this opinion of ours, that the formation of the visible world required a long time.

"The Almighty could have made all things in a single moment of time," to use Augustine's words (De catechizandis Rudibus, ch. XVII), and could have brought forth all things fashioned and completed faster than thought itself; He could have created the elements and compounds and bodies equipped with organs, both of plants and animals, together with their souls, and joined them to bodies. "But who has known the mind of the Lord, or who has been His counselor?" It is certain and established that He could have done this, but did not wish to -- which both geological observations confirm and is most clearly evident from the Scriptures, especially from Genesis chapters I and II, where Moses carefully enumerates successive works and reports that some were produced from others, not properly created, that is, made from nothing -- as fish from water, plants from earth, land animals and man. "And yet," says Petavius, "these things must be said to have been created from nothing, if they all existed simultaneously in a single moment" (Book I, ch. V, sec. 4). God could also, after the creation of matter, have entrusted matter itself to the laws of nature created along with it (which are nothing other than the universal wills of the Creator, or perhaps more precisely, His uniform and permanent wills), so that from these both celestial bodies would coalesce, and compounds would be formed, and all phenomena of nature would arise -- intervening directly by Himself only where the forces of nature were unequal to the work intended by the Creator. He could, by the very laws He Himself had established, not only preserve created things but also perfect them in various ways, combine them, and shape them in one way and another, and (to use the phrase of the Christian poet Marius Victor) "adorn formless things with superimposed forms" -- which He also does now. Thus in those first times immediately after creation He would have operated in the world in the same way as He has always operated after the creation of the first parents -- namely through the laws of nature, intervening Himself through the ministry of natural laws where these fall short. That it pleased God to act thus, geological investigations show, and Moses too seems to indicate in a certain place, which we shall point out below. Now then: if by the laws of nature both this globe which we inhabit and other similar globes coalesced, and what are called minerals and the strata of the earth's crust and mountains and other such things were formed -- certainly this was not the work of six of our days, but of a far longer time, during which the sons of God, namely the angelic intelligences, praising the Creator, exulted and admired His wondrous works and the most wise and most fruitful laws created by Him. But how great that interval of time was, we cannot determine. (Arnold, Abbot of Bonneval, De Opere sex dierum, says: "But to define the gap of that interval, during which, before the succession of times, the wisdom of God invisibly arranged the first works, is rash, especially since the canonical Scriptures have handed down nothing certain about this.")

God could certainly, by those very laws by which He still governs and directs the course of nature, not only assemble all bodies, but also separate the atmosphere from the denser matter, and through it separate the upper waters, that is, clouds, from the terrestrial waters, and gather these into one place or into several; He could have amplified seeds or tiny sprouts into trees, and made the stars of heaven visible to our earth. Now if God did not use the ministry of His laws, He must be said to have accomplished all things immediately -- yet nonetheless, like a human craftsman repeatedly putting his hand to the work, to have made things imperfect and then enlarged, so that after a brief interval, by means of great miracles, He would perfect and adorn them -- which would plainly have been to violate all the laws He had just imposed on nature at the very birth of nature. Unless perhaps someone suspects that the Creator substituted other laws for that time, a kind of provisional government (as they now say), different from those that now exist, which He would abrogate immediately after Adam was formed. All these suppositions bear no semblance of truth; indeed they seem rather absurd.

XVI. The Distance of the Fixed Stars and Stellar Parallax

Furthermore, the light of the fixed stars does not reach our earth except after a certain interval of years, and this because of their immense distance from us, although the velocity of light exceeds the velocity of sound by approximately 1,000,000 times. Astronomers determine the distance of the stars by the method they call parallax. It is well known that elevated bodies, if viewed from different locations, are referred by us to different points of the sky. The less the bodies are distant, the more these celestial points are distant from each other, and conversely. If the body's elevation or distance from the earth is very great, we always refer it to the same celestial points, that is, no parallax is observed. Now, let a star be observed from the two extreme points of the diameter of the earth's orbit, that is, by two observations, of which one follows the other after six months. The angle which each line of sight makes with a single perpendicular will be different, unless the distance is so great that it can be considered infinite; and thus the difference will be greater as the distance is smaller. Now the astronomers, even employing the most exquisite care and instruments, have found no such difference: and yet they would have noticed some, as they deduce from trigonometry, if the distance of the fixed stars from the earth did not exceed the radius of the earth's orbit by more than 206,000 times, that is, unless it exceeded eight million millions of leagues. From this it follows that none of the fixed stars which have been best observed to this day and which offer themselves most favorably for observation (indeed, probably none of the fixed stars at all), is so close to us that its light could reach us in less than two years.

More recent astronomers prefer to this method the observation of the relative position of two stars which appear very close to each other, but one is situated behind the other and much more elevated, in which position some variation can be noted. This parallax is so small that determining it is extremely difficult. The distinguished astronomer Bessel, by numerous observations and laborious investigations, determined the parallax of a double star in the constellation Cygnus, and it was so small that from it one deduces that rays of light from it do not reach us except after nearly ten years. The distinguished W. Struve attempted to determine the parallax of the star called alpha Lyrae, and it appeared so tiny to him that its light cannot reach us except after twelve years. The parallax of the most beautiful among the double stars of the southern sky, called alpha Centauri, determined by the astronomers Henderson and Maclear, appeared greater to them: the star seems less remote among those whose distance has been determined in some way: its light would reach us in three years and a little more. Faye had thought a slightly greater parallax, and therefore a smaller distance, should be assigned to another star of Ursa Major; but observations most diligently conducted by Peters, and by Ott. Struve and by Schluter, seem to show this parallax to be smaller than that of Cygnus determined by Bessel, and therefore this star to be more remote than that one.

But come now: Moses speaks of visible things, as St. Thomas teaches; therefore when he reports in verses 16-17 that God on the fourth day not only made the sun, moon, and stars, but also 'placed them in the firmament of the sky to give light upon the earth,' he certainly indicates that from that day or period, not only the sun and moon, but also at least a great number of stars, illuminated the earth with their light. Therefore, all the more on the sixth day could Adam contemplate the host of heaven, that is, the multitude of stars. However, if those days were of 24 hours, how many fixed stars could Adam have seen on the day he was created? How many in the first or second year since he had come into the light? At present only about eight thousand fixed stars present themselves to our eyes aided by no instrument; at that time Adam would not have seen even one fixed star, but only the five wandering stars: Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Venus, and Mercury, the last of which, because of its excessive proximity to the sun, is hardly ever visible for a brief time; and the other four rarely all appear above the horizon at the same time. These would have constituted the entire host of heaven for Adam to contemplate in the first years; which does not seem to me to agree well with the context of Genesis. To have recourse to miracles, without any witnesses, would be ridiculous: the situation does not deserve such a defender. For Suarez teaches, in agreement with the common opinion of theologians: 'Miraculous or extraordinary works are not to be accepted without necessity or sufficient testimony,' On the Work of the Six Days, Book II, Chapter VII. Hence it becomes more probable that the time which preceded the creation of man was not so brief. But let us descend from the starry heaven to the earth.

XVII. The Earth's Crust and Geological Strata

That only a small part of the terrestrial globe, namely the crust alone, has been permitted to human exploration, is sufficiently well established: only by conjecture and by giving free rein to the imagination do philosophers and poets descend lower. The thickness of the explored crust barely reaches one league, which is 1/1400 of the earth's diameter. Yet the exploration of this thin crust has been sufficient to determine many things with certainty, and many more probably, regarding geogony, and these harmonize excellently with the narrative of Moses, provided that geological operations are not bound to narrow limits of time. This is the sole postulate of geology; once granted, it offers itself as a not useless companion and friend to religion.

Let us briefly examine how this crust is constituted. The earth's outermost layer is composed of soil suitable for cultivation, or, as they now say, vegetable earth. In many places there appear, or are found beneath that soil, either deposits of river alluvium, or other deposits of continental waters, mostly calcareous (such as the stone called travertine), or bodies ejected by volcanoes; these are circumscribed and local formations, either recent or of not very ancient times, or certainly more recent than the more extensive and underlying formations.

Beneath this outermost layer, if one digs, a series of strata is found which usually, if we dig on a plain, closely approach a horizontal direction, one superimposed upon another, and they differ among themselves both in thickness and hardness, and in the nature of the mineral or inorganic substances of which they consist, as well as in the presence or absence, abundance and diversity of organic fossils, which are the remains of bodies belonging to the organic kingdoms, and which are often dug out from them. But if we go far enough, these fossils cease, and the distinction of strata becomes less evident; and if the excavation were carried still further, at last no sign of it would appear, but probably a compact rock mass of unknown depth would be found: for such a mass, as if a kind of foundation, is always seen to support the strata where the last of them is visible. If we dig on hilly or mountainous terrain, we find the same things, except that the strata tend to be more or less oblique. Sometimes they are vertical; but above these oblique or vertical strata, sometimes lofty horizontal ones extend: sometimes a stratum changes direction, and from oblique becomes horizontal, or oblique in the opposite direction: sometimes strata are broken off and one part is found lower, or two parts seem to have receded from each other, and a valley intervenes: often indeed the gap is found filled with mineral materials entirely different from the material of the walls. But if we dig on the summit of a mountain, where the rock is compact and no strata appear, then no matter how much we dig, we will find no strata, but always continuous rock, similar to those which appear to be the bases of strata on the plain and in smaller mountains. These continuous rocks (without strata), as well as others not dissimilar to them and immediately superimposed upon them, but divided into strata, and also often foliated or, as they say, schistose, and which likewise lack organic fossils, are usually called primitive or primordial. Fossiliferous is the name given to the terrains in which organic fossils are found, which terrains are superimposed upon those primitive ones.

Some writers, among them Gerdil, assumed as a certain truth that the primitive mountains were created together with the earth. But this doctrine can no longer be defended. For in the terrains they used to call primitive, far more than in others, the works of crystallization -- more or less distinct or confused, perfect or imperfect -- are observed, which manifestly reveal the operation of natural laws and presuppose an earlier fluid state. Secondly, in their physical characteristics those terrains show a great analogy with volcanic terrains, which also seems to indicate a similarity of origin. Then the substances that have been called primitive, such as granite, clearly show that they sometimes ejaculated themselves from the depths and insinuated themselves into the interstices of the overlying rocks. Furthermore, fossiliferous strata formed from marine sediments, as the marine remains show, often lie adjacent to the sides of primitive rocks: but these no longer exhibit the horizontal or nearly horizontal direction which they certainly had originally, but present a very oblique and sometimes vertical orientation; this new position they undoubtedly owe to those substances which we call primitive, erupting from the deep and thrusting upward whatever obstructed them -- which seems to have been possible only by the force of subterranean fire: from this, therefore, the present condition of those bodies must be said to derive its origin. Finally, there are found, at least in the primitive terrains that are divided into strata, within the very bowels of the rock, fragments of other stones which are themselves from primitive rocks, but of a different kind; which shows that they were neither primitive in the strict sense, nor created in the state in which they are now observed. Therefore all the terrains that are observed were, after the creation of matter, worked out by the forces of nature, that is, according to the laws imposed by the Creator upon corporeal nature. Guided by analogy, we can believe the same of the solid mineral substances below, whose appearance we are not permitted to enjoy. Indeed, no reason appears why God should be said to have immediately perfected the interior parts of the globe, while entrusting the crust, given to humans as a thing of lesser importance, to secondary causes. Now if the entire solid part of the earth was perfected by the forces of nature, this certainly was not the work of a few days. To assert that God miraculously accelerated the effects of nature would be inappropriate -- as I was noting just above with Suarez. Nor does it please Pereira to suppose God as breaking, in perfecting nature, the laws he himself imposed upon nature; and to have recourse in physics to unnecessary miracles, he says, is the same as confessing a lack of reasons -- which statement P. Nicolai reports and approves.

XVIII. Fossiliferous Terrains and the Succession of Life

This conclusion descends more evidently from a careful examination of the fossiliferous terrains, which were certainly formed after the creation of beings endowed with organic life, and after these had begun to multiply upon the earth and in the waters. Those terrains which extend immediately over the primitive ones are often greatly elevated on the sides of primitive mountains, and by themselves sometimes constitute quite large mountains. Those which appear more recent than these are generally less elevated. For terrains whose strata are superimposed upon other terrains are rightly judged to be more recent than them. If the earth had suffered no disturbances, these strata, which reveal themselves to have been formed in water, would be concentric and still covered by water. Therefore it was necessary that, before the creation of things which live upon the earth, part of the terrestrial crust be elevated above the waters, and the dry land appear. That this happened, the arrangement of mountains and the inclination of the strata show.

The strata are not always concordant, that is, parallel, but are often discordant, which occurs when the strata of a certain terrain show a particular direction or inclination, and the strata superimposed upon them show a different one. Since we clearly perceive that strata generated from marine sediment are considerably inclined, we must conclude that they were disturbed from their original position, that is, elevated. If above these sides inclined toward the horizon, other horizontal marine strata appear, we must conclude that these marine sediments were posterior to the inclination and disturbance of the underlying terrain, and that both before and after that disturbance, a fairly calm sea deposited the material of the strata there. These discordant arrangements of strata frequently present themselves on the sides of mountains; that is, in a mountain or a series of mountains, different strata do not agree. For example: here a certain terrain A appears inclined, but B superimposed upon it is horizontal; elsewhere A and B are concordant and inclined, but C superimposed upon them is horizontal; elsewhere terrains A, B, C are inclined, and D is superimposed horizontally, and so on. From this it is deduced that the first elevation of terrain A preceded the formation of B; the second occurred between the deposition of B and that of C; and the third between C and D.

Furthermore, often some of these terrains are not found in many regions, even though the lower and upper terrains are present; often in certain terrains there are found marsh sediments or, as they say, freshwater deposits, entirely separate from marine ones; often terrestrial remains are found, and especially a very great abundance of plant substances which show that they once flourished in the place from which they are dug out. From these facts it follows that certain regions, having emerged from the sea, after a certain period sank again beneath the waters, so that they might again receive additions from new marine sediments.

In the lowest and most ancient fossiliferous strata, together with certain zoophytes and testaceans unknown to our present seas, there are dug out certain crustaceans from the family called trilobites, which is divided into many genera and which geologists believe to have perished before the first existence of vertebrate animals: for it is not found in the upper strata nor in the sea as it now exists. In these terrains are carboniferous strata and anthracites. These terrains are immediately succeeded by the coal terrain, from which a very great quantity of fossil coal and many remains of plants are dug out, which reveal a fairly mature vegetation. Then small freshwater shells and certain sauroid fish, which are no longer found in present waters, begin to appear. Ascending higher, the remains of reptiles, namely of saurians (more closely related to lizards), have been discovered. In the upper terrain (called the Trias), there appear tracks believed to have been impressed by the feet of birds, and of another animal, probably one of the batrachian reptiles (of the frog family). In the terrain called Jurassic, besides plants and invertebrate animals, extraordinary aquatic saurians and pterodactyls -- that is, flying reptiles, probably insectivorous -- have been found. In this terrain, together with many marine fossils, the first remains of mammals are said to have been found, but from less perfect animals of this class: those remains are small jawbones from the animal called Didelphus Bucklandii. But to some it seems more probable that these bones should not be referred to that class; the distinguished Agassiz thinks it probable that they belong to some aquatic mammal. Indeed, the remains of mammals have never been found in the Cretaceous terrain (which is believed to be more recent than the preceding one), but rather fish, including freshwater ones, river turtles, marine and terrestrial saurians, and the remains of birds that inhabit the banks of water. Upon these terrains, which are called secondary, sit those which are called tertiary. In these, together with innumerable marine fossils (which more closely resemble present-day living creatures than the preceding ones do), the remains of terrestrial quadrupeds are dug out: but the more ancient among these differ so greatly from present-day living creatures that they constitute entirely different genera. The more recent ones belong to genera either very similar to those which now live, or the same as them, such as the genera Elephas, Rhinoceros, Hippopotamus, Castor, and Mus; some also belong to apes or to carnivorous beasts, but these are rare.

In many places, above these terrains of aqueous sediment, there appear those deposits which seem to be attributable to alluvial floods or to flowing and disturbed water, and yet they differ from the deposits of recent and present-day alluvial floods and are more ancient than them: they are called diluvial terrains or the Diluvium. From these terrains many animal remains are dug out, both from the genera just mentioned and from others, especially horse, deer, ox, and also bear, dog, hyena, cat, etc.

As the terrains are more recent, so the organic fossils, other things being equal, present themselves as less altered from their natural state, and the species of living things which appear are referred to higher orders and classes of both organic kingdoms, and the fossils more closely approach the plants and animals which live with us.

From this very brief sketch of the state which the terrains and fossils present, it follows that our earth suffered many vicissitudes and disturbances before historical times; that many regions were covered by the waves of the sea for a long time, and some more than once; that many species of animals, indeed entire families, perished entirely from the earth, with others succeeding in their place; finally, that the animal kingdom (nearly the same must be said of the plant kingdom) was not brought forth within the brief space of two solar days, but successively, gradually, and little by little; and that new classes of animals appeared only when many other creatures had not only already grown and multiplied upon the earth and filled the waters of the sea, but had already ceased to live and multiply. All of which things require not an interval of two or four solar days, but a series of ages. That this is not contrary to the Word of God, but only to the common interpretation of certain words in Chapter 1 of Genesis, I consider sufficiently proven, and I am confident it will be better demonstrated from the interpretation of that chapter.

XIX. Against Attributing All Fossils to the Noachic Flood

If anyone attributes all these phenomena and the distribution of fossils (I shall speak with Linnaeus) to the Noachic flood, which erupting suddenly, quickly passed away, this person is truly a stranger to the knowledge of nature, and sees with others' eyes, if he sees at all. 'The shells and whelks of the sea' which, to use Tertullian's words, 'wander on the mountains,' were once thought probably attributable to that flood; but now that the matter has been explored far more accurately, this opinion has lost all probability. And indeed, shall we attribute to the Noachic flood the stony spoils of zoophytes which on the island of Timor, as Peron attests, are elevated up to 223 fathoms above sea level? Or that extensive subterranean coral stratum which, extending on both sides of Mount Pugliasco (in the territory of Piacenza in Italy), Cortesi observed, who also describes a stone stratum everywhere perforated by worms, as they say, lithophagous, which is placed under other shell-bearing strata. Similar things are recorded by Ambrosio Soldani, Camaldolese Abbot, and by Baldassarri. Ric opposes three things to the common diluvial system: first, strata perforated by lithophagous organisms; second, polyp-colonies which are found in that same natural position which they exhibit in the sea; third, the distribution of shells or other aquatic creatures which are often not mixed confusedly and randomly, but are found distributed as if by families, which is also observed in the sea. This distribution is very obvious and has been noted by many, especially by Soldani and by Brocchi. But what is most noteworthy in the distribution and series of fossils is that a successive order of creation, rather than the disturbance of a tremendous cataclysm, is indicated and in a way placed before one's very eyes. Furthermore, who would think that continuous mountain ranges should be attributed to it, whose regular strata seem in no way to indicate the confused and disordered deposits of a flood -- for example the Apennines, which, generated for the most part by water, show marine remains in many places? Many of its other strata seem to lack these: but the nature, arrangement, and continuity of the strata declare one and the same origin for both; and where larger shells are absent, the smallest and microscopic marine particles often superabound, as Soldani also noticed in the past century.

Even if we were to grant -- which however the many things said above do not permit us to concede -- that all the fossils of the terrains called tertiary are remains of the Noachic flood, it would not follow that the more ancient fossiliferous strata should likewise be attributed to it; indeed, the opposite conclusion would legitimately follow. In truth, does not the diversity of effects reveal a diverse origin -- namely, the diversity of the nature, hardness, and inclination of the strata, as well as the different abundance and nature of the fossils which are found in the tertiary terrains, and especially in the more recent ones, more similar to present-day living things? If there was one epoch and one origin for all fossiliferous terrains, why do those which are more ancient contain few fossils but abound in metals, which are so lacking in strata as these are more recent? Why do those which teem most with fossils tend to be neither greatly elevated nor to differ much from a horizontal direction? Why do we always see the former placed beneath the latter, but never superimposed upon them? Why do we observe marine bodies better preserved in these, and usually their very shell not petrified, but never even one of their stony casts or molds? Why have mammals left remains only in the more recent ones? To which questions it is certainly not so easy to respond for those who defend that diluvial hypothesis.

Nevertheless, while we deny that all fossiliferous terrains should be attributed to the flood, we certainly do not deny that the truth of that most certain event is also confirmed by the testimony of the natural sciences, which truth, confirmed by profane tradition, no wise person doubts. But I am confident I shall be able to say something about this at another time. What we have set forth, if I see anything correctly, suffices to refute those who thought all fossiliferous terrains should be attributed to the sea occupying many regions either at the same time or successively during that interval of years which lies between the creation of Adam and the Noachic flood; which was the opinion of Gerdil and others. This indeed can be granted of certain more recent sediments, but not of fossiliferous terrains in general. Not to mention that this interval seems brief to geologists, that progressive distribution of fossils of which we have spoken would be, in this hypothesis, an effect without a cause.

ON THE FIRST VERSES OF GENESIS

XX. Approach to Explaining the Sacred Text

But let us now approach the explanation of the sacred text, which, after so many things we have set forth as preliminaries, I am confident will be briefer and easier.

'The narrative and entire arrangement of those six days,' says Theophilus of Antioch, 'no one can expound worthily, not even if he had infinite mouths and infinite tongues; nor if he spent innumerable years in this life would he be competent to explain these things as they deserve.' I certainly am not able to expound it worthily or as it deserves: yet I attempt to do, according to my measure, what in their own times Basil the Great, Ephrem the Syrian, John Chrysostom, Ambrose, Augustine, and other Holy Fathers did, and what the ancient theologians, among whom SS. Thomas and Bonaventure excelled -- namely, to elucidate and expound the narrative of Moses using the resources of today's sciences; which narrative, although it is simple and not contrived for show, nevertheless contains matters both very great and difficult, even taken according to the letter. The spiritual senses which are added upon the literal are certainly not to be despised, indeed they are sometimes to be very highly esteemed: but to treat of these is not the business of this place, where I have undertaken to expound the work of the six days according to the letter.

I shall bring forward the opinions of the Fathers (I shall speak with the distinguished prelate Joseph Rossi), yet not those which pertain to physical and astronomical matters: for in the age of the Fathers, the knowledge of such things was still rudimentary and incipient. This knowledge later began to be perfected by the diligence of philosophers; it is being perfected daily, and the more it is perfected, the more the science of the Hexaemeron is elucidated. A time will come when the depths of nature are revealed; and posterity will pass those judgments about us which we pass about the ancients, whom in natural science we see as children: then indeed the divinity of the Hexaemeron, shining forth in fuller light, will thrust itself even upon the eyes of opponents.

But the opinion which teaches that nothing material and corporeal is comprehended in that narrative, but that only intelligible things remote from the senses are proposed, which are foreshadowed by sensible things as by figures, and that this entire history of the world's origin is allegorical -- this opinion must be utterly rejected. This error is attributed to Origen, who, just as in other matters he is accused of having transferred everything to allegories and figurative senses, despising or condemning the simple and literal meaning of the Scriptures, so too he is accused of having perverted the Mosaic history of the world's birth with the same trifling.

To refute this madness of the Origenists at length is not the business of this place. Let it suffice to point out that it is against all the laws of hermeneutics, and that no one of sound mind interprets historical books in this way; and, what is the chief point, that it is rejected by the Holy Fathers and by the Church. Against Origen argues Epiphanius, Heresy LXIV, and more fully in the Ancoratus, and Sophronius of Jerusalem declaims against his followers. 'We are so far,' says Petavius, 'from allowing that what he (Moses) wrote about the creation of heaven and earth and the work of each day should be transferred to I know not what spiritual interpretations, that we think no mention is made in that entire narrative, Chapter I, except of corporeal things: and that Moses was entirely silent about angels and other beings lacking a body. For, since he was addressing the Jews, who were desperately attached to present things and could not imagine anything intelligible, he meanwhile leads them from sensible things to the Maker of all,' says Chrysostom. Gregory of Nyssa likewise, in his book on the Hexaemeron, considers that Moses discusses only corporeal matters, as do also Severian, Homily 1, Procopius, and Cyril in Against Julian II.

XXI. "In the Beginning" (Bereshith)

The first book of Sacred Scripture is called by the Greeks Genesis, which means origin or birth, because it takes its beginning from the narrative of the origin and birth both of the world and of the human race. It has pleased some to interpret Genesis as the origin of the earth; which does not seem admissible, although it is true that Moses here treats more of our earth's birth than of the origin of all things. The Hebrews call this book by its first word, Bereshith, according to the accepted custom by which 'they give names to their volumes from their beginnings,' as Jerome writes.

Moses begins the narrative from the creation of things; for there is nothing that commends God's relations with men, man's dependence on God, and God's majesty, omnipotence, and the other attributes connected with the notion of Him, as much as the notion of creation and the spectacle of the created world. Therefore the creeds of faith also take their beginning from God the Creator: and creation, as it is the first article of faith, so it is also the first historical event. The existence of God, whom we profess to be one, true, and good, Tertullian establishes from this visible universe, in Book I Against Marcion, whose fictitious and fabricated god -- whom Marcion introduced as different from the Creator and hitherto unknown -- he refutes principally by this objection: 'God ought to have been known from the works of some world, man, and age of his own... The god of Marcion ought to have produced at least one little chickpea of his own, so that he might be proclaimed as some new Triptolemus.' Hence through Jeremiah, chapter 10, verse 11, the Holy Spirit says: 'The gods who did not make the heavens and the earth, let them perish from the earth and from those things which are under the sky.' 'From creatures,' says Jerome, 'I understood the Creator; and through each thing which you made and which you daily work in the whole world, I was completely astonished and, having lost the sense of my humanity, was converted to a holy madness.'

'In the beginning,' that is, 'from the beginning of time,' as the Lateran Council has it, or 'in the beginning of time,' as Augustine says, or 'at the start of the world it began to be made, when,' as Ambrose says, before creatures there was no time. 'Together with time heaven and earth were created,' says Aquinas. 'But there could be no past time,' writes Augustine, 'because there was no creature by whose changeable motions it might be measured.' Therefore Ambrose said: 'In the beginning, that is, before time.' The beginning of a thing is prior to the thing itself, just as the first movement of the foot is prior to walking: therefore it is better said that the world was created together with time than in time. And not badly, unless I am greatly mistaken, someone said that God created the world 'in his eternity, outside of time' -- not to assert the world to be eternal, but to exclude any prior time. The very act of creation of those things which were created in the beginning filled no interval of time. 'All things,' wrote Gregory of Nyssa, 'came into being in a moment and without any space of time: the beginning does not allow us to think of any interval... As the point is to the line, so the moment is the beginning of time.' And St. Thomas: 'There is nothing of time except the now; whence nothing can come to be except according to some now; not because in that first now there is time, but because from it time begins.'

The Hebrew word bereshith seems to be derived from rosh, 'head'; whence Aquila (the translator who attempted to transfer not only words but also the etymologies of words, as Jerome says of him) rendered that word in this place as en kephalaioi. This word in the Bible constantly denotes that which is first: first in place or in time, as Deuteronomy 33:21, or in place, value, and dignity, as Numbers 24:20, or even in cause, as Proverbs 17:14, in which sense however I know the word was never used by Moses. When it signifies 'first in time,' it sometimes indicates the very beginning of time, as Deuteronomy 11:12, and often firstfruits either of produce -- Exodus 23:19; Leviticus 2:12, 23:10; Numbers 15:21, 18:12 -- or of sons, that is, the firstborn, whom the Hebrews are accustomed to call 'the firstfruits of strength' (reshith on), Genesis 49:3; Deuteronomy 21:17. Of these three meanings, the first, which signifies an order of time, no one fails to see as most fitting and most obvious for this passage. Therefore all the translators we know of (except the Jerusalem Targum, which translates 'in Wisdom') either followed it, as Onkelos, Pseudo-Jonathan, the Samaritan, the Arabic, the Ethiopic, the Persian, and the Vulgate; or can be considered to have followed it, as Aquila, the Syriac (who retained the Hebrew word), and the Septuagint, who translate en archei. Nor can that word be better rendered than with the Vulgate, 'in the beginning.'

Since this is uttered absolutely and indefinitely, it must be said to signify the beginning of all time whatsoever, that is, the moment at which time began its course. 'In the beginning you, Lord, founded the earth; and the heavens are the works of your hands.'

If Rabbi Maimonides rightly taught that Moses' purpose from the very beginning of the narrative was to overthrow the error of the Sabaeans who attributed eternity to the world, he certainly would have strayed from that aim if he had not employed the word bereshith with the intention of signifying the first moment of time. The Holy Fathers quite commonly accept that word in this sense. So Basil, so John Philoponus, so Procopius. I have already quoted the words of Ambrose and Augustine. The latter, Against the Adversaries of the Law and the Prophets, writes: 'So that the beginning may be that from which they began to exist. For they were not always co-eternal with God, but were made: they received a beginning from which they might begin to exist.' Tertullian interprets the first word of Genesis as primordium ('origin'), Book I Against Marcion, chapter 10; and Against Hermogenes, chapter 19, he says, 'the beginning is an inception; and that he found it to be so used for things beginning to be made... Thus beginning or inception is a word of commencement, not the name of some substance.' Cyril of Alexandria teaches that Moses wished to show that heaven and earth do not lack a beginning, and do not possess the same eternity as God, but rather came forth into the light in time. I add that the Fourth Lateran Council, chapter 2, decreed that God from the beginning of time created both creatures from nothing, namely the angelic and the mundane; in which definition, although Genesis is not cited, yet there can scarcely or hardly be any doubt that the Council Fathers had in view its first verse.

XXII. "In the Beginning" as the Word (the Son)

It must be acknowledged that among the ancients there was a very widespread and common opinion that by the term 'beginning' in this passage the Word, that is, the Son, is signified, although John Philoponus excludes this interpretation. Indeed, some affirmed, on the testimony of Jerome, that it was so read in the Hebrew codices, whom it would be futile to refute. Nevertheless, this opinion defends itself with arguments not to be despised, with the number of its authors, and with its antiquity. And first, we must admit that this meaning can underlie that word in this passage. For by the word itself, cause is sometimes signified. Now the Scriptures teach us that the Son of God, the eternal Word, is the cause of all creatures, both when they say that all things were created through him, and when they designate him by the name of 'beginning' -- Colossians 1:16-17; Hebrews 1:2; 2:10 -- and accordingly also call him 'the beginning of God's creation' or 'of creation,' Revelation 3:14, and of the works made by God, Proverbs 8:22. Why then, they ask, could the Son of God, the eternal Word, not be so called at the beginning of Genesis, so that the word bereshith would here have the same force as 'through the Beginning,' that is, 'through the Son, through the Word'? The preposition beth also has this force, as do the Greek en and sometimes the Latin in (which the translators used), not only in the sacred scriptures but also in profane writings; wherefore Theophilus of Antioch wrote concerning the first words of Genesis: 'In the beginning...,' that is, 'through the Beginning.' But if all things were created 'through him' (the Word) 'and in him,' if he himself is 'the beginning of creation,' was this to be passed over in silence in the history of creation? If other biblical writers, when they mentioned creation, did not omit the Word of God, should not Moses, the historian of the Cosmogony, have made mention of him? Especially since Christ himself testifies: 'For he (Moses) wrote about me,' John 5:46; which saying, however, is not necessarily referred to the first words of Genesis. Furthermore, Moses teaches that by God's speaking, or by his Word, the other works were made, by which he arranged the matter he had created into the various forms of things: 'When the Father speaks, the Word which is the Son is spoken,' says Augustine: it therefore seems probable that he taught that matter too was created through the Word.

XXIII. The Temporal Sense Preferred

It must be acknowledged that although the ancients preferred the widespread opinion, not all of them did, and that it was not entirely certain. For Tertullian interprets the first word of Genesis as primordium, or beginning, as we have seen. So also John Philoponus; and Ephrem contends that no other sense can underlie that word than 'in the beginning of time,' and this because of the Jerusalem context (or Targum), which does not agree even among the Hebrews (except among the Karaites) with the received reading. Nearly the same is said by Basil, Procopius, Chrysostom, and Petavius, On the Work of the Six Days, chapter 1, section 3. It should also be noted that Basil refers that passage from John the Evangelist ('in the beginning was the Word') to those words of Moses, not as though 'beginning' and 'Word' are the same thing, but as though John, hearing that 'in the beginning' placed by Moses, was prompted by it to add that, namely, in which all things began to be created -- the Word was. 'But if,' says Basil, 'Moses was discussing only corporeal things' (not mindful of angelic ones), 'then certainly nothing was more fitting than to say, In the beginning, that is, at the origin of time.' Hence in the first words of Moses, mention of the Son is not necessarily included; nor does the Lateran Council, which looking upon the first verse, saw there only the beginning of time, show it to be necessary for the definition of a dogma of faith.

XXIV. The Double Sense of Bereshith

Furthermore, there is the fact that the ancients preferred the widespread opinion which teaches that the Hebrew word bereshith joins a double sense, one signifying the beginning of time, the other signifying the Son or the Word, and that these should not be excluded. For Basil, Procopius, Severian, and Cyril, although they prefer the beginning of time, nevertheless also retain the other signification. Likewise Augustine, although he interprets it as the Word, does not reject the beginning of time, which the Lateran Council indeed approved; and Epiphanius, although he says the Son is signified, in time; and Ambrose, although he explains it as 'before time,' also teaches that it is to be understood as 'in the Son.' Thus these Fathers wished for a double sense, differing in that some considered the temporal signification more important, others the signification of the Son, which makes no difference to the matter.

Let us retain the beginning of time -- which is certainly the safer interpretation, which the Lateran Council recognized, and which better suits the context.

XXV. "God Created" (Bara)

'God created.' The Hebrew verb bara, which the Septuagint renders epoiese and the Vulgate creavit, generally signifies 'he made,' and in some places also 'he formed' or 'he established.' Sometimes it is employed in such a way that it does not necessarily signify production from nothing, as in Genesis 1:21, 27; 5:1-2; Deuteronomy 4:32; Psalm 89:13, 148:5; Isaiah 43:1, 7, 15; Ezekiel 21:30; Amos 4:13; Malachi 2:10. But, as Petavius teaches, Book I of On the Work of the Six Days, chapter 2, 'the more frequent usage is that it is applied principally to creation properly and genuinely so called, that is, to that which is from nothing.' And often Scripture uses this verb where it indubitably signifies creation from nothing.
