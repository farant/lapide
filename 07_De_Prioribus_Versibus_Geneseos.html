ON THE FIRST VERSES OF GENESIS

XXI. On Genesis, "In principio" (In the beginning)

The first book of Sacred Scripture is called by the Greeks Genesis, which means origin or birth, because it begins with the narrative of the origin and birth of both the world and the human race. Some have preferred to interpret Genesis as the origin of the earth; which does not seem admissible, although it is true that Moses here treats more of the birth of our earth than of the origin of all things. The Hebrews call this book by its first word, bereshith, according to the accepted custom by which "they give names to volumes from their beginnings," as Jerome writes (Hebrew Questions on Genesis).

"In the beginning God created heaven and earth": thus Moses begins; for there is nothing that commends God's relations with mankind, man's dependence on God, and God's majesty, omnipotence, and the other attributes connected with the concept of Him, as much as the concept of creation and the sight of the created world. Therefore the creeds also take their beginning from God the Creator: and creation, as it is the first article of faith, so also it is the first historical event. The existence of God, whom we profess to be one, true, and good, Tertullian establishes from this visible universe in Book I Against Marcion, whose fictitious and fabricated God -- whom Marcion introduced as different from the Creator and hitherto unknown -- he removes by this chief objection: "God ought to have been known from the works of some world, man, and age of His own... The God of Marcion ought to have produced at least one little chickpea of his own, so that he might be proclaimed as some new Triptolemus." Hence through Jeremiah, chapter 10, verse 11, the Holy Spirit says: "The gods who did not make the heavens and the earth shall perish from the earth and from those things which are under heaven." "From creatures," says Jerome, "I understood the Creator; and through each thing which You made and which You daily work in the whole world, I was utterly astonished and, having lost the sense of my humanity, I was converted into a holy madness (On Habakkuk III)."

"In the beginning," that is, "from the beginning of time," as the Lateran Council holds, whether "in the beginning of time," as Augustine says, or "in the beginning of the world, it began to be made, when," as Ambrose says, before creatures there was no time. "Heaven and earth were created simultaneously with time," says Aquinas (Part I, Question XLV, article 3, reply to objection 1). "Moreover, there could be no past time," writes Augustine, "because there was no creature by whose changeable motions it might be measured (City of God, book XI, chapter VI)." Therefore Ambrose said: "In the beginning, that is, before time (Hexaemeron, book I)." The beginning of anything is prior to it, just as the first step of the foot is prior to walking: wherefore the world is better said to have been created together with time than in time. And not badly, unless I am greatly mistaken, someone said that God created the world "in His eternity, outside of time" -- not to assert an eternal world, but to exclude prior time. The very act of creation of those things which were created in the beginning filled no interval of time. "All things," wrote Gregory of Nyssa, "existed in a moment and without any space of time: the beginning does not permit us to conceive any interval... As a point is to a line, so a moment is the beginning of time." And St. Thomas: "There is nothing of time except the now; whence nothing can be made except according to some now; not because in that first now there is time, but because from it time begins (cited passage, reply to objection 3)."

The Hebrew word reshith seems to be derived from rosh, head; whence Aquila (the interpreter who attempted to translate not only the words, but also the etymologies of the words, as Jerome says of him) (To Pammachius, epistle 58, otherwise 101, section 11) rendered that word in this place "in summary/in the chief point." This word in the Bible constantly designates what is first: first either in time, as Deut. XXXIII, 21, or in place, price, and dignity, as Num. XXIV, 20, or even in cause, as Prov. XVII, 14, in which sense however I know this word was never used by Moses. When it signifies what is first in time, it sometimes indicates the very beginning of time, as Deut. XI, 12, and often firstfruits either of produce, Exod. XXIII, 19; Lev. II, 12, XXIII, 10; Num. XV, 21, XVIII, 12, or of sons, that is firstborn, whom the Hebrews customarily call reshith on, "firstfruits of strength," Gen. XLIX, 3; Deut. XXI, 17. Of these three meanings, that the first, which signifies the order of time, is most fitting for this place and most obvious, no one fails to see. All interpreters whom we know therefore either followed it (except the Jerusalem Targum, which translates "in Wisdom"), such as Onkelos, Pseudo-Jonathan, the Samaritan, the Arabic, Ethiopian, Persian, and the Vulgate; or can be considered to have followed it, such as Aquila, the Syriac (who retained the Hebrew word), and the Septuagint, who translate "in the beginning." Nor can that word be better rendered than with the Vulgate, in principio [in the beginning]. Since this is stated absolutely and indefinitely, it must be said to signify the beginning of all time, or the moment at which time began its course (See Fr. Xav. Patrizi, On the Interpretation of Sacred Scripture, book II, Question I, articles 1 and 3). "In the beginning You, Lord, founded the earth; and the heavens are the works of Your hands (Psalm 101:26)."

If Rabbi Maimonides (Guide for the Perplexed) taught rightly, that this was Moses' purpose from the very beginning of the narrative -- to overthrow the error of the Sabaeans who attributed eternity to the world -- then he would certainly have strayed from that aim if he had not used the word bereshith with the intention of signifying the first moment of time. The Holy Fathers quite commonly take that word in this sense. So Basil, so John Philoponus, so Procopius (Basil, Hexaemeron, Homily 1; Philoponus, On the Creation of the World, book I, chapter 3; Procopius, on Genesis). I have already recited the words of Ambrose and Augustine. The latter, in Against the Adversaries of the Law and the Prophets, writes: "So that the beginning is that from which they began to exist. For they were not always coeternal with God, but were made: they received a beginning from which they began to exist." Tertullian interprets the first word of Genesis as "from the very start," in Book 1 Against Marcion, chapter 10; and in Against Hermogenes, chapter 19, he says, "the beginning is an inception; and we have found it is so used when things begin to be made... Thus beginning or inception is a word of commencement, not the name of some substance." Cyril of Alexandria teaches that Moses wished to show that heaven and earth do not lack a beginning, nor do they possess the same eternity as God, but that in time they rather came forth into light (Book II Against Julian). I add that the Fourth Lateran Council, chapter 2 (chapter 1, Firmiter), decreed that God from the beginning of time created both creatures out of nothing, namely the angelic and the mundane: in which definition, although Genesis is not cited, yet there can be scarcely or not even scarcely any doubt that the Council Fathers had their eyes fixed on its first verse.

XXII. "In principio" as the divine Word/Son

It must be admitted that among the ancients, a very widespread and quite common opinion held that by the name "beginning" in this place, the Word is signified, that is, the Son, although John Philoponus excludes this interpretation (Book I, On the Creation of the World, chapter 1). Indeed, some affirmed, on the testimony of Jerome (Hebrew Questions), that it was so read in the Hebrew codices, whom it would be vain to refute. Yet this opinion defends itself with arguments not to be despised, with the number of authorities, and with antiquity. And first, we must admit that this meaning can underlie the word in this place. For by the word itself, a cause is sometimes signified. And that the Son of God, the eternal Word, is the cause of all creatures, the Scriptures teach us, whether when they say that all things were created through Him (Wisdom 9:1; John 1:3; 1 Cor. 8:6; Ephesians 2:10; Colossians 1:16-17; Hebrews 1:2; 2:10); or when they designate Him by the name of beginning, Colossians 1:16-17; Hebrews 1:2; 2:10; and thus also "the beginning of God's creation" or "of creation," Apocalypse 3:14, and of the works made by God, Proverbs 8:22. Why then, they say, could the Son of God, the eternal Word, not be so called at the beginning of Genesis, so that the word bereshith here means the same as "through the beginning," that is, "through the Son, through the Word"? The preposition beth has this force also, as does the Greek en, and sometimes the Latin in (which the interpreters used), not only in the sacred Scriptures but also in profane writings; wherefore Theophilus of Antioch wrote of the first words of Genesis: "In the beginning...," that is, "through the beginning" (To Autolycus, book II, section 18). But if all things were created through Him (the Word) "and in Him," if He Himself is "the beginning of creation," was this to be left unsaid in the history of creation? If other writers of the Bible, where they commemorated creation, did not omit the Word of God, ought not Moses, the historian of the Cosmogony, to have remembered Him? Especially since Christ Himself testified: "For he (Moses) wrote about me," John 5:46; though that statement is not necessarily referred to the first words of Genesis. Moreover, Moses teaches that by God's speaking, that is by His Word, the remaining works were made, by which He arranged the matter He had created into the various forms of things: "When the Father speaks, the Word which is the Son is uttered," says Augustine (On Genesis Literally Interpreted, I, 5; Confessions XII, 19, 20, 28): it therefore seems probable that Moses taught that matter too was created through the Word.

That the Word was meant by "in the beginning" as referring to the Son of God was taught by, besides Origen (Homily 1 on Genesis, section 1), Theophilus of Antioch (To Autolycus, book I, section 3; book II, section 10), Clement of Alexandria (Stromata, book VII, section 7), Marius Victorinus the African (On the Generation of the Divine Word, 127), Evagrius (Consultation of Zacchaeus the Christian, II, chapter 3), Basil, Augustine (Homily 1), Ambrose (Hexaemeron, book I, chapter 9), as well as Bede, Eucherius, and others, whom neither Tertullian nor Hilary oppose, indeed not even Jerome, who concedes that those words can be taken in this sense (Questions on Genesis 1:1).

I do not dare to deny the probability of an opinion fortified by so many and such great supports (Patrizi, at the place cited, Question II). But that first, more obvious, and more common interpretation must by all means be retained. The truly literal sense of that phrase "in the beginning" here seems to me to be that which the Hebrews also easily understood, and which excludes both an eternal world and the creation of visible things by created spirits, namely: in the beginning of time, or before all things. Nor do I see how, with this obvious explanation set aside, the Hebrews could have ascended to the arduous knowledge of the divine Word -- they who, as we heard from Chrysostom in section 20, "could not imagine anything intelligible." This second meaning, concerning creation through the Word, I consider to be secondary, though nobler and spiritual, which was reserved to be "revealed" afterward in the course of time, when namely "the fullness of time had come."

Indeed, many of the Fathers who favor this interpretation also admit the former one, namely Basil, Ambrose, Augustine, and Tertullian.

St. Thomas, Question XLVI, article 6, explains that "in the beginning" means "in the beginning, namely, of time," and also -- which is nearly the same thing -- before all things, and also "in the beginning, that is, in the Son," to exclude the error of those who posited "two principles of creation, one of good things, another of evil things." "For just as the efficient principle is appropriated to the Father on account of His power, so the exemplary principle is appropriated to the Son on account of His wisdom; so that, just as it is said 'You made all things in wisdom' (Psalm 103), so it should be understood that God made all things in the beginning, that is, in the Son, according to what the Apostle says to the Colossians, chapter 1: In Him, namely the Son, all things were created." Albertus Magnus also defends both expositions (Summa, part II, treatise II, Question XLII).

XXIII. "Bara" (Created) — Hebrew lexicography

Created -- bara. This word in Moses signifies true creation (out of nothing, out of non-being) here and in verses 3 and 4 of chapter II; and sometimes something new and extraordinary brought about by God, as in Numbers 16:30, where Moses threatens "a new thing to be done by the Lord, that the earth opening its mouth should swallow them up," namely Dathan and Abiram with their followers.

About this word, and why it is used in verses 21 and 27 of this chapter, we shall speak below. Kocher, a man learned in Hebrew, writes: "The word bara is customarily applied to those things which by the Omnipotence of God come into existence as if from nothing in a marvelous way (Vindication of the Sacred Hebrew Text of Isaiah, at 48:7). David Kimchi says (Hebrew Roots) that bara is the passage from nothing to existence." Maimonides teaches the same (Guide for the Perplexed, book II, chapter 3). Let us hear Deyling (Sacred Observations, part I, observation 3, section 3): "The induction of examples, to which we must entirely defer in this part of the doctrine, teaches that bara is a word dedicated to God alone, and which is applied only to divine operations. It seems to me that Jacob Gousset hit upon the matter in his Commentary on the Hebrew Language under the word bara, when he made this order of words: so that asah is a certain genus, pa'al a subaltern species denoting the actions of rational agents, and bara a most specific species restricted to the actions of God. Therefore, just as pa'al is not attributed to brute causes, because it involves a property incommunicable to brutes, namely the power of reason; so bara is not communicated to creatures, because it involves an attribute incommunicable to creatures, namely infinity. Wherefore, to operate through infinity, or through infinite power, is what bara is judged to mean, and it is rightly explained as producing a thing out of nothing, or out of matter that is unprepared and unsuitable, for both of which the infinity of supernaturally operating power is required."

But, what is most important in this place, the word bara joined with bereshith can signify nothing other than creation out of nothing. Bereshith is never used as an adverb by the Hebrews, whence it should not be explained as: first He created, that is, the first action of God was the creation of heaven and earth. Nor can it be understood as meaning first, that is, the first thing created by God; for the preposition beth (in) stands in the way. Therefore the proper meaning of that word in this place is the one expressed by the Vulgate translator, rendering it in principio [in the beginning], that is, in the beginning of time, He created. But how could He have made or created in the beginning of time, when there was no creature, unless out of nothing, that is, out of non-existing things? If matter already existed, then surely there was neither a beginning of things nor of time.

Moreover, these words, "In the beginning God created heaven and earth," either signify nothing, or the production from nothing of universal matter, or its formation. But they certainly do not signify the formation; for after this creation, the earth is amorphos [formless], empty, void, and dark, and the earth properly speaking, that is, dry land, does not appear until the third day; nor is the first heaven, or "the firmament dividing between the waters and the waters," established before the second day; nor do the stars appear in the sky except on the fourth day. It remains therefore that Moses signified by those words that universal matter was produced from nothing by God. See Patrizi, Question I, articles 2, 3, 4.

This appears more clearly if we carefully consider verse 3 of chapter II, where we read: In it [the seventh day] He had rested from all His work, which God created [bara] to make [la'asot], where the difference between creating, that is out of non-existing things, and making or forming, clearly seems to reveal itself.

XXIV. Jewish and Christian testimony to creation ex nihilo

That the Jews understood the words of Moses in the sense we defend, and therefore still understand them so now, we easily perceive; since this opinion about the temporal creation of things has always been common among them (Brucker, Critical History of Philosophy, Period II, Part I, Book II, Chapter III, Section 1, On the Exoteric Philosophy of the Jews, section 2, numbers 31-34), of which there are abundant witnesses: Josephus in Antiquities, book I, chapter 1; Philo, On the Creation of the World; Eusebius, Preparation for the Gospel, book VII; and Moses Maimonides, who cites as the first of the thirteen articles of the Jewish creed the one in which God is believed to be the author of the world out of nothing. But we have a surer testimony. Solomon openly denies that the universe existed from all eternity, where he affirms that divine Wisdom existed before all the works of God, Proverbs 8:22, in the Hebrew, before the earth, verses 23-26, before the abyss, verse 24, before the fountains and rivers and mountains, verses 25-26, before the heavens, verse 27, that is before "God created heaven and earth," before there was "the empty and void earth with the dark abyss, and the waters."

Likewise Jesus the son of Sirach, attributing a beginning to the works of God, Sirach 16:26. Likewise that "mother, wondrous beyond measure, who watching her seven sons perish, exhorted them, filled with wisdom," and to the last one who still survived, she said: "I ask you, my child, to look at heaven and earth and all that is in them; and to understand that God made them out of nothing, and the human race also," 2 Maccabees 7:20-29. I do not know whether it has occurred to anyone to object that the mother of the seven martyrs spoke in her native tongue, verse 27, and therefore the very words read in that Greek book were not the ones she actually spoke: but that she, like Moses, said only the Hebrew word bara; and the Greek writer rendered it "out of nothing" (out of non-being) he made. But if this is so, we have the writer of a canonical book openly attesting that Moses' bara signifies creation out of non-being. Those who are heterodox and do not place this book among the canonical ones, must nevertheless acknowledge that a pious and learned man of that time, and one most studious of the law, did not in the least doubt that Moses' bara should be understood in this way; that in this sense it had been understood even by women, and therefore that this certain interpretation was accepted by all and unknown to none of the Jews.

Nor do they depart from the doctrine of their nation, but expound higher things, whether Paul, teaching that in Christ Jesus "all things were created in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible; all things through Him and in Him were created," and that He Himself is before all, that is, as the Greek words of his, as the context suggests we should understand them, before all things, Colossians 1:16-17; or whether John, positing "in the beginning the Word" (not matter together with it), through which "all things were made" (and therefore also the matter of heaven and earth), and adding, "and without Him was made nothing," or "without Him was made not even one thing," John 1:1-3. Therefore not even formless matter, or "the empty and void earth," or water, or the abyss.

Following these, the Catholic Church has always professed that Moses spoke of a true and non-eternal creation, and has always rejected a world without a Creator or without a beginning. The Fathers of the Church, beginning with Hermas, a writer of apostolic times, Shepherd, Book I, Vision 1, constantly and unanimously teach creation from non-being, that is, from nothing. The Lateran Council already praised thus professes: "We firmly believe that there is one sole true God... Creator of all things visible and invisible, spiritual and corporeal, who by His omnipotent power simultaneously from the beginning of time created both creatures out of nothing, the spiritual and the corporeal," etc. Where the Council intends to reject not only the eternity of creatures, but also, as St. Thomas teaches in his Opuscule on this Decretal, the error of Origen "who posited that God in the beginning created only spiritual creatures and afterward, when some of them sinned, created bodies to which, as if by certain chains, the spiritual substances would be bound, as if corporeal creatures had not been produced from the principal intention of God, because it was good that they should exist, but only for punishment."

Let us conclude: the idea or concept of creation, according to the mind of the Church and the Fathers, consists in this: that it is the production of a thing out of nothing or out of non-existing things; that before creatures existing in time, there was nothing else but God, and in God the idea of all things. Hence what St. Anselm teaches is true (In the Monologion): "Those things which were made from nothing were not nothing before they were made, as far as the concept of the maker is concerned. For indeed, I seem to see something which compels us not to carelessly distinguish in what respect those things which were made, before they were made, can be said to have been nothing. For in no way can anything be reasonably made by anyone, unless in the maker's concept there precedes something of the thing to be made, as an exemplar or, as is more aptly said, a form or likeness or rule. It is therefore clear that, before all things were made, there was in the concept of the supreme nature what or of what quality or when they would be: wherefore since it is clear that those things which were made were nothing before they were made, inasmuch as they were not what they now are, nor was there anything from which they might be made; yet they were not nothing as far as the concept of the maker is concerned, through which and according to which they were to be made."

XXV. "Elohim" (God) — the plural name

God -- Elohim: this is one of the Hebrew names of God, which, derived from El, "strong," particularly displays the power of God the Creator. This name has a plural termination: yet it cannot be taken except in the singular, when, as in this place, it is joined with a singular verb (created) and applied to the true God, whose unity is everywhere declared or presupposed in the Scriptures (Even where Scripture asserts the unity of God, it uses this word: Deuteronomy 4:33, 39; 6:4, and elsewhere). Voltaire interprets these words satirically: "The Gods made, or the Gods made [singular verb] heaven and earth." Guenee replies to him that in the Hebrew language certain names, plural in termination, must be taken as singulars, especially when the context of the sentence or the conjunction with a singular verb or adjective shows that the speech is about one person. Thus he says that although Mizraim, Ephraim, etc., end in the plural, they must be understood in the singular when the subject is a son of Ham or a grandson of Jacob. Adonim is plural: but when the sons of Jacob thus address their brother Joseph (Genesis 42:10), it cannot be translated "lords," but singularly, "lord." So it is with the word Elohim in our matter. Something similar exists in our languages, in which the plural "you" often indicates a single person and is a mark of respect. Therefore, Guenee adds, perhaps the Hebrews had become accustomed to inflecting in the plural those words that indicated power, force, or dignity, such as Elohim and Adonim. It is certain that Elohim in Hebrew, when attributed to God, is always construed with a singular verb: but when it is used of Angels or Princes, it is joined with a plural verb. Moreover, the Hebrews never understood a plurality of gods to be signified under this name, even though they were inclined to idolatry.

Certain teachers believed that the plurality of the divine Persons was indicated by this conjunction of the singular verb "created" with the plural Elohim, so that in the singular verb the unity of the divine Essence, acting by a single act externally in each work, would be signified, and in the name with its plural termination the plurality of Persons. At the very least it must be admitted that, to those to whom this mystery has become known through revelation, that construction appears more fitting.

XXVI. "Heaven and earth" — shamayim and eretz

Heaven and earth. The first word, shamayim, can also be translated "heavens": the termination is plural, but they never employ it with a singular termination. "By the name of heaven and earth every creature is signified which God made and established." So Augustine (On Genesis Against the Manichaeans, I, chapter 5): many others understand all spiritual and corporeal creatures. Let us set aside the spiritual ones for now: I do not doubt that at least the entire matter of the universe is comprehended by these words. In Exodus 31:17, heaven and earth as works of creation certainly comprehend all things. "In six days the Lord made heaven and earth, and on the seventh He rested from His work." Moses certainly excluded no creature when he began his canticle thus: "Hear, O heavens, what I speak; let the earth hear the words of my mouth," Deuteronomy 32:1. Elsewhere also the sacred writers signify the totality of things by the name of heaven and earth. We read in Isaiah 1:2: "Hear, O heavens, and give ear, O earth, for the Lord has spoken. I have nourished and brought up children, and they have despised me;" and in Jeremiah 23:24: "Shall a man be hidden in secret places, and I not see him, says the Lord? Do I not fill heaven and earth, says the Lord?" Paul seems to equate heaven and earth with the world and all things that are in it. For he thus addresses the Athenians, Acts 17:24: "God who made the world and all things that are in it, He being Lord of heaven and earth, does not dwell in temples made by hands." Profane writers too sometimes took heaven and earth for the totality of things, as Homer and Plato. By the author of the book On the World to Alexander, the world is defined: "The world is a certain assemblage of heaven and earth, and of those natures which are contained within them." According to Maimonides, the true interpretation of this verse is: "In the beginning God created the things above and the things below," which is to say all things, whether those in the heavens or those on the earth. Nor should anyone condemn this distribution of things on the grounds that the earth is the smallest part of the world. For not only was this distribution most suitable for Moses, since he wished to speak no less about the earth -- indeed much more about it -- than about the sky and stars, but it was almost necessary; for, as Christian Wolff (Natural Theology, part I, chapter 1, section 68) and Mazzocchi (Biblical Gleanings, volume I, page 14) observed, since the older Hebrew language lacked a word meaning "world," they had to resort to this periphrasis, which the Church also uses now in its creeds. Moreover, this distribution is very obvious and conformable to the nature of earth-dwellers: for there are two things that strike our senses -- the sky that hangs above us, and the earth we inhabit and which lies beneath us. Everyone, led by nature, divides bodies into those that are higher and remote, which we can reach only by sight, and those that are lower and nearer, which are accessible to our other senses as well. Aristotle (Meteorology, I) considers the world as distributed into two parts, namely the sublunary world and the one above. Alexander von Humboldt (Cosmos) acknowledges that the science of created things, as it pertains to humans, has two distinct parts, namely our earth and the celestial spaces, and that every human conception of nature, not including the most recent, has always separated heaven from earth as two regions, the upper and the lower, to have been separated. Let us conclude: by these words (heaven and earth), all substance, at least material substance, is contained.

Observe that those who think that bara here does not mean to create out of non-being, but to form, must necessarily say that in the beginning God formed all things. Now then, if He accomplished this absolutely in the beginning, what did He do afterward? What, for example, on the fifth or sixth day, which certainly came after the beginning? Will they say that "in the beginning" comprehends the entire span of six days? But this they could only say absurdly, at least after it has been proved that in those days long periods and a series of ages lie hidden.

Some think, among them Petavius (Book I, chapter 2, section 10), that those words, "In the beginning God created heaven and earth," do not contain some particular work which God wrought at the start before the six days, but are a general statement by which Moses encompassed all things that were made by God, and which he afterward described part by part and individually, as each thing was elaborated. Cyril of Alexandria had embraced this opinion (Book II Against Julian), and Augustine also mentions it (Book I On Genesis Literally Interpreted, chapter 3; and Confessions XII, 17). Indeed elsewhere (Book I Against the Adversaries of the Law) he cites it as not improbable. St. Thomas (Part I, Question LXVIII, article 1, reply to objection 1) attributes the same to Chrysostom: but the latter does not appear to Petavius himself to have actually taught this. Basil also mentions this opinion (Homily 3), but dissents from it.

Many others must disagree, and, if I see rightly, with just cause. For afterward the creation of matter is not narrated but presupposed. Immediately after those words we find "the earth empty and void, the abyss and water," which however they may be explained, are corporeal creatures; and afterward we see matter already created, for from it various bodies and even the body of Adam are fashioned. That the matter of the stars was created on the fourth day is not probable; and Petavius himself (Book I, chapter 10, section 10, and chapter 15, section 1) holds that it was created from the beginning. Add that those who think this way supposed that "in the beginning" comprehends the entire interval of six days, which, I think, they would not do if they were alive now and knew the current state of natural science. Moreover, we have proved that Moses in the first verse teaches the true creation of things from nothing: in the rest of the chapter he describes the formation of things from already-created matter; therefore he does not teach the same thing in verse 1 generally, and in the following verses more precisely and in detail; but he teaches different things, namely first the creation of non-existing matter, afterward the formation of material things from already-existing matter -- "He created in order to make," chapter II, verse 3: first he teaches that God "created in the beginning," afterward that He made things in time. It should be noted that those with whom we now dispute hold that the creation of things from nothing has been revealed as doctrine. Shall we then say that Moses is not to be understood thus: "In the beginning God created heaven and earth: and this, as soon as it was created, was formless," etc.; but rather thus: "In the beginning God created heaven and earth; and behold how God did this: the earth was formless and void," etc.? For why does he mention the earth here, if its creation is still to be narrated? And when was the earth formless and void? Before it was created? This opinion can perhaps be defended less absurdly only by one who denies that Moses speaks of true creation; which would be contrary both to his words and to the interpretation and doctrine of the holy Fathers and the Church. Is God not the author of the world, except as a sculptor is of a statue? And yet who would say: the sculptor thus made the statue of Apollo; the statue of Apollo was rough, etc.; and not rather: the marble was rough, etc.? "Once I was a trunk," says that statue of Priapus in Horace, Satires VII.

It must be said, therefore, that God at the beginning of the world, in the beginning of time, created all matter simultaneously, from which individual bodies were afterward to be formed. "First things were made, afterward they are declared to have been arranged," says Ambrose (Book I of the Hexaemeron, chapter 7); and Gregory (Moralia, I): "The substance of things was created simultaneously, but their specific form was not simultaneously formed; and what existed simultaneously through the substance of matter did not simultaneously appear through the specific form."

And that rich wisdom might make this world,
It generated all things at once; but after these seeds of things
It adorned the formless with superimposed forms.
(MARIUS VICTOR, Book I of the Poem on Genesis)

Solomon Ben-Melech, along with other learned Jews, thinks those first words should be interpreted: "In the beginning God created the essence of heaven and the essence of earth." The Syriac version and St. Ephrem the Syrian seem to agree with this (On Genesis I).

The simultaneous creation of matter would sufficiently explain that passage of Sirach: "He who lives forever, created all things together," 18:1. But Petavius says that nothing else is signified there, except that God created all things together, that is, equally and on the same footing, so that nothing can be claimed to have arisen and been fashioned from elsewhere (Petavius, On the Work of Creation, book I, chapter 5, section 4; On the Angels, book I, chapter 15, sections 11-12).

As a crowning observation on the first verse of Genesis, it is fitting to present the argument of Tertullian against Hermogenes (chapters 20, 21, 22). Moses narrates the worker, namely God, and the works, that is, heaven and earth, and is silent about the material from which God made these things. "If the material is not mentioned where both the works and the worker are mentioned, it is clear that He worked from nothing: for it would equally have been mentioned from what He worked, if He had worked from something... Since here, in John 1, both the maker is revealed, that is God, and the things made, that is all things, and through whom, that is, the Word (Verbum) -- would not the proper order have required declaring whence all things were made by God through the Word, if they had been made from something? Thus what did not exist, Scripture could not declare, and by not declaring it, sufficiently proved that it did not exist, because it would have declared it if it had existed." He adds, "There was not so great a necessity to signify openly that all things were made from nothing, as there would have been if they had been made from matter. For what is made from nothing, by the very fact that it is not shown to have been made from something, is manifested to have been made from nothing; and it is not in danger of being thought made from something, when it is not demonstrated from what it was made. But what is made from something, unless it is openly declared to have been made from something -- while that from which it was made is not shown -- is in danger first of seeming to have been made from nothing; and then even if it is of such a nature that it cannot seem not to have been made from something, it will equally be in danger of seeming to have been made from something far different than that from which it was made, while it is not set forth whence it was made... So thoroughly did the Holy Spirit establish this principle for His Scripture, that when something is made from something, He reports both what and whence. 'Let the earth bring forth the herb of grass, yielding seed according to its kind, and the fruit-bearing tree...' And again: 'And God said: Let the waters bring forth creeping things of living souls.' If therefore, when God brought forth other things from things already made, He showed through the prophet and declared what came from what -- if the Holy Spirit devoted such care to our instruction, that we might know what came from where -- would He not likewise have informed us about heaven and earth, signifying whence He had wrought them, if their origin consisted of some material? So that all the more they would appear to have been wrought from nothing, since nothing yet existed from which He might appear to have wrought them... I adore the fullness of Scripture, which manifests to me both the Maker and the things made. In the Gospel I find additionally the Minister and Agent of the Maker: the Word. But whether all things were made from some underlying matter, I have nowhere yet read." These and more from Tertullian. And indeed, a diligent and learned commentator on some poem notes whence the poet received what he here took from other historians or poets, but not what, received from no one, he produced from his own resources: when he notes nothing, he sufficiently shows that he believes his poet did not take those things from others, but produced them from his own genius.

XXVII. Creation from nothing demonstrated by reason

That the principal conclusion which we draw from verse 1 of Genesis and from our observations -- namely that the world is not eternal but was created by God from nothing -- is an article of faith, all Catholics agree. But some formerly, while saying this, understood it to mean that this truth, that the world had a beginning, is held by faith alone and cannot be proved demonstratively (See St. Thomas, Part I, Question 46, Art. 2, and Cajetan on this passage). But now, after the matter has been better considered, both the love of truth and the desire to defend religion urge that the very opposite must be said. Nor is it contradictory that some article of faith can be proved demonstratively: for although faith is "the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen" (Hebrews 11), nevertheless God can reveal to human beings a truth that is demonstrable by philosophical arguments, and once it has been revealed, it will itself become an article of faith. He who is ignorant of the demonstration or does not understand it will have faith alone as his argument; but he who knows and understands it ought to be so disposed in mind that he would believe that truth even if no demonstration, no human argument, were forthcoming. Some even among the Scholastics proved by reason that the world had a beginning: on this matter one should read Toletus on Aristotle's Physics (Francisco Toletus, S.J., Commentary on Aristotle's Physics, in book VIII, ch. 1 and book II, text 2, Question I), but above all Gerdil settled the question (Essay on a Demonstration... in the Works of Gerdil, Roman edition, vol. IV, p. 261; On Absolute Infinity, vol. V, p. 1; Essay on Theological Instruction, vol. X, p. 273). That no thing could have been created by God from eternity was the common view of the ancient Christians who treated this subject, and that they frequently and gladly used this argument against the Arians, Petavius asserts and proves (Dogmatic Theology: On God and His Properties, book III, ch. VI). Methodius the Martyr contended against Origen that the world was not created from eternity, nor could it have been, because it is impossible that what is circumscribed by the beginning of its production should, together with that which has no end or limit, likewise lack end and limit. Thus the disputation of Methodius has more bearing on our subject, because when he strips created things of eternity, it is not permissible to interpret him as stripping away only that eternity which is proper to God and is neither participated nor changeable. Methodius must necessarily be understood as speaking of participated and secondary eternity, which Origen attributed to a world created by God, and which Methodius denies to be possible. Zacharias of Mitylene proves at length that eternity belongs to God alone, and that the world cannot participate in it. "It is necessary," he says among other things, "that the maker be more ancient than his work, since what is made is posterior to its maker in both cause and time -- unless perhaps it should be involuntary and of the least consideration, like a shadow to a body. What kind of maker will be a maker, if he was not a willing maker of what he made? Or if, just as a shadow follows a body, the universe simply followed the maker by some chance, existing simultaneously with him?" John Damascene and Anselm in the Monologion, chapter XXVII, reason similarly. The author of the Confutation of Certain Dogmas of Aristotle, found among the works of St. Justin (In the most recent edition of the Works of St. Justin, Jena, 1849, these are found in vol. III, part I, p. 143), argues at length against the eternity of motion and the world; he says among other things: "If whatever comes into being did not exist before it came into being, then time is not eternal and without beginning: therefore neither is motion, whose number is time, eternal and without beginning." You will read more in Petavius from Athanasius, Gregory of Nyssa, Cyril of Alexandria, Maximus the Great, Augustine, Fulgentius, and others.

John Philoponus published a book against the eternity of the world and its champion Proclus. There, says Petavius, he raises those well-worn but very solid objections against Aristotle: that an actual and real infinite is posited if the world exists from eternity, contrary to what Aristotle himself held -- since infinitely many individual things of all kinds would have existed, which arose from an infinite interval; and the infinite could have been traversed and exhausted, since of infinite generations, the last in any given time can be designated. Add that that infinite span of time which has flowed from eternity would be composed of the finite intervals of individual lives -- of human beings, for example: but a finite quantity added to a finite quantity cannot produce something infinite. So far Petavius. To these add St. Bonaventure in book II of the Sentences, Question II, article 1.

If God did not create the universe of things from nothing, then certainly all the matter of the world is eternal. In this hypothesis, motion too must be posited as eternal, unless we imagine entirely arbitrarily that all that matter was at rest for the whole of eternity, and finally, after an eternal rest, received motion from God. In the first hypothesis, it is necessary to posit an infinite number of revolutions -- of the sun, for example, or of the earth -- that is, an infinite generated by a series of motions; which is an evident absurdity. For the series 1, 2, 3, 4, 5... will never arrive at infinity or yield an infinite, but always a number, that is, a sum of units. In number, infinity is sought only absurdly, and is found only in unity. The infinite and number are two mutually contradictory notions, of which one excludes the other, and both cannot stand together. If a number could become infinite through the addition of other numbers, there would be such a number that would become infinite by the addition of one unit; another number would be half of infinity; and infinity itself would be a number (that is, a sum of units) without a determinate number. But also innumerable other absurdities flow from that hypothesis. If the world had no beginning, its years were infinite, and its days infinite too; and the number of years would have had to equal the number of days -- otherwise it would not have been infinite -- which cannot happen unless one day equals one year, that is, unless 1=365. If in thought, ascending through ancient revolutions, we spend many years subtracting years from years and ages from ages, we are always at the beginning, always infinitely distant from eternity: we find the world a thousand ages before us no less ancient than it is now; its "raw and green old age" is indeed the greatest, but never increases. Nor will you escape so many absurdities by positing that those motions of matter were not regular, but as disturbed and irregular as you please. The number of motions, whether irregular or regular, leads us to the same absurdities. Do we wish to suppose (which I believe no one has believed) that all the atoms of matter remained perfectly at rest for the whole of eternity, and then afterwards -- like a sleeping army which, when "the trumpet sounded the terrible taratantara," rises up aroused and hastens to its ranks -- those resting corpuscles, awakened from their eternal slumber, changed their eternal rest into perpetual motion, and whether by chance (which was Epicurus's view) or by God's command and law, constituted the world properly so called? One might first ask why God finally imposed laws on matter which He had left lawless for all eternity. But even in this hypothesis the full force of the preceding argument remains. And indeed, matter and its atoms are by their essence inert and mobile, that is, apt to receive motion: therefore, if they always existed, they always could have been moved. Therefore in this hypothesis an eternal motion or infinite series of motions would necessarily be possible, which we have demonstrated to be impossible.

It should be noted that the same reasoning holds for rational creatures. For such a creature is of a changeable nature and mobile with spiritual motion; it is moved by various thoughts and desires; and although it is not always necessarily moved, but can remain unmoved for some time in one thought or feeling -- as for instance in admiration of something -- yet it is always necessarily capable of being moved. Given, then, such an eternal creature, an absurd infinite series of thoughts or of some other spiritual motion would either actually exist, or would necessarily be possible.

But it is not surprising if we do not sufficiently understand the creation of things from nothing or from non-existing things. We who do not sufficiently understand so many corporeal effects that are obvious to the senses -- what wonder if we cannot open where the key of the senses is absent? What wonder if we cannot adequately comprehend the works of God's incommunicable power? Between a mystery and an absurdity, however, there can be no doubtful choice for a person who is not insane, especially when we see all things full of mysteries. Yet by an obvious example the matter becomes, unless I am mistaken, somewhat more intelligible. When we, speaking to some other person, generate in his mind a perception of some idea, or some knowledge, or a feeling that was not previously in him (that is, previously it was nothing), then our perception or knowledge or feeling does not depart from us; likewise the feeling, knowledge, or perception of an idea produced in that person's mind through our words, although produced by us and more or less similar to ours, is nevertheless not ours, nor is it a mode of our spirit -- indeed it does not have every resemblance to our knowledge, idea, or feeling; for often the idea will be less clear or less adequate, the knowledge less full, and sometimes the feeling weaker, sometimes more intense. These things, then, are produced by a cause that is as distinct from the effects themselves as are the aerial waves by means of which it produced those effects.

The notion of a thing that is simultaneously created and eternal is contradictory: for eternal is that which never began to exist, and created is that whose existence had a beginning. Therefore the idea of God as Creator supposes the possibility of an impossible thing -- a thing that both had and did not have a beginning of existence. I admit that definition of the created -- namely, that the created is that whose existence had a beginning -- but I admit it because I see that an actually existing infinite series cannot exist, nor even be possible; because I neither can nor wish to admit the possibility of an impossible thing. But those who do not acknowledge this repugnance of an infinite series (such as those who posit an eternal world) -- I do not see how they can suppose that definition as something self-evident. For someone could respond that whatever is produced entirely by another, without the assistance of any other efficient, material, or instrumental cause, is created -- and that this could be eternal; just as a footprint eternally produced would be the trace of an eternal foot standing from eternity on sand or mud (Some, following Scotus, in book II of the Sentences, dist. 1, Question II, held that in the phrase "to create from nothing," the particle "from" signifies antecedence not in duration but in nature, and that some creature could have been created from eternity. More truly, St. Bonaventure, in book II, dist. II, art. 3, Question II, had taught that by that particle "from," what is declared is something congenital and native to creation: that nothingness precedes the creature in duration -- whence it follows that no thing could have been created from eternity). But let us set these things aside.

I therefore reply: the supposition of multiple instants or moments in eternity is contradictory, because the infinite series that would necessarily be possible if moments existed in eternity is contradictory; for these would be infinite in number, and in each of these moments something contingent could happen, or some action or passion of contingent things. Therefore the supposition on which the demonstration rests must be denied, and the foundation on which it was built collapses. As long as contingent things -- that is, creatures -- did not exist, not even time existed, nor were there multiple moments, which flowing time presupposes. These are "mysteries that cannot be understood," of which no one has had or ever will have an idea. What follows from that? Mortals cannot help finding mysteries in their thinking. It belongs to the intelligent person not to recoil from mysteries, but to take care not to fall into absurdities. I confess these things are not sufficiently comprehended by the human mind; yet since we arrive at them by reason itself as our guide, we must necessarily have some idea of them. In whatever system, although eternity is not very intelligible to us and far less comprehensible, yet something eternal must be admitted; and since it has been demonstrated that there is no time in eternity and no successive moments, so indeed without moments we must admit eternity. "Always," if this word intends to foist moments or instants upon us, gains nothing by this change of word. But if "always" of this kind means "from eternity," I say that God always (not from the beginning of eternity, for this expression is contradictory), from eternity, in His eternity, was able to create the world (that is, He had the power to create it), but only together with time; however, He never could nor ever can do absurd things, nor establish an eternal world: for an eternal world implies an actually infinite series, which involves a contradiction.

Even if we were to grant that eternity must be conceived as a time running without beginning and without end (like a straight line without beginning and without end, or like a circle), still it should not be conceded that God could have created an eternal world: for to create a world having a beginning implies nothing contradictory, but to create an eternal world (which would be to create at the beginning of a time without beginning) is an evident contradiction. From the fact that someone can do something non-contradictory, it cannot be deduced, except absurdly, that he can also accomplish what is contradictory. Therefore even in that supposition it would have to be said that God from eternity had the power to establish a temporal world, that is, one having a beginning of existence.

But let us return to the words of Moses.

XXVIII. Genesis 1:2 — "Tohu wabohu" (formless and void)

The earth was tohu wabohu -- "formless and void," as the Vulgate says; or "invisible and unformed," as the Septuagint; "desolate and uncultivated," as the Syriac version; "vanity and nothingness," as Aquila and Theodotion; "inert" or "empty and unformed," as Symmachus; "desolated" or "deserted and empty," as Onkelos. All these interpretations converge on the same meaning. Aben-Ezra considers both words to be substantives meaning the same thing: that in which there is nothing solid or subsisting. That God made the earth "from formless matter," we read in Wisdom 11. The Hebrew words cited appear in Jeremiah chapter 4, verse 23, designating a land laid waste and desolate. "I looked at the earth, and behold it was void and nothing" -- so the Vulgate renders it there. From these we gather that the earth (or whatever Moses indicates there by the name of earth) was at the beginning a formless heap of innumerable corpuscles.

"Before the sea and lands, and that which covers all things, the sky,
One was the face of nature in the whole world,
Which they called chaos, a rough and undigested mass."
(Ovid, Metamorphoses, book I)

But this chaos was not uncreated, not abandoned to blind fate and the fortuitous concourse of atoms, but was governed by the laws of the Creator; and so, pregnant with wonderful order and beauty, that rough and undigested mass existed. Augustine considers "the earth formless and void" to be the same as "heaven and earth" of verse 1, and to comprehend the whole of matter -- which he held should also be said of the abyss and of the water that are mentioned below. It seems probable that the matter of the earth was not then separated from the matter of the stars (or at least of some stars): certainly their separation is recorded only on the second day. Yet since Moses names the earth and it is sufficiently apparent that, as quickly as possible after indicating the creation of the whole world, he turns to the formation of our earth (just as later, having briefly narrated the beginnings of the human race, he is wholly occupied with Abraham and the Israelites), we may believe that he is here speaking of the matter of the earth (which he neither affirms nor denies to have been conjoined with other matter), or of the matter that afterwards became the terrestrial globe. We intend to speak only of this in what follows, although some things to be said in the explanation of these first verses could perhaps be applied to the matter of other celestial bodies as well.

J. G. Rosenmuller (Most Ancient History of the Earth, pp. 19-23) does not accept this chaos. "It is surprising," he says, "that many interpreters could have persuaded themselves that chaos is indicated by these words tohu wabohu... This opinion undoubtedly owes its origin to the fictions of Greek and Latin poets, imported by interpreters into Moses. But to explain Moses from the poets -- what else is that than to pour water drawn from turbid streams into a limpid spring? The Rabbis, who were not preoccupied with fictions regarding this matter and investigated the force and meaning of the words, did much better -- for example, Onkelos: 'desolate and empty'; Jonathan: 'desolate of the sons of men, and empty of all cattle,' with which the Jerusalem Targum agrees. And rightly so." I do not see how that exposition can please anyone which posits the emptiness of the earth only from human beings and from all cattle: for it was empty of every animal and tree and plant, and the earth properly so called -- that is, land free from waters -- did not yet exist. But that Rabbi Abbhu who, according to Maimonides (Guide for the Perplexed, part II, ch. XXX), deduced from Genesis "that God built worlds and destroyed them again," did not shrink from chaos; for that formless and empty earth was for him the ruin of a world destroyed by God. Following not the pagan poets (who seem to have received this from the tradition of their ancestors), but the sacred books, Christian interpreters posited that primeval confusion -- and not only on account of those two words, sufficiently obscure and peculiar to the Hebrew language alone, but both from that passage in Wisdom 11:18 about formless matter, and especially from the context of Moses. For the primeval earth is and is called a dark abyss, verse 2; dry land has not yet risen from the waters, and therefore terrestrial living things cannot exist on it, verse 9; the sun, moon, and stars are absent, verse 14; air, water, and earth are not separated but mixed together, verses 6 and 10: does not this state represent chaos? What wonder if it is so described by interpreters, and sometimes they use the name chaos, as Augustine, and the Christian poet Hilary in his poem to Leo:

"When black chaos covered all things, and the lofty mass
Of the formless world pressed down upon bodies from above,
And there was neither appearance nor form; God was working within."

Ernest Friedrich C. Rosenmuller, son of Johann Georg, teaches that Moses posits at the beginning the matter of heaven and earth created by God, "but crude and formless, which mass, since it had to be fitted as a suitable dwelling for living creatures, first had to have what lay confused and mixed in the crude and undigested mass separated and ordered.... That from the beginning there existed formless matter -- chaos, hyle -- in the mind of the writer, from which all things were subsequently shaped and fashioned, although this is not declared in express words, is nevertheless manifest from the fact that individual things are narrated in what follows as separated and distinguished from each other -- such as light from darkness, waters from waters, ocean from continent. What is designated by the name of heaven and earth in this verse 1 is merely the matter that had to be produced first of all." He adds that the concept of chaos was present in the minds of Theodotion, Onkelos, Aquila, Symmachus, and the Seventy Interpreters... "What Moses says -- that night lay upon that vast abyss of waters -- finds agreement in other ancients as well. Hesiod in the Theogony, verse 123: 'From Chaos were born Erebus and dark Night.' And from Orpheus, Timotheus the Chronographer is cited by Grotius, On the Truth of the Christian Religion, page 33: 'There was Chaos and dark Night concealing all things that are beneath the ether' (Annotations on the Old Testament, vol. I, third edition, Leipzig, pp. 55, 64, 65)."

Without cause, therefore, a certain learned Englishman (Greville Penn, A Comparative Estimate of the Mineral and Mosaical Geology, 2nd edition, V, 1, p. 197, London, 1825) seems to me to be fighting against this chaos.

The chaos, so to speak, of Moses differs from that of the pagans primarily in this: that it was created by God, and therefore is not eternal but posterior to creation. Ovid begins with an uncreated chaos, Metamorphoses I, verses 5-20, and then, in verse 21, introduces God, or a better nature, to order it. Moses too would have done the same, if he had thought the formless earth and the dark abyss to be eternal and not created by God, but merely ordered by Him; nor would he have first posited as made in the beginning what he believed to have been accomplished afterwards.

Both from sacred Scripture and from nature we are taught that, in establishing and perfecting the earth, God willed that there should be a constant progression from the simple to the compound, from the imperfect to the perfect, from the formless and crude to the adorned and polished. We can therefore think that God created only chemically simple substances or elements, which have been called the first principles and building blocks of bodies.

As for compound substances, "which consist of a council of first principles," as Lucretius says, they coalesced from those elements. Many Christian teachers held this view: of these it suffices to mention one -- John Damascene, book II of the Orthodox Faith, chapter V, who teaches that God made heaven and earth and all things in them from nothing; but some things immediately, namely the elements, and others He commanded to be formed from those very elements. It is credible that these elementary substances, when first created, were not joined together in larger masses, but rather separated into the smallest molecules, which are called atoms or monads or primary bodies: for there is no reason to admit physical compounds rather than chemical compounds, especially since the cohesion of physically united molecules resists chemical affinity and hence the formation of mixtures. I believe those atoms should be imagined as dispersed in space, and probably not so close to one another that they could immediately suffer the force of molecular attractions, as they are called, which act at the smallest distances -- otherwise, as will appear below, only for a single moment would that first state of things which is here described by Moses have obtained, and "the darkness over the face of the abyss" would scarcely have been worth mentioning. If it was so, as we think, that elementary matter could have been invisible not only from the absence of light, but also because of the smallness and mutual distance of the atoms.

Someone might conjecture that not even the elements were immediately and originally created by God, and that that first formless earth was nothing other than some primary matter from which the very elements of bodies were assembled. This view seems to have pleased some, and perhaps at some point even Augustine himself. This hypothesis is not entirely absurd, if we are not speaking of that prime matter which lacks all form, quality, and determination, which "is never found to subsist," as Bede (Book II, On First Principles, ch. I) writes, which to posit as existing "implies a contradiction," as St. Thomas teaches, which we cannot even imagine, and which is a mere abstraction of the intellect; but rather we are speaking of a single element of all elements. For it pleases some to suppose a homogeneous matter whose atoms or monads, joining together in different ways and numbers, would form the molecules of elements -- that is, of chemically simple bodies. Thus, they say, if the letters of the alphabet are imagined as formed by physical points close together, and if all the books that exist in a great library were written with letters of this kind, one point would be the true element of the letters, which are called the elements of words, and therefore of all the books with which that library is adorned. This, then, they say, would represent the earth (or, if you prefer, the whole universe), just as the letters represent what are called elements, and the point would exhibit the atom of this truly elementary primary matter. To weigh on the scales this hypothesis -- which is by no means necessary for understanding the sacred text, nor defined by physicists -- is not the task of this place; and I do not know whether it can ever be defined. Something of the darkness over the face of the first abyss will perhaps always remain.

XXIX. "Darkness upon the face of the abyss"

Darkness (was) upon the face of the abyss. The question is asked: first, what is the "abyss"? Second, why "darkness upon the face" of it? The Hebrews call an abyss a great and deep mass of waters, Gen. ch. VII, v. 11, and also the depths of the earth: "From the abysses of the earth you brought me back again," Ps. LXX, v. 20. That the abyss here refers to that formless and incoherent mixture -- or, so to speak, the chaotic fluid -- of which I was just speaking, is not peculiar to Augustine, but is the common opinion. Darkness was upon the abyss; because "there was not yet, as St. Ambrose says, light to illuminate, nor yet the sun: for afterward the luminaries of heaven were made" (De Opif., bk. I, ch. IX). Indeed every body is dark in itself; nor is any matter known to us that produces light -- that is, luminous ethereal vibrations -- without some other physical cause, by itself and its own nature. Therefore not even the matter of the sun is luminous in itself, nor could it shine until the cause -- whatever it may ultimately be -- of that splendor which gives it the dominion of the day, operated sufficiently.

What John Philoponus believes about the primordial darkness -- that it lasted only one moment, light following immediately -- "is mere conjecture, says Petavius (In Hexaem., ch. VII), and is supported by no argument." "I, however, he adds, would think that these first darknesses lasted for a considerable interval; and perhaps more than one or two days, or even longer: the measure of this time is uncertain to us. For divine Wisdom willed to proceed gradually in the creation of things, and to produce at first a rough and imperfect work, so that the Angels who were spectators might be educated step by step toward understanding the wisdom of the supreme Maker, and might arrive gradually through the traces of created things." That darkness is a substance and was made by God was once thought by some, whose simplicity is now laughed at, not refuted. But the far more foolish and truly impious error of the Manichaeans was more than sufficiently refuted by Basil, Ambrose, Augustine, and Titus of Bostra. For those ancient Manichaeans held that darkness was co-eternal with God, an adversary of God Himself, as a second principle of things. So much for the darkness: some difficulty lies in what follows.

The Spirit of God was moving over the waters. So the Vulgate. You ask: first, what are these "waters"? Second, who is this "Spirit of God," and how was he "moving over the waters"? "Water," says Augustine, is here the same as the abyss and "that unseen and formless matter"; and "it was called water, he says, because it lay easy and tractable beneath the one working upon it, so that all things might be formed from it (De Genes. contra Manich., bk. I, ch. V)." St. Thomas (I part., Quaest. LXVIII, art. 3, ad 3) believes "that Moses, on account of the invisibility of air and similar bodies, comprehends all such bodies under the name of water." Petavius (De Opif., bk. I, ch. III; V, ch. IV) says that the word "abyss" signifies water: "The abyss and water are one and the same thing... That water is understood by the name 'abyss' is the opinion of all interpreters." In St. Ephrem we read: "Darkness covering the abyss of waters." And Rabbi Solomon, similarly interpreting the above words: "Darkness was upon the face of the waters that were upon the earth." Gregory of Nyssa (In Hexaem., p. 18) thinks this primeval water was different from ordinary water that flows downward. St. Ephrem (In Genes., ch. I) held that water properly so called did not exist at that first time, and that it was created by God not together with heaven and earth, but afterward. Much more, I think, would Ephrem, Nyssen, Augustine, and other Fathers have thought that water differed from ordinary water, and would have denied it was created in the beginning, if in their time it had been understood that it was to be classified not among the elements but among the compounds. That confused mass of molecules could, it seems, have been called water, because it consisted in no small part of the elements of water, and possessing no cohesion, was entirely fluid, neither colored nor opaque. Mercury, though opaque, is so named -- as if "silver water" or "watery silver" (and so also among the Chinese) -- on account of its perfect fluidity and lack of cohesion. The waters of the sea covering the earth are indicated later: but nowhere is it said that this was so from the beginning.

XXX. "The Spirit of God was moving over the waters"

That the Spirit of God (ruach elohim) is God Himself or His power and efficacy, most assert, both Latins and Greeks; and specifically some say it is the Holy Spirit. So Cyril, bk. II Against Julian the Apostate, Basil, Augustine, and Jerome (Quaest. hebr. in Genes.). Athanasius is also cited for this interpretation. Let us hear Ambrose (Hexaem., bk. I): "The Spirit of God was moving over the waters: although some take this for the air, or some for the breath that we breathe and draw from the vital spirit of this atmosphere (did they have at that time at least a confused notion of the vital spirit or vital air existing in common air, which is now called oxygen?): we, however, agreeing with the opinion of the Saints and the faithful, understand it as the Holy Spirit."

Marius Victor sang:

And the sacred Spirit, hovering over the outstretched waves,
Was animating the nurturing waters, giving the seeds of things.

Hence the Church in the blessing of fonts sings to the Holy Spirit: "You were moving over the waters, about to warm them." Albert the Great (II part., tract. II, Quaest. XLVII) wrote: "According to Damascene and Gregory of Nyssa, that Spirit of the Lord seems to be air and fire... it must be said that according to Jerome and the Hebrew truth, it cannot be understood of a created spirit. For the Gloss says: In the Hebrew it has merachepheth, that is, he was brooding or warming, in the manner of a bird animating eggs with its heat. We understand, therefore, that it is said not of a spirit of the world, as many think, but of the Holy Spirit, because He is the life-giver of all things." St. Thomas (I part., Quaest. LXXIV, art. 3, ad 4) concurs with this. No one should scorn an interpretation fortified by such great authorities. No one will deny that the Spirit of God most fittingly signifies the third Person of the Trinity. With Jerome it may be said: God the Father, to whom omnipotence is attributed, in the beginning -- that is, in the Son, to whom wisdom is attributed and in whom all things were made -- created heaven and earth, which the Holy Spirit, to whom charity is attributed, lovingly cherished, so that He might make fruitful all created things.

But nevertheless I say here too what I was saying a little before on the occasion of the words "in the beginning," section XXII. This interpretation is indeed to be received with reverence, sufficiently established and most worthy of commendation: but it is rather spiritual, unless I am mistaken, than truly literal. It does not seem easy to believe that Moses, who was not committing hidden and abstruse things to the ears of the wise, but "was tempering his words and ideas to the capacity of a rough and dull people," as Petavius says, intended to indicate only the third Person of the Most Holy Trinity by those words, and not also something material, which could more easily and alone be understood in some way by the multitude of the Jews, and which would not be too far removed from the other things of which he had been speaking -- heaven, earth, the abyss, water, darkness, and light. This statement, "And the spirit of God moving over the face of the waters," seems to correspond in some way to the preceding one, "And darkness upon the face of the abyss": to darkness, that is, to the privation of corporeal light, something should correspond -- either similar to it, or analogous, or contrary, or at any rate something physical; which nevertheless could have been a figure of a most sublime reality, one that would be impenetrable to an unlearned populace. In ch. VIII, v. 1, it is said that after the flood God "brought a spirit over the earth, and the waters were diminished." The Targum of Jonathan and the Jerusalem Targum explain it as "a wind of mercies." Ambrose believes it was not wind, but the divine Spirit or the Holy Spirit. So also Theodoret. Yet the common opinion is that this Spirit was a wind. And this literal sense must be maintained, although the spiritual one may also be admitted. "After the truth of history, things are to be taken spiritually," says Jerome (Prologue to the Commentary on Isaiah).

But what will be the sense of our "Spirit of God" -- not so sublime, but more literal? We have heard that some believed this Spirit of God to be a thin, airy, or ethereal substance. Basil reports this interpretation, and Severian of Gabala (Homily I, On the Creation of the World) follows it: for he says one should certainly have mentioned the air; so Ps. CXLVII: "He will blow his spirit." Procopius believes that either God or air is indicated by that spirit. Standing for the interpretation of air are: St. Ephrem the Syrian, Theodoret (Question 8 on Genesis), Diodorus of Tarsus, and also Moses Maimonides. Nor does Augustine disagree, as we shall see below. Tertullian seems to approve this opinion (Against Hermogenes, ch. XXXII). Some interpret it not simply as air, but as wind, which is excellently defined by Augustine: "Wind is air that is moved and, as it were, fluctuating." So Aben-Ezra, who thinks it is called the Spirit of God because it was sent by the Lord to dry the earth -- which, however, is said ineptly, for the water receded and dry land appeared not before the third day. The Samaritan version has: the spirit of God blowing upon the face of the water; the Arabic version: the winds of God were blowing; and the Persian: the wind of God was blowing. The Targum of Onkelos also agrees. The Targum of pseudo-Jonathan, as well as the Jerusalem Targum, although they seem to explain this passage mystically, nevertheless favor this interpretation: for they write, a wind of mercy from the presence of God was blowing. The word "of God" or "of the Lord," as is well known, is added by the Hebrews to things that are greater or more powerful, or more excellent in their kind, in which the power of God shines forth more, from whom comes "every good gift and every perfect present." Thus "river of God," Ps. LXIV, 10; "mountain of God or mountains of God," Ps. XXXV, 6, and LXVII, 16; "cedars of God," Ps. LXXIX, 11. Nineveh is called, in the Hebrew text of the book of Jonah, a great city of God, ch. III, v. 3. We also read "an evil Spirit of the Lord," 1 Sam. ch. XVI, vv. 14, 15, 16, 23. "An evil Spirit of the Lord rushed upon Saul," ibid. ch. XVIII, v. 10. "An evil Spirit of the Lord came upon Saul," ch. XIX, v. 9. Nimrod "was a mighty hunter before the Lord," Gen. ch. X, v. 9. The rainbow is called "the bow of God," Gen. ch. IX, vv. 13, 14, and Sir. ch. XLIII, v. 12: "Look upon the rainbow, and bless Him who made it; it is very beautiful in its brightness, it has encircled the heaven with the circle of its glory: the hands of the Most High have opened it." The Psalmist on the wind: "He shall blow His spirit (God's), and the waters shall flow," Ps. CXLVII, v. 7, namely from the melting of hail and snow. And Isaiah: "The grass is dried up and the flower has fallen: because the spirit of the Lord has blown upon it," ch. XL, v. 7. Natural bodies and phenomena can be called "of God" because they proceed from the divine will, not from a created will.

Before we try to prove anything on this matter, the force of the Hebrew word merachepheth must be considered, which seems to be able to be literally explained as "moving"; and which the Seventy render as epephereto (was being carried over), and the Vulgate as ferebatur (was being borne). The verb rachoph is found three times in the Bible: here, Deut. ch. XXXII, v. 11, and Jer. ch. XXIII, 9. At the cited passage in Deuteronomy, Moses says: "As an eagle provoking its young to fly, and hovering over them, rachoph, He (God) spread His wings, and took him (Israel) up." Jeremiah moreover says: "My heart is broken within me; all my bones tremble (rachoph)." And the motion of trembling bones, and of an eagle hovering over its young and urging them to fly, is a gentle motion, not corresponding to a most violent wind, of the kind that some supposed this spirit of God to have been. Moreover, what would be the purpose of mentioning such a wind? What effects would it have? The combinations of homogeneous and heterogeneous molecules -- that is, chemical combinations and crystallizations -- were the first effects of all that were then to be produced, and a violent wind would have been not an aid but an impediment to them. "The Spirit of God," Ps. CXLVII, v. 7, which dissolves snow and hard hail into flowing water, is indeed a wind, but not a violent one; for a violent wind, especially if it blows from the polar region, on the contrary hardens ice ("the winds driving the cold," Virgil): but rather a gentle, warm, and southern wind.

The Syriac version, in place of "was being borne," has "cherishing" [fovens], which the Holy Fathers noted. Let us hear Basil (In Hexaem., oration 2): "I will tell you not my opinion, but that of a Syrian man, who was as far from the wisdom of the world as he was close to the knowledge of true things. He said, then, that the word of the Syrians had this meaning, and that because of its kinship with the Hebrew language, it came closer in some way to the sense of the Scriptures. And so he said this was the meaning of the passage. That word 'was being borne over,' he said, interpreters take for the word 'was cherishing,' and he attributed to the nature of the waters the power of bringing forth life, after the manner of a brooding bird, imparting a certain vital faculty to those things that were being warmed." Similarly Diodorus of Tarsus: "That 'was being borne over' is one word among the Hebrews, but among us (Greeks) it cannot be expressed in one word. For the meaning of the Hebrew expression that corresponds to that word is this: As a bird gently touching its eggs with its feathers warms them and impresses vital heat upon them," so the Spirit was being borne over the waters, cherishing and impressing heat. And Jerome: "In place of what in our codices is written ferebatur, in the Hebrew it has merachepheth, which we may call 'was brooding' or 'was cherishing,' in the likeness of a bird animating eggs with its heat." Ambrose has similar things to say, himself citing the Syriac version, as do Caesarius the brother of Gregory Nazianzen (Dialogue I), and Augustine (On Genesis literally, bk. I, ch. XVIII). Solomon Jarchi also holds this view. In this explanation, the motion must be considered as slight as possible, warming and heating: but a heating motion is not one of translation, but molecular, oscillatory, and imperceptible to the senses.

R. Nahmanides interprets this passage as referring to air, which he considers to be joined with the name Elohim in order to indicate its subtlety, by which it is superior to all other elements. It does not seem, however, that common or atmospheric air is being discussed here: the subject is not the creation which, as regards this air (or more truly as regards its elements), took place when "God created the heaven and the earth"; nor can it be understood that this air had already then formed a sphere around the watery globe, which would have been an atmosphere similar to ours, suitable for dividing between the waters and the waters, which Moses teaches was not made until the second day.

Will the Spirit of God, in the literal sense, be that substance far subtler than common air, most active, penetrating all bodies, endowed with the power of heating and in some way making fruitful, as it seemed to Galileo (In the Letter to Archbishop Dini, in the edition of the works of G. Galilei, Milan 1811, vol. VII, p. 569)? Something of this sort Augustine seems to have suspected when he wrote (On Genesis unfinished, ch. IV): "It can be understood in this way: that by the Spirit of God we understand the vital creature by which this entire visible world and all corporeal things are contained and moved, to which God Almighty granted a certain power of serving Him for operating in those things that are generated; which Spirit, since it is better than every ethereal body, because every invisible creature surpasses every visible creature, is not absurdly called the Spirit of God." Petavius (Bk. I, ch. III) annotates these words: "Therefore he makes this [Spirit] invisible and created -- I think also ethereal, but the most excellent in that kind -- in any case corporeal." Chrysostom subscribes to this interpretation in a certain way, hom. 3 on Genesis. For he judges that what is indicated is "a certain vital force and efficacy, residing upon the waters; so that the water was not standing still, but agitated and possessing vital force." Theophilus of Antioch (To Autolycus, bk. II, XIII) thought this Spirit had some relation to light and heat. "This," he says, "God gave to the creature for the generation of living things, as He gave a soul to man, so that this spirit would incorporate itself into the water, and the water, penetrated by it, would warm the creature, mingling itself with it." The author of a sermon among the works of St. Augustine (Sermon 134 On the times; now removed from Augustine's authorship, because it denies that the Spirit of God is God, which Augustine affirmed; in the Maurist edition it is sermon 157, Appendix vol. V) wrote: "This spirit is a thing of God, which was being borne over the waters; it is the seminal principle of the living condition: whence both the waters and the earth a certain material soul, which, just as the earth would not live unless this spirit of God animated it, whence both the earth brought forth crops and the water produced fish." These things seem rather obscure: but words, which are signs of ideas, cannot be sufficiently clear when the ideas are too obscure and imperfect, as were the notions pertaining to physical matters at that time. I suspect, however, that something true lies hidden in these words, and perhaps the conjecture of Augustine in his Unfinished Work on Genesis comes very close to the truth.

I will say what I think: but I will put forward my opinion with the intent of leaving it to those who can judge both biblical and physical matters to decide whether it should be considered probable or not. Chrysostom, Theophilus of Antioch, and Augustine, I think, if they were alive today, would not greatly disapprove of it.

Most schools of philosophy, both ancient and modern, have generally recognized the existence of a certain substance -- corporeal indeed, but far subtler than coarse, heavy, and ordinary matter, and not a little different from it -- which, being, so to speak, less material, is more worthy of the name "spirit." This has always been believed to exercise a great influence in physical phenomena. Now under the name of ether, now of fire, sometimes of subtle matter, or even of light, heat, or electric fluid, it always returns to the stage, and those who wish to admit only ponderable matter cannot easily expel it from the nature of things; for, led by nature, it keeps coming back. Without it, how, for example, are the effects of light to be understood? Modern scientists call this substance ether -- a word the Greeks used, and which Lucretius and Cicero, borrowing it from them, granted Roman citizenship. Ovid describes it thus (Metamorphoses, bk. I, v. 67):

The liquid ether, free from weight,
Having nothing of earthly dregs.

In our age, all optical effects -- luminous, calorific, and chemical -- of the rays of the sun or of other bodies, are usually traced back to its vibrations. It is also probable that it does not differ from the electric fluid, which not only manifests itself to all in the region of the clouds as the spirit of storms (Canon Angelo Bellani, speaking of the electric fluid, says: "This fluid, so active... I am led to regard it as the spirit of storms, of which the sacred Scriptures speak." Journal of Physics, Pavia, 1817), but is found in every body, and seems to have the greatest attraction with ponderable matter, and to combine more or less with molecules of various natures, bestowing on them those electro-chemical properties by which various bodies, variously constituted, reveal themselves.

To explain the phenomena of the world without this ether, I consider to be nearly the same as trying to explain the phenomena of man without admitting the mind. Indeed, this ether seems to be, as it were, the spirit of the corporeal world:

A spirit within nourishes, and, infused through the limbs,
Sets this whole mass in motion and mingles with the great body.
(Aeneid VI)

God certainly, when He created heaven and earth, did not omit this ether, by whose agency He was going to give heat and light to bodies that are in themselves dark and cold. He created it together with the ponderable molecules, and between these and it He co-created the law of attraction. By the force of this attraction, the ether joined itself to those monads or molecules, and from this conjunction they received those properties that chemists now observe in the atoms of simple bodies. Hence that molecular agitation had to arise which constitutes the heat of bodies -- a heat which, although it had not reached the degree that would make those corpuscles ignited and luminous, was nonetheless such as to produce certain chemical combinations among them, as soon as those of heterogeneous nature came into contact. Without this vivifying principle, in that immense mass of molecules, there would probably have been no chemical affinity, or this force would have been inert and, as it were, dead from the lack of heat. Perhaps not even the effects of universal gravitation could have followed, according to the hypothesis of the distinguished Mossotti (On the Forces Governing the Internal Constitution of Bodies, Turin, 1836; Lecture delivered at the opening of the course of Mathematical Physics at the University of Pisa, 1847), who holds that the law of gravitation must be derived from the attraction between ponderable molecules and the electric ether.

Therefore, when I hear of "the Spirit of God moving over the waters," or brooding over the waters, cherishing and making them fruitful, I can understand this as the ether -- corporeal indeed, but most excellent in its fineness and power -- by which "all corporeal things are contained and moved," which from the very moment of creation was moving over that immense mass of molecules and pervading it, which, brooding over the mingled seeds of bodies (in some way like "a bird animating eggs with its heat"), was cherishing and making them fruitful, proximately preparing the elements for the generation of compounds, and remotely for the generation of plants and for sustaining the life of animals. These words -- "to brood, to cherish, to make fruitful" -- just as they most fittingly suit the Holy Spirit in the spiritual sense, so in the more literal and proper sense they are congruent with that ether, which certainly, if ever anything was, was worthy to stand as a figure and type of the divine vivifying Spirit. The Jews indeed, when they read these words of Moses, did not have in their minds a sufficiently adequate idea of this ethereal spirit; but they too perceived with their eyes and senses illuminating and heating rays: and so they too could in some way understand a certain subtle substance moving, heating, or making fruitful those primeval waters.

Nor should it seem base to us that we seek the understanding of God's words through sensible creatures. For just as when we read through some learned volume, we are not instructed by the characters drawn in ink, but by the writer who employed them to reveal his thoughts: so when we learn something from this great book of nature, we are taught by its author, God. Most wisely St. Thomas wrote (III part., Quaest. XII, art. 5, ad 2): "Just as spoken words formed by a man are signs of his intellectual knowledge, so creatures made by God are signs of His wisdom. Hence it is said in Sirach 1 that God poured out His wisdom upon all His works. Just as, therefore, it is worthier to be taught by God than by man, so it is worthier to receive knowledge through sensible creatures than through human teaching."

It has been the opinion of some (In the apocryphal book 4 Esdras, ch. VI, 38, we read: "O Lord, speaking you spoke from the beginning of creation, on the first day saying: Let there be heaven and earth; and your word was a perfect work") that the first creation of heaven and earth, or at least the state of things described in verse 2, pertains to the first day on which light afterward appeared. But this day, others observe, seems more probably to begin from the divine command, "And God said: Let there be light," no differently than the five later days begin at God's command. Moreover, since "day" is "light," v. 5, it does not seem that a part of the day should be placed before the first light. Certainly the first creation preceded every day. Nevertheless, it should be noted that it scarcely matters, if those days are periods of indeterminate time, whether the first period is made longer by adding to it the time preceding the primordial darkness, or on the contrary is made shorter, and another dark period prior to every day is placed before it.

But let what has been said about the first state of things suffice: let us proceed to the first day.
