ON THE WORK OF THE FIRST DAY

(Verses 3, 4, 5)

Nor should it seem base to us that we seek understanding of the words of God through sensible creatures. For just as when we read through some learned volume, we are not instructed by the characters drawn in ink, but by the writer who employed them to make his meanings known: so when we learn something from this great book of nature, we are taught by its author, God. Most wisely St. Thomas wrote: "Just as words formed by a man are signs of his intellectual knowledge, so creatures fashioned by God are signs of His wisdom. Whence it is said in Ecclesiasticus 1 that God poured out His wisdom upon all His works. Just as, therefore, it is more worthy to be taught by God than by a man, so it is more worthy to receive knowledge through sensible creatures than through human teaching." (Footnote: III part., Quest. XII, art. 5, ad 2.)

But enough now about the first state of things: let us proceed to the first day.

XXXI. God's Speech: "Let there be light"

"God said: Let there be light, and there was light," verse 3. Here for the first time the legislator of the Hebrews introduces the Legislator of nature speaking and commanding. Theodore of Mopsuestia, as cited by Philoponus, thought that God used an actual voice, which Philoponus ridicules and judges absurd. Nevertheless Basil of Seleucia agrees with Theodore, as does Theodoret, who asserts that this was done by God "for the sake of the Angels and the invisible powers, so that they would know that at His command those things which do not exist come into being." Those who believed the angels to be corporeal -- which was the opinion of many of the ancients -- could think not unreasonably that the Creator of the world used a voice for their sake. But when we make them entirely spiritual, we understand that sonorous voice to be superfluous in designating the works. Therefore this is the common view of the ancients as well, and much more of modern writers, that no voice was uttered by God at that time, but that Moses wrote figuratively in this way, "because for those who are being taught, we judge it more useful to represent the command in a specific form," says St. Basil. Theodoret himself says the speech of God is His will. Ambrose should be heard: "The full voice of light signifies not the apparatus of arrangement, but shines forth in the effect of the operation. The Craftsman of nature spoke light and created it. The speech of God is His will; the work of God is nature."

This opinion satisfies me; yet I would caution that divine speech should be interpreted so as to mean not merely God's will in general, but specifically that singular and particular volition by which that particular effect was produced, so that God might truly be said to have commanded, that is, to have imposed a law. For to say that God willed something is too general: He always and everywhere wills; but when we say He commanded, we convey that a specific command was given, related to a distinct determination. Thus when light was made, by the will of God indeed, but by a new and particular will, God is rightly said to have spoken, to have commanded: because as human speech articulates thought into distinct words, so this particular volition is, as it were, an articulation or determination of the general and infinite divine will. To say briefly: the speech of God is His particular volition. And since God, although He is one and most simple, yet produces distinct effects, a distinct volition (if such language is permitted) corresponds to each effect: and thus the speeches of God are as many as the particular effects which proceed from Him.

Perhaps we may suitably add yet another thing. When Moses says that God spoke, or commanded, he indicates a command that is not only particular, but also in a certain sense new. God, although He wills all things from eternity, yet does not produce all things from eternity: for He wills each thing at its proper time. In the beginning God created heaven and earth: at a later time He commanded light. Therefore not everything that God willed was simultaneous, but there was an order and succession: and it is this order that Moses expresses by the words "God said." Therefore the speech of God signifies a particular volition, coming forth, as it were, at its proper time to produce its proper effect.

(Footnote 1: Philoponus, On Creation, I, 11. Footnote 2: Questions on Genesis, 7. Footnote 3: Homily 2 on the Hexaemeron, near the end. Footnote 4: Question 7 on Genesis. Footnote 5: Hexaemeron, sermo I, ch. IX.)

XXXII. The Nature of the First Light

It should be noted that the Hebrew words are more closely rendered literally as: Let there be light, and there was light, than as Let light be made, and light was made. The first rendering seems to indicate more that what is being dealt with here is the production of a phenomenon, rather than the creation or formation of some substance. Nevertheless I do not think that by Moses's words the question about the nature of light is settled -- a question which, like other matters of this kind, God left to human debate. Only this: "God said that light should shine out of darkness," II Corinthians IV.

In the Hebrew: Let there be light, and there was light (yehi or vayehi or): so the Septuagint also. The Vulgate: Let light be made. Both translations are equivalent in substance; but the literal Hebrew rendering more naturally suggests the appearance of a phenomenon than the substantial creation of a thing. (Footnote: Psalm 111:4.)

XXXIII. Augustine's Doubts; Critique of Scheuchzer

Much has been debated about this light of the first day. Augustine doubted whether it was spiritual or corporeal. And "if it was spiritual, it is not that true light," he says, "which must be understood as coeternal with the Father, through which all things were made, and which illuminates every man; but that of which it could be said: Wisdom was created before all things." Much less would Augustine allow that corporeal light to be coeternal with God, which the Greek Palamites and Eugubinus proposed. And so, "whatever light it may signify, he himself says, we must take it as having been made and created." And he interprets it as spiritual light, or the angelic nature, or the illumination thereof, or something of that kind. But that Moses meant corporeal and visible light, nearly all believe; and this is the only literal sense of this passage.

Scheuchzer, in his commentary on this passage, has attempted to explain this first light and indeed the entire Mosaic narrative of creation by means of a chemical hypothesis. He describes the formation of the atmosphere and the separation of elements with considerable ingenuity. But his hypothesis rests upon assumptions about the behavior of chemical elements that have since been superseded, and certain of his particular explanations are unsound.

These things are certainly contrary neither to reason nor to science: and that dense vapors were agglomerated around the earth in the earliest times is well indicated in the book of Job XXXVIII, 9. But since in Scheuchzer's opinion it is supposed that already from the first day the atmospheric space, or this first heaven, had become somewhat transparent and retained in itself scarcely anything other than air and water, it would follow that already from the very first day the atmosphere had reached approximately the state which is presented to us after the work of the second day (verses 6-7), in which the atmosphere is narrated as having been established. Moreover, in this opinion, the manifestation of the sun on the fourth day (verses 14-18) was merely the completion of atmospheric clarification, which made the stars visible to the earth. This clarification, therefore, the object of the divine command, would have taken place at least twice, and been the work of at least two of the six days, namely the first and the fourth. I said "at least": for if already from the first day the atmosphere existed and was not very different from the present one, even if it were much more humid -- then what, I ask, was the work of the second day, if not a less imperfect clarification of the same air, by which that portion of the atmosphere lying between the clouds and the lower waters became transparent? As happens when a lower fog dissolves or rises, but the sky remains covered with a continuous cloud. It does not seem, however, that it can be granted that in Moses's narrative one and the same operation is introduced as having been commanded by God two or three times.

XXXIV. Pianciani's Chemical Cosmogony Theory

It is easily understood, it seems to me, what this light was, if we attend to the laws imposed by God on nature. Elementary molecules, obeying the law of universal gravity, necessarily had to approach one another, and thus those which had been preordained by the Creator for forming our earth would, being associated, constitute this globe. Therefore, as these molecules encountered one another from every direction, after they had sufficiently approached each other, chemical combinations had to be accomplished according to chemical laws. Thus from simple or elementary molecules, compounds were produced; and among these, a great quantity of water vapor was generated. And lest we flee to hypotheses that are entirely unnecessary, we can conjecture that the first combinations were those which require not at all an elevated temperature, but for which the ordinary temperature of the earth, even in winter, suffices. Thus iron combines with oxygen, when it is very finely ground, and not without the emission of luminous heat: for iron truly burns when exposed to atmospheric air. So also the metals called nickel, cobalt, and uranium. Likewise at ordinary temperature, with the emission of fire, chlorine combines with antimony (stibium) and certain other metals, and iodine with phosphorus. These or similar processes generated some light, and at the same time raised the temperature: from which new combinations necessarily arose, and light and heat increased more and more. Among these new combinations, indeed one of the principal ones, must be considered the synthesis of water, which produces a great quantity of luminous heat; and since there was present the greatest abundance of those elements of which water consists (namely oxygen and hydrogen), heat and light must have increased immensely, and new affinities must have been excited therefrom. Together with water, or because of the heat produced by nascent water, there could be generated what are called fixed alkalis and earths, such as potash (kali), soda (natron), lime, magnite, alumina, silica, etc., and acids, for example carbonic, sulphurous, sulphuric, phosphoric acid, and sulphides and chlorides, etc. From these combinations new increases of heat and light occurred. Meanwhile these syntheses and combustions, together with the light and heat of which they were both the effects and the causes, were diffused through space. More could be added; but it is not necessary: for even these things will perhaps seem excessive to some. Now imagine some observer placed in space, directing the sharpness of his eyes toward the newly born earth. What will he see? At first, indeed, nothing, because darkness is upon the face of the deep: but after a certain time he will see light, at first perhaps moderate and gentle, but rapidly growing, and soon, from excessive brilliance, preventing any other sight to his eyes. Therefore nature itself leads us to acknowledge that the single appearance of light was the first phenomenon on the earth. Nor was anything in this happening by chance or at random, but all things were governed by the command and wisdom of the Creator.

John Andrew De Luc (Letters on the Physical History of the Earth, to Prof. Blumenbach, Letters 2 and 3) supposed that the earth, after creation, was a great mass of dust particles conglomerated from various substances, and he further asserted that water did indeed then exist, but not in liquid state, from a lack of heat which, just as it is necessary for chemical combinations, is likewise required for water to attain that liquid state. Therefore, in order for the water to be dissolved, and the other substances in it, it was necessary for that dusty mass to be penetrated by fire to a certain depth, from which the resulting solution would possess the temperature necessary for chemical operations. Fire, says De Luc, is made from the combination of light with the element of fire: therefore it was necessary for light to be joined with all the other elements: from this union alone the origin of all geological phenomena is to be traced. Now this coupling of light with the terrestrial mass cannot be traced to any physical cause known to us. Therefore nature itself makes us certain of the first command of God reported by Moses. It would not be difficult to refute these things by the doctrines of sounder physico-chemistry; but in this place I do not think it necessary. It will be enough to observe that these things were entirely devised arbitrarily; nor does it seem plausible that, if that substance which De Luc calls light and considers so necessary truly existed, it was not then created by God when He gave existence to the other corporeal substances. Moreover, fire or luminous heat is more suited to drying the terrestrial mass and resolving water into vapors than to liquefying the substance of water and dissolving other mineral substances within it. The degree of heat that suffices for the boiling of water is far inferior to the temperature of luminous heat; and even if we suppose that the boiling point for that dissolution was not 100 degrees Celsius (212 degrees Fahrenheit), as in the current atmospheric state, but higher on account of the pressure of the atmosphere -- which at that time was of a far greater weight -- still, this boiling point always remains below the degree at which heat becomes luminous. (Footnote: On the History of the Earth, vol. I.)

XXXV. "God saw the light" / "He divided light from darkness"

God saw the light, that it was good. Good indeed and beautiful is light (the Hebrew tov signifies both good and beautiful); but the meaning of this statement is better perceived, unless I am mistaken, if according to the doctrine set forth, that light is considered as having generated so many things useful to us, especially water. But although God bestowed this praise both on the light and on His other works (verses 10, 12, 18, 21, 25), He is nevertheless not said to have bestowed it on the whole corporeal substance when He produced it from nothing in the beginning. Why is this? Mazzocchius answers: "Because He Himself by Himself brought forth the entire corporeal substance from nothing, which is wondrous; but then it was not yet good, because it had not yet been arranged and adorned. Afterwards, however, when from that same substance which He had created He had formed things useful to man and beneficial to the rest of living creatures, then He said it was good; which means that He made it good and bestowed goodness upon it." In a similar mode, we may say that the formless and sterile mass of dust was not yet good, that is, not yet useful and beautiful; but when from it there had arisen chemical effects, including light and all that was to follow from it -- then it had become good, useful, and beautiful.

And God divided between the light and the darkness. Basil prudently notes that the words "He divided" do not mean that God Himself made a wall or barrier between light and darkness, but that He so constituted the nature of things that light and darkness should be divided and separated in appropriate manner: for light and darkness are by their very nature contraries and are unable both to exist at the same time: never in the same place and time is a union of light and darkness possible, and so by their very essence they are divided. God furthermore willed that neither perpetually nor in every place should either light or darkness prevail, but He divided their kingdoms between them.

XXXVI. God's Naming of Day and Night

God called the light (or the period of light) "day" and the darkness (the period of darkness) "night," verse 5. The Latins sometimes conversely call the day itself "light," as in that saying of Tullius: "This is the hundredth light since the death of P. Clodius." Surely one would be simple-minded who believed that God then actually uttered these words, though no one was listening -- that is, that He made them resound through empty spaces. "It was not yet a matter of names, says Augustine; afterward the terms 'day' and 'night' were applied." "He called" is a metonymy: God willed that for some time light should reign and produce that which is called day, and that for some time darkness should reign, through which (that is, from the absence of light) that state of things is produced which we call night. "It is understood, teaches St. Thomas, everywhere by the phrase 'He called,' that is, He gave the nature and property so that it could be so called." (Footnote: Part I, Question 68, art. 2, at the end.) Furthermore, to impose names is a sign of dominion: for the custom prevailed among Eastern peoples that whoever was subject to another's dominion received a name from him, 4 Kings 23:34; 24:17; Dan. 1:6-7. When dominion over the animals is granted to Adam, his first act of dominion is the imposition of names, Gen. 2:19. In like manner, by this imposition of names, the supreme dominion of God over day and night -- which is over all time -- seems to be indicated here, just as shortly after, in verses 8 and 10, the dominion of God over the heavens, earth, and seas -- which is over all things that exist in space -- is demonstrated by the names "heaven, earth, and seas" imposed by Him. "Yours is the day, and yours is the night" (Ps. 73:16). "Yours are the heavens, and yours is the earth. The sea you created..." (Ps. 88:12).

It is remarkable that even among the pagans a memory of the names of day and night having been divinely imposed is found. For Proclus says that day and night are gods, "whose names we received from the gods themselves." Mazzocchi thinks that Proclus drew this from this passage of Genesis, or at least the one from whom Proclus received it. Mazzocchi was not able to see Fronto, more ancient than Proclus, the tutor of Marcus Aurelius, epistle 3 on the Alsian holidays: for Fronto's writings were still hidden in the Ambrosian library. (Footnote: Whence the illustrious Angelo Mai, Cardinal of the Holy Roman Church, a man most deserving of letters, extracted and published much of Fronto's work.) Here are his words pertinent to our subject: "They say that Father Jupiter, when he was establishing human affairs from the beginning, struck the age through its middle with one blow and split it into two everywhere equal parts: one part he clothed with light, the other with darkness: he called them day and night: and to night he assigned rest, to day business."

In this verse 5, most scholars think the word "day" has a double meaning ("he called the light day".... "one day"), namely first referring to the period of light, and second to the civil day composed of day and night. The Syriac version uses two different words, a fact that L. Hirzel, praising the care of the scribe, notes. But truly this does not seem necessary to me: rather I would believe it more probable; since Moses immediately before these words said that God called the light "day" -- which means that the day is truly the period of light itself -- rather, I say, I would believe that the first day is called by him that period of time, however long it may have been, which is encompassed between the beginning and end of the primeval light, and which the darkness preceded. "The beginning of the day is the voice of God: Let there be light," says Ambrose. And Basil writes: "The state that existed in the world before the generation of light was not night, but darkness." And Augustine: "Those shadows were not yet night, for day had not yet preceded: for God divided between the light and the darkness, and first called the light day, then the darkness night, and from the making of light until the next morning one day was reckoned: it is clear that those days began from light, and each was terminated at morning after the light had passed." So think many, among whom are Chrysostom, John Philoponus -- who attempts to refute Theodore of Mopsuestia who taught the contrary -- and Victorinus, who wrote a little book on this question, published with other works by P. Sirmond. There is not much strength in the argument to prove that these days began from evening, namely that the day among the Hebrews drew its beginning from evening; especially since those Jewish days extended from evening to evening, while the Mosaic days, if they began from evening because evening is named first, would have to be said to have ended with morning: which both greatly differs from Hebrew custom and is absurd, because the day would be the same as the night. (Footnote 3: On the Pentateuch, Syriac version. Leipzig, 1825, p. 63. Footnote 4: The period of light is also called by Censorinus the natural and true day. On the Birthday, ch. 23. Footnote 5: Sermon 79, On Various Subjects.)

Concerning the words "evening" and "morning" we have said much above, in sections 11 and 12. It would seem credible that on the first day morning occurred when that light began, and evening when it was failing and growing weaker: for it is not plausible that it reached the highest degree of splendor and then suddenly ceased altogether.

XXXVII. Duration of the Primeval Luminous Heat

The span of time during which that luminous heat lasted, whether while the terrestrial globe was being formed or after it was already sufficiently constituted, I certainly cannot determine. We may conjecture, however, that that greatest of all conflagrations did not cool down very quickly. For it must be supposed that this newly born globe of the earth was entirely ignited, at least from the surface to a certain depth, both from the chemical combinations that were occurring there and from the ignited particles falling from the upper region by which the mass of the globe itself was growing. Moreover, this surrounding upper region, being itself ignited, preserved the maximum heat of the globe. But if the conflagration extended beyond the sublunary spaces and reached out to other celestial bodies, the immense force of radiation converging from all parts of the sky toward the earth must have long impeded and delayed the cooling of the earth itself, which could have occurred from the radiation of its own heat. It is fitting here briefly to note that a human being, had one been present at that time, would not have been able to measure this period of ignition and light; for since light was shining everywhere and always, those alternations of darkness and light by which we measure time were lacking. At length the earth's surface lost that temperature of luminous heat (and the surrounding space, which was occupied by particles of various kinds, lost it even more quickly): but it should not be supposed that the work of the second day began immediately. For this presupposes true and properly so-called, or liquid, water existing upon the face of the earth, verses 6 and 7: but water, as soon as the splendor of light had ceased -- indeed even long after that -- must have retained so much heat that it could not for a long time maintain a liquid state, even granting that it is probable that the degree of temperature at which water boils, on account of the greater atmospheric pressure, was somewhat more elevated during that period. Therefore this second span of time could have constituted the evening which preceded the second day or period. For anyone to whom this does not appeal, one may consider this period of darkness of which we are speaking as an interval interposed between the first and second day: for it is not necessary that these Mosaic days immediately succeeded one another with no intervening span of time, section 11.

Note well: I strongly commend the wisdom that shines forth in the words of Moses, but I assert nothing about his knowledge of natural things. Whether he himself understood those physical truths which lay hidden beneath the surface of his words, and which the wise men of his own time no less than those of later centuries were ignorant of; or whether, being himself ignorant of these things too, he wrote only what he had received either from an immediate revelation of God or from the tradition of his forefathers divinely transmitted -- on this matter I wish to define nothing. The Hebrews of that age certainly could not understand truths of this kind, but nor was it necessary: it was enough that they believed. As regards the work, for example, of the first day, it sufficed for them to believe that, after the creation of things, light or fire had shone forth by God's will; and that this light was not God -- which the Egyptians appear to have taught -- but merely the work of God.

If any should think that natural explanations of the primeval light ought to be rejected and that in this matter one must have recourse only to the supernatural, I would indeed wish that they recall to memory the teaching of Aquinas, who in the last article concerning the work of the first day (Part I, Question 67, art. 4, reply to objection 3), speaking of the "emission and contraction of light" posited by Basil (section 35), says: "Moreover, it is not in the nature of a luminous body to retract its light in its own presence, but this can happen miraculously. In the first institution of nature, however, a miracle is not sought, but what the nature of things possesses, as Augustine says."

ON THE WORK OF THE SECOND DAY

(Verses 6, 7, 8)

XXXVIII. The Firmament / Expanse (Raqia)

Moses teaches that on this day, at God's command, a firmament was made dividing between the upper and lower waters, and that the firmament was called "heaven" by God. Concerning God's speaking I shall add nothing beyond sections 31 and 36. The "firmamentum" of the Vulgate and the Stereoma of the Alexandrian translators is in Hebrew raqia. Scholars quite generally affirm that this word is well rendered as "extension" or "expansion" from the root raqa, meaning "stretched out, expanded." "For with this signification," says Pererius, "it is very frequently used in the sacred writings, as one may observe in Isaiah 62; Psalm 135; Jeremiah 10, and other passages." By "extension" they understand not something altogether empty -- which does not exist (for it is not a substance) -- but containing only a subtle and invisible matter within itself; empty, if you will, but not in the absolute sense so as to exclude all matter, nor in the sense of the Physicists (who call that space from which elastic fluids have been extracted as far as possible the "Torricellian" and "Boylean" vacuum), but in the common sense in which we call cups, flasks, chests, boxes, and the like "empty" when they lack the things they customarily contain or are designed to hold, and contain only air. Nevertheless it must be admitted that at least in certain passages of Scripture, such as the one cited by Pererius, Psalm 135:4, that word can signify firmness or stability no less than extension. For the version of the Septuagint and the Vulgate (firmamentum) is not to be despised, since, as Jean Le Clerc observes, among the Syrians the verb rakah means to press or compress. Michaelis wrote: "From what we have established concerning the meaning of the verb, from the usage of the Hebrew and Syriac languages, the noun raqiagh will not be 'expanse' but 'something made firm,' or, to use the word of the Vulgate, 'firmament,' Gen. 1:6; it can hardly be anything other than the atmosphere, bearing the clouds; and likewise in Ezekiel 1:21-22, where the base...pavement is that of God's thundering chariot." (Footnote: Supplement to the Hebrew Lexicon, no. 2886.) I add that the Hebrews could have used a word that properly signifies 'made firm' to signify 'extended.' Thus Luke 16:26: "Between us and you a great chasm esteriktai [has been fixed]," that is, "has been extended."

With these preliminary remarks made, the question is asked first, what Moses signified by the name "firmament"; second, what by the name "waters." I am indeed surprised that Petavius wrote that of all things contained in this Mosaic narrative, the most difficult is the work of the second day, in which the firmament was made, which between the waters interposed separates the upper from the lower. This seems to me far easier to explain than the work of the first day.

XXXIX. The Atmosphere as Firmament; Waters Above and Below

And in this matter the simplest explanation of all seems to me to be preferred: namely, that the Firmament, or this extension, is the air or atmosphere, or that part of it which is not very elevated and thin, but which is the lower part near the earth, where clouds rest, through which birds fly, and to which man also, aided by aerostatic balloons, sometimes ascends. This portion of the atmosphere is certainly called "heaven" (which Moses teaches is the same as the firmament) both in the Scriptures and in common speech, from which the sacred writers do not shrink: for thus we read: "He who covers the heaven with clouds" (Footnote: Psalm 146); and "a gloomy and red sky" (Footnote: Matthew 16); and these too are common expressions: "the birds of heaven," and the like. A little below we shall hear Moses saying "the winged creature to fly over the face of the firmament of heaven," verse 20 in the Hebrew. St. Thomas says: "It can be understood that by the firmament, which is read to have been made on the second day, one should not understand that firmament in which the stars are fixed, but that part of the air in which clouds are condensed... Whence Augustine, in the second book on Genesis literally, commending this interpretation says: 'I judge this consideration most worthy of praise: for what he said is neither against faith, and it can be believed on the basis of readily available evidence.'" (Footnote: Part I, Question 68, art. 1, in the body of the article.) Mazzocchi writes that this opinion which he himself follows rests on the consensus of the Fathers and Theologians. Indeed Rupert, Eugubinus, Pererius, Gregory of Valencia, Petavius, and many others agree with it. Nor do Basil, Ambrose, and Olympiodorus (in Nicetas's Catena on Job, chapter 38), and other ancient writers, seem averse to it in certain passages. Basil refutes "those who under the guise of the anagogical sense and of certain more sublime names took refuge in allegories, asserting that certain spiritual powers, I know not what, devoid of body, are figuratively adumbrated in the waters: of whom the more excellent, they say, took their place in the higher region above the firmament, while the inferior ones sank down below in the regions nearest the earth and in material places." (Footnote: Homily 3 on the Hexaemeron.) I do not deny, however, that elsewhere "firmament," or "firmament of heaven," is given a broader sense and extended to denote the very spaces where the stars are, in verses 14, 15, and 16.

Thus heaven, when it is taken in that first, more august sense, and this seems to be the primitive and more proper sense of the word shamayim ("heaven"); for in verse 8, "God called the firmament (this firmament of the second day) 'heaven';" elsewhere it is taken in a second, broader sense, and sometimes it is understood in a still nobler and spiritual signification, so as to signify the place in which God manifests Himself to the blessed Spirits. If, as it is common, so also true is the interpretation of the word shamaim that it signifies "where water is," this splendidly confirms what we are saying: namely, that originally this word signified the place where clouds are and from which rain falls; or "the heaven which is called air," if one may use the words of Lucretius. (Footnote: See A Lapide on this passage.)

It follows from what we have said about the firmament that the upper or super-celestial waters are none other than clouds and aqueous vapors, by whose massing together clouds are formed, and which, when condensed, visible, and vesicular, physicists call vapors. Other passages of Scripture seem to confirm this simple explanation. Psalm 104, in which the work of the six days is described in lyrical colors, first presents God "clothed with beauty and wrapped in light as with a garment." Behold the work of the first day: the next day follows close behind. "Stretching out the heaven like a skin (a tent): You who cover its upper parts with waters. You who make the cloud Your chariot, who walk upon the wings of the winds." You see here that nothing else is mentioned in the work of the second day except clouds and air. In the book of Job we read that God "binds (encloses) the waters in His clouds" (Footnote: Chapter 26, verse 8); and in Jeremiah: "He who made the earth by His strength... and by His wisdom stretched out the heavens. At His voice He gives a multitude of waters in the heaven, and raises clouds from the ends of the earth. He makes lightning for rain." (Footnote: Chapter 10, verses 12-13.) Here, in the heaven that God stretched out, he mentions a multitude of waters: but what kind of waters these are, you can see: they are those which by God's laws are raised from the earth in the state of vapor, condense into clouds, and fall as rain. Proverbs 8:28 reads in the Vulgate: "When He was strengthening the ether above and balancing the springs of waters" -- and Pererius notes that the word which is rendered "ether" properly signifies "clouds." (Footnote: There follows verse 4: 'Who makes His angels spirits, and His ministers a burning fire'; which seems to be understood of winds and celestial fire. Petavius thinks this passage probably refers, from the Hebrew, to storms and lightning (On the Angels, lib. II, ch. V, sections 4, 6). Yet since Paul (Hebrews 1) expounds this very passage concerning the Angels, I do not doubt that the discourse here is also about Angels, at least in a spiritual sense. Those words can be most aptly explained in this manner: 'Who makes His Angel-ministers swift as winds and efficacious as fire.' So Theodoret and St. Thomas (On the Epistle to the Hebrews); and the explanation is quite apt, says Suarez, as Genebrardus, Bellarmine, and others there relate (On the Angels, lib. I, ch. VI).)

Therefore there was at that time around the earth, in the elevated region of the air, a kind of vault of clouds: and God, by means of this aerial expanse which we call the atmosphere, divided them, or, what is the same thing, through the air He caused them to be divided from the waters that were beneath the atmosphere, namely from the waters covering the face of the earth. Nor is the atmosphere improperly called a firmament. For it by its weight and pressure contains the terrestrial waters, lest by excessive evaporation (which would happen even in ordinary temperatures and would have happened much more in this period, when the temperature was more elevated) they should ascend to the region of the clouds and be confused with them: likewise by its weight and pressure it holds the clouds suspended on high, which, if this obstacle were removed, would fall to the earth. Indeed clouds, and the vapors of which they consist, ascend all the more the greater the air pressure is, which the small mercury column in barometers indicates. Thus Moses explains this phenomenon -- an easy one, to be sure, but one that could excite the admiration of the Hebrews, no less than that of Pliny who wrote: "What can be more marvelous than waters standing in the sky?" (Footnote: Natural History, lib. XXXI.)

With these premises laid down, it is now easy to set forth the entire series of events that succeeded one another on this second day. Thus the matter seems to me to be conceived as follows. As soon as in the earth and in the surrounding space that first conflagration subsided, as the temperature descended below the degree of luminous heat, darkness -- perhaps total -- again occupied the earth. For even though very probably the sun was then emitting rays by which light is propagated, as not a few have said, section 30; nevertheless, as St. Ephrem also noted, the path of the solar rays was obstructed by the very dense vapor and opaque particles disseminated through the space, and they did not reach the earth, except perhaps very few and feeble ones. And indeed that immense quantity of water, which had been recently generated, and which, because the temperature was still too elevated, could not remain on the earth's surface, encircled the earth on all sides as a very dense fog of vesicular vapor, as they call it, with which probably countless other opaque particles, whether metallic or of other kinds, were mingled and truly "made the cloud its (the earth's) garment, and wrapped it in darkness, as in swaddling clothes of infancy." (Footnote: Job 38:9.)

But gradually the opaque particles precipitated, and when the temperature had fallen sufficiently to permit it, a very great quantity of water also fell and spread over the terrestrial globe. Meanwhile many terrestrial substances were dissolved in it, and that darkness was gradually dissipated. After so many precipitations, that great corpuscular sphere which encircled the terrestrial globe became sufficiently transparent. Yet the condensed aqueous vapor, and that indeed abundantly, remained in the elevated regions of this sphere, where the cooling had been greater than in the lower part; and thus it formed a sort of cloudy vault, which was separated by that transparent atmosphere from the water covering the earth: this therefore "was dividing between the upper and lower waters," or what is the same thing, it separated between the waters which were under this aerial expanse, and between the waters which had settled above it. That cloudy vault indeed blocked the sight of the stars, but it allowed solar light to pass through sufficiently, just as also now happens when clouds cover the sky. What difference there might have been between that atmosphere and the one we now use, I do not dare to say for certain: perhaps the condition and state of it was such that it would by no means allow the cloudy vault to be split or dissipated. Meanwhile it is certain and established that all these things were done by the plan of the laws imposed by God, namely by the will of the Creator and Preserver of nature: therefore God alone is rightly said to have commanded and done these things with the best right. For His will is the one efficient cause of natural effects, inasmuch as these do not depend on the will of creatures.

Moses does not report all these things which happened on the second day: it is enough for him if he narrates that the atmosphere separating the waters from the waters was made by divine command; by which thing our earth was proceeding not a little toward that perfection which the Maker of things had decreed to bestow upon it before the creation of man.

XL. Against Literal Waters Above the Stars

On the occasion of this passage many have held that real waters, which they also called elemental, had been spread above the region of the air and through the entire heaven, even through the heaven of the fixed stars and above it. We have no need of these waters (which some affirmed were far purer and more genuine than those which the earth sustains) to explain the words of Moses; indeed this supposition does not at all agree with them. For where would a firmament or expanse (a real one, of course, for what does not exist no one has made) be found that separates the super-stellar waters from the terrestrial ones, and by its pressure prevents the conjunction of the former with the latter? Furthermore, by what means or to what end are waters held suspended in the middle of space, lest they rush down to the earth or to some celestial globe? Some fled to the power of God; but Augustine (On Genesis, literally, II, ch. I) prudently admonished them: "Now how God has established the natures of things according to His scriptures, it is fitting for us to inquire; not what He may wish to do in them or from them for the miracle of His power." Petavius (On Creation, lib. I, ch. XI, 3) should also be heard: "Nor does it escape me that it can be divinely brought about that in the highest part of the world, however fluid and flowing the moisture, it contains itself, nor flows through the underlying liquid and even penetrable bodies. But because it is contrary to the nature of waters to be restrained there, it is not probable that that supreme Maker," nor that a gentle no less than powerful Craftsman, at the very origin of things and of all nature, would especially prescribe to each thing its own measure, order, and plan, which He wished to be preserved for the future; when, as Scripture says (Proverbs VIII, 27, 29), He enclosed the abysses by a fixed law and a circle; when He was strengthening the ether above, and balancing the springs of waters, and setting a law for the waters, lest they should cross their boundaries: it is not, I say, consistent that He would then have begun that which is extraordinary and violent, and would have commanded this same thing to be constant and perpetual.

Some have said that clouds are distinguished in the Scriptures from "the waters which are above the heavens." But in Psalm 148, where all heavenly and earthly things are invited to the praise of God, I see no mention of clouds, which nevertheless strike the eyes with their very conspicuous magnitude: only "all the waters which are above the heavens" are invited. In the canticle indeed, as we call it, of the three youths, there is mention of both. For first one reads: "Bless the Lord, O heavens. Bless the Lord, all waters that are above the heavens." And afterward a review of meteorological things is made, which, before we descend to earth, is concluded with these words: "Bless the Lord, lightnings and clouds." But the upper waters are both the disseminated vapors, which only diminish the transparency of the air, and clouds, whether they are stretched out into cirrus forms, or gathered into cumulus forms, or extended into strata, which are the three chief forms of clouds. The clouds which are named with lightnings are storm clouds, which bear the appearance neither of cirrus, nor cumulus, nor of a single uniform stratus or elevated mist, but a disturbed and broken form, which indicates tumult and disturbance in the sky. One can therefore respond: in these verses phenomena rather than substances are enumerated (nights, days, light, darkness, lightnings); then lightnings and clouds mean the same as lightnings of clouds (which explanation most appeals to me), or lightnings and the clouds which hurl them. The storm cloud is a species; the upper waters are the genus. Moreover in this canticle certain species are called upon after their genus: as after all the works of the Lord, individual things are called; after earth, mountains; after the sons of men, Israel; after Israel, the priests of the Lord.

I would not assert that water exists nowhere except on our globe and in its atmosphere. Water can exist, and indeed most abundantly, in many other globes, and that it is found in at least some is also probable. Whether indeed anything about these things was revealed to Moses, I do not know. But let no one of sound mind believe that he is speaking about waters of this kind, rather than about those which the Jews could perceive with their senses when they were reading these things; or persuade himself that a firmament was made by God which separates between terrestrial waters and those, for example, which are located on the planet Mars.

XLI. The Feeble Light of the Second and Third Days

There is no mention in Moses of that feeble light, by no means comparable with the light of the first day, which we have attributed to this second day (and will attribute to the third), nor of the preceding darkness, unless we say that he indicates both by the words day, morning, and evening, which is very probable. At least the end of the first day indicates some darkness. That there was some light on the second and third days is the common opinion of the Doctors. "Again a new morning, the first evening now dispelled, the restored light made," says Marius Victor, treating of the second day. And indeed to posit any of these Mosaic days without light seems to be the same as positing a day without a day, verse 5.

You will perhaps say: the works which we have indicated require a span of time certainly far longer than one solar day. But the sun itself was producing the day, even though it did not show its face to the earth. Therefore this second day was certainly not one single day, nor begun by only one morning and concluded by one evening, but the sum of many and indeed very many days, each of which had its own morning and its own evening.

This difficulty we have already anticipated in the preceding sections. We must always keep before our eyes in the exposition of this chapter the saying of Augustine (On Genesis, literally, lib. V, ch. v): "We must not think of those days as these solar days..." Furthermore, we must remember that Moses is not here acting as the historian of some particular person or people or region, but of the entire terrestrial globe. When he treats of the history of mankind, if he mentions day or night, we will understand this to be said of the region where those people will be about whom he is speaking. When he says, for example in chapter XIV, that Abraham attacked by night those who were leading Lot captive, everyone will easily understand that it was then night in that very region, but not among his antipodeans. But in verse 8 of chapter I, of what region is Moses speaking? Everything was sea. On that watery globe, always from the time the sun's light began to appear, it was day, just as it is now on the terraqueous globe, even when the sky is covered with clouds, unless perchance some unusual cause takes away light from the hemisphere that should then be illuminated: but with this cause removed, not indeed in all parts of the globe, but on the globe nevertheless there is perpetual day. So in this second day or period let us hold that, after the intervening evening or darkness, there was continuous day, namely from the time the solar rays began to pass through the cloudy stratum, the orb of the earth never lacked this light, however feeble it may have been. Before this light appeared, there was evening; when first this light arose, then it was the morning of the second day. But there was evening again, as this day declined, about which we will speak when treating the third day.

Only this one thing I advise: it is not necessarily required of us that we posit the absence of light in these passages as absolutely complete; for here night or darkness is not mentioned, but only evening. The morning, however, at verse 13, will be the return of that light which inaugurates the third day.

XLII. Evening and Morning of the Second Day

We can also well interpret that verse 8 from the Hebrew. And there was (or there had been) a mixing (erev), and there was a dividing, or an opening, or a first birth (boqer): the second day. At the beginning of this period there was mixing, or there had been immediately before it, both because of the absence of light, and because the upper waters were mixed with the lower: there was no sea, no clouds separated through clear air from the globe lying beneath; but afterward there was also morning, because the restored light made a division between the upper celestial waters and the lower terrestrial waters; that dark and misty cloud which clothed the terrestrial globe and enveloped the nascent sea began to open, and through it a way was opened for the solar light, and first a transparent atmosphere was born between the waters and the waters, as well as light dividing in various parts of the globe between the domain of day or light, and of night or darkness.

Perhaps in the earliest times, when the temperature was far more elevated, the rotation of the earth and its atmosphere was slower, whose mass and bulk then existed far greater than they are now. But I do not believe that by this single greater length of days, which that rotation measures, the phenomena can be sufficiently accounted for, without our being concerned about another sense of those words day, evening, and morning. Even if we grant that in that early time the period of terrestrial rotation was longer, this difference could not have been so great as to be sufficient for explaining the effects which geologists have observed: therefore nothing can be so reconciled, nothing explained. There is therefore no reason to linger in this kind of investigation. For what would it matter if at that time the period of terrestrial rotation were contained in forty-eight or fifty hours? What even if in one or two months? I note, however, that the mass situated in space around the earth and rotating together with it was not so great after the second day, indeed not even throughout this entire day, after the globe was already covered with waters, nor was the temperature at its maximum, if it permitted the liquid state of the waters, nor was it very elevated on the third day when the earth brought forth herbs and trees: for it is well known that only a certain moderate mean temperature suits terrestrial plants, which is now between 32 degrees Fahrenheit (0 degrees of the centigrade and Reaumur thermometers), and 99.5 degrees Fahrenheit; nor do seeds germinate any longer that have experienced a temperature above 110.75 degrees Fahrenheit. On the fifth and sixth days, moreover, a temperature must be admitted that would be suitable for aquatic and terrestrial animals, and for man himself.

XLIII. The Firmament Is Not Solid Crystal

Before we leave this day of the firmament, it must be noted that the name of firmament by no means indicates here any kind of solid heaven, for example a crystalline one, of the sort which certain ancient Philosophers, much later than Moses, devised. Not to repeat what was written in section 38: "The firmament was named not from the proper condition of its nature," as I may say with Petavius, "but from its effect, because it separates the waters as if it were the strongest wall." Thus can be understood that passage from the book of Job XXXVII, 18: "Have you perhaps fashioned the heavens with him, which are most solid as if of cast bronze?" This is the one passage in the Scriptures which seems to favor solid heavens of this kind, which human imagination has contrived. Petavius says that the Hebrew word, which the Vulgate renders as "heavens," properly means clouds; and that the final words mean "like a cast mirror" (On Creation, lib. I, ch. x, 9). But we are little concerned about that passage: for it has no authority. For those words do not belong to the author of the book; nor does he here introduce God speaking thus, nor Job himself, but that Elihu, who has no authority, and whose manner of speaking seems afterward to be rebuked by God with these words: "Who is this that wraps up sentences in unskilled words?" chapter XXXVIII, verse 2.

The Alexandrine translators add at verse 8: "And God saw that it was good," said of the firmament: so Basil, and Ambrose imitating him, Chrysostom, and the other Greeks. This, however, is read neither in our Vulgate, nor in the Hebrew text, or in the Samaritan text, nor in the Samaritan, Syriac, and Arabic versions, nor in the Targum of Onkelos, nor finally in the Greek versions of Aquila, Symmachus, etc. It is therefore customarily asked why the second day lacks its own approval and commendation which is accorded to the works of the other days by the cited words: "And God saw," etc. This seems to be the reason for the matter. The work of this day was the distribution of the waters; but this was not completed by the firmament alone: for once the upper waters had been divided, it remained that these should recede from certain parts of the earth's surface, so that the dry land might appear, which could be clothed with the greenness of plants and sustain animals. Therefore to the work not yet completed, commendation is not denied but deferred and truly bestowed at verse 10, when it is now complete: with the addition of a second approval for the special work of the third day, the production of plants, which is mentioned in verse 12. Some, however, prefer to assign the emergence of the earth and the gathering of the waters to the second day, so that they leave no day without its own approval. Accordingly, what we read at verse 9 -- "And God said: Let the waters be gathered" -- they understand as if it were written: "God had said." But since the second day is closed at verse 8 with the customary formula "And there was evening," etc., this opinion seems less commendable. Moreover, as I already advised in section 30, if these days are periods of unknown duration, it matters little whether certain effects are assigned to the second or to the third.

ON THE WORK OF THE THIRD DAY

(Verses 9, 10, 11, 12)

XLIV. Gathering of Waters / Appearance of Dry Land

A twofold work of God Moses now sets before us: for he narrates that the waters were gathered into one place, a portion of the earth was laid bare and a dwelling place prepared for the habitation of future living creatures; then that the earth was clothed with the richness and abundance of plants.

God commands: Let the waters under the heaven be gathered into one place, verse 9 in the Hebrew, which the Vulgate translates "which are under heaven," as also the Septuagint (and the Targum of Jonathan: Let the lower waters which were left below under the heavens be gathered). Mazzocchi thinks that the Hebrew word which is rendered "let them be gathered" can be more faithfully rendered, though hardly in good Latin, as "let them be encaved" (that is, let them be stored away in a cavity) -- the waters ... in one place. Let the dry land appear. Earth not covered by waters is called by the Latins also "dry ground." Virgil: "Sea-coots play on the dry ground." Pliny: "Neither in the sea nor on dry ground." The Latin Church, at Psalm 94, presents for reading indiscriminately: "And his hands formed the dry land. And his hands founded the dry land."

Many believe, such as Petavius and Mazzocchi, that the phrase "into one place," as the Vulgate says with the Hebrew, or "into one gathering" (synagogen), as the LXX have it, must be understood distributively, that is, that each collection of waters flows into some one place; and if there are several collections, that nevertheless all water settles in a certain and predetermined place for itself. Basil taught that, since there are many collections of waters, Moses speaks of the chief and greatest of all; he adds that any lakes and ponds are not properly collections of waters: but those that are larger, as also those which, like the Caspian, are called seas, flow into the Ocean through open or hidden subterranean channels, and are united with it. So also Theodoret, Procopius, and Bede. Yet it is very probable that in that first time when the dry land appeared -- that is, when certain lands were uncovered and stripped of waters -- it is very probable, I say, that all the great collections of waters (as will appear from what is to be said) communicated with one another and so were gathered into one place and one congregation, with perhaps some small pond excepted.

To verse 9 the Septuagint adds: And the water which was under the heaven was gathered together into its congregations, and the dry land appeared; which reading the Greeks accept, and with them Ambrose. But this appendix is absent from the Hebrew text and the Samaritan, as from the Vulgate and other versions; and it is only implicitly asserted in those words, and it was so.

That God called the collection of waters "seas" is found in the Hebrew of verse 10. The Vulgate renders it in the plural as "congregations": so also the Septuagint as systemata, collections or confluences of waters. The Hebrews also called lakes "seas," whether the water was fresh or salt, such as the Dead Sea and the Sea of Galilee; which other nations sometimes also did. (Footnote: Mazzocchi, Spicileg. biblic., vol. I, pp. 22-24.)

XLV. What Caused the Waters to Recede

Let us now inquire what was the cause or occasion by which the waters receded and the dry land appeared. Was the cause simply God's command alone, "at the voice of whose thunder the waters feared and fled," as the Psalmist says poetically (Psalm 104:7-8), while "the mountains ascended and the plains descended into the place which He had founded for them"? Certainly those effects had no other first cause. But that that command of God was entirely distinct from the laws by which He Himself governs the world (which would mean it was a miracle), this does not seem necessary, and therefore according to the accepted rules of the wise should not be admitted. Some say that the sacred text indicates as the sole instrument of the works of creation the commands of God: "Let there be, let them be gathered"; and they add that God, unlike a human being, does not need external means for working. It should be noted first that creation is not the issue here: for nothing in this work is said to be created. When Moses indicates the first cause, which alone was appropriate to the end He intended, he certainly does not deny that in some law of God (that is, in some uniform and permanent volition of God, by which He afterward produced many other effects and indeed now produces them) this particular volition was included. God certainly does not need external means: but we should not ask "what divine power can do for the miracle of His power," but rather what He who "orders all things sweetly" is accustomed to do. It certainly seems more worthy of God's wisdom that He, through laws established by Himself in the beginning and co-created with matter, first brought it about that the newly born earth was covered with waters, so that while it lay hidden beneath them it might receive great accretions from the sediments of the water; but that afterward now these, now those parts of the earth should emerge from the waters, so that they might sustain plants and terrestrial living creatures. When I conceive of things thus, I am indeed compelled to exclaim: "In wisdom You have made all things" (Psalm 104:24). See how many effects proceed from few and simple laws!

Behold how, by one volition of God, all things proceed toward their goal. "This opinion (I use the words and teaching of Suarez, On the Work of the Six Days, II, ch. VII) is more philosophical and more in conformity with reason, and should be adhered to more firmly, when Scripture does not compel otherwise." The same author teaches: "One should not have recourse to the first cause when effects can be reduced to secondary causes" (On Angels, lib. I, no. 8).

On the contrary, if I were to say that the earth was covered with waters solely by some particular command of God, and afterward by another command of God contrary to the first the earth itself emerged from the waters, without any cause or useful effect of this washing being apparent: I would indeed fear that I might expose Scripture to the mockeries of the wise men of this world, which we have already heard must be most carefully guarded against; but it is useful to hear more.

Scripture attributes natural effects to God: truly indeed and philosophically. For all effects which do not arise from created will must necessarily arise from the will of the Creator alone. And this doctrine suffices abundantly for most people: for those who have learned and hold it far surpass both those who of old "attending to the works, did not recognize who was the Craftsman; but either fire, or wind, or swift air, or the circuit of the stars, or the great water, or the sun and moon, they thought to be the gods that rule the world" (Wisdom 13:2); and the more recent pseudo-philosophers, who by the paths of the sciences have returned to ancient ignorance, and think that fire, water, the sun, universal attraction, affinity, and other laws of nature are the rulers of the world and the universe -- laws which, when they reject the Creator, are effects without a cause and laws without a lawgiver. Yet that which suffices for faith does not suffice also for science, whose task it is to endeavor to refer those effects which certainly did not arise from some particular will of God (such as creation and miracles) to some general law established by Him. Timaeus in Cicero teaches (Cicero, Timaeus, ch. XIV) that "he who professes himself a lover of intelligence and wisdom" must necessarily search out the first causes of nature, and then the secondary causes. And the Theologian has need of this science indeed, wherever some connection exists between natural and supernatural things, so that he may be able to refute those who contradict and resist the truth; to show that even in the sacred sciences (although the dogmas are immutable, because they are truth) there is "progress; so that what was formerly believed more obscurely may be understood more clearly through his exposition; so that through him posterity may rejoice in understanding what antiquity, not understanding, revered; yet transmitting the same doctrines which he learned, so that when he speaks in a new way, he does not say new things" (See Vincent of Lerins, Commonitorium, chapters XXIII, XXVIII).

XLVI. Geologists Agree with Moses

We return to the recession of the water, in which matter it happily turns out that geologists commonly agree with Moses. For they deduce from their observations that the earth was once covered with waters.
