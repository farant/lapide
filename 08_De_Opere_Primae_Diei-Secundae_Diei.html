ON THE WORK OF THE FIRST DAY

(Verses 3, 4, 5)

XXXI. God's Speech: "Let there be light"

"God said: Let there be light, and there was light," verse 3. Here for the first time the legislator of the Hebrews introduces the Legislator of nature speaking and commanding. Theodore of Mopsuestia, as cited by Philoponus (John Philoponus, book I, On Creation, ch. XXII), thought that God used an actual voice, which Philoponus ridicules and judges absurd. Nevertheless Basil of Seleucia agrees with Theodore, as does Theodoret (Question IX on Genesis), who asserts that this was done by God "for the sake of the Angels and the invisible powers, so that they would know that at His command those things which do not exist come into being." Those who believed the angels to be corporeal -- which was the opinion of many of the ancients -- could think not unreasonably that the Creator of the world used a voice for their sake. But when we make them entirely spiritual, we understand that sonorous voice to be superfluous in designating the works. Therefore this is the common view of the ancients as well, and much more of modern writers, that no voice was uttered by God at that time, but that Moses wrote figuratively in this way, "because for those who are being taught, we judge it more useful to represent the command in a specific form," says St. Basil (Oration 2). Theodoret himself says the speech of God is His will. Ambrose should be heard (Hexaemeron, book I, ch. IX): "The full voice of light signifies not the apparatus of arrangement, but shines forth in the effect of the operation. The Craftsman of nature spoke light and created it. The speech of God is His will; the work of God is nature."

The question has been asked why God is said to have uttered no voice when He made heaven and earth, as He is said to do afterward in the works of each successive day. Would it not have been a magnificent opening: In the beginning God said: Let there be heaven, let there be earth; and there were heaven and earth? The reason for this, at least the principal and most obvious one, seems to me to be this. The words of God are His commands. The Persian version, where the Hebrew has: Let there be light (verse 3), Let there be an expanse (verse 6), Let there be luminaries (verse 14), puts it as if God were speaking to another: Be thou light, Be thou a firmament, Be ye luminaries. Now commands are given only to those who can obey, not however to nothing -- that is, to a non-existent thing which, since it does not exist, cannot obey. Existing things, even though they are irrational and inanimate and do not understand His commands, nevertheless obey God, just as iron obeys the magnet and the hand that brings the magnet near. God did not command the matter of heaven and earth which did not yet exist, but He commanded already created and existing matter to be moved with that vibratory motion which generates light; the air to form an atmosphere dividing between the upper and lower waters; the waters to recede from that surface of the earth which was to appear, etc. Most rightly, therefore, Moses distinguished between the creation of matter, in which he used the verb of creation, and the formation of things from created matter. In that first operation God works but does not command: for He does not command Himself. In the second operation He both works and commands, which two things, however, when God's operations upon irrational things are concerned, are one ("In the work of creation He uses the verb of creation, because in it bodies are simply produced from nothing. In the work of distinction He uses the words 'let there be' and 'made,' because that is not made from nothing, but is a determination to form and place: which on the part of the work is properly 'becoming,' and on the part of the maker is properly 'making.'" Albert the Great, Summa, part II, Tract. 11, Quest. LV). Hence it is confirmed that He made heaven and earth in the beginning out of nothing: for otherwise whence this difference? Why does the Lord not command heaven and earth in the beginning? Why is He silent there, but speaks afterward? Voice succeeds silence, because existing creatures had succeeded nothing, or non-existent things.

Jerome writes on that passage of Matthew, He commanded the winds and the sea: "From this passage we understand that all creatures perceive the Creator. For those whom He rebuked and those to whom He gave commands perceive the one commanding: not by the error of the heretics who think all things are animate, but by the majesty of the Creator, to whom those things insensible to us are sensible," on Matthew VIII.

Not only Augustine (section 22), but many Church Fathers explain those speeches of God in Genesis chapter 1 as signifying that God made through the Word those things which are narrated as having been made. So Irenaeus (Against Heresies IV, ch. XXXII), Theophilus of Antioch (Book II to Autolycus), Athanasius (Against the Arians, oration 2, section 31), Gregory of Nyssa (Against the Jews, testimony 1), and Tertullian (Against Praxeas, ch. VII).

It was the opinion of some (In the apocryphal book 4 Esdras, ch. VI, 38: "O Lord, speaking You spoke from the beginning of creation, on the first day saying: Let there be heaven and earth; and Your word was the accomplished work") that the first creation of heaven and earth, or at least the state of things described in verse 2, belonged to the first day, in which light afterward appeared. But this day, others observe, seems more probably to begin from the divine command, And God said: Let there be light, just as the five subsequent days have their beginning when God commands. Furthermore, since the day is light (verse 5), it does not seem that a part of the day should be placed before the first light. Certainly the first creation preceded every day. Nevertheless it should be noted that it matters very little, if those days are periods of indeterminate time, whether the first period is made longer by adding to it the time preceding the primordial darkness, or conversely is made shorter, and another dark period prior to every day is placed before it.

XXXII. The Nature of the First Light

It should be noted that the Hebrew words are more closely rendered literally as: Let there be light, and there was light, than as Let light be made, and light was made. The first rendering seems to indicate more that what is being dealt with here is the production of a phenomenon, rather than the creation or formation of some substance. Nevertheless I do not think that by Moses's words the question about the nature of light is settled -- a question which, like other matters of this kind, God left to human debate. Only this: "God said that light should shine out of darkness," II Corinthians IV.

Now if someone requests light from another so that darkness may be dispelled, he is requesting nothing other than that the place where he is be illuminated; which is done either by opening windows, or by lighting a lamp or bringing one already lit; he does not seek that some imponderable substance come forth from bodies where it lay hidden, nor that the ether, which is everywhere and therefore also diffused through his own room, acquire a certain vibratory motion.

Voltaire thinks he overturns Moses's narrative by writing that all the ancients believed light was not emitted from the sun, but only that the matter of light diffused through space was set in motion by it -- but that it was afterward demonstrated that light is emitted from the sun. But indeed, if Moses taught that light is not emitted from the sun, but that light is generated by it when it communicates motion to the light, or rather to the ether, diffused through space, then Moses should be congratulated as the precursor of this doctrine, which seems to have been demonstrated by modern optics. But what men older than Moses or even his contemporaries thought about light, we are all equally, I think, ignorant. Later philosophers did not all deny that light is emitted from the sun; indeed some held it as certain, such as the disciples of Democritus and Epicurus, as we learn even from Lucretius (On the Nature of Things, book II); but no one, neither among the ancients nor among the moderns, demonstrated that emission theory. Voltaire adds that the light diffused by God through space, so that when set in motion by the sun it would illuminate the world and would itself be divided from darkness and produce day, could not have accomplished these things before the sun existed: that this is altogether contrary both to physical science and to reason. To which indeed nothing more foolish can be said, or more contrary to physical doctrine and reason; nay, even more repugnant to common sense and ordinary experience. Does not light appear, does it not drive away darkness, is it not divided from it, even when the sun is absent, if any ignited or burning body is present? The aurora borealis, volcanic mountains, the conflagration of a great city or forest, festive fires artfully arranged, streets shining with many torches, halls, cities, temples, theatres -- do they not emit light, do they not put darkness to flight? No man is so dull and stupid (even dogs and cats could testify) as to be ignorant that rooms are illuminated at night by lamplight, and that a kitchen and hall shine well enough even from a hearth fire. How then could Voltaire not imagine light without the sun? It was sufficient, in the earliest times, for any light whatever to illuminate our earth or a great part of it, so that there would be day: for to Moses, day is the time of light, not the time of the sun. "God called the light Day," verse 5.

XXXIII. Augustine's Doubts; Critique of Scheuchzer

Much has been debated about this light of the first day. Augustine doubted whether it was spiritual or corporeal (On Genesis Unfinished, ch. 1 and following). And "if it was spiritual, it is not that true light," he says (On Genesis Literally I, ch. III and XVII): "which must be understood as coeternal with the Father, through which all things were made, and which illuminates every man; but that of which it could be said: Wisdom was created before all things." Much less would Augustine allow that corporeal light to be coeternal with God, which the Greek Palamites and Eugubinus proposed. And so, "whatever light it may signify, he himself says, we must take it as having been made and created." And he interprets it as spiritual light (On Genesis Literally I, ch. III; City of God, book XI, ch. VII and IX; Confessions, book XII, ch. IX and XVI), or the angelic nature, or the illumination thereof, or something of that kind. But that Moses meant corporeal and visible light, nearly all believe; and this is the only literal sense of this passage.

What the ancients wrote on the occasion of this light, when light had not yet been shed on natural science, and perhaps nothing in physics was more obscure than light, to now report and subject to examination would be futile at the very least. "Pardon to the ancients." Physical science, however, would not disapprove the opinion of certain ancient Hebrews who said that this light was the light of the sun, although the sun was not yet visible to the earth (In Maimonides, Guide for the Perplexed, p. 276; Buxtorf edition). St. Thomas followed this opinion (In II Sentences, dist. XIII; Summa, I part., Quest. LXVII, art. 4, ad 1), and after him Augustinus Eugubinus (In Cosmopoeia), Catharinus, Nicolas Garcia de Londagno, Quadros, and Pererius, who wrote that those attempting to explain it another way, even perhaps truthfully, are "held entangled and encumbered by nearly inexplicable difficulties." Scheuchzer (Sacred Physics on Genesis) thought that the rays of the sun -- already created and already shining with its own light -- did not reach the earth in the beginning because they were blocked by a dense and opaque mixture of corpuscles of every kind; but that afterward, when those opaque molecules had largely precipitated, solar light penetrated to the earth's surface, though the face of the sun was still not visible to the earth, because a great mass of watery vapor and immense clouds covered the sky. Exactly as happens now when the sky is overcast. So also think the English authors of the Universal History.

These things are certainly contrary neither to reason nor to science: and that dense vapors were agglomerated around the earth in the earliest times is well indicated in the book of Job XXXVIII, 9. But since in Scheuchzer's opinion it is supposed that already from the first day the atmospheric space, or this first heaven, had become somewhat transparent and retained in itself scarcely anything other than air and water, it would follow that already from the very first day the atmosphere had reached approximately the state which is presented to us after the work of the second day (verses 6-7), in which the atmosphere is narrated as having been established. Moreover, in this opinion, the manifestation of the sun on the fourth day (verses 14-18) was merely the completion of atmospheric clarification, which made the stars visible to the earth. This clarification, therefore, the object of the divine command, would have taken place at least twice, and been the work of at least two of the six days, namely the first and the fourth. I said "at least": for if already from the first day the atmosphere existed and was not very different from the present one, even if it were much more humid -- then what, I ask, was the work of the second day, if not a less imperfect clarification of the same air, by which that portion of the atmosphere lying between the clouds and the lower waters became transparent? As happens when a lower fog dissolves or rises, but the sky remains covered with a continuous cloud. It does not seem, however, that it can be granted that in Moses's narrative one and the same operation is introduced as having been commanded by God two or three times.

Certain modern writers have asserted by conjecture that this light is the ether which, when set in motion, generates light by its waves. But, as I have said, the ether must be held to have been created from the beginning together with heaven and earth. Moreover, ether differs from light as air differs from sound; for it does not shine by itself, unless waves are excited in it by another body through communication of motion; and therefore it cannot by itself be called or (light), which is light or luminous heat or fire, nor is it opposed to darkness, nor should it be called day (verses 4, 5).

John Andrew De Luc (Letters on the Physical History of the Earth, to Prof. Blumenbach, Letters 2 and 3) supposed that the earth, after creation, was a great mass of dust particles conglomerated from various substances, and he further asserted that water did indeed then exist, but not in liquid state, from a lack of heat which, just as it is necessary for chemical combinations, is likewise required for water to attain that liquid state. Therefore, in order for the water to be dissolved, and the other substances in it, it was necessary for that dusty mass to be penetrated by fire to a certain depth, from which the resulting solution would possess the temperature necessary for chemical operations. Fire, says De Luc, is made from the combination of light with the element of fire: therefore it was necessary for light to be joined with all the other elements: from this union alone the origin of all geological phenomena is to be traced. Now this coupling of light with the terrestrial mass cannot be traced to any physical cause known to us. Therefore nature itself makes us certain of the first command of God reported by Moses. It would not be difficult to refute these things by the doctrines of sounder physico-chemistry; but in this place I do not think it necessary. It will be enough to observe that these things were entirely devised arbitrarily; nor does it seem plausible that, if that substance which De Luc calls light and considers so necessary truly existed, it was not then created by God when He gave existence to the other corporeal substances. Moreover, fire or luminous heat is more suited to drying the terrestrial mass and resolving water into vapors than to liquefying the substance of water and dissolving other mineral substances within it. The degree of heat that suffices for the boiling of water is far inferior to the temperature of luminous heat; and even if we suppose that the boiling point for that dissolution was not 100 degrees Celsius (212 Fahrenheit), but 200 or 210 degrees Celsius (corresponding to a pressure of 17 atmospheres), this heat would still be far below the degree of luminous heat or ignition: it would not, therefore, deserve the name of light (or).

XXXIV. Pianciani's Chemical Cosmogony Theory

It is easily understood, it seems to me, what this light was, if we attend to the laws imposed by God on nature. Elementary molecules, obeying the law of universal gravity, necessarily had to approach one another, and thus those which had been preordained by the Creator for forming our earth would, being associated, constitute this globe. Therefore, as these molecules encountered one another from every direction, after they had sufficiently approached each other, chemical combinations had to be accomplished according to chemical laws. Thus from simple or elementary molecules, compounds were produced; and among these, a great quantity of water vapor was generated. And lest we flee to hypotheses that are entirely unnecessary, we can conjecture that the first combinations were those which require not at all an elevated temperature, but for which the ordinary temperature of the earth, even in winter, suffices. Thus iron combines with oxygen, when it is very finely ground, and not without the emission of luminous heat: for iron truly burns when exposed to atmospheric air. So also the metals called nickel, cobalt, and uranium. Likewise at ordinary temperature, with the emission of fire, chlorine combines with antimony (stibium) and certain other metals, and iodine with phosphorus. These or similar processes generated some light, and at the same time raised the temperature: from which new combinations necessarily arose, and light and heat increased more and more. Among these new combinations, indeed one of the principal ones, must be considered the synthesis of water, which produces a great quantity of luminous heat; and since there was present the greatest abundance of those elements of which water consists (namely oxygen and hydrogen), heat and light must have increased immensely, and new affinities must have been excited therefrom. Together with water, or because of the heat produced by nascent water, there could be generated what are called fixed alkalis and earths, such as potash (kali), soda (natron), lime, magnesia, alumina, silica, etc., and acids, for example carbonic, sulphurous, sulphuric, phosphoric acid, and sulphides and chlorides, etc. From these combinations new increases of heat and light occurred. Meanwhile these syntheses and combustions, together with the light and heat of which they were both the effects and the causes, were diffused through space. More could be added; but it is not necessary: for even these things will perhaps seem excessive to some. Now imagine some observer placed in space, directing the sharpness of his eyes toward the newly born earth. What will he see? At first, indeed, nothing, because darkness is upon the face of the deep: but after a certain time he will see light, at first perhaps moderate and gentle, but rapidly growing, and soon, from excessive brilliance, preventing any other sight to his eyes. Therefore nature itself leads us to acknowledge that the single appearance of light was the first phenomenon on the earth. Nor was anything in this happening by chance or at random, but all things were governed by the command and wisdom of the Creator.

He said: Let there be light; He was commanding, that is, that luminous heat or fire should appear both then and afterward, whenever chemical combinations became sufficiently strong; and bodies obeyed His command, just as even now in luminous combinations, which are combustions, they obey. The sacred writer indicates only the first cause, namely God's command, and the visible effect; about other effects he is silent. For since he had not set himself the task of teaching cosmogony or chemistry to the Hebrews, it was not necessary for his purpose to discourse about the rest. Unless I am mistaken, this Mosaic light appears so evident that to laboriously seek another light seems to me the same as lighting a smoky torch of cheap tallow while the sun shines at noon.

That pagan writer of keen judgment (the author of the book On the Sublime Style of Oration, which circulates under the name of Longinus, Section VII) admired the sublimity of this expression: "Let there be light, and there was light," in which "the legislator of the Jews declared the power of the divine Being in a manner worthy of it." Is not the wisdom even more admirable in Moses's words, by which in a few words he declares what science reveals to us only after so many centuries? The word or (light) could well be rendered as fire (ur: fire, hearth; Ezekiel V, 2); for the root or, remaining in the Arabic language, means he set on fire, he burned. Nor is this interpretation new. Gregory of Nyssa thought that first light was fire, which from the formless and confused mass, by the nod and command of God, suddenly burst forth and illuminated all things with sudden brightness. Also Severian of Gabala (Homilies 1 and 4, On the Creation of the World), Caesarius in Dialogue I, question 58, and St. John Damascene believed that light was fire. To these add that in Mark XIV, ur (light) signifies fire, and the Vulgate renders it excellently: "He was sitting with the servants by the fire." In common usage, by the name of fire we indeed understand something in a state of luminous combustion or ignited by extreme heat.

It should be noted that Moses does not say this light was in the heavens, which he writes in verses 14, 15, 17 about the sun and stars; but generally, that at God's command there was light. Petavius thought that in "that vast nebula and vapor which Scripture calls water and the deep," this light appeared, which he says was the view of Julius Africanus and Bede. But Moses does not place it solely on the earth or above the face of the earth; rather, using an indefinite expression, he does not prevent us from representing to our minds a far greater conflagration, or even a universal one, which the argument from analogy seems to make probable. We, however, are speaking only about the earth. In any case, that image represents nothing sad, but rather something magnificent, a spectacle no less full of joy than of majesty. For that conflagration was innocent, one which not only did no damage, but also generated water and other things useful or necessary to future living beings.

St. Ephrem says more than once (On Genesis, book I, ch. II) that God could not only create plants, but also make them grow and bear fruit without water; but since He had decreed to entrust the fertility of the earth to water, therefore, he says, He willed that water should also cooperate in their first formation, as did light; therefore He produced both water and light before plants and grasses. Thus we may say that God could have created water from the beginning; but since He had determined that it should not be produced otherwise in the course of ages than from the coupling of its elements, He willed that these should pre-exist and serve for the formation of water properly so called. Which we can likewise say about other inorganic compounds; indeed more about these than about plants, which do not come into being from water and light alone without a pre-existing seed.

To those saying that the first "light was a certain luminous cloud, which afterward, when the sun was made, returned into pre-existing matter," St. Thomas objects: "Scripture at the beginning of Genesis commemorates the institution of nature which afterward persists: hence it should not be said that something was then made which afterward ceased to exist" (I part., Quest. LXVII, art. 4, ad 2). This might seem to be objectable to our explanation. But the answer would be easy, if anyone raised this objection. For we say that no substance was made on the first day which afterward ceased to exist. Only in our view was God's law about light being produced from chemical combinations, so to speak, promulgated at that time; which law persists still, and by its power light is always being generated. Moreover, very probably the light in that first period was kindled not only on the earth but also in the sun, perhaps also in the fixed stars, and in these it persists. And those substances which we suppose were then made from that light or fire, such as water and earths, have by no means afterward ceased to exist.

XXXV. "God saw the light" / "He divided light from darkness"

God saw the light, that it was good. Good indeed and beautiful is light (the Hebrew tov signifies both good and beautiful); but the meaning of this statement is better perceived, unless I am mistaken, if according to the doctrine set forth, that light is considered as having generated so many things useful to us, especially water. But although God bestowed this praise both on the light and on His other works (verses 10, 12, 18, 21, 25), He is nevertheless not said to have bestowed it on the whole corporeal substance when He produced it from nothing in the beginning. Why is this? Mazzocchius answers (Spicilegium biblicum): "Because He Himself by Himself wrought, no approbation follows. Then, after universal matter had been prepared, He is written here and in what follows to have assigned nearly each individual work to creatures to accomplish: Let the earth bring forth ... likewise elsewhere: Let the waters bring forth creeping things ... and again elsewhere: Let the earth bring forth cattle and creeping things. It is not surprising, then, if the supreme Craftsman is said to have measured these same works, after they were made, against the rules of His art, that is, of divine wisdom, and finally to have approved them." Thus it seems to be confirmed that the production of heaven and earth in verse 1 was a true creation, but not so the production of light on this first day.

He divided the light from the darkness (Vulgate); He separated between the light and between the darkness (Hebrew). These words are by no means opposed to what has been said so far. For Moses does not narrate that light arose in one region of the sky and set in the opposite after twelve hours. Those who say this did not draw it from Genesis, but from that propensity of our mind by which we often judge remote things unknown to the senses from present and known ones. Moses's statement is general: "there was light" -- which refers neither to Judea nor to Egypt more than to Mexico or to Maryland, especially since there was then no region, no island, and no continent. Basil, Gregory of Nyssa (section 34), Ephrem, and Procopius of Gaza thought that light was diffused everywhere. "The air was illuminated, says Basil (Homily 2 on the Hexaemeron), indeed it had the light mixed with itself, whole in the whole, transmitting the swift distributions of brightness to its own boundaries in every direction; for upward it reached to the very ether and to the sky, and in breadth it illuminated all the parts of the world, both northern and southern, eastern and western, in a brief moment of time." Ambrose agrees: "Suddenly the air shone bright, and the darkness trembled at the brightness of the new light; the radiance of the light poured throughout the whole world repressed them and, as it were, plunged them into the depths." John Damascene shows himself to be of the same opinion; for with Basil he believes the distinction of day and night at that time was made by "the effusion and contraction of light," or, as Eustathius, Basil's translator, explains, "by the diffusion of the principal light, now withdrawing itself, now restoring itself again." So the exposition of the faith published under Justin's name asserts that night was made "by the contraction and gathering of the light." Augustine mentions this opinion in Book I of On Genesis Literally, ch. XVI: "If we wish to understand that emission and contraction of light as day and night, we do not see a reason why it would happen: for there were not yet animals for whom this alternation would be healthfully provided." He also reports this opinion as not improbable in Book IV, ch. III. Basil (and Procopius with him) interprets "He divided the light" as: "that is, God rendered the nature of each incapable of any mixture, and set each in opposition to the other." Since darkness is the absence of light, it is contradictory both to exist at the same time: never in the same place and time is a union of light and darkness possible, and so by their very essence they are divided. God furthermore willed that neither perpetually nor in every place should either light or darkness prevail, but He divided their kingdoms between them.

And there was evening and morning (and there was evening and there was morning, Heb.), one day. That this first day is called not "first" but "one" has seemed to Philo, Origen, Basil, Caesarius, Procopius, and others to carry some mystical and hidden meaning; but it seems clear that this should rather be attributed to a certain property of the Hebrew language, by which cardinal numbers are frequently used in place of ordinals, as in Gen. 8:5, Num. 1:1 in Hebrew. In the New Testament, what Matthew (28:1) calls "the first of the Sabbath," John (20:1) calls "one of the Sabbath."

In this verse 5, most scholars think the word "day" has a double meaning ("he called the light day" ... "one day"), namely first referring to the period of light, and second to the civil day composed of day and night. The Syriac version uses two different words, a fact that L. Hirzel (On the Pentateuch, Syriac version, Leipzig, 1825, p. 63), praising the care of the scribe, notes. But truly this does not seem necessary to me: rather I would believe it more probable; since Moses immediately before these words said that God called the light "day" -- which means that the day is truly the period of light itself (The period of light is also called by Censorinus the natural and true day, On the Birthday, ch. 23) -- rather, I say, I would believe that the first day is called by him that period of time, however long it may have been, which is encompassed between the beginning and end of the primeval light, and which the darkness preceded. "The beginning of the day is the voice of God: Let there be light," says Ambrose. And Basil writes: "The state that existed in the world before the generation of light was not night, but darkness." And Augustine (Sermon 79, On Various Subjects): "Those shadows were not yet night, for day had not yet preceded: for God divided between the light and the darkness, and first called the light day, then the darkness night, and from the making of light until the next morning one day was reckoned: it is clear that those days began from light, and each was terminated at morning after the light had passed." So think many, among whom are Chrysostom, John Philoponus -- who attempts to refute Theodore of Mopsuestia who taught the contrary -- and Victorinus, who wrote a little book on this question, published with other works by P. Sirmond. There is not much strength in the argument to prove that these days began from evening, namely that the day among the Hebrews drew its beginning from evening; especially since those Jewish days extended from evening to evening, while the Mosaic days, if they began from evening because evening is named first, would have to be said to have ended with morning: which both greatly differs from Hebrew custom and is absurd, because the day would be the same as the night.

Concerning the words "evening" and "morning" we have said much above, in sections 11 and 12. It would seem credible that on the first day morning occurred when that light began, and evening when it was failing and growing weaker: for it is not plausible that it reached the highest degree of splendor immediately, or was extinguished in a moment, but it is more credible that it grew gradually and little by little up to maximum splendor, and after a certain time declined by degrees, and was finally altogether extinguished. But I dare not assert this, because the phrase "there was evening" is placed before the other phrase "and there was morning." Therefore one of these two views may be held: either that the first day, taking its beginning from the first appearance of light, then had its evening when that light began to fail; and the morning mentioned there was the beginning and, as it were, the dawn of the second day -- which accords excellently with Augustine's teaching in sections 9 and 11; or that Moses' words should be explained thus: "and there was darkness (which was not day, nor part of the day, but preceded the day), and there was morning (it was growing light, and then the first day came to be)."

That this latter interpretation is the more probable will appear in what follows. If, however, one preferred to interpret the words erev (evening) and boqer (morning) according to the origins of the words (section 11), Moses' words could be explained thus: "and there was commingling, and there was opening" -- or thus: "and there had been commingling, and there was morning, that is, an opening and first birth, a first bringing-forth." Indeed, just as the darkness that existed in the beginning seems less properly called "evening" -- which in Latin is an imperfect darkness after light and day -- so the name "commingling" suits it excellently, not only because in darkness nothing distinct appears, no shape, no color, but especially because in that first period nothing retained its proper form, and the air, lacking light, and all the elemental substances were mixed together and confused, including those that were destined to form the first compound bodies. That commingling and total darkness are not properly part of the day, but they are mentioned because the beginning of the day presupposes that they preceded it; for from their cessation the day comes into being. And it was fitting that the first light be called "morning" even antonomastically, and an "opening" and "first birth," because, as if the abyss had been opened, from that dark womb light came forth, by which the corporeal world then first appeared and was brought to light, and the firstborn among composite substances were then for the first time produced.

XXXVI. God's Naming of Day and Night

God called the light (or the period of light) "day" and the darkness (the period of darkness) "night," verse 5. The Latins sometimes conversely call the day itself "light," as in that saying of Tullius: "This is the hundredth light since the death of P. Clodius." Surely one would be simple-minded who believed that God then actually uttered these words, though no one was listening -- that is, that He made them resound through empty spaces. "It was not yet a matter of names, says Augustine; afterward the terms 'day' and 'night' were applied." "He called" is a metonymy: God willed that for some time light should reign and produce that which is called day, and that for some time darkness should reign, through which (that is, from the absence of light) that state of things is produced which we call night. "It is understood, teaches St. Thomas (Part I, Question 68, art. 2), everywhere by the phrase 'He called,' that is, He gave the nature and property so that it could be so called." Furthermore, to impose names is a sign of dominion: for the custom prevailed among Eastern peoples that whoever was subject to another's dominion received a name from him, 4 Kings 23:34; 24:17; Dan. 1:6-7. When dominion over the animals is granted to Adam, his first act of dominion is the imposition of names, Gen. 2:19. In like manner, by this imposition of names, the supreme dominion of God over day and night -- which is over all time -- seems to be indicated here, just as shortly after, in verses 8 and 10, the dominion of God over the heavens, earth, and seas -- which is over all things that exist in space -- is demonstrated by the names "heaven, earth, and seas" imposed by Him. "Yours is the day, and yours is the night" (Ps. 73:16). "Yours are the heavens, and yours is the earth. The sea you created..." (Ps. 88:12).

It is remarkable that even among the pagans a memory of the names of day and night having been divinely imposed is found. For Proclus says that day and night are gods, "whose names we received from the gods themselves." Mazzocchi thinks that Proclus drew this from this passage of Genesis, or at least the one from whom Proclus received it. Mazzocchi was not able to see Fronto, more ancient than Proclus, the tutor of Marcus Aurelius, epistle 3 on the Alsian holidays: for Fronto's writings were still hidden in the Ambrosian library (whence the illustrious Angelo Mai, Cardinal of the Holy Roman Church, a man most deserving of letters, extracted and published much of Fronto's work). Here are his words pertinent to our subject: "They say that Father Jupiter, when he was establishing human affairs from the beginning, struck the age through its middle with one blow and split it into two everywhere equal parts: one part he clothed with light, the other with darkness: he called them day and night: and to night he assigned rest, to day business."

XXXVII. Duration of the Primeval Luminous Heat

The span of time during which that luminous heat lasted, whether while the terrestrial globe was being formed or after it was already sufficiently constituted, I certainly cannot determine. We may conjecture, however, that that greatest of all conflagrations did not cool down very quickly. For it must be supposed that this newly born globe of the earth was entirely ignited, at least from the surface to a certain depth, both from the chemical combinations that were occurring there and from the ignited particles falling from the upper region by which the mass of the globe itself was growing. Moreover, this surrounding upper region, being itself ignited, preserved the maximum heat of the globe. But if the conflagration extended beyond the sublunary spaces and reached out to other celestial bodies, the immense force of radiation converging from all parts of the sky toward the earth must have long impeded and delayed the cooling of the earth itself, which could have occurred from the radiation of its own heat. It is fitting here briefly to note that a human being, had one been present at that time, would not have been able to measure this period of ignition and light; for since light was shining everywhere and always, those alternations of darkness and light by which we measure time were lacking. At length the earth's surface lost that temperature of luminous heat (and the surrounding space, which was occupied by particles of various kinds, lost it even more quickly): but it should not be supposed that the work of the second day began immediately. For this presupposes true and properly so-called, or liquid, water existing upon the face of the earth, verses 6 and 7: but water, as soon as the splendor of light had ceased -- indeed even long after that -- must have retained so much heat that it could not for a long time maintain a liquid state, even granting that it is probable that the degree of temperature at which water boils, on account of the greater atmospheric pressure, was somewhat more elevated during that period. Therefore this second span of time could have constituted the evening which preceded the second day or period. For anyone to whom this does not appeal, one may consider this period of darkness of which we are speaking as an interval interposed between the first and second day: for it is not necessary that these Mosaic days immediately succeeded one another with no intervening span of time, section 11.

Note well: I strongly commend the wisdom that shines forth in the words of Moses, but I assert nothing about his knowledge of natural things. Whether he himself understood those physical truths which lay hidden beneath the surface of his words, and which the wise men of his own time no less than those of later centuries were ignorant of; or whether, being himself ignorant of these things too, he wrote only what he had received either from an immediate revelation of God or from the tradition of his forefathers divinely transmitted -- on this matter I wish to define nothing. The Hebrews of that age certainly could not understand truths of this kind, but nor was it necessary: it was enough that they believed. As regards the work, for example, of the first day, it sufficed for them to believe that, after the creation of things, light or fire had shone forth by God's will; and that this light was not God -- which the Egyptians appear to have taught -- but merely the work of God.

If any should think that natural explanations of the primeval light ought to be rejected and that in this matter one must have recourse only to the supernatural, I would indeed wish that they recall to memory the teaching of Aquinas, who in the last article concerning the work of the first day (Part I, Question 67, art. 4, reply to objection 3), speaking of the "emission and contraction of light" posited by Basil (section 35), says: "Moreover, it is not in the nature of a luminous body to retract its light in its own presence, but this can happen miraculously. In the first institution of nature, however, a miracle is not sought, but what the nature of things possesses, as Augustine says."

(Verses 6, 7, 8)

XXXVIII. The Firmament / Expanse (Raqia)

(Note: The pages containing the beginning of sections XXXVIII and XXXIX -- pages XCIV and XCV of the original commentary -- were not included in the scanned source material for this translation. The text resumes mid-section XXXIX below.)

XXXIX. Waters Above and Below

That the Craftsman should be considered gentle no less than powerful, inasmuch as at the very origin of things and of all nature, He especially prescribed to each thing its own measure, order, and law, which He willed to be observed thereafter -- when, as Scripture says (Proverbs VIII, 27, 29), He surrounded the deeps with a fixed law and circle; when He established the ether above and balanced the fountains of the waters, and set a law for the waters that they should not transgress their bounds -- it is not consistent, I say, that He should then have begun something extraordinary and violent, and should have commanded that very thing to be constant and perpetual.

Certain people have said that clouds are distinguished in the Scriptures from "the waters which are above the heavens." But in Psalm CXLVIII, where all heavenly and earthly things are invited to praise God, I see no mention of clouds, which nevertheless strike our eyes with their very conspicuous magnitude: only "all waters that are above the heavens" are invited. In the canticle, indeed, of the three youths, as we call it, both are mentioned. For first we read: "Bless the Lord, O heavens. Bless the Lord, all you waters that are above the heavens." And afterward a review of meteorological things follows, which, before we descend to earth, is terminated with these words: "Bless the Lord, lightning and clouds." But the upper waters are both disseminated vapors, which merely diminish the transparency of the air, and clouds, whether they extend into cirrus formations, or are heaped into cumulus formations, or spread out in strata, which are the three principal forms of clouds. The clouds that are named together with lightning are storm clouds, which have the appearance neither of cirrus, nor of cumulus, nor of a single uniform stratum or elevated fog, but a disturbed and broken form, which indicates the tumult and disturbance of the sky. One can therefore respond: in these verses phenomena are enumerated rather than substances (nights, days, light, darkness, lightning); and further, that lightning and clouds are the same as the lightning of clouds (which interpretation most appeals to me), or lightning and the clouds that hurl it. Storm clouds are a species; the upper waters are the genus. In this canticle, moreover, certain species are called upon after their genus: as after all the works of the Lord, individual ones are named; after the earth, mountains; after the sons of men, Israel; after Israel, the priests of the Lord.

I would not assert that water exists nowhere except on our globe and in its atmosphere. Water can exist, and indeed most abundantly, in many other globes, and that it is found in at least some is also probable. Whether indeed anything about these things was revealed to Moses, I do not know. But let no one of sound mind believe that he is speaking about waters of this kind, rather than about those which the Jews could perceive with their senses when they were reading these things; or persuade himself that a firmament was made by God which separates between terrestrial waters and those, for example, which are located on the planet Mars.

The vapor remained in the elevated regions of this sphere, where the cooling had been greater than in the lower part; and thus it formed a sort of cloudy vault, which was separated by that transparent atmosphere from the water covering the earth: this therefore "was dividing between the upper and lower waters," or what is the same thing, it separated between the waters which were under this aerial expanse, and between the waters which had settled above it. That cloudy vault indeed blocked the sight of the stars, but it allowed solar light to pass through sufficiently, just as also now happens when clouds cover the sky. What difference there might have been between that atmosphere and the one we now use, I do not dare to say for certain: perhaps the condition and state of it was such that it would by no means allow the cloudy vault to be split or dissipated. Meanwhile it is certain and established that all these things were done by the plan of the laws imposed by God, namely by the will of the Creator and Preserver of nature: therefore God alone is rightly said to have commanded and done these things with the best right. For His will is the one efficient cause of natural effects, inasmuch as these do not depend on the will of creatures.

Moses does not report all these things which happened on the second day: it is enough for him if he narrates that the atmosphere separating the waters from the waters was made by divine command; by which thing our earth was proceeding not a little toward that perfection which the Maker of things had decreed to bestow upon it before the creation of man.

XL. Against Literal Waters Above the Stars

On the occasion of this passage many have held that real waters, which they also called elemental, had been spread above the region of the air and through the entire heaven, even through the heaven of the fixed stars and above it. We have no need of these waters (which some affirmed were far purer and more genuine than those which the earth sustains) to explain the words of Moses; indeed this supposition does not at all agree with them. For where would a firmament or expanse (a real one, of course, for what does not exist no one has made) be found that separates the super-stellar waters from the terrestrial ones, and by its pressure prevents the conjunction of the former with the latter? Furthermore, by what means or to what end are waters held suspended in the middle of space, lest they rush down to the earth or to some celestial globe? Some fled to the power of God; but Augustine (On Genesis, literally, II, ch. I) prudently admonished them: "Now how God has established the natures of things according to His scriptures, it is fitting for us to inquire; not what He may wish to do in them or from them for the miracle of His power." Petavius (On Creation, lib. I, ch. XI, 3) should also be heard: "Nor does it escape me that it can be divinely brought about that in the highest part of the world, however fluid and flowing the moisture, it contains itself, nor flows through the underlying liquid and even penetrable bodies. But because it is contrary to the nature of waters to be restrained there, it is not probable that that supreme Maker, nor that the Craftsman should be considered gentle no less than powerful, inasmuch as at the very origin of things and of all nature, He especially prescribed to each thing its own measure, order, and plan, which He wished to be preserved for the future; when, as Scripture says, He enclosed the abysses by a fixed law and a circle; when He was strengthening the ether above, and balancing the springs of waters, and setting a law for the waters, lest they should cross their boundaries: it is not, I say, consistent that He would then have begun that which is extraordinary and violent, and would have commanded this same thing to be constant and perpetual."

XLI. The Feeble Light of the Second and Third Days

There is no mention in Moses of that feeble light, by no means comparable with the light of the first day, which we have attributed to this second day (and will attribute to the third), nor of the preceding darkness, unless we say that he indicates both by the words day, morning, and evening, which is very probable. At least the end of the first day indicates some darkness. That there was some light on the second and third days is the common opinion of the Doctors. "Again a new morning, the first evening now dispelled, the restored light made," says Marius Victor, treating of the second day. And indeed to posit any of these Mosaic days without light seems to be the same as positing a day without a day, verse 5.

You will perhaps say: the works which we have indicated require a span of time certainly far longer than one solar day. But the sun itself was producing the day, even though it did not show its face to the earth. Therefore this second day was certainly not one single day, nor begun by only one morning and concluded by one evening, but the sum of many and indeed very many days, each of which had its own morning and its own evening.

This difficulty we have already anticipated in the preceding sections. We must always keep before our eyes in the exposition of this chapter the saying of Augustine (On Genesis, literally, lib. V, ch. v): "We must not think of those days as these solar days..." Furthermore, we must remember that Moses is not here acting as the historian of some particular person or people or region, but of the entire terrestrial globe. When he treats of the history of mankind, if he mentions day or night, we will understand this to be said of the region where those people will be about whom he is speaking. When he says, for example in chapter XIV, that Abraham attacked by night those who were leading Lot captive, everyone will easily understand that it was then night in that very region, but not among his antipodeans. But in verse 8 of chapter I, of what region is Moses speaking? Everything was sea. On that watery globe, always from the time the sun's light began to appear, it was day, just as it is now on the terraqueous globe, even when the sky is covered with clouds, unless perchance some unusual cause takes away light from the hemisphere that should then be illuminated: but with this cause removed, not indeed in all parts of the globe, but on the globe nevertheless there is perpetual day. So in this second day or period let us hold that, after the intervening evening or darkness, there was continuous day, namely from the time the solar rays began to pass through the cloudy stratum, the orb of the earth never lacked this light, however feeble it may have been. Before this light appeared, there was evening; when first this light arose, then it was the morning of the second day. But there was evening again, as this day declined, about which we will speak when treating the third day.

Only this one thing I advise: it is not necessarily required of us that we posit the absence of light in these passages as absolutely complete; for here night or darkness is not mentioned, but only evening. The morning, however, at verse 13, will be the return of that light which inaugurates the third day.

XLII. Evening and Morning of the Second Day

We can also well interpret that verse 8 from the Hebrew. And there was (or there had been) a mixing (erev), and there was a dividing, or an opening, or a first birth (boqer): the second day. At the beginning of this period there was mixing, or there had been immediately before it, both because of the absence of light, and because the upper waters were mixed with the lower: there was no sea, no clouds separated through clear air from the globe lying beneath; but afterward there was also morning, because the restored light made a division between the upper celestial waters and the lower terrestrial waters; that dark and misty cloud which clothed the terrestrial globe and enveloped the nascent sea began to open, and through it a way was opened for the solar light, and first a transparent atmosphere was born between the waters and the waters, as well as light dividing in various parts of the globe between the domain of day or light, and of night or darkness.

Perhaps in the earliest times, when the temperature was far more elevated, the rotation of the earth and its atmosphere was slower, whose mass and bulk then existed far greater than they are now. But I do not believe that by this single greater length of days, which that rotation measures, the phenomena can be sufficiently accounted for, without our being concerned about another sense of those words day, evening, and morning. Even if we grant that in that early time the period of terrestrial rotation was longer, this difference could not have been so great as to be sufficient for explaining the effects which geologists have observed: therefore nothing can be so reconciled, nothing explained. There is therefore no reason to linger in this kind of investigation. For what would it matter if at that time the period of terrestrial rotation were contained in forty-eight or fifty hours? What even if in one or two months? I note, however, that the mass situated in space around the earth and rotating together with it was not so great after the second day, indeed not even throughout this entire day, after the globe was already covered with waters, nor was the temperature at its maximum, if it permitted the liquid state of the waters, nor was it very elevated on the third day when the earth brought forth herbs and trees: for it is well known that only a certain moderate mean temperature suits terrestrial plants, which is now between 32 degrees Fahrenheit (0 degrees of the centigrade and Reaumur thermometers), and 99.5 degrees Fahrenheit; nor do seeds germinate any longer that have experienced a temperature above 110.75 degrees Fahrenheit. On the fifth and sixth days, moreover, a temperature must be admitted that would be suitable for aquatic and terrestrial animals, and for man himself.

XLIII. The Firmament Is Not Solid Crystal

Before we leave this day of the firmament, it must be noted that the name of firmament by no means indicates here any kind of solid heaven, for example a crystalline one, of the sort which certain ancient Philosophers, much later than Moses, devised. Not to repeat what was written in section 38: "The firmament was named not from the proper condition of its nature," as I may say with Petavius, "but from its effect, because it separates the waters as if it were the strongest wall." Thus can be understood that passage from the book of Job XXXVII, 18: "Have you perhaps fashioned the heavens with him, which are most solid as if of cast bronze?" This is the one passage in the Scriptures which seems to favor solid heavens of this kind, which human imagination has contrived. Petavius says that the Hebrew word, which the Vulgate renders as "heavens," properly means clouds; and that the final words mean "like a cast mirror" (On Creation, lib. I, ch. x, 9). But we are little concerned about that passage: for it has no authority. For those words do not belong to the author of the book; nor does he here introduce God speaking thus, nor Job himself, but that Elihu, who has no authority, and whose manner of speaking seems afterward to be rebuked by God with these words: "Who is this that wraps up sentences in unskilled words?" chapter XXXVIII, verse 2.

The Alexandrine translators add at verse 8: "And God saw that it was good," said of the firmament: so Basil, and Ambrose imitating him, Chrysostom, and the other Greeks. This, however, is read neither in our Vulgate, nor in the Hebrew text, or in the Samaritan text, nor in the Samaritan, Syriac, and Arabic versions, nor in the Targum of Onkelos, nor finally in the Greek versions of Aquila, Symmachus, etc. It is therefore customarily asked why the second day lacks its own approval and commendation which is accorded to the works of the other days by the cited words: "And God saw," etc. This seems to be the reason for the matter. The work of this day was the distribution of the waters; but this was not completed by the firmament alone: for once the upper waters had been divided, it remained that these should recede from certain parts of the earth's surface, so that the dry land might appear, which could be clothed with the greenness of plants and sustain animals. Therefore to the work not yet completed, commendation is not denied but deferred and truly bestowed at verse 10, when it is now complete: with the addition of a second approval for the special work of the third day, the production of plants, which is mentioned in verse 12. Some, however, prefer to assign the emergence of the earth and the gathering of the waters to the second day, so that they leave no day without its own approval. Accordingly, what we read at verse 9 -- "And God said: Let the waters be gathered" -- they understand as if it were written: "God had said." But since the second day is closed at verse 8 with the customary formula "And there was evening," etc., this opinion seems less commendable. Moreover, as I already advised in section 30, if these days are periods of unknown duration, it matters little whether certain effects are assigned to the second or to the third.

ON THE WORK OF THE THIRD DAY

(Verses 9, 10, 11, 12)

XLIV. Gathering of Waters / Appearance of Dry Land

A twofold work of God Moses now sets before us: for he narrates that the waters were gathered into one place, a portion of the earth was laid bare and a dwelling place prepared for the habitation of future living creatures; then that the earth was clothed with the richness and abundance of plants.

God commands: Let the waters under the heaven be gathered into one place, verse 9 in the Hebrew, which the Vulgate translates "which are under heaven," as also the Septuagint (and the Targum of Jonathan: Let the lower waters which were left below under the heavens be gathered). Mazzocchi thinks that the Hebrew word which is rendered "let them be gathered" can be more faithfully rendered, though hardly in good Latin, as "let them be encaved" (that is, let them be stored away in a cavity) -- the waters ... in one place. Let the dry land appear. Earth not covered by waters is called by the Latins also "dry ground." Virgil: "Sea-coots play on the dry ground." Pliny: "Neither in the sea nor on dry ground." The Latin Church, at Psalm 94, presents for reading indiscriminately: "And his hands formed the dry land. And his hands founded the dry land."

Many believe, such as Petavius and Mazzocchi, that the phrase "into one place," as the Vulgate says with the Hebrew, or "into one gathering" (synagogen), as the LXX have it, must be understood distributively, that is, that each collection of waters flows into some one place; and if there are several collections, that nevertheless all water settles in a certain and predetermined place for itself. Basil taught that, since there are many collections of waters, Moses speaks of the chief and greatest of all; he adds that any lakes and ponds are not properly collections of waters: but those that are larger, as also those which, like the Caspian, are called seas, flow into the Ocean through open or hidden subterranean channels, and are united with it. So also Theodoret, Procopius, and Bede. Yet it is very probable that in that first time when the dry land appeared -- that is, when certain lands were uncovered and stripped of waters -- it is very probable, I say, that all the great collections of waters (as will appear from what is to be said) communicated with one another and so were gathered into one place and one congregation, with perhaps some small pond excepted.

XLV. What Caused the Waters to Recede

Let us now inquire what was the cause or occasion by which the waters receded and the dry land appeared. Was the cause simply God's command alone, "at the voice of whose thunder the waters feared and fled," as the Psalmist says poetically (Psalm 104:7-8), while "the mountains ascended and the plains descended into the place which He had founded for them"? Certainly those effects had no other first cause. But that that command of God was entirely distinct from the laws by which He Himself governs the world (which would mean it was a miracle), this does not seem necessary, and therefore according to the accepted rules of the wise should not be admitted. Some say that the sacred text indicates as the sole instrument of the works of creation the commands of God: "Let there be, let them be gathered"; and they add that God, unlike a human being, does not need external means for working. It should be noted first that creation is not the issue here: for nothing in this work is said to be created. When Moses indicates the first cause, which alone was appropriate to the end He intended, he certainly does not deny that in some law of God (that is, in some uniform and permanent volition of God, by which He afterward produced many other effects and indeed now produces them) this particular volition was included. God certainly does not need external means: but we should not ask "what divine power can do for the miracle of His power," but rather what He who "orders all things sweetly" is accustomed to do. It certainly seems more worthy of God's wisdom that He, through laws established by Himself in the beginning and co-created with matter, first brought it about that the newly born earth was covered with waters, so that while it lay hidden beneath them it might receive great accretions from the sediments of the water; but that afterward now these, now those parts of the earth should emerge from the waters, so that they might sustain plants and terrestrial living creatures. When I conceive of things thus, I am indeed compelled to exclaim: "In wisdom You have made all things" (Psalm 104:24). See how many effects proceed from few and simple laws!

Behold how, by one volition of God, all things proceed toward their goal. "This opinion (I use the words and teaching of Suarez, On the Angels, lib. I, no. 8) is more philosophical and more in conformity with reason, and should be adhered to more firmly, when Scripture does not compel otherwise." The same author teaches: "One should not have recourse to the first cause when effects can be reduced to secondary causes."

On the contrary, if I were to say that the earth was covered with waters solely by some particular command of God, and afterward by another command of God contrary to the first the earth itself emerged from the waters, without any cause or useful effect of this washing being apparent: I would indeed fear that I might expose Scripture to the mockeries of the wise men of this world, which we have already heard must be most carefully guarded against; but it is useful to hear more.

Scripture attributes natural effects to God: truly indeed and philosophically. For all effects which do not arise from created will must necessarily arise from the will of the Creator alone. And this doctrine suffices abundantly for most people: for those who have learned and hold it far surpass both those who of old "attending to the works, did not recognize who was the Craftsman; but either fire, or wind, or swift air, or the circuit of the stars, or the great water, or the sun and moon, they thought to be the gods that rule the world" (Wisdom 13:2); and the more recent pseudo-philosophers, who by the paths of the sciences have returned to ancient ignorance, and think that fire, water, the sun, universal attraction, affinity, and other laws of nature are the rulers of the world and the universe -- laws which, when they reject the Creator, are effects without a cause and laws without a lawgiver. Yet that which suffices for faith does not suffice also for science, whose task it is to endeavor to refer those effects which certainly did not arise from some particular will of God (such as creation and miracles) to some general law established by Him. Timaeus in Cicero teaches (Cicero, Timaeus, ch. XIV) that "he who professes himself a lover of intelligence and wisdom" must necessarily search out the first causes of nature, and then the secondary causes. And the Theologian has need of this science indeed, wherever some connection exists between natural and supernatural things, so that he may be able to refute those who contradict and resist the truth; to show that even in the sacred sciences (although the dogmas are immutable, because they are truth) there is "progress; so that what was formerly believed more obscurely may be understood more clearly through his exposition; so that through him posterity may rejoice in understanding what antiquity, not understanding, revered; yet transmitting the same doctrines which he learned, so that when he speaks in a new way, he does not say new things" (See Vincent of Lerins, Commonitorium, chapters XXIII, XXVIII).

XLVI. Geologists Agree with Moses

We return to the recession of the water, in which matter it happily turns out that geologists commonly agree with Moses. For they deduce from their observations that the earth was once covered with waters.
