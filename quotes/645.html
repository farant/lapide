A sick man feels for a sick man, and a hungry man for a hungry man, all the more intimately as they are closer in condition; for just as pure truth is seen only by a pure heart, so a brother's misery is more truly felt by a wretched heart; but in order to have a wretched heart on account of another's misery, you must first acknowledge your own, and you will find your neighbor's state of mind in your own, so that from yourself you may know how to help him.

— St. Bernard, in Cornelius a Lapide, Commentary on Exodus, Chapter II — "And When She Could No Longer Conceal Him"
