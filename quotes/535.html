See here in the words of Laban the character of the world. First, although he knew that by his own treachery he had given the just man reason to flee, he conceals this and casts all blame upon the just man. Thus the world conceals its own sins and casts all blame upon the pious. Secondly, Laban pretends to be a friend when he was an adversary. Thus the world speaks one way and thinks another: woe to the double-hearted! Thirdly, he reveals his own impiety and folly when he says: "Why have you stolen my gods?" It is impiety that he worships idols; it is folly that he calls them gods, when they cannot protect themselves from thieves. Fourthly, he says: "You have acted foolishly"; thus to the world whatever the pious do seems foolish. The just man did not act foolishly in seeking his homeland when oppressed, but the world acts foolishly in despising the heavenly homeland.

— Cornelius a Lapide, Commentary on Genesis, Chapter XXXI — "Why Have You Acted This Way?"
