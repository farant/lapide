    Organization

    • Church Institutions
      • Apostolic See — Episcopal jurisdiction of the Pope as Bishop of Rome; supreme authority on Scripture and its preservation
      • Church at Nola — Church built by St. Paulinus at Nola, with two shrines — one for the Sacred Eucharist and one for the sacred volumes — with inscribed verses
      • Church of Ghent — The local Church (diocese) of Ghent; its renewed discipline under Van der Burch described as a new radiance for all Belgium
      • Metropolitan Church of Mechlin — Cathedral church of the Archdiocese of Mechlin; Van der Burch presided as Dean
      • The Church — The universal body of Christian believers founded by Christ
      • The Presbytery — The body of ordained priests/elders; Paul writes to Timothy about the grace given by the laying on of hands of the presbytery
      • Vatican Press — Printing press of the Holy See; used for the authoritative edition of the corrected Vulgate under Clement VIII
    • Councils
      • Council of Nicaea — Ecumenical council (325) whose decrees against the Arians are cited by Athanasius regarding the meaning of creation
      • Council of Palestine — Council held under Pope Victor around A.D. 198; defined that the world was created in spring at the vernal equinox and that the first day was March 25
      • Council of Sirmium — Council held at Sirmium; Lapide dismisses it as an assembly of Arians whose decrees are not definitions of faith
      • Council of Trent — Ecumenical council of the Catholic Church (1545-1563); Session IV decrees on canonical Scripture and the Vulgate reproduced in this document
      • Fifth Lateran Council — Ecumenical council (1512-1517); referenced by the Council of Trent regarding penalties for unauthorized printing
      • Lateran Council (under Innocent III) — Ecumenical council (1215) referenced for its decree establishing the chair of sacred Letters
    • Dioceses
      • Archdiocese of Cambrai — Ancient archdiocese in the southern Low Countries/northern France; Van der Burch was inaugurated as its Archbishop
      • Archdiocese of Canterbury — English archdiocese; St. Edmund served as its Archbishop, spending days and nights in sacred Letters
      • Archdiocese of Mechlin — Catholic archdiocese in the Spanish Netherlands with its see at Mechlin; Van der Burch served as Dean and Vicar-General here
      • Diocese of Alutina — Diocese whose bishop Fundanus surrendered the sacred books to persecutors out of fear, after which a miraculous storm saved the books from burning
      • Diocese of Amiens — Catholic diocese in northern France; diocese of Augustin Crampon, annotator of this edition
      • Diocese of Avila — Spanish diocese; Blessed Alfonso Tostado served as Bishop of Avila and wrote extensively on the Old Testament
      • Diocese of Capua — Italian diocese; St. Benedict saw the soul of Germanus, Bishop of Capua, being carried to heaven by angels
      • Diocese of Ghent — Catholic diocese in Flanders; Van der Burch served as its Bishop before transfer to Cambrai
      • Diocese of Rochester — English diocese; Bl. John Fisher served as its bishop and refused transfer to any wealthier see
      • Diocese of Tinnin — Hungarian diocese (modern Croatia); seat of Bishop Andreas Dudecius, who appeared as legate at the Council of Trent but later apostatized
    • Governments
      • Assyrian Monarchy — One of the ancient monarchies that must be known to understand the Old Testament prophets and historical books
      • Council of Flanders — Governing council of the County of Flanders; Van der Burch's grandfather served as its President
      • Greek Monarchy — One of the ancient monarchies required for understanding the Old Testament
      • Holy Roman Empire — Supranational political entity in central Europe; Van der Burch held the title of Prince of the Holy Roman Empire
      • Iconoclast Emperors — Byzantine emperors Constantine Copronymus and Leo the Isaurian who promulgated Iconoclasm; opposed by Theodore the Studite
      • Kingdom of Aragon — Kingdom ruled by Alfonso, who boasted of having read the entire Bible with glosses and commentaries fourteen times
      • Kingdom of Egypt (Ptolemaic) — The Ptolemaic kingdom of Egypt; Ptolemy Philadelphus commissioned the Septuagint translation and deposited it in the Alexandrian library
      • Kingdom of Sicily — Kingdom ruled by Robert, who told Petrarch that letters were dearer to him than his kingdom
      • Medes Monarchy — One of the ancient monarchies required for understanding the Old Testament
      • Parliament of Mechlin — Supreme court of the Spanish Netherlands, sitting at Mechlin; Van der Burch's father served as its first President
      • Persian Monarchy — One of the ancient monarchies required for understanding the Old Testament
      • Privy Council — Advisory council to the sovereign of the Spanish Netherlands; Van der Burch's father served as President
      • Roman Empire — The Roman state and its institutions; referenced for military heroes and for the veneration of Moses among the Romans
      • Roman State — The Roman state as a chronological entity; its founding is used for cross-referencing sacred chronology
      • Seleucid Dynasty — Dynasty founded after the death of Alexander the Great; its dating system is used by the books of the Maccabees
      • Spanish Netherlands — Southern provinces of the Low Countries under Spanish Habsburg sovereignty; ruled by Archdukes who governed civil and ecclesiastical affairs
    • Institution
      • Academy of Athens — Philosophical school founded by Plato in Athens
      • Albigensians — Medieval heretical movement in southern France that proscribed the Old Testament
      • Alexandrian Library — The great library at Alexandria; Ptolemy Philadelphus had the Septuagint and Hebrew manuscripts deposited there through Demetrius
      • Alexandrian School — Early Christian catechetical school at Alexandria, parent of Doctors and Prelates; founded by St. Mark, with Origen as its most famous teacher
      • Anabaptists — Radical Reformation sect that proscribed the Old Testament, boasting of being moved and taught by the enthusiasm of the spirit
      • Anthropomorphites — Heretical sect whose originator was Audaeus; held that man is the image of God according to the body and therefore that God is corporeal
      • Arians — Followers of the heresy of Arius who denied the full divinity of Christ
      • Calvinists — Protestant movement following John Calvin, who used Old Testament passages to argue for predestination and God as author of evil
      • Ebionites — Early Jewish-Christian heretical sect; Symmachus, the fourth translator of Hebrew Scripture into Greek, was first an Ebionite
      • Essenes — Early Jewish-Christian community at Alexandria identified as the first Alexandrian Christians; spent entire days studying sacred volumes
      • Libertines — Heretical sect that set up their own reason and inclination as the guide of faith and morals, rather than Scripture
      • Lutherans — Protestant movement; referenced through Matthias Flacius Illyricus whose teaching on original sin is condemned
      • Manichaeans — Heretical sect founded by Curbicus the Persian (called Manes and Manichaeus); proscribed the Old Testament; St. Augustine wrote 33 books Against Faustus in refutation
      • Marcionites — Heretical sect founded by Marcion, who proscribed the Old Testament and taught it was produced by evil angels; Tertullian wrote four books against them
      • Master of the Sacred Palace — Office in the papal household; St. Dominic was created the first holder, and from that time the dignity adhered to the Order of Preachers
      • Olympiads — Ancient Greek chronological system used by pagan historians for dating
      • Origenists — Followers of Origen's theological errors; Gregory Thaumaturgus received a creed from St. John to set against them
      • Peripatetic School — Philosophical school of Aristotle; Justin chose a Peripatetic master but despised him for hawking wisdom for a price
      • Platonic School — Philosophical school of Plato; Justin went to a Platonist but was deceived with a vain hope of wisdom, before finding truth in the Prophets
      • Priscillianists — Heretical sect that, like the Manichaeans, taught there are two principles of things or two gods
      • Saxon Lutherologists — Lutheran theologians from Saxony who argued at the Regensburg disputation for proscribing traditions and establishing Scripture alone as the judge of faith controversies
      • School at Nisibis — Schools of sacred Scripture established at Nisibis among the Syrian Hebrews in the time of Emperor Justinian
      • School at York — School of sacred Letters in England where Alcuin publicly taught; St. Ludger traveled from Frisia to hear him
      • Simonians — Heretical sect founded by Simon Magus, who proscribed the Old Testament by teaching it was produced by a sinister power and evil angels
      • Stoic School — Philosophical school; St. Justin attached himself to a Stoic teacher but heard nothing about God from him
      • Studios Monastery — Celebrated monastery at Constantinople named from its founder and the study of sacred Letters; Theodore the Studite combated the Iconoclast emperors from here around 800 AD
      • The Pythagoreans — Philosophical school founded by Pythagoras
      • The Seventy Translators — The translators of the Hebrew Bible into Greek (the Septuagint); Lapide attributes the division and naming of the Pentateuch to them
      • The Synagogue — Jewish assembly and place of worship
    • Publishers
      • Cramoisy — Parisian publisher; printed Lapide's commentary on the Proverbs of Solomon in 1635
      • Ludwig Vives (Publisher) — Bookseller and publisher in Paris; published the 1891 Crampon edition of Lapide's commentaries
      • Nutius/Moretus — Printers of Antwerp; granted permission to print Lapide's Pentateuch commentaries in 1616
      • Poussielgue-Rusand — Parisian publisher; published Lacordaire's Letters to a Young Man on the Christian Life (1858)
    • Religious Orders
      • Franciscan Order — Religious order founded by St. Francis of Assisi; Francis granted his followers the study of sacred Letters, and St. Anthony of Padua was the first from this family to teach them
      • Louvain College — Jesuit college at Louvain; mentioned by Lapide as bearing esteem toward Van der Burch
      • Order of Preachers — Catholic religious order founded by St. Dominic in 1216
      • Province of Flandro-Belgica — Jesuit administrative province covering the southern Low Countries; Charles Scribani served as Provincial Superior
      • Society of Jesus — Catholic religious order founded by St. Ignatius of Loyola in 1540; Cornelius a Lapide was a member
    • Universities
      • University of Cologne — University descended from the Parisian academy; described as the daughter of Paris and mother of Louvain
      • University of Louvain — Catholic university in Louvain; where Lapide held the chair of Sacred Scripture for over twenty years
      • University of Paris — University established by Charlemagne at Paris; described as the mother of Cologne and the grandmother of Louvain
