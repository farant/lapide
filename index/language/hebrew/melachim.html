מְלָכִים

Transliteration
melakhim
Old Hebrew
𐤌𐤋𐤊𐤉𐤌
Meaning
Kings — Hebrew name for the books of Kings (3-4 Kings in the Vulgate enumeration)
Root
מלך

The Hebrew name for the books of Kings (3-4 Kings in the Vulgate enumeration). Jerome argues it is better to say Melachim ("Kings") than Mamlachot ("Kingdoms").

References in Commentary

  • Clemens Hieronymi Du Culte — Helmeted Prologue P12 — Jerome identifies Melachim as the fourth book of the Prophets, meaning "of Kings"
    "Fourth, Melachim, that is, of Kings, which is contained in the third and fourth volume of Kings."
  • Clemens Hieronymi Du Culte — Helmeted Prologue P13 — Jerome argues it is better to say Melachim ("Kings") than Mamlachot ("Kingdoms"), because it describes one Israelite people, not many nations
    "And it is much better to say Melachim, that is, of Kings, than Mamlachot, that is, of Kingdoms. For it does not describe the kingdoms of many nations, but of one Israelite people, which is comprised of twelve tribes."
  • Clemens Hieronymi Du Culte — Helmeted Prologue P2 — Jerome lists Melachim among the five books considered double
    "five books are considered by most to be double: Samuel, Melachim, Dibre hajamim, Ezra, Jeremiah with Cinoth"
  • Clemens Hieronymi Du Culte — Helmeted Prologue P29 — Jerome calls these books his own translation
    "Read therefore first my Samuel and my Melachim -- mine, I say, mine."
  • Clemens Hieronymi Du Culte — Jerome To Paulinus P20 — Jerome uses the Hebrew name again in his survey of the biblical books for Paulinus
    "Melachim, that is, the third and fourth book of Kings, from Solomon to Jeconiah, and from Jeroboam son of Nebat to Hosea"
