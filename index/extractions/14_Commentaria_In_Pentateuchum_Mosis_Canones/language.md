# Language — 14_Commentaria_In_Pentateuchum_Mosis_Canones

## Hebrew

### olam (עוֹלָם)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/olam`
- **Script**: עוֹלָם
- **Transliteration**: olam
- **Meaning**: "eternal, age, hidden time" — signifies an age whose limit is not perceived
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p5` — Lapide explains that "eternal" in Hebrew does not mean absolute eternity but a hidden period of time; derives from the root alam (to hide)
    text: " the Hebrew olam, that is, "eternal," signifies an age, inasmuch as it is hidden, or whose limit and end is not perceived. For the root alam means to hide or conceal"

### alam (עָלַם)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/alam`
- **Script**: עָלַם
- **Transliteration**: alam
- **Meaning**: "to hide, to conceal"
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p5` — Root of olam, explaining the etymology of "eternal" as "hidden"
    text: "For the root alam means to hide or conceal"
- **Related**: olam

### banim (בָּנִים)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/banim`
- **Script**: בָּנִים
- **Transliteration**: banim
- **Meaning**: "sons" — etymologically linked to abanim (stones) and bana (he built)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p11` — Lapide explains that Hebrews call sons banim, as if abanim (stones), from bana (he built), because families are built from sons as from stones
    text: " the Hebrews call sons banim, as if abanim, that is, "stones," from the root bana, that is, "he built"; for from sons as from stones the houses and families of parents are built"

### abanim (אֲבָנִים)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/abanim`
- **Script**: אֲבָנִים
- **Transliteration**: abanim
- **Meaning**: "stones" — connected etymologically by Lapide to banim (sons)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p11` — Etymology linking sons and stones
    text: "the Hebrews call sons banim, as if abanim, that is, \"stones\""
- **Related**: banim, bana

### bana (בָּנָה)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/bana`
- **Script**: בָּנָה
- **Transliteration**: bana
- **Meaning**: "he built"
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p11` — Root verb from which banim derives, explaining the metaphor of building families
    text: " from the root bana, that is, "he built"; for from sons as from stones the houses and families of parents are built"
- **Related**: banim, abanim

### saphan (שָׁפָן)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/saphan`
- **Script**: שָׁפָן
- **Transliteration**: saphan
- **Meaning**: "coney, hare, or hedgehog" — a general Hebrew animal name with different referents in different passages
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p34` — Lapide explains that Hebrew animal names are general and common to many species
    text: "saphan, Leviticus 11:5, signifies the coney; but Proverbs 30:26, it signifies the hare; Psalm 104:18, however, it signifies the hedgehog"

## Greek

### ankon (ἀγκών)
- **Slug**: `greek/ankon`
- **Script**: ἀγκών
- **Transliteration**: ankon
- **Meaning**: "elbow"
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p20` — Lapide explains the Roman name Ancus derives from the Greek word for elbow
    text: " Ancus was named from his crooked elbow, says Festus — for "elbow" in Greek is called ankon"

### hen dia dyoin (ἓν διὰ δυοῖν)
- **Slug**: `greek/hen-dia-dyoin`
- **Script**: ἓν διὰ δυοῖν
- **Transliteration**: hen dia dyoin
- **Meaning**: "one through two" — the rhetorical figure hendiadys
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p46` — Lapide defines hendiadys as a figure dividing one thing into two
    text: "a figure by which one thing is divided into two, whence it is more correctly called hen dia dyoin, that is, one through two"
