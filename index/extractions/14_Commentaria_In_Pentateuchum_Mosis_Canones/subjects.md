### Biblical Canon (Hebrew)
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/biblical-canon`
- **Description**: The Hebrew division of the Old Testament into three classes -- Torah (Law), Nebiim (Prophets), and Ketubim (Hagiographa) -- with twenty-two books corresponding to the Hebrew letters, as attested by Jerome.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#argumentum-p2` — Lapide explains the threefold Hebrew division of the Old Testament and identifies the Torah as the Pentateuch.
    text: "The Hebrews, as St. Jerome attests in his Helmeted Prologue, count as many books of Sacred Scripture — that is, of the Old Testament — as they have letters, namely twenty-two, and divide them into three classes: namely, Torah, that is, the Law; Nebiim, that is, the Prophets; and Ketubim, that is, the Hagiographa."
  - `#argumentum-p3` — The Former and Latter Prophets are enumerated.
    text: "They count a double set of Prophets, the Former and the Latter: they call the Former Prophets Joshua, Judges, Ruth, and the four books of Kings; the Latter Prophets they reckon as Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the twelve minor prophets."
  - `#argumentum-p4` — The Hagiographa are enumerated.
    text: "The Hagiographa they count as Job, the Psalms, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, the Song of Songs, Daniel, the books of Chronicles, Ezra, and Esther."
- **Related people**: Jerome, Moses
- **Related works**: Jerome's Helmeted Prologue (Prologus Galeatus)
- **Related verses**: none

### Mosaic Authorship of the Pentateuch
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/inspiration`
- **Description**: The authorship of the Pentateuch attributed to Moses, attested by Christ himself, with discussion of later editorial additions by Joshua or others, and the sources of Moses' knowledge (tradition, divine revelation, and personal observation).
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#argumentum-p6` — Moses is affirmed as the author of the Pentateuch, attested by all Greeks and Latins and by Christ himself.
    text: "The author of the Pentateuch is Moses: so teach all the Greeks and Latins, indeed Christ himself, as is clear from John 1:17 and 45; John 5:46, and elsewhere."
  - `#argumentum-p7` — Certain passages were added or arranged by Joshua or later editors, including the death of Moses in Deuteronomy and the praise of Moses' meekness in Numbers 12:3.
    text: "Moses simply wrote the Pentateuch in the manner of a diary or annals; yet Joshua, or someone like him, arranged these same annals of Moses into order, divided them, and added and interwove certain passages."
  - `#argumentum-p8` — Moses' threefold sources: tradition, divine revelation, and personal observation.
    text: "Moses learned and received these things partly by tradition, partly by divine revelation, and partly by personal observation: for the things he narrates in Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy, he himself was present to see and do."
  - `#argumentum-p9` — Debate over when Moses wrote the Pentateuch: Pererius says during exile in Midian; Theodoret, Bede, and Tostatus say after the departure from Egypt while leading the people.
    text: "both Genesis and the four following books were written by Moses after the departure of the Hebrews from Egypt, when he himself was occupied in the wilderness as a leader, high priest, prophet, teacher, and lawgiver of the people"
- **Related people**: Moses, Joshua, Jerome, Pererius, Theodoret, Bede, Tostatus, Eusebius, Baruch, Jeremiah
- **Related verses**: John 1:17, John 1:45, John 5:46, Numbers 12:3, Genesis 14:15, Numbers 21:14-15, Numbers 21:27, Proverbs 25:1

### Scripture Interpretation (Literal Sense)
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/scripture-interpretation`
- **Description**: The primacy of the literal and historical sense in interpreting Scripture, especially in the Pentateuch, against Origen's purely allegorical reading. The canon that Moses' narratives about creation, paradise, Adam, and Eve are to be taken historically and properly.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p2` — Canon 1 establishes that Moses' narration is historical, simple, and plain, not symbolic or allegorical. This is against Origen, who thought all should be expounded allegorically. The Church condemns Origen's allegories.
    text: "his narration is not symbolic, not allegorical, not mystical, but historical, simple, and plain; and therefore the things he narrates about paradise, Adam, Eve, and the creation of all things completed successively over the space of six days, etc., are to be taken historically and properly, as they sound."
  - `#canons-p41` — Canon 36 affirms there can be multiple literal senses of Scripture, including both historical and prophetic, supported by Augustine (Confessions XII), Thomas Aquinas (ST I, q.1, a.10), and the Lateran Council.
    text: "That there can be multiple literal senses of Holy Scripture — not only typical and typically subordinated ones, but even dissimilar and disparate ones — is taught by St. Augustine, Confessions book 12"
  - `#canons-p45` — Canon 40 distinguishes the rigor of the literal sense (where all words must be applied to the thing signified) from the freedom of the allegorical sense, where the Fathers do not always observe the rigor of history.
    text: "In the literal sense, all sentences and all words must be explained and applied to the thing signified; but this is not necessary in the allegorical sense."
- **Related people**: Origen, Basil of Caesarea, Jerome, Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, Ambrose
- **Related works**: Basil's Homilies on the Hexaemeron, Augustine's Confessions XII, Thomas Aquinas' Summa Theologiae I q.1 a.10
- **Related verses**: Genesis 1:1

### Scripture and Philosophy
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/scripture-and-tradition`
- **Description**: The principle that philosophy and natural science must be subordinated to Sacred Scripture, not the reverse -- because all number, order, and measure of nature derive from the word of God.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p3` — Canon 2 establishes the priority of Sacred Scripture over philosophical opinions and natural reason.
    text: "Philosophy and natural science must be adapted to Sacred Scripture and to the word of God, from whom all number, order, and measure of nature derives, says St. Augustine. Therefore, conversely, Sacred Scripture must not be twisted to fit the opinions of philosophers, or to the light and dictate of nature."
- **Related people**: Augustine
- **Related works**: none
- **Related verses**: none

### Typology
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/typology`
- **Description**: The method of reading Old Testament events, persons, and things as prefigurations (types) of New Testament realities (antitypes). Lapide explains how Scripture simultaneously embraces both type and antitype, with a twofold literal sense (historical and prophetic), and how one type can figure two contrary things in different respects.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p40` — Canon 35 explains how Scripture simultaneously embraces both type and antitype, with some things better suiting the type and others the antitype, producing a twofold literal sense -- historical and prophetic.
    text: "Scripture, especially in the prophecies, from time to time simultaneously embraces both the type and the antitype, that is, the thing which the words properly signify, and at the same time the allegory which that thing represents"
  - `#canons-p40` — Examples of simultaneous type and antitype: God speaks to the serpent and through it to the devil (Genesis 3:14), Moses promises both any prophets and Christ proper (Deuteronomy 18:18), and Isaiah describes the fall of the king of Babylon through Lucifer (Isaiah 14:11).
    text: " So in Genesis 3:14, God speaks to the serpent, and through it to the devil lurking within it. Hence he says some things which properly belong to the serpent, such as: "Upon your breast you shall crawl, and you shall eat earth"; and some which properly belong to the devil"
  - `#canons-p44` — Canon 39 teaches that one thing can be a type of two contrary things in different respects. The flood is a type of both baptism (for Noah who survived) and punishment (for the wicked who were submerged). Christ is a stone of salvation for the pious but a stone of stumbling for unbelievers.
    text: "One thing can be a figure of two even contrary things, but in different respects. Thus the flood, insofar as Noah survived it through the ark, was for the faithful a type of baptism; but insofar as the wicked were submerged by it, it was a type of the punishment to be inflicted on the reprobate at the last judgment."
- **Related people**: Augustine, Basil of Caesarea, Moses, Balaam, Isaiah, David, Solomon
- **Related works**: Augustine's Epistle 99 to Evodius, Basil on Isaiah 2
- **Related verses**: Genesis 3:14, Deuteronomy 18:18, Numbers 24:17, 2 Samuel 7:12, Isaiah 14:11, Isaiah 7:14

### Allegory and Spiritual Sense
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/spiritual-interpretation`
- **Description**: The allegorical sense of Scripture as distinct from the literal, its proper use and limits, and the principle that a solid allegory ought to correspond to and be subordinated to history.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p45` — Canon 40: Jerome, Gregory, Origen, and others allow the allegory to be free, but a proper allegory ought to correspond to the history. David's adultery is taught by Augustine and Ambrose as a type of Christ's love for the Church of the Gentiles.
    text: "a proper and solid allegory ought to correspond to the history, and the more aptly it corresponds, the more suitable it is; indeed, otherwise it is not a proper sense of Scripture, but rather an accommodated one."
  - `#canons-p2` — Canon 1 condemns Origen's purely allegorical method that overturned the letter and literal sense, with Jerome's judgment that Origen made his own intellect the mysteries of the Church.
    text: "This is against Origen, who thought all these things should be expounded allegorically and symbolically, and thus overturned the letter and the literal sense."
- **Related people**: Origen, Jerome, Gregory the Great, Augustine, Ambrose, Thomas Aquinas
- **Related works**: Basil's Homilies on the Hexaemeron (Homilies 3 and 9)
- **Related verses**: Hosea 5 (Jerome's retraction)

### Christ Prefigured (in Prophetic Literature)
- **Slug**: `theology/christology/christ-prefigured`
- **Description**: Old Testament prophecies that simultaneously refer to a historical figure and to Christ as antitype, including Isaiah's Emmanuel, Balaam's star from Jacob, and the Prophets' descriptions of Christ as both avenger and redeemer.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p23` — Canon 18: Christ is called Emmanuel (Isaiah 7:14), Wonderful Counselor (Isaiah 9:6), and the Orient (Zechariah 6:12), not as a change of name but to signify what he will be.
    text: " Thus in Isaiah 7:14, Christ is called Emmanuel; and in chapter 8, verse 3: "Make haste to seize the spoils, hasten to plunder"; and in chapter 9, verse 6: "Wonderful, Counselor, God, Mighty, Father of the world to come, Prince of Peace""
  - `#canons-p47` — Canon 42: The Prophets signify Christ's redemption through a twofold name of slaughter and salvation, vengeance and peace. Balaam sings of Christ the Savior (Numbers 24:17), the Psalmist describes him in Psalm 109:6, and Isaiah describes his consolation (Isaiah 61) and vengeance (Isaiah 63).
    text: "Moses and the other Prophets are accustomed to signify the redemption of Christ by a twofold name, and usually a paired one — namely slaughter and salvation, vengeance and redemption, indignation and peace, blood and safety, ransom and victory."
  - `#canons-p48` — The temporal liberations from Egypt and Babylon prefigured the spiritual liberation by Christ, who slays the sinner as idolater and raises him as worshipper of God.
    text: "Christ therefore slew nations and men, and raised up others — indeed the very same ones; because, for example, he slew Peter the idolater, the drunkard, the adulterer, and raised up the same man and made him Peter the worshipper of God, sober, chaste, etc."
  - `#canons-p49` — The sinner represents a twofold person: man (to be liberated) and demon/vice (to be vanquished). Christ's fury fights against the demon and vices to establish the kingdom of God in man.
    text: "So then the fury of Christ fights against the demon and his followers, namely the vices, and expels them from man, in order to establish the kingdom of God in man and to restore man to himself and to God."
- **Related people**: Balaam, Isaiah, Moses, David, Peter
- **Related verses**: Isaiah 7:14, Isaiah 8:3, Isaiah 9:6, Isaiah 61, Isaiah 63, Numbers 24:17, Psalm 109:6, Zechariah 6:12, 2 Samuel 7:12

### Salvation History
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/salvation-history`
- **Description**: The Pentateuch as a chronicle of the world, weaving together history and chronology from creation through the patriarchs to the death of Moses, with a sacred chronology tracing the ages from Adam to Christ.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#argumentum-p5` — The Pentateuch is a chronicle of the world, tracing creation through the patriarchs, the Exodus, the giving of the Law at Sinai, the wilderness wanderings, and Deuteronomy's repetition of the laws.
    text: "The Pentateuch, that is, this fivefold volume of Moses, is a chronicle of the world. For its purpose is to weave together the history and chronology of the world, and the deeds of the patriarchs from the first creation of the world up to the death of Moses."
  - `#argumentum-p9` — Moses formed and instructed a commonwealth and Church of God from the assembly of the Jews, so that they might recognize, love, and worship God the Creator from the creation and governance of things.
    text: "he himself was occupied in the wilderness as a leader, high priest, prophet, teacher, and lawgiver of the people, and was forming and instructing a commonwealth and Church of God from the assembly and Synagogue of the Jews, so that they might recognize, love, and worship God the Creator"
  - `#sacred-chronology` — The Sacred Chronology presents a synopsis from Adam to Christ, organized by biblical events with years from the beginning of the world.
    text: "it seemed good to present here a brief and probable chronology, useful and pleasant for the reader, in which, as in a synopsis, one might at a single glance survey the ages and times of individual persons or notable events in Sacred Scripture"
  - `#sacred-chronology-p34` — Christ's birth is fixed as the culmination of sacred chronology.
    text: "Christ therefore was born in the year of the world 3950."
- **Related people**: Moses, Adam, Eve, Noah, Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, Christ
- **Related works**: none
- **Related verses**: Genesis 5, Genesis 7, Exodus 2:15, Acts 13:21, Jeremiah 25:12, Jeremiah 29:10

### Prophecy
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/prophecy`
- **Description**: The nature of prophetic discourse in Scripture, including the prophet's mind being carried away by exalted prophetic light, the leaping from type to antitype, and the twofold literal sense (historical and prophetic) in prophetic texts.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p40` — The prophets leap from one thing to another because the mind of the Prophet is carried away by the most exalted prophetic light, in which all things are close and connected, and one thing seems to be the figure of another.
    text: "For the mind of the Prophet is carried away by the most exalted prophetic light, in which all things are close and connected, and one thing seems to be the figure of another; hence the Prophets often leap from one thing to another, both for the reason just stated and for elegance"
  - `#canons-p16` — Canon 15: Scripture promises things to persons that are fulfilled not in themselves but in their descendants, as the land of Canaan promised to Abraham was fulfilled in his posterity.
    text: "Scripture is accustomed to promise certain things to certain persons that are not fulfilled in themselves but in their descendants, to signify that God bestows these things on the descendants for the sake of the original recipients"
  - `#canons-p47` — Canon 42: The redemption of Christ is signified through paired names of slaughter and salvation, vengeance and redemption.
    text: "the Prophets, making no distinction between enemies and citizens, introduce Christ coming to redeem mankind as an armored commander who, driven by divine fury, rushes upon men and overturns, tramples, and slays whomever he meets."
- **Related people**: Moses, Balaam, Isaiah, Ezekiel, Jeremiah
- **Related verses**: Genesis 13:14, Genesis 27:29, Numbers 24:17, Isaiah 14:11, Isaiah 63, 2 Samuel 7:12, Deuteronomy 18:18

### Divine Law and the Old Covenant
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/scripture-and-tradition`
- **Description**: The nature, names, and binding force of the laws given in the Pentateuch, including their fivefold designation as precepts, judgments, justices, testimonies, and testament (covenant). Also the concept of divine law as binding under varying degrees of sin.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p8` — Canon 7: The fivefold naming of God's laws -- precepts, judgments, justices, testimonies, and testament (covenant) -- each name signifying a different aspect of the divine legislation.
    text: "The laws of God are called, first, precepts, statutes, or observances, because they prescribe things to be kept or avoided; second, they are called judgments, because they direct and settle disputes among men"
  - `#canons-p8` — The covenant dimension: God made a covenant with Jews and Christians alike on the condition that they keep his laws.
    text: "on this condition God made a covenant with both the Jews and the Christians: that he would be their God and Father, if they would keep his laws."
  - `#canons-p39` — Canon 34: All precepts of the Pentateuch, even judicial ones, are of divine law because sanctioned by God, though some bind only under venial sin due to lightness of matter.
    text: "All the precepts of the Pentateuch, even the judicial ones, are of divine law, because they were sanctioned by God; some of them, however, do not seem to have been binding under mortal sin, but only under venial sin, on account of the lightness of the matter"
  - `#canons-p5` — Canon 4 discusses how "eternal" in the old law means perpetual only relative to the Jewish commonwealth, and that the old law was to last until the new Evangelical law through Christ.
    text: "the old law is said to endure forever, that is, always — not absolutely, but with respect to the Jews: because that law lasted as long as the Jewish commonwealth and Synagogue lasted, namely throughout the whole time of Judaism, until the new law should succeed it"
- **Related people**: Moses, Augustine, Horace
- **Related works**: Pererius on Genesis
- **Related verses**: Jeremiah 31:32, Leviticus 19:19, Deuteronomy 22:6

### Theophany and Angelic Mediation
- **Slug**: `theology/god/providence`
- **Description**: The question of whether God appeared visibly to the patriarchs in person or through angels. Lapide argues for angelic mediation: all Old Testament theophanies were made through angels who bore the person of God, as supported by Acts 7:30 and Galatians 3:19.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p17` — Canon 16: Although Cyprian, Hilary, and Nazianzen thought God appeared bodily to Abraham and Moses, it is more true that all such apparitions were through angels bearing God's person. Stephen teaches that the one who appeared to Moses was an angel (Acts 7:30), and Paul calls the lawgiver at Sinai an angel (Galatians 3:19).
    text: "it is more true that all these apparitions were made through angels, who in assumed bodies bore the person of God, and are therefore called God."
  - `#canons-p17` — The Council of Sirmium's canon 14, defining that the Son of God wrestled with Jacob (Genesis 32), is reinterpreted as meaning an angel represented the Son of God, and the Council itself is dismissed as partly Arian.
    text: "what the Council of Sirmium, canon 14, defines — that the one who wrestled against Jacob, Genesis 32, was the Son of God — understand this to mean that it was an angel who represented the Son of God."
- **Related people**: Cyprian, Hilary, Gregory Nazianzen, Dionysius, Jerome, Augustine, Gregory the Great, Stephen, Paul, Abraham, Moses, Jacob
- **Related works**: Dionysius' Celestial Hierarchy ch.4, Jerome on Galatians 3, Augustine's Trinity III, Gregory's Morals I
- **Related verses**: Acts 7:30, Galatians 3:19, Genesis 32, Exodus 19-20

### Symbolic and Enigmatic Legislation
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/literal-and-moral`
- **Description**: Moses' use of Egyptian hieroglyphic method, symbols, and riddles in his laws, as described by Clement and the high priest Eleazar. The dietary laws and other precepts are presented as having symbolic and moral significance, comparable to the symbolic riddles of Pythagoras.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p32` — Canon 27: Moses, educated in Egyptian wisdom, uses hieroglyphic method in his laws through symbols and riddles, as Clement of Alexandria teaches. Eleazar the high priest told Aristeas that Moses' precepts are symbolic and enigmatic, comparable to Pythagoras' symbols and Egyptian hieroglyphics.
    text: "Moses, says Clement (Stromata, book 6), because he was educated in all the wisdom of the Egyptians, from time to time uses their hieroglyphic method in his laws, and transmits them through symbols and riddles."
  - `#canons-p32` — Jerome gives Pythagorean riddles as parallels: "Do not step over the balance" means do not transgress justice; "Do not poke fire with a sword" means do not provoke an angry person; "Do not eat the heart" means cast sorrow from your mind.
    text: " "Do not step over the balance," that is, do not transgress justice. "Do not poke fire with a sword," that is, do not provoke an angry person with words. "The crown must not be plucked," that is, the laws of cities must not be picked at but preserved. "Do not eat the heart," that is, cast sorrow from your mind."
- **Related people**: Moses, Clement of Alexandria, Aristeas, Eleazar (high priest), Pythagoras, Jerome, Ptolemy Philadelphus
- **Related works**: Clement's Stromata VI, Jerome's Against Rufinus III
- **Related verses**: none

### Sin and Legal Uncleanness
- **Slug**: `theology/soteriology/grace`
- **Description**: The metonymic use of "sin" in the Pentateuch to denote not sin properly so-called but the sacrifice for sin, the punishment of sin, or legal/ritual uncleanness (e.g., from menstruation, leprosy, contact with the dead, or childbirth).
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p7` — Canon 6: "Sin" in Leviticus is taken metonymically for (1) the sacrifice offered for sin, (2) the punishment of sin, or (3) legal uncleanness from menstrual blood, seed, leprosy, or contact with the dead. Childbirth (Leviticus 12:6) and leprosy (Leviticus 14:13) are called "sin" meaning legal uncleanness.
    text: " "Sin" is often, especially in Leviticus, taken metonymically: first, for the sacrifice offered for sin; second, for the punishment of sin; third, for the irregularity or legal uncleanness contracted from the flow of menstrual blood, of seed, of leprosy, or from contact with a dead body."
- **Related people**: Moses
- **Related works**: none
- **Related verses**: Leviticus 12:6, Leviticus 14:13

### Hebrew Naming Conventions
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/biblical-canon`
- **Description**: The significance of names in Hebrew culture and Scripture: God and the Hebrews imposed names based on events, as omens, warnings, or wishes for the future. Names are explored as prophetic acts. Extended comparison with Roman, Greek, and Germanic naming conventions.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p18` — Canon 17: When Scripture imposes a new name, the old is not removed but the new is added, so the person may be called by either. Examples: Jacob/Israel, Gideon/Jerubbaal, Simon/Cephas.
    text: "When Sacred Scripture imposes a new name on someone, it is to be understood as not taking away the prior name, but adding the later one to the former, so that the person may be called by either name"
  - `#canons-p19` — God and the Hebrews imposed names from events, and the names were like omens or wishes for the future. This is clear in Adam, Eve, Seth, Cain, Noah, Abraham, Ishmael, Isaac, Jacob.
    text: "God and the Hebrews imposed names on their people from events, namely names that signified an event, whether present or future; and then the names were like omens, or warnings, or wishes for the future"
  - `#canons-p24` — Canon 19: Men and women of old bore many names, so the same person may be called differently in different biblical books.
    text: "Men and women of old bore many names: hence it is not surprising if the same person in Scripture is called now by one name, now by another."
- **Related people**: Jacob (Israel), Gideon (Jerubbaal), Simon (Cephas), Abraham, Isaac, Esau
- **Related works**: none
- **Related verses**: Genesis 35:10, Judges 6:32, Genesis 26:34, Genesis 36:2

### Redemption and Soteriology
- **Slug**: `theology/soteriology/grace`
- **Description**: Christ's redemptive work as simultaneously vengeance against sin and salvation of the sinner -- the sinner as a twofold person (man to be liberated, demon/vice to be vanquished), with the temporal liberations from Egypt and Babylon prefiguring Christ's spiritual liberation.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p48` — The temporal liberations from Egypt and Babylon were types accomplished through blood and slaughter; in Christ's redemption, the same persons are both enemies and friends, both slain and redeemed.

  - `#canons-p49` — The sinner subsists in a twofold nature: man (to be liberated) and demon/vice (to be vanquished). Christ's fury fights against the vices to establish God's kingdom in man.
    text: "The sinner represents a twofold person, and subsists in a twofold nature, as it were — namely that of a man and that of a demon, or of vice and sin. The former is a soldier, the latter an enemy of Christ; the former was to be liberated, the latter to be vanquished."
- **Related people**: Christ, Peter, Moses
- **Related works**: none

### Hebrew Rhetorical Figures in Scripture
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/literal-and-moral`
- **Description**: A comprehensive catalogue of Hebrew rhetorical and linguistic figures used throughout the Pentateuch, including synecdoche, metonymy, catachresis, enallage, prolepsis, litotes, hypallage, and hendiadys, with exegetical rules for interpreting each.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#canons-p4` — Canon 3 (prolepsis): Moses uses anticipation, calling cities by names given later, as Dan for Laish (Genesis 14:14) and Segor for Bala (Genesis 14:2).
    text: "Moses often uses prolepsis or anticipation: for he calls cities and places by the name that was given to them much later."
  - `#canons-p6` — Canon 5 (enallage of senses): The Hebrews interchange senses, especially using sight for any sense -- sight for touch (John 20:29), smell (Exodus 5:21), taste (Psalm 33:9), hearing (Exodus 20:18).
    text: "The Hebrews by enallage often interchange one sense with another, and especially take sight for any sense"
  - `#canons-p9` — Canon 8 (synecdoche): Genus for species ("to make a kid" means to sacrifice it); part for whole ("to fill the hand" means to consecrate); "to uncover nakedness" means relations; "to open someone's ear" means to whisper or reveal.
    text: " In the Pentateuch, synecdoche is frequent. Thus the genus is taken for the species: "to make a kid, a lamb, a calf" means to sacrifice a kid, a lamb, a calf."
  - `#canons-p10` — Canon 9 (metonymy): "Hand" for power or punishment, "mouth" for word or precept, "soul" for life, "man of blood" for murderer, "I raise my hand" for swearing.
    text: " "I raise my hand," that is, with raised hand I call the Lord of heaven as witness and swear by God."
  - `#canons-p11` — Canon 10 (catachresis): "Father" for author or inventor (God is "father" of rain, devil is "father" of lies, Tubalcain is "father" of instrument players); "mouth" of the sword for its edge; "daughters" for subordinate towns.
    text: " the "father" of something is said to be he who is the author, founder, or inventor of the thing, or he who is first and foremost in that thing."
  - `#canons-p12` — Canon 11 (real verbs for verbal/mental): The priest "cleanses" the leper means declares him clean; "I have set you to uproot" means to prophesy about uprooting; "I sanctify you" means I command you to be holy.
    text: "The Hebrews often take real verbs for verbal or mental ones."
  - `#canons-p26` — Canon 21 (abstract for concrete): "Abomination" for abominable thing, "desire" for desired thing, God called "our hope" meaning the thing hoped for.
    text: " The Hebrews frequently put the abstract for the concrete, as "abomination" for an abominable or abominated thing"

### Biblical Chronology
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/salvation-history`
- **Description**: The computation of years from creation to Christ, including the patriarchal genealogies, the dating of the flood, Abraham, the Exodus, the Temple of Solomon, the Babylonian captivity, and the nativity of Christ. Includes the principle that one year's discrepancy in chronology is normal.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#sacred-chronology-p2` — Lapide presents the chronology as a synopsis where one might survey at a single glance the ages and times of notable events, crediting Father Henricus Samerius.
    text: "as in a synopsis, one might at a single glance survey the ages and times of individual persons or notable events in Sacred Scripture, and their distances from each other."
  - `#sacred-chronology-p3` — The principle that one year in chronology makes no difference: the same event is recorded one year earlier or later depending on whether completed or begun years are counted.
    text: "it is a common axiom of chronologists that one year in chronology makes no difference in the reckoning of times, and therefore should not be considered significant."
  - `#sacred-chronology-p4` — Pagan dating systems: from Ninus and Semiramis, from the flood of Ogyges and Inachus, from Troy's destruction, from the Olympiads, and from the founding of Rome.
    text: "the pagans reckon their times first from Ninus and Semiramis, who founded the first monarchy of the Assyrians, in whose time Abraham lived."
  - `#sacred-chronology-p33` — From Adam to the flood, 1656 years; to the end of the flood, 1657 years.
    text: "From Adam up to the flood, 1656 years elapsed, as is gathered from Genesis 5 and 7; up to the end of the flood, however, 1657 years elapsed, for the flood lasted a full year"
  - `#sacred-chronology-p98` — Final computation: From the creation of the world to the birth of Christ, 4191 solar years, 4320 lunar years, and 5625 priestly years.
    text: "Therefore from the creation of the world to the birth of Christ, 4191 solar years intervened, but 4320 lunar years, and 5625 priestly years."
- **Related people**: Adam, Noah, Abraham, Jacob, Joseph, Moses, Solomon, Cyrus, Christ, Henricus Samerius, Dr. Sepp, Clinton
- **Related works**: Clinton's Fasti Hellenici, Sepp's La Vie de N.-S. Jesus-Christ, des Vignoles' Chronology of Sacred History
- **Related verses**: Genesis 5, Genesis 7, Genesis 8, Acts 13:21, Jeremiah 25:12, Jeremiah 29:10, 4 Kings 25
