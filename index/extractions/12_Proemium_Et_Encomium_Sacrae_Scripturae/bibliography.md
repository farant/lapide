# Bibliography — 12_Proemium_Et_Encomium_Sacrae_Scripturae

## Hermes Trismegistus

### (General reference / theological sayings)
- **Slug**: `hermes-trismegistus/works`
- **Also known as**: Mercury Trismegistus; Mercurius Ter Maximus
- **Description**: Attributed sayings of the legendary Egyptian sage Hermes Trismegistus, described as "nearly contemporary with Moses." Lapide cites his cosmological metaphor of the universe as a book of divinity.
- **Year**: legendary (attributed to ~15th c. BC in the text; Hermetic corpus dates ~2nd–3rd c. AD)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#section-one-p3` — Hermes Trismegistus quoted: the universe is a book of divinity
    text: "That famous Egyptian Theologian, nearly contemporary with Moses, Mercury, in the opinion of the Gentiles called Trismegistus, long pondering within himself by what method he might most fittingly describe the universe, at last burst forth with this: 'The universe,' he said, 'is a book of divinity, and this dimly-lit age is a mirror of divine things.'"
  - `#moses-most-ancient-p6` — Mercury Trismegistus identified as the most ancient of all Gentile sages, grandson of the elder Mercury, contemporary of Moses
    text: "For this Mercury Trismegistus, says Saint Augustine, in book 18 of The City of God, chapter 39, was the grandson of the elder Mercury, whose maternal grandfather was Atlas the astrologer, and a contemporary of Prometheus, and he flourished at the time when Moses lived."
- **Related people**: Hermes Trismegistus, Moses
- **Related subjects**: Natural theology, cosmology

## Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite

### On the Divine Names
- **Slug**: `pseudo-dionysius/on-the-divine-names`
- **Also known as**: De Divinis Nominibus
- **Description**: Mystical-theological treatise on the names attributed to God, attributed to Dionysius the Areopagite.
- **Year**: ~late 5th–early 6th c. AD (attributed to 1st c.)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-i-p3` — Dionysius quoted at length from chapter 1 on proceeding from Sacred Scripture as a principle
    text: "By no reasoning," he says, "ought it be presumed to say or think anything about the super-substantial and most secret divinity, beyond what the sacred oracles have handed down to us..."
- **Related people**: Pseudo-Dionysius
- **Related subjects**: Theology, mysticism, divine names

### On Mystical Theology
- **Slug**: `pseudo-dionysius/on-mystical-theology`
- **Also known as**: De Mystica Theologia
- **Description**: Short treatise on apophatic theology and the ascent to God through negation.
- **Year**: ~late 5th–early 6th c. AD (attributed to 1st c.)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-i-p3` — Cited on mystical theology reaching God by negation, ending in silence; contrasted with symbolic theology's breadth
    text: "And again in the book On Mystical Theology, he teaches that spiritual and mystical Theology, which reaches the super-substantial hidden mystery and the darkness of God by transcending all created things through negation, without symbols, is narrow and so compressed as to end in silence"
- **Related people**: Pseudo-Dionysius
- **Related subjects**: Apophatic theology, mysticism

### On the Celestial Hierarchy
- **Slug**: `pseudo-dionysius/on-the-celestial-hierarchy`
- **Also known as**: De Coelesti Hierarchia
- **Description**: Treatise on the orders of angels and heavenly intelligences.
- **Year**: ~late 5th–early 6th c. AD (attributed to 1st c.)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-ii-p19` — Dionysius quoted from the opening of the Celestial Hierarchy on proceeding from Sacred Scriptures received from the Fathers
    text: "Let us proceed with all our strength to understand the Sacred Scriptures, as we have received them from the Fathers, to be contemplated, and let us speculate, insofar as we can, on the distinctions and orders of the heavenly spirits"

- **Related subjects**: Angelology, scriptural exegesis

## Justin Martyr

### Dialogue with Trypho
- **Slug**: `justin-martyr/dialogue-with-trypho`
- **Also known as**: Dialogus cum Tryphone Judaeo
- **Description**: Justin's apologetic dialogue with a Jewish interlocutor, in whose prologue he narrates his philosophical journey to Christianity.
- **Year**: ~155–160
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-i-p5` — Justin's autobiographical account of wandering through philosophical schools (Stoic, Peripatetic, Pythagorean, Platonist) until directed to the Prophets by a mysterious philosopher
    text: "As he himself testifies at the beginning of his dialogue against Trypho, eager for Philosophy and that true wisdom which leads to God, he wandered in vain through the more illustrious sects of the Philosophers in a remarkable circuit"
  - `#chapter-v-p19` — The divine catechist of Justin quoted, recommending prayer for understanding the Prophets
    text: "But you, with prayers and entreaties above all, desire that the gates of light be opened to you: for these things are not perceived and understood by anyone, unless God and Christ have granted him understanding."
- **Related people**: Justin Martyr
- **Related subjects**: Philosophy, conversion, apologetics

### Exhortation to the Greeks (Cohortatio ad Graecos)
- **Slug**: `justin-martyr/exhortation-to-the-greeks`
- **Also known as**: Paranaesis; Cohortatio ad Graecos
- **Description**: Apologetic work attributed to Justin, arguing that the Greeks drew their wisdom from Moses and the Egyptians.
- **Year**: ~2nd c. (authorship disputed)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p6` — Justin cited on Greeks drawing wisdom from Egyptians and from Moses; includes oracle about the Chaldeans and Hebrews
    text: "Hear Saint Justin in his Exhortation, or Paranaesis to the Greeks, in which he teaches throughout that the Greeks drew their wisdom and knowledge of God from the Egyptians, and these from Moses."
  - `#praises-of-moses-p7` — Justin cited: Moses wrote in Hebrew before Greek letters existed; Plato's Timaeus cited via Justin on Egyptian priest telling Solon "You Greeks are always children"
    text: "Moses wrote his history in Hebrew, when the letters of the Greeks had not yet been invented."
  - `#praises-of-moses-p7` — Justin cited: from Diodorus, Orpheus, Homer, Solon, Pythagoras, Plato, and the Sibyl learned from Moses through the Egyptians
    text: "from Diodorus he teaches that Orpheus, Homer, Solon, Pythagoras, Plato, the Sibyl, and others, when they had been in Egypt, changed their opinion about many gods"
  - `#moses-most-ancient-p4` — Justin cited: Diodorus lists six ancient legislators with Moses first
    text: "Diodorus, the most esteemed of all historians, says Saint Justin in his Exhortation to the Greeks, lists six ancient legislators, and first of all Moses"
- **Related people**: Justin Martyr, Moses, Plato, Pythagoras, Orpheus, Diodorus Siculus
- **Related subjects**: Chronology, Greek dependence on Moses, apologetics
