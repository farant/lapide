# Years — 12_Proemium_Et_Encomium_Sacrae_Scripturae

### ~250 BC
- **Slug**: `bc/3rd-century/50s/250`
- **Description**: Ptolemy Philadelphus commissioned 72 Hebrew scholars (six from each tribe), chosen through Eleazar the high priest, to translate the Old Testament from Hebrew into Greek. They completed the work in 70 days with complete agreement, producing the Septuagint. The version was deposited in the Alexandrian library through Demetrius.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#the-septuagint-p2` — The Septuagint translation commissioned by Ptolemy Philadelphus
    text: "To what end, 250 years before Christ, did He put it into the mind of Ptolemy Philadelphus, son of Ptolemy Lagus (who succeeded his brother Alexander the Great in the kingdom of Egypt), to choose, through Eleazar the high priest, six of the most learned men from each tribe of the Hebrews — that is, 72 interpreters — to translate the Old Testament from Hebrew into Greek, and He assisted them so that in 70 days, with the complete agreement of all, they accomplished the work"
- **Related people**: Ptolemy Philadelphus, Ptolemy Lagus, Alexander the Great, Eleazar (high priest), Demetrius, Justin, Cyril, Clement of Alexandria, Augustine, Tertullian
- **Related places**: Alexandria, Egypt

### ~128 AD (12th year of Emperor Hadrian)
- **Slug**: `ad/2nd-century/20s/128`
- **Description**: Aquila of Pontus, the second translator of Sacred Scripture from Hebrew after the Seventy, translated the Hebrew Scripture into Greek in the 12th year of Emperor Hadrian. His fidelity is questioned because he defected from the Christians to the Jews.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#the-septuagint-p3` — Aquila's Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible
    text: "The second interpreter of Sacred Scripture from Hebrew after the Seventy, according to Epiphanius, was Aquila of Pontus, who in the 12th year of the Emperor Hadrian translated the Hebrew Scripture into Greek; but because he defected from the Christians to the Jews, his fidelity is not sufficiently trustworthy."
- **Related people**: Aquila of Pontus, Emperor Hadrian, Epiphanius
- **Related places**: Pontus

### Reign of Emperor Commodus (~180–192 AD)
- **Slug**: `ad/2nd-century/80s/180`
- **Description**: Theodotion, a proselyte Jew and former Marcionite, produced a Greek translation of the Hebrew Scripture under Emperor Commodus. His version of Daniel was received and followed by the Church.
- **Occurrences**:

    text: "After him, with greater fidelity, came Theodotion, a proselyte Jew though formerly a Marcionite, under the Emperor Commodus, whose version in Daniel the Church received and follows."
- **Related people**: Theodotion, Emperor Commodus
- **Related places**: (none specified)

### ~303 AD (Diocletian persecution)
- **Slug**: `ad/4th-century/00s/303`
- **Description**: St. Lucian, a priest and martyr, undertook a new edition of the Old Testament from Hebrew into Greek under Emperor Diocletian (the seventh translator). Separately, Maximian and Diocletian ordered by edict that the books of Moses and other Sacred Scripture be surrendered for burning; many faithful resisted and obtained the laurel of martyrdom.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#the-septuagint-p5` — St. Lucian's translation under Diocletian
    text: "Seventh was St. Lucian, a priest and martyr, under Diocletian, who undertook a new edition from Hebrew into Greek."
  - `#moses-most-ancient-p8` — Diocletian and Maximian's edict to burn the sacred books
    text: "When Maximian and Diocletian ordered by edict that the books of Moses and the other books of Sacred Scripture be handed over to them for burning, the faithful resisted, preferring to die rather than surrender them."
- **Related people**: St. Lucian, Emperor Diocletian, Emperor Maximian
- **Related places**: (none specified)

### 731 AD
- **Slug**: `ad/8th-century/30s/731`
- **Description**: Death of Venerable Bede, who had devoted his entire life to meditating on the Scriptures from the age of seven. On his deathbed he labored to finish his commentary on the Gospel of St. John, calling for a scribe and singing the Gloria before expiring.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iv-p29` — Death of Venerable Bede
    text: "he most peacefully breathed forth his spirit, to be blessed with the vision of God in reward for his labor for the faith, in the year from the Virgin's delivery 731."
- **Related people**: Venerable Bede
- **Related places**: England

### 755 AD
- **Slug**: `ad/8th-century/50s/755`
- **Description**: Martyrdom of St. Boniface among the Frisians. When the Frisians swung a sword at his head, he held up a Gospel codex as a shield; the book was cut through the middle but not a single letter was destroyed.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iv-p31` — Martyrdom of St. Boniface
    text: "when in the year of the Lord 755 the Frisians swung a sword at his head, he held up this codex as a spiritual shield, and by a remarkable miracle, although the book was cut through the middle by the sharp sword, nevertheless not a single letter was destroyed by that cut."
- **Related people**: St. Boniface
- **Related places**: Frisia

### ~800 AD
- **Slug**: `ad/9th-century/00s/800`
- **Description**: Theodore the Studite presided over the Studios monastery at Constantinople (after St. Plato), leaving monuments of genius and piety from the sacred Letters. He contended with the Iconoclast Emperors Constantine Copronymus and Leo the Isaurian, slew the heresy, and consecrated the trophies of the holy faith.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iv-p28` — Theodore the Studite at the Studios monastery
    text: "St. Plato presided over it; after him Theodore the Studite, around the year of the Lord 800, left so many monuments of his genius and piety from the sacred Letters"
- **Related people**: Theodore the Studite, St. Plato, Emperor Constantine Copronymus, Emperor Leo the Isaurian
- **Related places**: Constantinople

### 1247 AD
- **Slug**: `ad/13th-century/40s/1247`
- **Description**: St. Edmund, Archbishop of Canterbury, spent his days and nights in sacred Letters with great reverence, always kissing the Holy Bible before opening it. While on an embassy reading the Bible at night, a candle fell onto the book; upon waking, he found the codex completely intact and unharmed.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#saintly-examples-p3` — St. Edmund's devotion to Scripture and the miracle of the unburned Bible
    text: "St. Edmund, Archbishop of Canterbury, in the year of salvation 1247, spent his days and nights in sacred Letters, passing the very nights sleepless, with such reverence that whenever he opened the Holy Bible, he first honored it with a kiss."
- **Related people**: St. Edmund of Canterbury
- **Related places**: Canterbury

### ~3000 AM (~1000 BC, the Olympiad era)
- **Slug**: `bc/10th-century/00s/1000`
- **Description**: The Olympiads began either at the beginning of the reign of Jotham or at the end of the reign of Uzziah, approximately the three thousandth year from the creation of the world. Everything before the Olympiads in profane history is filled with fables and ignorance, making Moses and the Hebrews the only certain source for three thousand years of world history.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#utility-ii-p4` — Pagan history before the Olympiads is unreliable
    text: "But the Olympiads began either at the beginning of the reign of Jotham, or at the end of the reign of Uzziah, that is, after the three thousandth year from the creation of the world and more: so that for three thousand years, you have no certain history of the world except this one of Moses and the Hebrews."
- **Related people**: Jotham (king), Uzziah (king), Moses
- **Related places**: (none specified)

### ~250 years before Petrarch (~1100 AD)
- **Slug**: `ad/11th-century/00s/1100`
- **Description**: Robert, King of Sicily, told Francis Petrarch under oath that letters (especially sacred ones) were far dearer to him than his kingdom. The "250 years ago" is reckoned from Petrarch's time (c. 1350), placing the event approximately in the early 14th century (note: Robert of Sicily reigned 1309–1343, so the 250-year figure is approximate from Lapide's perspective, not Petrarch's).
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#utility-iv-p7` — Robert of Sicily's love of sacred letters
    text: "Francis Petrarch relates that 250 years ago, Robert, King of Sicily, was so delighted with letters, especially sacred ones, that he said to him under oath: 'I swear to you, Petrarch, that letters are far dearer to me than my kingdom, and if I had to be deprived of one or the other, I would more calmly part with the crown than with letters.'"
- **Related people**: Francis Petrarch, Robert (King of Sicily)
- **Related places**: Sicily

### Relative chronology of Moses (from St. Cyril's reckoning)

The following dates are given relative to the birth of Moses, from a chronology attributed to St. Cyril (Against Julian, book 1). They do not correspond to absolute AD/BC years but are included as the most detailed chronological data in the text.

#### 7th year of Moses
- **Slug**: `relative/moses/year-7`
- **Description**: Birth of Prometheus, Epimetheus, Atlas (brother of Prometheus), and Argus the all-seeing.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p12` — Cyril's chronology of Moses and the Gentiles
    text: "In the seventh year of Moses they say Prometheus and Epimetheus were born, and Atlas, the brother of Prometheus, and moreover Argus the all-seeing."
- **Related people**: Prometheus, Epimetheus, Atlas, Argus, St. Cyril

#### 35th year of Moses
- **Slug**: `relative/moses/year-35`
- **Description**: Cecrops first reigned at Athens, surnamed Diphyes; said to be the first to sacrifice an ox and to name Jupiter the supreme god among the Greeks.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p12` — Cecrops's reign at Athens
    text: "In the thirty-fifth year of Moses, Cecrops first reigned at Athens, who was surnamed Diphyes: they say he was the first among men to sacrifice an ox, and named Jupiter the supreme god among the Greeks."
- **Related people**: Cecrops, St. Cyril
- **Related places**: Athens

#### 67th year of Moses
- **Slug**: `relative/moses/year-67`
- **Description**: The flood of Deucalion in Thessaly; Phaethon consumed by fire in Ethiopia.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p12` — Deucalion's flood and Phaethon
    text: "In the sixty-seventh year of Moses they say there was the flood of Deucalion in Thessaly; and moreover in Ethiopia the son of the Sun, as they say, Phaethon, was consumed by fire."
- **Related people**: Deucalion, Phaethon, St. Cyril
- **Related places**: Thessaly, Ethiopia

#### 74th year of Moses
- **Slug**: `relative/moses/year-74`
- **Description**: Hellen, son of Deucalion and Pyrrha, gave the Greeks their name (previously called Greeks, now Hellenes).
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p12` — Hellen names the Greeks
    text: "In the seventieth fourth year of Moses a certain man called Hellen, the son of Deucalion and Pyrrha, gave the Greeks the appellation of his own name, whereas previously they were called Greeks."
- **Related people**: Hellen, Deucalion, Pyrrha, St. Cyril

#### 120th year of Moses
- **Slug**: `relative/moses/year-120`
- **Description**: Dardanus founded the city of Dardania, when Amyntas ruled among the Assyrians, Sthenelus among the Argives, and Ramesses (also called Egypt, brother of Danaus) among the Egyptians.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p12` — Dardanus founds Dardania
    text: "In the one hundred and twentieth year of Moses, Dardanus founded the city of Dardania, when Amyntas ruled among the Assyrians, Sthenelus among the Argives, and Ramesses among the Egyptians; he himself was also called Egypt, the brother of Danaus."
- **Related people**: Dardanus, Amyntas, Sthenelus, Ramesses/Egypt, Danaus, St. Cyril
- **Related places**: Dardania

#### 160th year after Moses
- **Slug**: `relative/moses/year-160`
- **Description**: Cadmus reigned at Thebes; his daughter was Semele, from whom Bacchus was born. Linus of Thebes and Amphion (musicians) flourished. Phinehas son of Eleazar assumed the priesthood among the Hebrews after Aaron's death.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p12` — Cadmus at Thebes, Phinehas assumes priesthood
    text: "In the one hundred and sixtieth year after Moses, Cadmus reigned at Thebes, whose daughter was Semele, from whom Bacchus was born, as they say, from Jupiter. There were also at that time Linus of Thebes and Amphion, musicians. At that time also Phinehas, the son of Eleazar, the son of Aaron, assumed the priesthood among the Hebrews, Aaron having died."
- **Related people**: Cadmus, Semele, Bacchus, Linus, Amphion, Phinehas, Eleazar, Aaron, St. Cyril
- **Related places**: Thebes

#### 195th year after Moses

- **Related places**: Molossia

- **Related people**: Esebon, Agamemnon, Vaphres, Teutamus, St. Cyril
- **Related places**: Troy/Ilium

### Reign of Emperor Justinian (~527–565 AD)
- **Slug**: `ad/6th-century/30s/530`
- **Description**: Schools of sacred Scripture were established at Nisibis in the time of Emperor Justinian, as attested by Junilius Africanus. Under the same Emperor, Pope Agapetus endeavored to introduce similar schools at Rome, as Cassiodorus narrates.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iv-p20` — Schools of Sacred Scripture at Nisibis and Rome under Justinian
    text: "Regarding the schools of sacred Scripture established at Nisibis in the time of the Emperor Justinian, the witness is Junilius Africanus, a bishop, in his book to Primasius. The same schools under the same Emperor, Agapetus the Pontiff endeavored to introduce at Rome"
- **Related people**: Emperor Justinian, Junilius Africanus, Pope Agapetus, Cassiodorus, Primasius
- **Related places**: Nisibis, Rome

- **Related places**: Rome (Lateran)

- **Related places**: (none specified)
