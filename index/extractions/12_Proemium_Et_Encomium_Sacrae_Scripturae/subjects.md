### Excellence and Dignity of Sacred Scripture
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/excellence`
- **Description**: The supreme excellence, dignity, and authority of Sacred Scripture as God's own letter to humanity, surpassing all human learning and philosophy, containing divine wisdom that illumines, converts, and sanctifies.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#section-one-p5` — The universe is a book of divinity, but Scripture is a superior revelation: God employed another pen to set before us living images of divine things.
    text: "It therefore seemed good to the divine and boundless goodness — that is, to the most wise scribe, writing swiftly and with wondrous condescension — to employ another pen, to set before us other tablets"
  - `#section-one-p6` — Scripture described as letters written by God to us, a domestic oracle surpassing Apollo's tripod, where God Himself speaks.
    text: "how wonderful it is to have always at hand the sacred books of divine Scripture — the very letters, I say, written by God to us, and the indubitable witnesses of the divine will"
  - `#section-one-p7` — St. Charles Borromeo read Scripture with bare head and bended knee, treating it as the oracle of God.
    text: "St. Charles Borromeo was thinking when he used to read Sacred Scripture, as if they were the oracles of God, only with bare head, and bended knee, reverently reading"
  - `#section-one-p13` — St. Gregory rebukes Theodore for negligent Scripture reading: Scripture is a letter from Almighty God to His creature.
    text: "The Emperor of heaven, the Lord of angels and men, has sent his letters to you for your life, and you neglect to read them eagerly! For what is Sacred Scripture but a kind of letter from Almighty God to his creature?"
  - `#chapter-ii-p17` — Scripture is an incomprehensible sphere of learning whose center is everywhere and circumference nowhere, being the Word of the divine mind.
    text: "It is an incomprehensible sphere of learning whose center is everywhere and circumference nowhere — for Sacred Scripture is the Word of God"
- **Related people**: St. Charles Borromeo, St. Gregory the Great, Mercury Trismegistus
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/inspiration`, `theology/scripture/necessity`

### Inspiration of Sacred Scripture
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/inspiration`
- **Description**: The doctrine that the sacred writers were instruments — pens — of the Holy Spirit, who dictated the content of Scripture, so that the same authority belongs to Moses as to Paul.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#sacred-writers-as-pens-p2` — Moses, David, Isaiah, Peter, Paul, and John drew wisdom from the fountain of truth; their tongues and hands were pens of the Holy Spirit.
    text: "the tongues and hands of these sacred Writers were nothing other than the pens of the same Holy Spirit, so much so that they seem not so much to have been different writers, as different pens of one writer"
  - `#chapter-iv-p15` — St. Paul attributes the ability to interpret Scripture not to natural intellect but to distributions of graces of the Spirit: wisdom, knowledge, prophecy, discernment, tongues, interpretation.
    text: "to one is given through the Spirit the word of wisdom, to another the word of knowledge, to another faith, to another the grace of healing, to another the working of miracles, to another prophecy, to another the discernment of spirits, to another kinds of tongues, to another finally the interpretation of speeches"
  - `#chapter-iv-p14` — St. Peter teaches that no prophecy of Scripture is made by private interpretation, but holy men of God spoke inspired by the Holy Spirit.
    text: "No prophecy of Scripture is made by private interpretation; for prophecy was not brought at any time by the will of man, but the holy men of God spoke, inspired by the Holy Spirit"
- **Related people**: St. Peter, St. Paul, St. Gregory, Theodoret
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/excellence`, `theology/scripture/scripture-interpretation`

### Necessity and Fruit of Sacred Scripture
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/necessity`
- **Description**: Sacred Scripture is necessary both for salvation and for sound theology; it supplies the principles of faith from which theology draws its conclusions, and its reading bears transformative spiritual fruit.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-i-p2` — Sacred Scripture contains all the beginnings of theology; whoever separates scholastic theology from Scripture imagines offspring without a mother, a house without foundations.
    text: "Sacred Scripture lays the foundations of Theology, from which the Theologian, by the reasoning of the mind, as a mother begets offspring, generates and brings forth new demonstrations. Therefore whoever thinks he can separate Scholastic theology from Sacred Scripture by serious study, imagines offspring without a mother, a house without foundations"
  - `#chapter-i-p7` — The Psalmist calls blessed not the one who pours forth God's words to others but the one who meditates on His law day and night; such a one is like a tree planted beside running waters.
    text: "The Royal Psalmist calls blessed not the one who pours forth the words of God to others, but the one who meditates on his law day and night; such a one, he says, is like a tree planted beside running waters"
  - `#chapter-i-p8` — Scripture breathes a vital spirit into readers; the Maccabees, having only the holy books for consolation, stand firm with unconquered virtue.
    text: "I know not what vital spirit the divine words breathe into readers by a hidden influence, so that if you compare them with the writings of the most learned and holiest men, however ardent, you would judge these to be lifeless and those to be living and breathing life"
  - `#chapter-ii-p25` — The Council of Trent begins the reform of the Church from Sacred Scripture.
    text: "the holy Council of Trent begins the reform of the Church from Sacred Scripture, and in its entire first decree On Reform, prescribes as carefully as it does at length that the reading of Sacred Scripture be either established or restored everywhere"
- **Related people**: St. Thomas Aquinas, Dionysius the Areopagite
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/excellence`, `ecclesiology/councils/trent`

### Scripture as Foundation of Theology
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/scripture-and-theology`
- **Description**: The relationship between Sacred Scripture and Scholastic theology as inseparable disciplines: Scripture supplies the principles of faith from which theology draws conclusions, and the two must be joined as sisters.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-i-p2` — Theology is a science of conclusions drawn from principles certain by faith contained in Scripture; whoever separates them imagines a house without foundations.
    text: "Theology is nothing other than a science of conclusions drawn from principles certain by faith, and therefore it is the most august of all sciences, as well as the most certain: but the principles of faith and faith itself are contained in Sacred Scripture"
  - `#chapter-i-p3` — Dionysius the Areopagite everywhere professes himself to proceed supported by Sacred Scripture as by a principle and a brilliant torch.
    text: "that divine Dionysius, whom all antiquity regarded as the summit of theologians and the 'bird of heaven,' who everywhere, when disputing about God and heavenly things, professes himself to proceed supported by Sacred Scripture as by a principle and a brilliant torch"
  - `#chapter-ii-p18` — Take Scripture away from Scholastic theology and you produce not theology but philosophy; join the two and you earn every mark of both theologian and philosopher.
    text: "Take Sacred Scripture away from Scholastic theology, and you will produce not theology, but philosophy; you will be a philosopher, not a theologian. Join the two, intertwined with each other, and you will earn every mark of both theologian and philosopher"
  - `#the-fathers-p5` — St. Thomas gave an illustrious example that scholastic theology and Scripture should be inseparably joined as sisters.
    text: "St. Thomas, the prince of the Scholastics, gave us an illustrious example, that we should inseparably join Scholastic theology with Sacred Scripture, as though they were sisters"

### Object and Breadth of Sacred Scripture
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/breadth`
- **Description**: Sacred Scripture has for its object everything knowable and embraces all disciplines — physics, ethics, metaphysics, history, geometry — either formally or eminently, serving as queen and mistress of all sciences.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-ii-p2` — Origen describes Scripture as an intelligible world constituted by four elements: history (earth), moral understanding (water), natural science (air), and theology (fire/ether).
    text: "Divine Scripture is an intelligible world, constituted by its four parts, as by four elements; whose earth is, as it were, in the middle like a center, namely history; around which, in the likeness of waters, the abyss of moral understanding is poured"
  - `#chapter-ii-p6` — Physics is handed down in Genesis, Ecclesiastes, Job; Ethics in Proverbs, Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus; Metaphysics in Job and Psalms; History from Genesis through Maccabees.
    text: "Physics, even in its primeval form and from its very origin, is handed down in Genesis, in Ecclesiastes, in Job; Ethics, through the briefest maxims and sentences in Proverbs, Wisdom, and Ecclesiasticus"
  - `#chapter-ii-p7` — All liberal disciplines and sciences serve as handmaids to Scripture as their queen; this sacred science encompasses all things.
    text: "all the liberal disciplines, all languages, all sciences and arts — which are each contained within certain limits — serve as handmaids to Sacred Scripture, as to their mistress and queen"
  - `#chapter-ii-p23` — St. Basil compares Scripture to a fully stocked workshop supplying medicines for every disease; the Church drew constancy, wisdom, faith, humility, and renewal from it.
    text: "St. Basil rightly compares it to a most fully stocked workshop, which supplies medicines of every kind for every disease"
- **Related people**: Origen, St. Basil, St. Augustine, St. Thomas Aquinas
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/excellence`, `exegesis/methods/four-senses`

### The Four Senses of Scripture
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/four-senses`
- **Description**: The doctrine that Scripture expresses at least four meanings in a single phrase — literal/historical, allegorical, tropological (moral), and anagogical — making it far more difficult than profane writings.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iii-p3` — Scripture surpasses all other writings in having not only significance of words but also of things signified; the literal, allegorical, tropological, and anagogical senses.
    text: "while other writings express only one meaning in a single phrase, this one expresses at least four meanings. For it has significance not only of words but also of the things signified by them; whence it follows that the literal sense conveys the understanding of the historical event... the allegorical sense, portends something prophetic about Christ the Lord; in the tropological sense, recommends something suited to the formation of morals; and rising still higher in a third way, through anagogy proposes the heavenly Mysteries"
  - `#chapter-ii-p2` — Origen maps the four senses to four cosmic elements: history to earth, tropological to water, allegorical to air, anagogical to fire.
    text: "just as you fit the historical sense to earth and the tropological to water, so rightly you may fit the allegorical to air, and the anagogical to fire and the ether"
- **Related people**: Origen, St. Jerome, St. Gregory the Great
- **Related subjects**: `exegesis/methods/allegory`, `exegesis/methods/literal-sense`, `theology/scripture/breadth`

### Allegorical Interpretation
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/allegory`
- **Description**: The principles and limits of allegorical (mystical) interpretation of Scripture: it is a true sense intended by the Holy Spirit, must be grounded in the historical sense, and cannot be freely invented.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iii-p6` — The mystical sense is the principal sense of Scripture; the entire Old Testament narrates historical deeds but above all signifies Christ symbolically.
    text: "as regards the mystical sense — that this is the principal sense of Scripture, the entire Old Testament proclaims, which most directly narrates the deeds of that time, or things to be done, but above all signifies Christ everywhere symbolically"
  - `#chapter-iii-p7` — Jonathan's arrow analogy: the historical sense is prior but the mystical is more important; from the mystical sense the theologian may draw the strongest argument, provided it is the genuine sense.
    text: "the historical sense of Scripture is the prior one, but the mystical is the more important; and from this latter, as from the former, the theologian may draw the strongest argument for establishing his doctrine, provided it is certain that it is the genuine sense"
  - `#chapter-iii-p8` — It is a pernicious opinion to hold that spiritual senses are mere fabrications anyone can adapt to any passage.
    text: "to hold that the spiritual senses are mere fabrications, and that anyone can by his own invention adapt them to any passage... this is a pernicious opinion to hold, and more dangerous still to put into practice"
  - `#chapter-iii-p10` — The Fathers who engaged most with allegory did not rashly impose it but bound it to the reality so it agreed fittingly in all respects.
    text: "filled with the same Spirit, they did not rashly impose it wherever it seemed to smile upon them, or to bolster their own ideas... but they so bound it to the reality that it agreed fittingly in all respects"
  - `#chapter-iii-p11` — Jerome teaches that allegory must correspond to history; he rebukes himself for once explaining Obadiah allegorically without grasping the historical meaning first.
    text: "St. Jerome, writing on Hosea chapter 10, teaches that to apply tropologically to Christ what is commonly said of the king of Assyria... is impious; and in his prologue to Obadiah, he rebukes himself for having once explained that prophet allegorically without yet having grasped his historical meaning"
  - `#the-authors-method-p2` — Lapide describes his middle way between those who dwell only on the letter (Jewish and base) and those who delight excessively in allegorical speculation alone (inept), following Nazianzen.
    text: "one must proceed by a middle way between those who, with a coarser intellect, dwell on the letter, and those who delight excessively in allegorical speculation alone: for the one is Jewish and base, the other inept and worthy of a dream-interpreter"
  - `#the-authors-method-p2` — Origen's excess in allegory: fleeing and destroying historical truth, interpreting Eve's formation, paradise trees, and garments of skin mystically.
    text: "Origen went astray in this extreme, when, fleeing — indeed, destroying — the historical truth, he often substitutes something symbolic in its place"
- **Related people**: Origen, St. Jerome, St. Augustine, St. Gregory Nazianzen
- **Related subjects**: `exegesis/methods/four-senses`, `exegesis/methods/literal-sense`, `exegesis/methods/typology`

### Literal Sense of Scripture
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/literal-sense`
- **Description**: The primacy of the literal/historical sense as the foundation upon which all other senses must rest, and the difficulty of attaining even this one sense correctly.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-ii-p3` — Scripture embraces all knowledge even in the literal sense alone, which holds the first place and must above all be pursued.
    text: "Sacred Scripture, in its sense — not only the mystical, but even in the literal sense alone, which holds the first place and which above all must be pursued — embraces all knowledge and everything knowable"
  - `#chapter-iii-p4` — From the four senses you can scarcely attain even one genuine sense.
    text: "from these you can scarcely attain even one genuine sense; how then will you so easily and rashly promise the other three?"
  - `#chapter-iii-p12` — Even the historical sense alone requires many aids: it is hidden in Hebrew and Greek idiom, in a style different from all others, often soaring to great heights.
    text: "as for the historical sense, even if that alone were to suffice for you, how many and how great are the aids required? How often is it hidden! How deeply concealed in the Hebrew or Greek manner of expression"
  - `#the-authors-method-p2` — Lapide's stated method: first track down the historical sense with exact investigation; prefer the reading most consonant with the text; defend the Vulgate edition.
    text: "I may first track down the historical sense with exact investigation; wherever it will be different among various authors, I will indicate it; and in so great a multitude of opinions... I will prefer and select the one most consonant with the text"
- **Related people**: St. Thomas Aquinas, St. Jerome
- **Related subjects**: `exegesis/methods/four-senses`, `exegesis/methods/allegory`

### Typology: Old Testament as Figure of the New
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/typology`
- **Description**: The Old Testament as a prefiguration of Christ and the New Covenant; all things said in the Old Testament pertain to Christ and Christians in either the literal or allegorical sense.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#utility-iv-p2` — Since Christ is the end of the law, all things in the Old Testament pertain to Christ; it everywhere has an allegorical sense besides the literal. Paul: "these things happened as figures of us."
    text: "since Christ is the end of the law, all things said in the Old Testament pertain to Christ and Christians, either in the literal or allegorical sense; and in this the Old Testament surpasses the New, because the Old everywhere has, besides the literal sense, an allegorical sense"
  - `#utility-vi-p2` — The Old Testament was a prelude to the New, bearing testimony to it like John the Baptist to Christ; at the Transfiguration Moses and Elijah appeared to bear witness.
    text: "the Old Testament was a prelude to the New, and bore testimony to it, just as St. John the Baptist did to Christ the Lord"
  - `#utility-vi-p3` — Christ appeals to Moses: "if you believed Moses, you would also believe Me: for he wrote about Me." Philip to Nathanael: "Him whom Moses wrote of in the law, and the prophets, we have found — Jesus."
    text: "if you believed Moses, you would perhaps also believe Me: for he wrote about Me; but if you do not believe his writings, how will you believe My words?"
  - `#moses-and-christ-p2` — Moses was an express sign and type of Christ: as the sun illuminates the day and the moon the night, so Christ illuminated Christians and Moses the Jews.
    text: "Moses was an express sign and type of Christ; and therefore just as the sun illuminates the day, and the moon the night, so Christ illuminated Christians in the new law, and Moses the Jews in the old"
- **Related people**: Moses, Christ, Eusebius, St. Paul
- **Related subjects**: `exegesis/methods/allegory`, `theology/scripture/old-testament`

### Difficulty of Sacred Scripture
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/difficulty`
- **Description**: Sacred Scripture is far more difficult to understand than all profane writings, requiring knowledge of Hebrew and Greek idiom, reading of the Fathers, and divine assistance, not merely natural intellect.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iii-p2` — Scripture is far more difficult than all profane writings because it expresses four meanings in a single phrase.
    text: "I maintain that Sacred Scripture is far more difficult to understand than all profane writings — Greek, Latin, Hebrew, and any others"
  - `#chapter-iii-p13` — The sacred books encompass the thoughts of the Holy Spirit; one must raise oneself aloft to soar through divine utterances to divine thoughts.
    text: "the sacred books encompass in their words the thoughts of the Holy Spirit and the wisdom of the eternal Word: so that one must not creep along the ground, but raise oneself aloft"
  - `#chapter-iii-p19` — Understanding Kings, Maccabees, Daniel requires knowledge of Gentile history, monarchies, customs of nations, geography, and cosmography.
    text: "how much Gentile history of various kinds must be known! How many monarchies — of the Assyrians, Medes, Persians, Greeks, and Romans — must be thoroughly learned!"
  - `#chapter-iv-p24` — The difficulty is increased by Hebrew and Greek idioms scattered everywhere; Augustine teaches that knowledge of languages is a great remedy against unknown signs.
    text: "against unknown signs," says Augustine, chapters 11 and 13, "a great remedy is the knowledge of languages." For there are certain words which cannot pass into the usage of another language through translation"
  - `#chapter-iv-p25` — St. Chrysostom asserts there is not a syllable in the sacred Letters in whose depths some great treasure does not lie hidden; we need divine grace to approach them.
    text: "there is not a syllable, not even a single stroke in the sacred Letters, in whose depths some great treasure does not lie hidden"
- **Related people**: St. Jerome, St. Augustine, St. Chrysostom, St. Gregory the Great
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/scripture-interpretation`, `exegesis/methods/four-senses`

### Scripture Interpretation: Authority of Church and Fathers
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/scripture-interpretation`
- **Description**: Scripture must be interpreted not by private judgment but under the guidance of the Church, the Fathers, and apostolic tradition, with the Council of Trent's decrees as guardrails.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-ii-p29` — Jerome rebukes those who claim Scripture for themselves without learning: they fit incongruous testimonies to their own meaning, twisting Scripture to suit their will.
    text: "The art of the Scriptures alone," he says, "is one that everyone everywhere claims for themselves... nor do they deign to know what the Prophets and Apostles meant, but they fit incongruous testimonies to their own meaning"
  - `#chapter-ii-p30` — Many bend Scripture like wax, twisting it into alien senses against the decrees of the Fathers, Canons, Councils, and the Council of Trent.
    text: "they bend Scripture like wax in every direction, transform it into every shape by a marvelous metamorphosis, and like gamblers with the divine words play with it as the lot falls"
  - `#chapter-iv-p3` — Hebrew tradition: Moses received on Sinai not only the law but also its explanation, commanded to reveal hidden mysteries to Joshua, and they to their successors.
    text: "Moses received on Mount Sinai from God not only the law but also the explanation of the law, and that he was commanded to write down the law, but to reveal its hidden mysteries and meanings to Joshua, and Joshua to the priests"
  - `#chapter-iv-p21` — Dionysius and Clement teach that Scriptures were handed down to be taught to disciples and transmitted to posterity in continuous succession.
    text: "St. Dionysius, the disciple of the Apostle Paul, and Clement, the disciple of St. Peter, teach that the Scriptures were handed down to them, so that they too might teach them to their own disciples, and transmit them to posterity in a continuous succession"
  - `#chapter-iv-p18` — Basil and Gregory studied Scripture for 13 years in a monastery, not from their own presumption but from the writings and authority of the elders who received the rule of interpretation from apostolic succession.
    text: "the divine volumes," says Rufinus, book XI of his History, chapter IX, "they studied through commentary not from their own presumption, but from the writings and authority of the elders, whom they knew had likewise received from apostolic succession the rule of interpretation."
- **Related people**: St. Jerome, St. Basil, St. Gregory Nazianzen, Dionysius the Areopagite, St. Clement
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/difficulty`, `ecclesiology/councils/trent`

### The Old Testament: Value and Necessity for Christians
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/old-testament`

### The Six Utilities of the Old Testament
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/six-utilities-ot`
- **Description**: Lapide's systematic enumeration of six benefits of the Old Testament: (I) it establishes faith, (II) it is richer in content than the New, (III) the New cannot be understood without it, (IV) it surpasses the New in allegorical richness, (V) it provides figures, examples, and maxims, (VI) it served as forerunner to the New.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#utility-i-p2` — The Old Testament establishes faith: from it we know creation, the immortal soul, original sin, the Cherubim, paradise, God's providence.
    text: "the Old Testament, just like the New, establishes faith. Whence, I ask, do we know the world's beginning, creation, and Creator, unless because by faith we believe that the ages were fashioned by the word of God?"
  - `#utility-ii-p2` — The Old Testament is far richer than the New: abundant ethics in Proverbs, politics in Moses's judicial laws, oracles in the Prophets, history from the founding of the world.
    text: "the Old is far richer than the New. You may see abundant ethics in Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and Ecclesiasticus: admirable politics in the deeds and judicial and ceremonial laws of Moses"
  - `#utility-iii-p2` — Without the Old Testament the New cannot be understood: the Apostles and Christ frequently cite it.
    text: "without the Old Testament, the New cannot be understood: the Apostles and Christ frequently cite it, and still more frequently allude to it"
  - `#utility-iv-p2` — The Old Testament surpasses the New in allegorical richness because it everywhere has allegorical, anagogical, and tropological senses.
    text: "in this the Old Testament surpasses the New, because the Old everywhere has, besides the literal sense, an allegorical sense, and often also an anagogical and tropological one: the New almost lacks the allegorical"
  - `#utility-v-p2` — God provided that from the Old Testament one might draw figures, examples, maxims, and oracles for every instruction of an honorable life.
    text: "from the Old Testament one might draw so great a variety of figures, examples, maxims, and oracles, not only for faith, but for every instruction of an honorable life"
  - `#utility-vi-p2` — The Old Testament was a prelude and forerunner to the New, bearing testimony to it like John the Baptist to Christ.
    text: "the Old Testament was a prelude to the New, and bore testimony to it, just as St. John the Baptist did to Christ the Lord"
- **Related people**: St. Paul, Christ, St. Augustine, Eusebius
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/old-testament`, `exegesis/methods/typology`

### The Septuagint Translation
- **Slug**: `exegesis/textual-criticism/septuagint`
- **Description**: The translation of the Old Testament from Hebrew into Greek by 72 interpreters under Ptolemy Philadelphus, their miraculous agreement, and the subsequent history of Greek and Latin translations.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#the-septuagint-p2` — God put it into Ptolemy Philadelphus's mind to choose 72 interpreters through Eleazar the high priest to translate the Old Testament; in 70 days they agreed on the same words, each working in a separate cell.
    text: "to choose, through Eleazar the high priest, six of the most learned men from each tribe of the Hebrews — that is, 72 interpreters — to translate the Old Testament from Hebrew into Greek, and He assisted them so that in 70 days, with the complete agreement of all, they accomplished the work"
  - `#the-septuagint-p3` — After the Seventy, subsequent translators included Aquila of Pontus (under Hadrian), Theodotion, Symmachus, and two anonymous translators found in jars.
    text: "The second interpreter of Sacred Scripture from Hebrew after the Seventy... was Aquila of Pontus, who in the 12th year of the Emperor Hadrian translated the Hebrew Scripture into Greek"
