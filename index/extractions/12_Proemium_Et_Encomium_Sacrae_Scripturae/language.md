### אֲשֶׁר רִיק קוֹרֵא לְמוֹרֶה לֹא אָמֵן לְבוֹרֵא (Ascher ric core lemore lo omen lebore)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/ascher-ric-core-lemore`
- **Transliteration**: asher riq qore lemoreh lo omen lebore
- **Old Hebrew**: 𐤀𐤔𐤓 𐤓𐤉𐤒 𐤒𐤓𐤀 𐤋𐤌𐤓𐤄 𐤋𐤀 𐤀𐤌𐤍 𐤋𐤁𐤓𐤀
- **Meaning**: "He who easily and rashly trusts a teacher, distrusts the Creator" — Hebrew proverbial adage cited by Lapide to warn against credulity toward philosophers
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-ii-p11` — Lapide cites this Hebrew adage after recounting the errors of Pythagoras on metempsychosis
    text: "Is not the well-known Hebrew adage most true here: ascher ric core lemore lo omen lebore, that is, 'He who easily and rashly trusts a teacher, distrusts the Creator'?"

### צָוָה (Tsava)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/tsava`
- **Transliteration**: tsavah
- **Old Hebrew**: 𐤑𐤅𐤄
- **Meaning**: "To ordain, to provide, to dispose" — a Hebrew verb whose range of meaning Lapide explains to refute Calvin's argument that God commanded evil; it signifies all of God's providence, both positive and permissive
- **Root**: צוה
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#utility-i-p7` — Lapide explains the Hebrew tsava as a key Hebraism: it means "to ordain, to provide, to dispose" and covers all of God's providence (positive, negative, and permissive), refuting Calvin's claim that "the Lord commanded Shimei to curse David" means God authored evil
    text: "whoever does not know what the Hebrew tsava means, that is, 'God commanded Shimei,' etc., will not escape Calvin's clutches; but whoever knows the Hebraism, namely that tsava means to ordain, to provide, to dispose, and signifies all of God's providence, both positive and negative and permissive, will blow away this weapon like a spider's web."
- **Related**: `person/historical/calvin`, `subject/theology/providence`

### μόσχος / μοσχεύματα (Moschos / Moschevmata)
- **Slug**: `greek/moschos`
- **Transliteration**: moschos / moschevmata
- **Meaning**: "Calf" / "calf-slips" (i.e., shoots, propagations) — Lapide, citing Augustine, explains a Greek translation difficulty in Wisdom 4:3 where moschevmata (shoots from a tree) was confused with a derivative of moschos (calf)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iv-p24` — Lapide, via Augustine, explains the Greek wordplay: the translator derives moschevmata ("calf-slips") from moschos ("calf"), but mischevmata actually means shoots or propagations cut from a tree
    text: "the translator uses a Greek construction, and derives, as it were, from moschos (calf) the word moschevmata, that is, from 'calf' the word 'calf-slips'; but mischevmata are actually shoots or propagations, new sprigs cut from a tree and planted in the ground."
- **Related**: `verse/wisdom/4`, `subject/theology/scripture/translation`

### אֶהְיֶה (Ehyeh)
- **Slug**: `hebrew/ehyeh`
- **Transliteration**: ehyeh
- **Old Hebrew**: 𐤀𐤄𐤉𐤄
- **Meaning**: "Who is" or "I am who I am" — the divine name revealed to Moses (Exodus 3:14), cited by Lapide via Justin to show Plato's dependence on Moses
- **Root**: היה
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p10` — Lapide, citing Justin, explains that Plato called God "to on" (that which is) because he learned from Moses, who calls God "ehyeh" (who is, or I am who I am)
    text: "he likewise called Him 'to on,' that is, 'that which is,' just as Moses calls Him 'ehyeh,' that is, 'who is,' or 'I am who I am.'"
- **Related**: `greek/to-on`, `person/biblical/moses`, `subject/theology/divine-names`

### τὸ ὄν (To on)
- **Slug**: `greek/to-on`
- **Transliteration**: to on
- **Meaning**: "That which is" — Plato's philosophical term for God, which Lapide (via Justin) argues was borrowed from Moses's "ehyeh"
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#praises-of-moses-p10` — Lapide explains that Plato called God "to on" because he learned the concept from Moses, who calls God "ehyeh"
    text: "Plato learned about God from Moses, whence he likewise called Him 'to on,' that is, 'that which is,' just as Moses calls Him 'ehyeh,' that is, 'who is,' or 'I am who I am.'"
- **Related**: `hebrew/ehyeh`, `person/historical/plato`, `subject/theology/divine-names`

### διήγησις (Diegesis)
- **Slug**: `greek/diegesis`
- **Transliteration**: diegesis
- **Meaning**: "Narration, exposition" — Lapide notes the Greek reading of Ecclesiasticus where the Latin has "discourse," the Greek has diegesis (narration, exposition)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#chapter-iv-p11` — Lapide cites the Greek term from Ecclesiasticus, glossing diegesis as "the narration, the exposition" of famous men
    text: "he will preserve the discourse (in Greek diegesis -- the narration, the exposition) of famous men"
- **Related**: `verse/ecclesiasticus`, `subject/theology/scripture/study-of-scripture`
