# Verses — 01_Preliminares

## Dedicatory Letter (`#dedicatory-letter`)

### John 3:30
- **Slug**: `john/3/30`
- **Text**: "He must increase, but I must decrease"
- **Context**: Lapide applies John the Baptist's words about Christ to Van der Burch's rising career — as Van der Burch ascended through ecclesiastical offices, Lapide foresaw his own decrease. The Latin quotes the Vulgate: *Illum oportet crescere, me autem minui.*
- **Type**: Direct quotation, explicitly attributed
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#dedicatory-letter` ~p3 (line 93) — "But what Saint John the Baptist said of Christ — 'He must increase, but I must decrease' — this I long foresaw concerning your Most Illustrious Lordship and myself"
- **Related people**: St. John the Baptist

### Exodus 4:10,13
- **Slug**: `exodus/4/10` and `exodus/4/13`
- **Text**: "I beseech you, Lord, I am not eloquent, neither heretofore nor since you have spoken to your servant; but I am slow of speech and of tongue: I beseech you, Lord, send whom you will send."
- **Context**: Moses' reluctance to accept God's call, cited as a parallel to Van der Burch's own reluctance to accept the archbishopric. The Latin explicitly cites "Exodi iv."
- **Type**: Direct quotation, explicitly cited ("In Exodus iv he says")
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#dedicatory-letter` ~p11 (line 141) — Moses declining the leadership, provoking God's anger
- **Related people**: Moses

### Numbers 12:3
- **Slug**: `numbers/12/3`
- **Text**: "he was the meekest of all mortals"

The Vulgate reads: *Erat enim Moyses vir mitissimus super omnes homines qui morabantur in terra.* The Latin version says *S. Scriptura* attributes this praise.
- **Type**: Paraphrase, attributed to "Holy Scripture"
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#dedicatory-letter` ~p14 (line 157) — "Holy Scripture bestows this praise upon Moses: that he was the meekest of all mortals"
- **Related people**: Moses

### Wisdom 8:1
- **Slug**: `wisdom/8/1`
- **Text**: "reaches from end to end mightily, and orders all things sweetly"
- **Context**: Lapide quotes the Sage (Sapientia) on divine providence, applying it to Van der Burch's governance style. The Latin cites "teste Sapiente" and quotes: *attingit a fine usque ad finem fortiter, et disponit omnia suaviter.* This becomes Van der Burch's motto: "Sweetly and Strongly" (*Suaviter et fortiter*).
- **Type**: Direct quotation, attributed to "the Sage"
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#dedicatory-letter` ~p15 (line 161) — "That most wise providence of God which divinely governs the whole world, as the Sage testifies, 'reaches from end to end mightily, and orders all things sweetly.'"
- **Related people**: Solomon (implied author of Wisdom)

The phrase *Cibum esurientibus frangit* echoes Psalm 146:7 (Vulgate): *qui dat escam esurientibus* / *Dominus solvit compeditos.*
- **Type**: Direct quotation (within a patristic quotation), attributed to "Scripture"
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#dedicatory-letter` ~p17 (line 169) — within Gregory Nazianzen's speech: "he did what Scripture says: He breaks bread for the hungry, satisfies the poor with loaves, nourishes them in famine"

### Numbers 6:1-21 (Nazirites)
- **Slug**: `numbers/6/1-21`
- **Text**: (referenced as "Numbers v" in the English / "Num. v" in the Latin)
- **Context**: Lapide states that Moses "instituted the Nazirites and dictated laws for them in Numbers v." The laws of the Nazirites are actually in Numbers 6:1-21 in modern versification; Lapide's citation "Numbers v" may reflect older chapter divisions or a slight error (Numbers 5 contains the law of jealousy/trial of bitter water; Numbers 6 is the Nazirite vow). The Latin reads "Num. v."
- **Type**: Explicit citation (book and chapter)
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#dedicatory-letter` ~p20 (line 181) — "Moses instituted the Nazirites and dictated laws for them in Numbers v."
- **Related people**: Moses
- **Note**: The Nazirite legislation is in Numbers 6, not Numbers 5. This may be a citation error in the original text, or it may reflect older chapter numbering conventions.

## Council of Trent Decrees (`#trent-decrees`)

### Canonical Books of the Old Testament (group reference)
- **Slug**: (group reference — not individual verses)
- **Text**: The Trent decree lists all canonical books of the Old Testament
- **Context**: The Council of Trent, Session IV, lists the complete canon of Scripture. The Old Testament books enumerated are:
  - **Pentateuch**: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy
  - **Historical**: Joshua (Josue), Judges, Ruth, four books of Kings, two of Paralipomenon (Chronicles), first and second of Esdras (Ezra), Nehemiah (Nehemias), Tobit (Tobias), Judith, Esther
  - **Wisdom**: Job, Psalms (150 psalms), Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Canticle of Canticles (Song of Songs), Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus (Sirach)
  - **Prophets**: Isaiah (Isaias), Jeremiah (Jeremias) with Baruch, Ezekiel (Ezechiel), Daniel
  - **Minor Prophets**: Hosea (Osee), Joel, Amos, Obadiah (Abdias), Jonah (Jonas), Micah (Michæas), Nahum, Habakkuk (Habacuc), Zephaniah (Sophonias), Haggai (Aggæus), Zechariah (Zacharias), Malachi (Malachias)
  - **Maccabees**: two books of the Maccabees
- **Type**: Canonical enumeration
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#trent-decrees` ~p3 (line 358) — "Of the Old Testament: the five books of Moses, to wit, Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy; Josue, Judges, Ruth..."
- **Note**: This is not a citation of any particular verse but a formal dogmatic enumeration of the entire Old Testament canon.
