### Biblical Canon (Hebrew)
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/biblical-canon`
- **Description**: The structure of the Hebrew Bible as twenty-two (or twenty-four) books arranged in three orders -- Law, Prophets, Hagiographa -- corresponding to the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet, as enumerated by Jerome in his Helmeted Prologue.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p2` — Jerome establishes that there are twenty-two letters among the Hebrews, attested by the Syriac and Chaldean cognate languages, and that twenty-two books of the Old Testament correspond to these letters as the alphabet of divine instruction.
    text: "so twenty-two books are reckoned, by which, as by letters and beginnings, the still tender and nursing infancy of the righteous man is instructed in the teaching of God."
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p8` — The five books of Moses constitute the Torah, the first division of the Hebrew canon.
    text: "These are the five books of Moses, which they properly call Torah, that is, the Law."
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p9` — The second order of the Prophets begins with Joshua son of Nave.
    text: "The second order they make of the Prophets, and they begin with Joshua the son of Nave, who among them is called Josue ben Nun."
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p18` — The third order possesses the Hagiographa.
    text: "The third order possesses the Hagiographa."
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p28` — Summary of twenty-two books yielding an alternative count of twenty-four, linked to the twenty-four elders of the Apocalypse.
    text: "And so the books of the old law come equally to twenty-two: that is, five of Moses, eight of the Prophets, and nine of the Hagiographa. Although some write Ruth and Cinoth among the Hagiographa and think these books should be counted in their own number, and that thereby there are twenty-four books of the ancient law"
- **Related people**: Jerome
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/apocrypha`, `theology/scripture/hebrew-alphabet`

### Apocrypha
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/apocrypha`
- **Description**: Books placed outside the Hebrew canon by Jerome -- Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, the Shepherd of Hermas, and the Maccabees -- identified as non-canonical in the Helmeted Prologue.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p29` — Jerome states that whatever falls outside the twenty-two canonical books is to be placed among the apocrypha, and explicitly names Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, the Shepherd, and Maccabees.
    text: "whatever is outside of this is to be placed among the apocrypha. Therefore the Wisdom which is commonly ascribed to Solomon, and the book of Jesus son of Sirach, and Judith, and Tobit, and the Shepherd, are not in the canon. The first book of the Maccabees I found to be Hebrew. The second is Greek, which can be proved also from its very style."
- **Related people**: Jerome, Solomon, Jesus son of Sirach
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/biblical-canon`

### Hebrew Alphabet (Mystical Significance)
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/hebrew-alphabet`
- **Description**: The mystical significance of the twenty-two Hebrew letters, their correspondence to the books of the Old Testament, the five doubled letters (Caph, Mem, Nun, Pe, Sade), and the alphabetical structure underlying several psalms, Lamentations, and Proverbs.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p2` — Jerome details the twenty-two Hebrew letters attested by Syriac and Chaldean cognates, the five doubled letters with different medial and final forms, and the alphabetical acrostic structure of several psalms, Lamentations, and Proverbs.
    text: "That there are twenty-two letters among the Hebrews is attested also by the language of the Syrians and Chaldeans, which is largely cognate with Hebrew; for they too have twenty-two elements with the same sound but different characters."
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p2` — The five doubled Hebrew letters and their correspondence to five double books.
    text: "Furthermore, five letters among the Hebrews are doubled: Caph, Mem, Nun, Pe, Sade; for the beginnings and middles of words are written differently through these letters than their endings. Whence also five books are considered by most to be double"
- **Related people**: Jerome
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/biblical-canon`

### Jerome's Translation of Scripture
- **Slug**: `exegesis/textual-criticism/jerome-translation`
- **Description**: Jerome's own account of his translation project in the Helmeted Prologue, presenting his work as a personal offering to God's temple and defending it against detractors, with an appeal to the Hebrew original as the standard of truth.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p29` — Jerome defends his translation labor as a humble offering to the temple of God, compares himself to those who offer skins and goats' hair, and asserts the reader should compare his work against the Hebrew truth.
    text: "In the temple of God each one offers what he can: some offer gold, silver, and precious stones; others offer fine linen and purple and scarlet and hyacinth; it goes well with us if we offer skins and goats' hair."
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p29` — Jerome claims ownership of his translation through diligent study and emendation, and challenges critics to verify against the Hebrew.
    text: "Read therefore first my Samuel and my Melachim -- mine, I say, mine. For whatever we have both learned by more frequent translating and hold by more careful emending is ours."
- **Related people**: Jerome
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/biblical-canon`, `exegesis/textual-criticism/vulgate-editions`

### Scripture Study and Teaching Authority
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/scripture-study`
- **Description**: The necessity of diligent study and qualified teachers for understanding Scripture, illustrated by examples from pagan learning (Pythagoras, Plato, Apollonius), apostolic practice (Paul at Gamaliel's feet, Paul visiting Peter), and the Ethiopian eunuch's encounter with Philip. Jerome insists that Scripture alone among arts requires a guide, and warns against the presumption of those who teach without learning.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome praises Paulinus's zeal for learning, citing Pythagoras, Plato, and Apollonius who traversed the world to learn from teachers, and then Paul who went up to Jerusalem to see Peter, because "the living voice has a certain hidden power."
    text: "For the living voice has a certain hidden power, and poured from the mouth of the author into the ears of the student, it sounds more forcefully."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Paul learned the law at the feet of Gamaliel, Timothy was instructed from infancy in sacred letters, and Titus was required to have knowledge of the Scriptures as a bishop's virtue.
    text: "Paul the apostle boasts that he learned the law of Moses and the Prophets at the feet of Gamaliel, so that armed with spiritual weapons he might afterward say with confidence"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — The priest's duty to respond when asked about the law, citing Malachi/Haggai and Deuteronomy; the distinction between "just rusticity" and "learned justice" from Daniel.
    text: "So great is the office of the priest to respond when asked about the law."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — The Ethiopian eunuch demonstrates that even devout readers need a teacher: Philip came and showed him Jesus hidden in the letter.
    text: "you might understand that you cannot enter the sacred Scriptures without a guide who shows the way."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome condemns those who presume to teach Scripture without learning: the chattering old woman, the doddering old man, the wordy sophist, and those who corrupt sentences and drag Scripture to their own will.
    text: "The art of the Scriptures alone is one that everyone everywhere claims for themselves."
- **Related people**: Jerome, Paulinus, Paul, Gamaliel, Timothy, Titus (bishop), Philip, Ethiopian eunuch, Pythagoras, Plato, Apollonius of Tyana
- **Related places**: Jerusalem, Damascus, Arabia, Athens, Rhodes, Memphis, Ethiopia
- **Related subjects**: `exegesis/methods/spiritual-interpretation`, `theology/scripture/logos`

### Spiritual Interpretation of Scripture
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/spiritual-interpretation`
- **Description**: The principle that Scripture has a spiritual sense beyond the letter, requiring divine revelation to be understood. Jerome affirms "the law is spiritual" and uses the images of the sealed book of Revelation and the Ethiopian eunuch to show that Christ is the hidden key to all Scripture.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome declares the law is spiritual and needs revelation, citing the sealed book of the Apocalypse that only the one with the key of David can open.
    text: "For the law is spiritual, and there is need of revelation for it to be understood, and with unveiled face we contemplate the glory of God."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — The Ethiopian eunuch held the book and sounded the words on his lips but did not know the one he worshipped in the book; Philip showed him Jesus hidden in the letter.
    text: "Philip came and showed him Jesus, who lay hidden, shut up in the letter."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p12` — Leviticus: every syllable and the vestments of Aaron breathe forth heavenly mysteries.
    text: "the individual sacrifices, nay almost every single syllable, and the vestments of Aaron, and the entire Levitical order breathe forth heavenly mysteries."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p20` — The Books of Kings: surface history vs. hidden meaning, where the Church's struggles and the wars of heretics are narrated.
    text: "If you look at the history, the words are simple; if you examine the hidden meaning in the text, the smallness of the Church and the wars of heretics against the Church are narrated."
- **Related people**: Jerome, Philip, Ethiopian eunuch, David
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/scripture-study`, `exegesis/methods/typology`

### Typology
- **Slug**: `exegesis/methods/typology`
- **Description**: The Old Testament as prefiguration of Christ and the Church: Joshua as a type of the Lord, Judges' princes as types, Ruth fulfilling Isaiah's prophecy, Samuel prefiguring the transition from old to new priesthood, Esther as a type of the Church, and Deuteronomy as a prefiguration of the Gospel law.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p16` — Joshua bears the type of the Lord not only in deeds but in his name, and through his conquests describes the spiritual kingdoms of the Church and the heavenly Jerusalem.
    text: "I come to Joshua son of Nave, who bears the type of the Lord not only in his deeds but even in his name; he crosses the Jordan, overthrows the kingdoms of the enemies, divides the land for the victorious people"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p17` — In Judges, as many princes as there are, so many types there are.
    text: "In the book of Judges, as many princes of the people, so many types there are."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p19` — Samuel shows the old law abolished in the death of Eli and slaying of Saul, and attests the mysteries of new priesthood and kingdom in Zadok and David.
    text: "Samuel, in the death of Eli and the slaying of Saul, shows the old law abolished. Furthermore, in Zadok and David, it attests the mysteries of a new priesthood and a new kingdom."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p14` — Deuteronomy is the prefiguration of the Gospel law: everything prior is made new from the old.
    text: "Deuteronomy also, the second law and prefiguration of the Gospel law -- does it not contain the things that are prior in such a way that yet all things are new from the old?"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p40` — Esther, in the type of the Church, frees the people from danger.
    text: "Esther, in the type of the Church, frees the people from danger"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Christ was predestined and prefigured in the Law and the Prophets; the Prophets are called seers because they saw him whom others did not see.
    text: "he who was hidden in mystery was predestined before the ages; but predestined and prefigured in the Law and the Prophets. Whence the Prophets are also called seers, because they saw him whom the rest did not see."
- **Related people**: Jerome, Joshua, David, Eli, Saul, Zadok, Esther, Haman
- **Related subjects**: `exegesis/methods/spiritual-interpretation`, `theology/christology/christ-prefigured`

### Prophecy
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/prophecy`
- **Description**: The prophetic dimension of Scripture: the prophets as seers who perceived Christ hidden in mystery, the Twelve Prophets as prefiguring more than the letter, and the individual prophetic missions from Hosea through Malachi, culminating in the rejection of Israel and calling of the nations.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — The Prophets are called seers because they saw Christ whom others did not see; Abraham saw his day and rejoiced; the heavens were opened to Ezekiel.
    text: "Whence the Prophets are also called seers, because they saw him whom the rest did not see. Abraham saw his day and rejoiced."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p21` — The twelve prophets, compressed into one volume, prefigure much more than what sounds in the letter.
    text: "The twelve prophets, compressed into the narrow compass of a single volume, prefigure much more than what sounds in the letter."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p26` — Jonah prefigures the passion of the Lord by his own shipwreck and calls the world to repentance.
    text: "Jonah, that most beautiful dove, prefiguring the passion of the Lord by his own shipwreck, calls the world to repentance, and under the name of Nineveh announces salvation to the nations."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p33` — Malachi announces the rejection of Israel and calling of the nations: God's name is great among the nations from the rising of the sun to its setting.
    text: "Malachi, openly, and at the end of all the Prophets, concerning the rejection of Israel and the calling of the nations"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p34` — Isaiah seems to weave not prophecy but a Gospel.
    text: "The first seems to me to weave not prophecy but a Gospel."
  - `#du-culte-p9` — Lacordaire divides the history of Jesus Christ into prophetic, evangelical, and apostolic times: in the first, Christ is awaited and prepared for.
    text: "The history of Jesus Christ is thus divided into three periods distributed across four thousand years: the prophetic times, the evangelical times, and the apostolic times."
  - `#du-culte-p15` — The power of the psalms and prophecies comes from their relationship to Jesus Christ; in them the veil falls and the person of Christ takes shape.
    text: "in the psalms and the prophecies, the veil falls, the mystery clears, the person of Jesus Christ takes shape; one perceives Him being born of a virgin, one follows His steps and His sorrows, one witnesses His death, one sees Him triumph on the third day"
- **Related people**: Jerome, Lacordaire, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Daniel, Jonah, Malachi, Abraham, David
- **Related subjects**: `exegesis/methods/typology`, `theology/christology/christ-prefigured`, `theology/scripture/inspiration`

### Logos (The Word)
- **Slug**: `theology/christology/logos`
- **Description**: Jerome's discussion of the Greek term Logos as applied to Christ in the prologue of John's Gospel: it signifies word, reason, reckoning, and the cause through which all things subsist -- all rightly understood in Christ. Neither Plato nor Demosthenes knew this wisdom.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome explicates the multiple meanings of Logos in Greek and identifies them all with Christ, contrasting the ignorance of Plato and Demosthenes.
    text: "For the Word (Logos) in Greek means many things: it is both word, and reason, and reckoning, and the cause of each thing through which all individual things that exist subsist -- all of which we rightly understand in Christ. This the learned Plato did not know; this the eloquent Demosthenes was ignorant of."
- **Related people**: Jerome, John the Evangelist, Plato, Demosthenes
- **Related subjects**: `theology/christology/wisdom-of-god`, `theology/scripture/scripture-study`

### Wisdom of God and Worldly Wisdom
- **Slug**: `theology/christology/wisdom-of-god`
- **Description**: Christ as the wisdom and power of God, hidden in mystery and predestined before the ages, contrasted with the false wisdom of the world. True wisdom destroys false wisdom; Paul speaks the wisdom of God among the perfect, not the wisdom of this age.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome identifies Christ as the power and wisdom of God, hidden in mystery, in whom all treasures of wisdom and knowledge are hidden, predestined and prefigured in Law and Prophets.
    text: "The wisdom of God is Christ; for Christ is the power of God and the wisdom of God. This wisdom is hidden in mystery, about which the title of the ninth psalm is inscribed"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — True wisdom destroys false wisdom; Paul speaks wisdom among the perfect, not of this age or its princes.
    text: "True wisdom will destroy false wisdom; and although the foolishness of the preaching of the cross exists, nevertheless Paul speaks wisdom among the perfect -- wisdom, however, not of this age, nor of the princes of this age"
- **Related people**: Jerome, Paul
- **Related subjects**: `theology/christology/logos`, `theology/scripture/scripture-study`

### Inspiration of Scripture
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/inspiration`
- **Description**: The divine authorship of Scripture: God moved the will of the writers, aroused and directed their thoughts, so that the Scriptures are not merely an admirable human edifice but a divine work, in which the prophets contributed only the garment of their style. Lacordaire emphasizes the unity of one intelligence presiding over four thousand years of composition.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#du-culte-p6` — Lacordaire describes the Scriptures as traced under the inspiration of God's breath, with God moving the writers' wills and directing their thoughts, making them a divine edifice of infinite truth.
    text: "they were traced under the inspiration of the breath of God, who moved the will of the writers, aroused and directed their thoughts, and that thus they are not merely an admirable edifice of antiquity, of unity, and of holiness, but a divine edifice, the substantial work of infinite truth"
  - `#du-culte-p6` — A single intelligence presides over four thousand years and many authors; the variable accidents of the human element make the immutable divinity of substance appear all the more.
    text: "A work of four thousand years, the hand of many appears in it, but a single intelligence presides over it, and it is the meeting of the one and the many over so long a span that is the first miracle of this sublime composition."
  - `#du-culte-p6` — The first line of Genesis dispels both idolatry (seeing God everywhere) and pantheism (seeing Him nowhere).
    text: "from the very first line, the error of man in his infancy and the error of degenerate man fall at our feet, together with the fictions of idolatry, which sees God everywhere, and the negations of pantheism, which sees Him nowhere."
  - `#du-culte-p7` — Lacordaire reflects on the sweetness and power of the word of God as compared to the word of man.
    text: "What must the word of God be for one who knows how to recognize it and hear it? What must it be to be able to say to oneself: God inspired this thought; it is He who speaks to me through it"
  - `#du-culte-p44` — Genius alone does not dictate such things as the parable of the Prodigal Son; heaven dictated them.
    text: "Genius alone does not dictate such things, and heaven, which dictated them, will never manifest itself in an accent that surpasses language."
- **Related people**: Lacordaire, Moses
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/scripture-study`, `theology/scripture/prophecy`, `theology/scripture/salvation-history`

### Salvation History
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/salvation-history`
- **Description**: The narrative unity of Scripture as the history of God's plan for the world, from creation through the patriarchs, the formation of the people of God, the prophets, the coming of Christ, his death and resurrection, the apostolic mission, and the founding of the Church -- all oriented toward Christ as the center.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#du-culte-p4` — Religion is the first among human things and must have a history that is first; Jesus Christ holds in the annals of the world a place no conqueror, philosopher, or lawgiver could attain.
    text: "No matter how deeply one digs into antiquity or descends again to modern ages, nothing appears with the character of our Scriptures, nor anything with the majesty of Jesus Christ."
  - `#du-culte-p6` — The history of Jesus Christ is the history of heaven and earth, containing the plans of God, primordial laws, the succession of events, the directions of providence, prophecies, and above all the figure of Christ illuminating everything.
    text: "The history of Jesus Christ is the history of heaven and earth. There must be found the plans of God for the world, the primordial and universal laws, the beginnings of races"
  - `#du-culte-p8` — Scripture announces the Savior from the first page under the shades of paradise; the promise transmitted to patriarchs gains clarity from book to book, through David, the prophets, captivity, the rebuilding of the temple, through to Simeon's canticle and the coming of Christ.
    text: "From the very first page, under the stirring shades of the earthly paradise, it announces to us the coming of the Savior of men. This promise, transmitted to the patriarchs, takes on from book to book a clarity that fills all events and drives them toward the future"
  - `#du-culte-p8` — All times meet in Christ: the Gospel succeeds the law and the prophecies; truth, fulfilling the figure, shines upon the past.
    text: "Jesus Christ has come. The Gospel succeeds the law and the prophecies, and truth, fulfilling the figure, shines upon the past, which it explains after having received its testimony. All times meet in Christ, and history takes under His steps its eternal unity."
  - `#du-culte-p9` — The three periods: prophetic, evangelical, and apostolic times distributed across four thousand years.
    text: "The history of Jesus Christ is thus divided into three periods distributed across four thousand years: the prophetic times, the evangelical times, and the apostolic times."
- **Related people**: Lacordaire, David, Simeon, Cyrus
- **Related places**: Jerusalem, Babylon, Bethlehem
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/inspiration`, `theology/scripture/prophecy`, `theology/christology/christ-prefigured`

### Christ Prefigured in the Old Testament
- **Slug**: `theology/christology/christ-prefigured`
- **Description**: The theme of Christ hidden and prefigured throughout the Old Testament -- in events, types, and prophecies -- who is the soul and purpose of all history, perceived dimly under figures by the prophets and clearly only in the Gospel.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Christ was predestined and prefigured in the Law and the Prophets; Abraham saw his day and rejoiced.
    text: "predestined and prefigured in the Law and the Prophets. Whence the Prophets are also called seers, because they saw him whom the rest did not see. Abraham saw his day and rejoiced."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p38` — David sounds forth Christ on the lyre and raises up the risen one from the underworld.
    text: "David, our Simonides, our Pindar and Alcaeus, our Horace too, Catullus and Serenus, sounds forth Christ on the lyre, and on the ten-stringed psaltery raises up the risen one from the underworld."
  - `#du-culte-p8` — From the first page of Scripture, under the shades of paradise, the Savior is announced; events are preparation and prefiguration.
    text: "From the very first page, under the stirring shades of the earthly paradise, it announces to us the coming of the Savior of men."
  - `#du-culte-p15` — In Moses and the historical books, Christ hides beneath events; in the psalms and prophecies the veil falls and His person takes shape.
    text: "Considered in the books of Moses and the history of the Hebrew people, Jesus Christ hides beneath events; He is their soul and their purpose, but in a hidden manner that appears only through the revelation of times and of facts."
- **Related people**: Jerome, Lacordaire, David, Abraham, Moses
- **Related subjects**: `exegesis/methods/typology`, `theology/scripture/prophecy`, `theology/scripture/salvation-history`

### The Gospel
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/gospel`
- **Description**: The unique power and character of the Gospel as the living account of the life of Christ, surpassing all prophecy. Lacordaire compares the Gospel's impact to the first sight of the Alps; it stops the reader and makes him cry out. The Gospel reveals Christ no longer in prophecy but in His true and perceptible form.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#du-culte-p16` — The capital moment of Scripture is the Gospel: the veil falls forever and what was hidden manifests in true form. A man has appeared -- God himself -- and we are about to hear him.
    text: "But the capital moment of Scripture is not there; it is in the Gospel, that is to say, in the living and personal account of the life of Christ."
  - `#du-culte-p17` — The Gospel needs no precautions; however far one is from Christ, it strikes a great blow at the door of the soul. Lacordaire compares it to the first sight of the Alps.
    text: "it is impossible not to feel before that luminous and merciful figure one of the greatest blows ever struck at the door of a human soul."
  - `#du-culte-p18` — The Gospel is the history of a man such as the earth had never seen: born poor, lived poor, died poor, yet filled the world with his name with a breadth and duration leaving no room for anything human.
    text: "It is a man who was born poor, who lived poor, and who died poor; who, from his very poverty, did not make a pedestal for any greatness"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p44` — Jerome describes the four Evangelists as the four-horse chariot of the Lord and true Cherubim, covered with eyes, going wherever the Holy Spirit carries them.
    text: "Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John -- the four-horse chariot of the Lord and the true Cherubim, which is interpreted as 'multitude of knowledge' -- are covered with eyes throughout the whole body"
- **Related people**: Lacordaire, Jerome, Matthew, Mark, Luke, John
- **Related subjects**: `theology/christology/passion-of-christ`, `theology/scripture/salvation-history`

### Passion of Christ
- **Slug**: `theology/christology/passion-of-christ`
- **Description**: The death of Christ on the cross as the summit of history and poetry -- at once most true and most beautiful. Lacordaire meditates on how an innocent just man dying by the ultimate punishment without deserving it reaches the summit of the pathetic, and the account of his end finds the path to every heart.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#du-culte-p46` — The account of Christ's passion rises to the highest accent of history and poetry. A life without stain, charity without limits, the complete sacrifice of self: this explains the divine sympathy his death has obtained from contemporaries and posterity.
    text: "after the account of the public life of Christ comes that of his passion and death. The Gospel, so great up to that point, rises there to the highest accent of history and poetry -- that is to say, of what man possesses that is at once most true and most beautiful."
  - `#du-culte-p46` — An innocent just man dying by the ultimate punishment without deserving it reaches the summit of the pathetic; the entire world is but a faint echo of his story.
    text: "an innocent just man who dies by the ultimate punishment without having deserved it reaches the summit of the pathetic, and if he lived and spoke as Christ did, the entire world will be nothing but a faint echo of his story."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p26` — Jonah prefigures the passion of the Lord by his own shipwreck.
    text: "Jonah, that most beautiful dove, prefiguring the passion of the Lord by his own shipwreck"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p29` — Habakkuk contemplates Christ on the cross: "His glory covered the heavens."
    text: "Habakkuk, the strong and unyielding wrestler, stands upon his watch and sets his foot upon the fortress, so that he may contemplate Christ on the cross"
- **Related people**: Lacordaire, Jerome, Jonah, Habakkuk, Jesus Christ
- **Related subjects**: `theology/scripture/gospel`, `theology/christology/christ-prefigured`

### Psalms and Prophetic Poetry
- **Slug**: `theology/scripture/psalms`
- **Description**: The psalms of David and the prophetic books as the summit of biblical literature, surpassing all secular poetry in power because their source is the love of God and their object is Christ. Lacordaire compares David's Miserere and Isaiah's chapter 53 favorably against Homer, Virgil, Racine, Shakespeare, and Lamartine.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#du-culte-p14` — At the Psalms and the Prophets, prose gives way to poetry and narrative to enthusiasm; the heart is seized by passionate admiration surpassing Homer or Virgil.
    text: "when you arrive at the Psalms of David and the Prophets, a new world will open before you. Prose will give way to poetry, narrative to enthusiasm"

- **Description**: The singular character of Paul's apostolate and epistles. ### History and the Reading of Scripture
- **Slug**: `spirituality/lectio-divina`
- **Description**: Lacordaire's meditation on how to read Scripture: as a practice requiring patience, repeated return, and interior transformation. Scripture does not yield its savor on first reading; one must conquer the spirit of the flesh before knowing the spirit of God. Scripture is "a well dug by the hand of God" -- go to the bottom and the treasure is yours.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#du-culte-p10` — Lacordaire describes his first reading of Scripture without faith, and how thirty years of faith revealed its true character. Scripture communicates nothing but what is of God; even its language of passion lets only the divine part be perceived.
    text: "one must return to it patiently and for a long time; one must exercise oneself in it and nourish oneself on it to grasp its savor; one must conquer the spirit of the flesh, as the apostle Saint Paul says, before knowing and feeling the spirit of God"
  - `#du-culte-p10` — Scripture as a well dug by the hand of God: the miner digs with bleeding hands and finds riches only at the bottom.
    text: "Scripture is a well dug by the hand of God: go to the bottom, and the treasure will be yours."
  - `#du-culte-p13` — Lacordaire's practical counsel: read at leisure, without haste; stop when weary, return when refreshed; drink little but frequently.
    text: "Read, then, the books of Moses and the historical books of the Old Testament; read them at leisure, without any haste, remembering that you are reading the most ancient of the monuments of the human mind."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p48` — Jerome exhorts Paulinus to live among the Scriptures, meditate on them, know nothing else, seek nothing else: "Does it not seem to you already here on earth a dwelling place of the heavenly kingdom?"
    text: "I pray you, dearest brother, to live among these things, to meditate on them, to know nothing else, to seek nothing else. Does it not seem to you already here on earth a dwelling place of the heavenly kingdom?"

### Detachment and Poverty
- **Slug**: `morals/virtues/poverty`
- **Description**: Jerome's exhortation to Paulinus to renounce worldly possessions: cut rather than untie the rope, sell what you have despised, count all spent as gain. For the believer the whole world is wealth; for the unbeliever even an obol is lacking. The apostles left only a boat and nets; the widow's two coins surpassed Croesus.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p48` — Jerome urges Paulinus to hasten in renouncing the world, citing the apostles who left behind only a boat and nets, the widow who cast two small coins, and the principle that the miser lacks what he has as much as what he does not have.
    text: "No one about to renounce the world can profitably sell what he has despised in order to sell. Whatever you have spent from your own resources, count as gain."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p48` — He who offered himself gave everything to God; he easily despises all things who always considers that he is going to die.
    text: "He easily despises all things who always considers that he is going to die."
- **Related people**: Jerome, Paulinus
