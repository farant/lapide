## Jerome

### The Helmeted Prologue (Prologus Galeatus)
- **Slug**: `jerome/prologus-galeatus`
- **Also known as**: Praefatio in Libros Samuelis et Malachim; Preface to the Books of Samuel and Kings
- **Description**: Jerome's preface to his translation of Samuel and Kings, which serves as a general introduction to the Hebrew canon. It establishes the twenty-two books of the Hebrew Bible corresponding to the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet, distinguishes canonical from apocryphal books, and defends Jerome's translation work.
- **Year**: ~391
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#helmeted-prologue` — The full text of the Helmeted Prologue is reproduced as the first section of this file
    text: "I. THE HELMETED PROLOGUE."
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p2` — Jerome establishes the twenty-two-letter Hebrew alphabet and its correspondence with the books of the Old Testament
    text: "That there are twenty-two letters among the Hebrews is attested also by the language of the Syrians and Chaldeans"
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p28` — Jerome discusses whether twenty-two or twenty-four books comprise the Old Law, connecting the latter to the twenty-four elders of the Apocalypse
    text: "And so the books of the old law come equally to twenty-two"
  - `#helmeted-prologue-p29` — Jerome concludes the prologue, noting it applies to all books he translated from Hebrew, and asks for prayers against his detractors
    text: "This prologue, as a helmeted beginning of the Scriptures, can apply to all the books which we have translated from Hebrew into Latin"
- **Related people**: Jerome
- **Related subjects**: Biblical canon, Hebrew alphabet, translation

### Letter to Paulinus (Epistola LIII)
- **Slug**: `jerome/letter-to-paulinus`
- **Also known as**: Epistola LIII; Jerome to Paulinus of Nola
- **Description**: Jerome's letter to Bishop Paulinus of Nola, encouraging the study of Scripture and surveying all the books of the Bible in order, demonstrating that each requires learned interpretation. A foundational text on biblical hermeneutics.
- **Year**: ~394
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus` — The full text of the letter is reproduced as the second section of this file
    text: "II. JEROME TO PAULINUS."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome opens with examples of ancient seekers of knowledge (Pythagoras, Plato, Apollonius, Paul) to commend Paulinus's zeal for learning
    text: "Brother Ambrosius, bringing me your little gifts, delivered at the same time most delightful letters"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p10` — Jerome begins his systematic survey of the books of the Old Testament, starting with Genesis
    text: "Presumably Genesis is perfectly clear"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p43` — Jerome transitions to the New Testament
    text: "Let me touch briefly on the New Testament as well."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p48` — Jerome concludes with exhortations to Paulinus to renounce the world and devote himself to Scripture
    text: "I pray you, dearest brother, to live among these things, to meditate on them, to know nothing else, to seek nothing else."
- **Related people**: Jerome, Paulinus of Nola
- **Related subjects**: Biblical hermeneutics, study of Scripture

## Philostratus

### Life of Apollonius of Tyana
- **Slug**: `philostratus/life-of-apollonius`
- **Also known as**: Vita Apollonii; Τα ες τον Τυανέα Απολλώνιον
- **Description**: Philostratus's biography of the Neo-Pythagorean philosopher Apollonius of Tyana, written in eight volumes, describing Apollonius's extensive travels through Persia, India, Ethiopia, and beyond.
- **Year**: ~220
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome cites Philostratus as having written about Apollonius's travels in eight volumes, as an example of the lengths to which seekers of knowledge go
    text: "Philostratus wrote about this most fully in eight volumes."
- **Related people**: Philostratus, Apollonius of Tyana
- **Related subjects**: Classical philosophy, travel, biography

## Homer

### Iliad and Odyssey (general reference)
- **Slug**: `homer/works`
- **Description**: Homer's epic poems, referenced by Jerome in the context of Homerocentones (centos composed from Homeric lines) and by Lacordaire as the summit of human literary genius.
- **Year**: ~8th century BC
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome refers to Homerocentones as parallel to Virgiliocento, centos that misuse poetic lines
    text: "As though we had not read Homerocentones and Virgiliocento"
  - `#du-culte-p14` — Lacordaire invokes Homer alongside Virgil as the summit of human poetic genius, to which David and Isaiah are compared and found superior
    text: "if it is capable of opening itself, it surrenders to a passionate admiration that it has known only in reading Homer or Virgil"
  - `#du-culte-p15` — Lacordaire lists "Homer's songs on the wrath of Achilles" as the pinnacle of nature's poetry, inferior to David's Miserere
    text: "Homer's songs on the wrath of Achilles"
- **Related people**: Homer
- **Related subjects**: Classical literature, poetry, Homerocentones

## Virgil

### Eclogues
- **Slug**: `virgil/eclogues`
- **Also known as**: Bucolica
- **Description**: Virgil's pastoral poems, cited by Jerome specifically for Eclogue IV (the "Messianic Eclogue") as an example of misreading pagan poetry as Christian.
- **Year**: ~42–39 BC
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p3` — Jerome quotes Eclogue IV.6 as part of his critique of Virgiliocento
    text: "Now the Virgin returns, the Saturnian kingdoms return;"
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p4` — Jerome quotes Eclogue IV.7
    text: "Now a new offspring is sent down from high heaven."
- **Related people**: Virgil, Jerome
- **Related subjects**: Classical literature, Virgiliocento, Messianic prophecy (pagan)

### Aeneid
- **Slug**: `virgil/aeneid`
- **Also known as**: Aeneis
- **Description**: Virgil's epic poem, cited by Jerome for lines repurposed in Virgiliocento as pseudo-Christian texts, and by Lacordaire as an example of the heights of human genius.
- **Year**: ~29–19 BC
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p6` — Jerome quotes Aeneid I.664 as repurposed to depict the Father speaking to the Son
    text: "My son, my strength, my great power alone."
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p8` — Jerome quotes Aeneid II.650 as repurposed for the crucifixion scene
    text: "Such things he kept recalling, and remained fixed."
  - `#du-culte-p15` — Lacordaire lists "those of Virgil on the misfortunes of Aeneas" as among the greatest works of human genius, still inferior to the psalms and prophecies
    text: "those of Virgil on the misfortunes of Aeneas"
- **Related people**: Virgil, Jerome, Lacordaire
- **Related subjects**: Classical literature, Virgiliocento, epic poetry

## Titus Livius (Livy)

### History of Rome (Ab Urbe Condita)
- **Slug**: `livy/ab-urbe-condita`
- **Also known as**: Ab Urbe Condita Libri; History of Rome from Its Foundation
- **Description**: Livy's monumental history of Rome, referenced by Jerome as so famous that it drew visitors from Spain and Gaul.
- **Year**: ~27 BC – AD 14
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome cites Livy as an example of a learned man whose fame drew seekers from the ends of the world
    text: "We read that certain nobles came from the farthest borders of Spain and Gaul to Titus Livius, flowing with the milky fountain of eloquence"
  - `#du-culte-p5` — Lacordaire invokes Livy as a product of dying Rome's greatness
    text: "it was Rome that produced Livy before dying"
- **Related people**: Livy
- **Related places**: Rome, Spain, Gaul
- **Related subjects**: Classical historiography

## Aeschines

### Against Ctesiphon (Oration)
- **Slug**: `aeschines/against-ctesiphon`
- **Also known as**: Κατά Κτησιφῶντος; In Ctesiphontem
- **Description**: Aeschines' oration against Ctesiphon, indirectly cited via the anecdote of Aeschines reading Demosthenes' response (On the Crown) in exile at Rhodes and sighing at the power of the living voice.
- **Year**: 330 BC
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#jerome-to-paulinus-p2` — Jerome recounts the anecdote of Aeschines in exile praising Demosthenes' oration to illustrate the power of the living voice
    text: "Aeschines, when he was in exile at Rhodes and that oration of Demosthenes was read which he had delivered against him, while all marveled and praised it, sighed and said: 'What if you had heard the beast itself sounding forth its own words?'"
- **Related people**: Aeschines, Demosthenes
- **Related places**: Rhodes
- **Related subjects**: Rhetoric, oratory
