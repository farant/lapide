### Lateran Council (under Innocent III)
- **Slug**: `council/lateran-iv`
- **Also known as**: Fourth Lateran Council, Lateran Council
- **Description**: Ecumenical council (1215) under Pope Innocent III. Referenced here for the decree chapter Firmiter on the Supreme Trinity and the Catholic Faith, which teaches that God created both spiritual and corporeal creatures simultaneously at the beginning of time.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#in-the-beginning-p9` — The Lateran Council, chapter Firmiter, interprets "in the beginning" as "in the Son"
    text: "the Lateran Council, chapter Firmiter, on the Supreme Trinity and the Catholic Faith"
  - `#in-the-beginning-p12` — The Lateran Council teaches that angels were created simultaneously with the corporeal world
    text: "the angels were not created before the corporeal world, but simultaneously with it by God, as the Lateran Council teaches"
  - `#heaven-and-earth-p16` — The Lateran Council under Innocent III defines that God created spiritual and corporeal creatures simultaneously at the beginning of time
    text: " the Lateran Council, under Innocent III: "It must be firmly believed that God from the beginning of time created from nothing both creatures at once: the spiritual and the corporeal, the angelic and the worldly.""
- **Related people**: innocent-iii
- **Related organizations**: church/apostolic-see

### Council of Nicaea
- **Slug**: `council/council-of-nicaea`
- **Also known as**: Nicene Council
- **Description**: Ecumenical council (325). Referenced indirectly through St. Athanasius's epistle inscribed with the decrees of the Council against the Arians, in which he discusses the meaning of "to create" in Sacred Scripture.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#he-created-p7` — Athanasius's epistle inscribed with the decrees of the Council of Nicaea against the Arians defines "to create" as making something from nothing
    text: "St. Athanasius, in the epistle inscribed with the decrees of the Council of Nicaea against the Arians"
- **Related people**: athanasius
- **Related organizations**: institution/arians

### Arians
- **Slug**: `institution/arians`
- **Also known as**: Arian heretics
- **Description**: Followers of the heresy of Arius who denied the full divinity of Christ. Referenced here as the opponents against whom the Council of Nicaea issued decrees, and against whom Athanasius wrote.

  - `#he-created-p7` — Athanasius's epistle with the decrees of the Council of Nicaea was written against the Arians
    text: "St. Athanasius, in the epistle inscribed with the decrees of the Council of Nicaea against the Arians"
  - `#verse-3-p2` — Athanasius argued against the Arians that God's willing is His doing
    text: "God's willing is His doing, says St. Athanasius, Sermon 3 Against the Arians"
- **Related people**: arius, athanasius
- **Related organizations**: council/council-of-nicaea

### Council of Palestine
- **Slug**: `council/council-of-palestine`
- **Also known as**: —
- **Description**: Council held under Pope Victor around A.D. 198. Defined that the world was created in spring at the vernal equinox, and that the first day of the world was March 25.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#verse-12-p12` — The Council of Palestine defined that the world was created at the vernal equinox and that the first day was March 25
    text: " the Council of Palestine, held under Pope Victor in the year of Christ 198, defined this very thing. This Council proves its opinion from the word "let it germinate": for in spring the earth begins to germinate. It also teaches that the world was created at the vernal equinox"
  - `#verse-14-p11` — The Council of Palestine defined that the moon was produced at the full, opposite the sun
    text: "as the Council of Palestine defined above"
- **Related people**: pope-victor

### The Seventy Translators
- **Slug**: `institution/septuagint-translators`
- **Also known as**: Septuagint translators, LXX, the Seventy
- **Description**: The translators of the Hebrew Bible into Greek. Their translation is frequently cited in Genesis 1 as an alternative rendering alongside Aquila, Symmachus, and Theodotion.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#verse-2-p3` — The Seventy translate "tohu vevohu" as "invisible and unordered"
    text: "Hence the Seventy [LXX] here translate, the earth was invisible and unordered"
  - `#verse-4-p5` — The Septuagint reads "He divided between the light and the darkness"
    text: "The Hebrew and the Septuagint have: He divided between the light and the darkness."
  - `#verse-6-p15` — The Septuagint includes "and God saw that it was good" on the second day, unlike the Hebrew and Vulgate
    text: "The Septuagint here, as on the other days, does have "and God saw that it was good;" yet in the Hebrew, Chaldean, Theodotion, Aquila, Symmachus, and the Vulgate, this is lacking."

### Manichaeans
- **Slug**: `institution/manichaeans`
- **Also known as**: Manichees
- **Description**: Heretical sect that posited two gods or principles. Referenced in Genesis 1 as erring about dual principles of creation, and their error about evil in creation is refuted by the repeated phrase "God saw that it was good."
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#god-elohim-p3` — The Manichaeans err in saying there are two principles or two gods, one good and one evil
    text: "Pythagoras, the Manichaeans, and the Priscillianists err, who say that there are two principles of things, or two gods: one good, the creator of spirits; the second evil, the creator of bodies."
  - `#verse-4-p2` — The phrase "God saw that it was good" is directed against the Manichaeans, showing nothing evil was produced by God
    text: "against the Manichaeans we may know that nothing evil, but all things good, were produced by God"
- **Related organizations**: institution/priscillianists
- **Related people**: pythagoras

### Stoic School
- **Slug**: `institution/stoic-school`
- **Also known as**: Stoics
- **Description**: Philosophical school. Referenced in Genesis 1 for the error of holding that the world was created by God from eternal unbegotten matter, and also as among those affirming that the world was made by God from the sight of its order and beauty.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#he-created-p12` — The Stoics erred in saying the world was created from eternal unbegotten matter
    text: "the error of Plato and the Stoics, who said that the world was indeed created by God, but from eternal and unbegotten matter"
  - `#he-created-p13` — The Stoics affirm that the sight of the world's beauty proves it was made by God
    text: "all the schools of philosophers that held anything more divine affirm by unanimous consent that nothing so proves both that the world was made by God and that it is administered by His care, as the very sight of the whole world and the consideration of its beauty and order. So Plato, the Stoics, Cicero, Plutarch, and Aristotle"
- **Related people**: plato, cicero, plutarch, aristotle

### Anthropomorphites
- **Slug**: `institution/anthropomorphites`
- **Also known as**: Audaeans
- **Description**: Heretical sect whose originator was Audaeus, holding that man is the image of God according to the body and therefore that God is corporeal.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#verse-26-p10` — The Anthropomorphites held that man is the image of God according to the body
    text: "The Anthropomorphites, whose originator was Audaeus (hence they are called Audaeans), thought that man is the image of God according to the body, and therefore that God is corporeal; but this is heresy."
- **Related people**: audaeus
