### 198
- **Slug**: `ad/2nd-century/90s/198`
- **Description**: The Council of Palestine, held under Pope Victor, defined that the world was created at the vernal equinox and that the first day of the world was March 25. The council also held that Christ was incarnated on March 25 and that after 34 years He either suffered or rose from death on that date.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#verse-12-p12` — Council of Palestine on the vernal equinox and the creation of the world
    text: "Third, because the Council of Palestine, held under Pope Victor in the year of Christ 198, defined this very thing. This Council proves its opinion from the word "let it germinate": for in spring the earth begins to germinate. It also teaches that the world was created at the vernal equinox, proved from the fact that God then divided light from darkness into equal parts, which occurs at the equinox. It adds that the first day of the world was March 25, on which also the Blessed Virgin received the Annunciation, and Christ was incarnated in her, and on which after 34 years He either suffered or rose from death. It is certain that this day was a Sunday."
- **Related people**: Pope Victor
- **Related places**: Palestine

### ~600
- **Slug**: `ad/6th-century/00s/600`
- **Description**: Anastasius of Sinai, a monk and later Bishop of Antioch and Martyr, wrote eleven books of Hexameron on Genesis around this year, in which he allegorically expounds the first chapters of Genesis concerning Christ and the Church.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#introduction-p7` — Anastasius of Sinai's Hexameron
    text: "Furthermore, Anastasius of Sinai, a monk and later Bishop of Antioch and Martyr, in the year of our Lord 600, wrote eleven books of Hexameron on Genesis, in which he allegorically expounds the first chapters of Genesis concerning Christ and the Church."
- **Related people**: Anastasius of Sinai

### ~1400
- **Slug**: `ad/15th-century/00s/1400`
- **Description**: Thomas the English Doctor (Doctor of York) wrote commentaries on Genesis around this year. Lapide clarifies that these works are by the English Doctor of York, not by St. Thomas Aquinas (the Angelic Doctor), as attested by St. Antoninus and Sixtus of Siena.
- **Occurrences**:
  - `#introduction-p8` — Thomas the English Doctor wrote on Genesis
    text: "Thomas the Doctor also wrote -- not the holy Angelic Doctor, but the English one, namely the Doctor of York, around the year of Christ 1400."
- **Related people**: Thomas (Doctor of York), St. Thomas Aquinas, St. Antoninus, Sixtus of Siena
- **Related places**: York
