LXXXII. Angels Exist and Are Not Eternal

LXXXII. It seems to me very appropriate, and I hope it will seem so to the reader as well, if I add some matters in this place that pertain to the creation of the Angels. That there exist certain intelligent substances, distinct both from human beings and human souls and from all other sensible things — whom Scripture and the Church call holy Angels or wicked spirits — and that such substances are not eternal: this the sacred Scriptures, the Fathers and Doctors of the Church presuppose, teach, and insist upon. Let us hear St. Thomas: «It is said in Proverbs 8, in the person of Wisdom begotten (from eternity): 'The Lord possessed me at the beginning of His ways, before He made anything from the beginning'; but the Angels were made by God: therefore the Angels at some point did not exist. God alone — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — is from eternity. For the Catholic faith holds this without doubt, and every contrary position must be rejected as heretical. For God produced creatures by making them from nothing, that is, after they had been nothing.»

The Apostle Paul, Colossians 1:16, teaches that «in Him (the Son of God) all things were created in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether Thrones or Dominions or Principalities or Powers. All things were created through Him and in Him, and He is before all things, and in Him all things hold together.» In the canticle of the three youths, the Angels are listed first among the creatures of God: «Bless the Lord, all ye works of the Lord... Bless the Lord, ye Angels of the Lord.» But if the Angels were created, they are no longer eternal; for creation from eternity is contradictory, as we have already demonstrated.

That the Angels were created by God, not only the Christian faith but also the opinion of philosophers acknowledges, especially the Platonists, whose master Plato reports that the lesser gods were established by the supreme God, and that the rest was subsequently made and perfected by them.

(2) Similar are the words of the same Wisdom, Ecclesiasticus 24: «I came forth from the mouth of the Most High, the firstborn before every creature.»

It has been debated whether the Angels were created together with the corporeal world. St. Basil wrote: «For there was, as it appears, something even before this universe, which we can indeed contemplate with our mind, yet it has not been set down in any writings, since it would in no way have been fitting for those who were still uneducated and infantile in knowledge. There was indeed, prior to the creation of the world, a state more ancient, suited to those powers superior to the world, prior in time, everlasting, perpetual; and in it the Creator and Maker of all things produced creatures... namely rational and invisible natures, and the entire order of intelligible beings, whatever surpasses our mind, whose very names cannot even be discovered. For these fill up the substance of the invisible world, as Paul teaches us in these words: because in Him all things were created, whether visible or invisible, whether Thrones or Dominions or Principalities or Powers or Virtues or hosts of Angels or Archangels.»

This state, prior in time and eternal, suited to substances superior to the world, is not easily understood; it seems to be interpreted as an age, or indeed as eternity. Vasquez thinks that by those words a certain imaginary interval, prior to all time, is signified. Suarez believed that eternal state should be understood not of eternity a parte ante, but a parte post. To Petavius it seems rather that the divinity itself, or the Word of God, is indicated, by whom and in whom, as in a place and space, the celestial spirits were contained before this world and this time existed. What is certain is that Basil believed nothing is truly everlasting except God, and he agreed with Paul, in the very passage he cites, that the Son of God «was before all Thrones, Dominions, and Principalities.» Therefore, when he says that the Angels were made in God's eternity and before time (namely before this time of ours, subject to changes, which we measure by the motion of bodies), he does not teach that they were created from eternity.

Ambrose, in his Hexaemeron, where he usually follows Basil and sometimes translates him word for word, says: «The Angels, Dominions, and Powers, although they began at some point, were nevertheless already in existence when this world was made.»

Jerome, on chapter 1 of the Epistle to Titus, writes: «Six thousand years of our world have not yet been completed; and how many previous eternities, how many ages, how many origins of centuries must we suppose there were, in which the Angels, Thrones, Dominions, and all the other Virtues served God, and at God's command stood without the vicissitudes and measures of time?» These things are said on the occasion of Paul's words, verse 2: «In hope of eternal life, which God, who does not lie, promised before the ages of time.» Jerome and Augustine read 'before eternal times,' and so Jerome employs that expression as if he were speaking of eternities in this passage. But these eternal times were not coeternal with God, as Augustine says, who elsewhere writes that Paul calls those times 'eternal' which have no time before them. The Apostle's words, here and in Romans 16:25, seem to signify merely all past times. Moreover, Jerome knew very well that the Scriptures teach there is one eternal Creator, and he did not attribute eternity to the Angels; in the same passage he teaches that only «immortality was given to the Angels by the bounty of God.»

LXXXIII. When Were the Angels Created?

LXXXIII. When, then, were the Angels created? Together with the corporeal world? Or rather before this visible world, as we have seen certain Fathers of the Church teach? On this point, St. Thomas says, a twofold opinion of the Saints is found, and this is a very ancient and famous question. The most common opinion of theologians, however, holds that the Angels were created simultaneously with corporeal creation. Regarding this controversy I say first that neither side can be demonstrated by natural reason; for it depends not on the nature of things — I use Vasquez's words — but on the will of God alone, at what point of time the Angels were produced. But we can investigate the will of God by no reasoning except from what we see has been done. Since, therefore, it is not established from the things we see done whether the Angels were produced simultaneously in duration with corporeal creation, or before it, we can understand nothing about God's will concerning the point of their production from the things themselves.

But neither from the tradition of the Fathers, if we set aside the authority of the Church, can we derive a sufficiently firm argument for settling this question. A certain Greek writer, in a manuscript codex cited by Petavius, says: «Almost all the teachers of the Church throughout the world teach that every intelligible and angelic nature came into existence from nothing before this world.» But this consensus of the Doctors is false — unless it is understood to mean that the Angels existed before the completed and adorned world, that is, before the creation of animals. That the Angels were created before all bodies is taught, along with Basil, by Origen, who nevertheless admits that it was not made sufficiently clear when the Angels were created; then by Gregory of Nazianzus, Zacharias of Mytilene, Damascene, Severian of Gabala, and other Greeks, especially John Philoponus, who took up the patronage and defense of that opinion. Moses the Syrian Bishop hands down the same teaching, although he asserts that his countrymen adhered to the contrary opinion. Among the Latins, besides the already cited Ambrose and Jerome, Hilary of Poitiers, Cassian, and others (see Petavius, cited passage) seem to support this opinion.

LXXXIV. The Opposing View: Angels Created With the Corporeal World

LXXXIV. On the other side, Epiphanius says: «The divine word clearly declares that the Angels were neither produced after the stars nor established before heaven and earth. For that is a certain and immutable statement: before heaven and earth, nothing whatsoever of created things existed.» Theodore of Mopsuestia wrote: «It is no less foolish when some, even of those who are considered pious and profess to believe in the divine Scriptures, think that the Angels and certain invisible powers existed before visible things, since they can bring forth no proof of this from divine Scripture.» Theodoret teaches similar things, yet adds that, provided it is granted that the nature of the Angels was created, it does nothing against faith or piety if someone says they were established before heaven and earth. Basil of Seleucia does not make the Angels prior to the creation of heaven and earth. Procopius of Gaza considers it more probable that the Angels were created together with heaven.

Augustine, City of God, book XI, chapter 19, and elsewhere, thought they were created on the first day and were included under the name of light; yet in chapter 32, he grants everyone the freedom to follow what he wishes, provided it is agreed that the Angels were created by God. Gregory the Martyr seems to agree with this same opinion, though he judges them to have been created before the light. Junilius Africanus asserts they were produced together with heaven and earth. Eucherius understands that the Angels were created under the signification of light, that is, on the first day. So also Isidore of Seville, who therefore, when he says the Angels were made before every creature of the world and absolutely before every creature, must be interpreted generously. The same must be said of Bede, who teaches that the Angels were made at the very beginning of creation together with heaven, earth, air, and water; therefore, when he says they were created before the creation of the world, he must be understood as speaking of an order of preeminence, not of a quantity of time, as he himself immediately adds, and not of the completed and formed world.

Rupert, Ansbert, and the author of the commentaries on the Psalms that are placed among Jerome's works hold the same view. Hugh of St. Victor thinks the Angels were made after heaven, since they were created in heaven itself — which is read in almost the same words in Peter Lombard. Gennadius of Marseilles says: «In the beginning God created heaven and earth and water from nothing, and while darkness still concealed the water and water hid the earth, the Angels were made, and all the heavenly Powers, so that God's goodness would not be idle, but would have beings in which to display His goodness through many previous intervals of time.» Where Suarez understands those 'many intervals' as many ages. Gennadius therefore held that either many ages or at least a long span of time intervened between the creation of heaven and earth, and the light of the first day. From all this it is clear that the question cannot be resolved by the votes of the Fathers.

LXXXV. Scriptural Evidence Examined

LXXXV. But can the matter be definitively settled from the authority of the sacred books? The passages cited from the Scriptures in favor of the first opinion certainly have no force. Concerning the passage from the Epistle to Titus I have just said something. What is cited from Job 38 and 40 is a feeble weapon without impact. Nevertheless, Suarez may be consulted, book 1, On the Angels, chapter 3, sections 20, 21, 22.

But neither do the passages cited for the second opinion produce any conviction, as Vasquez and Petavius demonstrate: those adduced from Luke chapter 10, verse 18 — «I saw Satan falling like lightning from heaven» — and from Apocalypse 12, where Michael's battle with the devil in heaven is described. The same authors show that neither does that passage from Ecclesiasticus chapter 18 — «He who lives forever created all things at once» — supply a sufficiently firm argument. I have explained this passage elsewhere (section 26).

But from that passage — «In the beginning God created heaven and earth» — the matter seems to be sufficiently proved. The sacred and most truthful Scriptures, says Augustine, say this: «In the beginning God made heaven and earth,» so that it is understood He made nothing beforehand, because it would rather have been said that He made this 'in the beginning,' if He had made anything before all the other things He made. Therefore God did not create the Angels before the creation of heaven and earth; for otherwise He would not have created heaven and earth in the beginning of His works and before all His other works. Suarez says this argument is highly probable.

Furthermore, is it certain that the creation of the Angels was entirely passed over by Moses? Julian the Apostate indeed objected this to Moses, that he had spoken only «of the heavens and of earthly bodies, so that God, according to him, was the creator of nothing incorporeal.» But some ancients thought that Moses mentioned the creation of the angels either under the name of heaven, as Origen, Bede, and Strabo held; or of light, which did not displease Augustine. I will readily grant that Moses does not speak explicitly and in a particular place about the production of the angels: perhaps because, as Cyril says, those things which are remote from the senses are understood by us with difficulty; nor did he wish to commit to writing things which could not be grasped by readers; a consideration which applies especially to the Hebrews, as Chrysostom observes. But it must certainly be judged that he spoke of these things, at least implicitly: for, being about to make frequent mention of the angels, he did not want them to be thought uncreated.

I do not indeed think that he intended us to understand angels by the name of the heavens, but rather that he comprehended under the name of heaven all those things which were called celestial, among which the angels hold first place. This is clear from Psalm 148, in which, as Suarez observes, what Moses had said implicitly seems to be expounded and elucidated in a certain way. For first the celestial beings are invited generally to the praise of God: «Praise the Lord from the heavens, praise Him in the heights;» then it is declared what is meant by the name of celestial beings: «Praise Him all His angels, praise Him all His powers. Praise Him sun and moon... for He spoke and they were made, He commanded and they were created.»

LXXXVI. The Fourth Lateran Council Decree

LXXXVI. Most especially, however, the fourth decree of the Lateran Council favors this doctrine and the interpretation of the words of Moses that has been set forth, where it says: «God, the Creator of all things visible and invisible, spiritual and corporeal, who by His omnipotent power simultaneously from the beginning of time fashioned from nothing both creatures, spiritual and corporeal, angelic namely and mundane, and then the human, as it were a common creature constituted of spirit and body.» Although the intention of the Lateran Fathers was not to settle this question, but to condemn other errors, as I have indicated elsewhere with St. Thomas (section 24); nevertheless it must be admitted with Suarez that the Council in that formula also professes the aforesaid doctrine, and interprets the first words of Genesis in the manner just explained.

Some say that the word 'simultaneously' should be understood as 'equally' or 'alike,' and does not refer to time. But, as the same Suarez observes, that word 'simultaneously' corresponds to the subsequent 'and then,' and is opposed to it. Now the adverb 'then' is certainly a particle of time; therefore the other particle 'simultaneously' also indicates in this place not only identity of Creator, but also identity of time. Furthermore, the angels were created from the beginning of time, and likewise from the beginning of time the corporeal or mundane creature was fashioned from nothing; therefore no great interval of time intervened between the two creations.

However the opening of Genesis may be understood, nevertheless those who proposed that formula certainly had the first words of Moses before their eyes: for the formula itself seems to be a kind of paraphrase of those words. See how aptly each corresponds to the other: «In the beginning: from the beginning of time — created: by His omnipotent power He simultaneously fashioned from nothing — God: God the creator of all things visible and invisible, spiritual and corporeal — heaven and earth: both creatures, spiritual and corporeal, angelic namely and mundane.»

By the force of this canon, Francis De Sylvestris of Ferrara taught that this doctrine now pertains to the faith, because it has been defined by the Church. But this is to claim too much, for, as Suarez holds, 'The Council did not say this with the express purpose of defining it, but incidentally and as if doing something else. For the Council did not intend in that chapter to define new dogmas, but to profess ancient ones, and so St. Thomas explained it in Opusculum XXIII, where he expounded that decretal, and so also it has been received by the common understanding of the Church.'

Indeed, for the Angelic Doctor in the Summa, this opinion is the more probable, and he even seems to judge the contrary improbable. Yet he adds: 'Although the contrary is not to be regarded as erroneous, especially on account of the opinion of Gregory of Nazianzus, whose authority in Christian doctrine is so great that no one has ever presumed to bring calumny against his words, just as neither against the teachings of Athanasius, as Jerome says.' Hence some, such as Vasquez and Petavius, deny that it is permissible to affix any censure to that opinion: indeed Petavius, although in a certain passage incidentally he seems to favor the common opinion of theologians, nevertheless when he offers and dedicates the third volume of his Theological Dogmas to God, the creator of angels before all things, he seems to adhere to the contrary doctrine.

Others hold that this doctrine cannot be defended after the Lateran Council without incurring some note of temerity. Suarez says, 'from the words of the Council, no small authority has accrued to this opinion, and therefore it is not without temerity to now hold the contrary. So judge Valentia, vol. I, Question XII, point 2, and Banez at the said article 3... nor is any injury done thereby to the more ancient Fathers, because in those times the matter was more controverted and less clarified. And a proposition can at one time not be temerarious, and later become so, by the intervention of the Church's authority and by common agreement and consensus.' Antonius Cassinius does not dare to say that the opinion is certainly temerarious, but he calls it probably temerarious. 'A temerarious proposition,' he says, 'is generally one which is put forward without the rule of reason; which applies also to that which is called temerarious only negatively. But a positively temerarious proposition, of which we are speaking, is one which in a theological matter opposes the common sense of the learned, without any weighty and previously undetected or unconsidered argument of either reason or authority. Now all sacred interpreters and doctors after the Lateran Council, having weighed everything on both sides, agree that the angels were created within the six days of the divine work; even those who utterly deny that this was defined in that Council,' etc.

LXXXVII. The Long Temporal Interval

LXXXVII. I shall add only one observation which, if I am not mistaken, is of some importance, and which the ancient theologians could not have added. We have already shown at length that it is demanded by philosophy and permitted by theology that we interpose a long interval of time between the creation of the corporeal world and the production of man. Therefore in like manner it will be permissible to place a long interval of time between the production of man and the creation of the angels. Now this opinion does not differ very much from that of the ancients of which we were speaking. In this way it is not indeed entirely reconciled, I confess, with the common doctrine of the theologians; but nevertheless it coheres for the most part. And from this you may observe how the human sciences, though most remote from one another, furnish mutual aids and arguments to each other. Who would ever have thought that the progress of the natural sciences would bring us those things from which this theological controversy about spirits, however imperfectly, could be resolved?

What those ancients maintained — that angels existed for some, and indeed even for many ages before that epoch which is commonly called the beginning of the world, that is, before man was created — this we too can concede, and we certainly do not contradict the Lateran decree, provided we confess that God simultaneously from the beginning of time created both the angels and bodies from nothing. Those ancients could now, with a few words changed, set forth their opinions, because they would oppose neither the Lateran synod nor the doctrine of the more recent theologians.

For example, Jerome could say: «Not yet are six thousand years of our human race completed, and how many prior ages, how many origins of centuries must we judge to have preceded, in which the angels, thrones, dominations, and the other powers served God and at His command stood firm.» Ambrose could affirm: «For a long time before the very creation of man, the Cherubim and Seraphim with the sweetness of their melodious voice say: Holy, holy, holy.» Nor would anyone rightly reproach Genebrard if he were to write that the opinion seemed truer to him «which teaches that the angels preceded the origin» of the human race «by I know not how many ages.»

It would suffice to have substituted for 'the origin of corporeal things, the origin or creation of man' (which epoch is commonly confused with the epoch of the creation of the world) or even the origin of the animal kingdom. It is pleasing finally to observe that among those who held that the angels were created long before the corporeal world, there were some who attributed bodies to the angels — not indeed gross and heavy ones, such as ours are, but the most tenuous, ethereal or fiery. Among these are reckoned Ambrose and Cassian, with whom Basil agrees in his book On the Holy Spirit, who is cited among the supporters of this opinion by John of Thessalonica. Basil moreover supposed that before this world subject to change and corruption, there existed a certain superior light, more splendid than ours, in which the angels dwelt: and that as soon as the heaven was created, that light remained in the supracelestial region, and when the celestial light was shut off from the lower parts by the interposition of the body of the heaven, darkness began.

From this it is shown that Basil held the opinion that long before that epoch which is called the creation of the world, a certain true light had been created, which, like our own, could be intercepted by the interposition of an opaque body. Severianus of Gabala says that the light made on the first day is fire, and adds: «But the celestial Powers also are fire; and that superior fire is akin to this lower fire of ours.» From all of which it follows that certain Fathers posited not only true spirits or intelligences, but also something corporeal, prior to the formation of this visible world.

It must be noted that certain Fathers of the Church indeed posited that angels had bodies, but did not for that reason deny them an entirely incorporeal mind. We too are not incorporeal: yet we are not for that reason entirely corporeal, and lacking a truly spiritual and incorporeal soul. Cassian teaches that angels have a body according to themselves, but he does not call those minds which have bodies corporeal. Substances that are only corporeal never are said to have a body.

Augustine does not shrink from the corporeality of the angels in several places. In Book II of On the Trinity, chapter VII, and Book III, chapter I, he has this: «Their own body, to which they are not subject but which they rule as subject to them, changing and turning it into forms suited and adapted to their actions, according to the power bestowed on them by the Creator.» Consider then: a thing that thinks and wills, that changes a body as it pleases, is certainly not matter, according to Augustine, who establishes and proves the complete spirituality of the thinking principle in several books, and especially in the book On the Greatness of the Soul. In that hypothesis of Augustine, angels are spiritual substances which are served by the most subtle material organs.

Who would think that Basil, who so openly asserts the spirituality of our soul that he believes we can from it come to know God as incorporeal — who would think he denied a truly spiritual mind to the superior rational powers, which surpass our own mind? I could add more from Basil himself, from Ambrose, and from others; but in a matter not pertaining to the history of the Mosaic creation, it is not necessary to dwell further.

LXXXVIII. Corollarium

LXXXVIII. That the angels were produced long before the creation of heaven and earth which is described at the beginning of Genesis — this it was permissible for St. Gregory of Nazianzus, St. Jerome, Hilary, John of Damascus, and others to assert, without however sinning against the faith or piety in any way — which Theodoret acknowledges, although he was devoted to the contrary opinion; it was permissible for them without incurring any note of heresy, and since they wrote before the Fourth Lateran Council, without incurring a note of temerity either. Gregory retained the title of 'Theologian,' and the supreme authority which he enjoys in the Church; Jerome was called the greatest Doctor in interpreting the Scriptures.

The same was permissible for Ambrose, «who thought nothing free and exempt from material composition except the sole substance of the adorable Trinity.» It was permissible for Abbot Serenus — or more truly for Cassian, who introduces him as speaking — yet Cassian «by no means considered the angels, archangels, and the other powers to be incorporeal,» but held them «to have according to themselves a body by which they subsist, though much more tenuous than ours.»

It was permissible for Basil the Great, who attributed to the angels «an airy or fiery substance,» and not only held them to be more ancient than the creation of heaven and earth, but also asserted that a certain light existed before the created world — more splendid indeed than this lower light of ours, but nevertheless akin to it.

This was permissible for them, though no compelling natural reason urged it. Much more, therefore, will it now be permissible for us to posit a long interval of time — not indeed before the creation of heaven and earth made by God in the beginning, but between this and the creation of man — since strong natural reasons, supported by the most numerous and most certain observations, persuade us that this is true and established.
