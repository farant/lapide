I. The Name of the Pentateuch

That which holds first place in all the codices of the sacred library of the Hebrew nation that exist today, and in its ancient versions -- the collection of five books -- is called by the Hebrews, "five fifths of the Law," or more concisely, "five fifths," from their number. Each individual book is called "a fifth part." The Greek term Pentateuchos, "a fivefold volume," corresponds exactly to that Hebrew appellation. Moreover, the entire Pentateuch is commonly called by the Hebrews Torah, "the Law," because it contains the religious and civil constitutions established by Moses at divine command.

For the word is compounded from pente, five, and teuchos, which is known to denote both a vessel and a volume or book. Wherefore those err who suppose that Pentateuchos is said by ellipsis for "he pentateuchos biblos." On this matter Stange dissertated more copiously than necessary in his short Commentary entitled, Of what kind is the Pentateuch?, inserted in the Analekten fur das Studium der exeg. und Systemat. Theologie, vol. I, p. 22 ff., where he prescribes that one should write not Pentateuchus Mosaicus but Pentateuchum Mosaicum, since the Greek teuchos, as everyone knows, is of neuter gender. We indeed follow the received usage of speech, which has prevailed through many centuries, according to which Pentateuchus is treated as a Latin noun of masculine gender.

If you except the Nazarenes (whether they should be counted among Jews or Christians is doubtful), whom John Damascenus in his book On Heresies, section 19, says held that the Pentateuch was not by Moses and substituted other books for it. After them a certain Ptolemaeus, a Gnostic writer, in his epistle to Flora, which Epiphanius preserved in Heresy 33, maintained that the entire Law was distributed threefold: some things have God as their author; other things are owed to the plan and design of Moses himself; still other things must be referred to the elders of the people. Jerome in his book Against Helvidius says: 'Whether you wish to call Moses the author of the Pentateuch, or Ezra the restorer of the same work, I do not object.' Similarly Tertullian in his book On the Apparel of Women, ch. III: 'The entire instrument of Jewish literature is known to have been restored by Ezra.' This opinion, approved by many Greek and Latin Fathers, rests on the traditions of the Hebrews concerning the sacred books reviewed by Ezra and the Synagogue, and arranged in the order in which they now appear; see Buxtorf's Tiberias, book I, ch. X.

II. That the Pentateuch Was Written by Moses Is an Ancient and Common Opinion. On What Arguments It Rests.

All Hebrew and Christian antiquity attributes the Pentateuch to Moses as its author, and this not from some vain opinion or uncertain report, but from reasons drawn either from the work itself or supported by other testimonies.

And as for the arguments drawn from the Pentateuch itself, which prove Moses to be its author: at the end of the work, Deut. XXXI, 9-13, 22, 24-26, Moses testifies in express words that this entire book was written by him from beginning to end, and was handed over to the leaders of the people and the priests, to be kept in the inner sanctuary of the sacred tabernacle beside the Ark of the Covenant, and to be read aloud to all the people every seventh year, on the days of the Feast of Tabernacles. And throughout the work itself, Moses states that individual laws and narratives of events were recorded by him in this book. Thus at Exod. XVII, 14, Moses says that at divine command he inscribed in a book the ambush which the Amalekites laid for the Hebrews when they were weary and weak from their journey, the battle that followed, and the decree of future destruction upon them -- a book undoubtedly already begun, in which he had already written many things, as is inferred from the fact that he wrote "in the book" with the article, by which he indicates that he is speaking of a certain and well-known book. After the Israelites had made a covenant with Jehovah at Mount Sinai and had received the laws from Moses, Moses wrote all the words of Jehovah in a book of the covenant, which he read aloud to the whole people, Exod. XXIV, 4, 7. When later many other precepts had been added to these, Moses was again commanded to commit them to writing, Exod. XXXIV, 27. Furthermore, at Num. XXXIII, 1, 2, Moses is said to have recorded the departures and journeys of the Israelites, as they are read there, at Jehovah's command. The book of Numbers closes, XXXVI, 13, with these words: These are the precepts and laws which Jehovah gave to the Israelites through Moses in the plains of Moab by the Jordan, opposite Jericho. Finally in Deuteronomy, which contains the recapitulation, explanations, and supplements of the laws handed down in the three preceding books, Moses in his exhortations to the people again and again mentions this law, XVII, 18, 19, XXXI, 9-11, and the book of this law, XXVIII, 61, XXIX, 19, 20, 26. Especially notable is the passage XXVIII, 61, where in the speech in which Moses threatens the Israelites with all manner of calamities if they do not obey the divine precepts, among other things these words are read: Every disease also, and every plague, which is not written in the book of this law, may Jehovah send upon you. From which it follows that Moses handed over this book, in which those threats had been written, to the people, and therefore committed his own words to writing.

* The adversaries reply that these passages, far from proving the authenticity of the Pentateuch, on the contrary overthrow it. They reason thus: If Moses himself had written the Pentateuch as it now exists, the frequent mention of this task imposed on him by God would be useless; indeed, it is remarkable that such a note is added to this or that law or narrative only at intervals, not everywhere and to every individual law or narrative; finally, it seems awkward for a writer, after completing a narrative, to append this: And he himself (the author) wrote it. These formulae of speech cannot be rightly explained unless it be granted that the author of the Pentateuch presupposes the existence of Mosaic documents, which he calls the book of the law or of the covenant, and from which he intends to distinguish the history written by himself.

Herbst responds: Moses, in his last addresses to the people, in order to indicate briefly and in one expression what the Hebrews should attend to, frequently mentions the book of the law, that is, a specific and well-known one, containing everything they must observe and do, so that they may be and remain the people of the divine covenant. By these words he intimates that his book of the law is to be regarded as the book of the covenant; indeed he expressly, though indirectly, signifies this when he adds that the curses of the covenant are contained in that book, Deut. XXIX, 20. Since therefore the hypothetical writer of the Pentateuch allows this book to be designated by Moses himself as the book of the covenant, it is clear that he does not use such a designation for the purpose of making a distinction between his own history (the present-day Pentateuch) and truly Mosaic documents. For it is not he, but Moses who so calls his book, and this not to separate it from other writings, but to mark it with a proper and fitting name. Furthermore, if anyone thinks it remarkable that God's command to commit this or that to writing is mentioned by Moses only at intervals, would he not rightly be even more surprised if such a command had been added everywhere and to every individual narrative or law? Finally, as for the writer appending the clause after completing a narrative, He himself (the author) wrote it -- this is so far from being something inept and unusual that other writers of no small repute have done the same, e.g. Abdollatif, at the end of his Memorabilia of Egypt.

Therefore without good reason the adversaries assert that the passages cited above cannot be understood unless it is assumed that they indicate a distinction between the Pentateuch and other truly Mosaic documents. Now it must be examined whether that book of the covenant mentioned at Deut. XXIX, 20, XXX, 10, which Moses has in mind and is about to hand over to the priests as the code of the theocratic constitution of the Israelites, is the entire Pentateuch, or Deuteronomy only, or a collection of precepts and laws scattered throughout the Pentateuch as it now exists, or finally a completely lost book from which the author (on this hypothesis) of our Pentateuch had drawn many fragments and chapters. This last cannot be admitted. For who would believe that such a book, so earnestly commended by Moses himself, so necessary, indeed sacred and venerable to the Israelite nation, ever perished while the republic stood? The first therefore must be admitted -- namely, that the expression 'book of the covenant' designates the Pentateuch.

To these passages found in the Pentateuch itself, in which Moses expressly declares himself its writer, are added many passages from the remaining books of the Old Testament, in which this same work is mentioned under the name of the Law of Moses, the Law which Jehovah gave through Moses. In the book of Joshua, which although it is much more recent than the age of this leader, was nevertheless for the most part undoubtedly composed from records, if not exactly contemporary, certainly quite ancient, almost word for word, as is the custom of Oriental writers -- we read in ch. I, vv. 7, 8, that immediately after the death of Moses, Joshua was admonished to read the book of the law of Moses continually; and Joshua in his address to the people, ch. XXIII, vv. 3-16, exhorts the leaders of the Israelites to do all things written in the book of the law of Moses; and he mentions not only the laws, but also the deeds recorded in it, the threats and promises set forth in it. Also at the final assembly which Joshua is said to have held, ch. XXIV, he briefly recapitulates the chief points of the Pentateuch, renews the covenant with Jehovah, and adds it to the book of the law of Jehovah, v. 26, which is undoubtedly the same book that is called the book of the law of Moses at I, 7, 8; XXIII, 6; VIII, 32, and the book of the law at VIII, 34. David on his deathbed exhorts his son Solomon, I Kings II, 3, to keep all the precepts, laws, and statutes, as they are written in the law of Moses. The passage II Kings XIV, 6 admits of no doubt whatsoever, in which Amaziah, king of Judah, is said to have spared the sons of the rebels, as it is written (prescribed) in the book of the law of Moses, with the words that pertain to this matter immediately quoted from it -- which are those very words that are still read today at Deut. XXIV, 16. At II Kings XXII, 8, the book of the law, which a little further on at ch. XXIII, v. 2, is called the book of the covenant, and at II Chron. XXXIV, 14, more precisely the book of the law written through Moses, is said to have been found in the temple by Hilkiah the high priest, from which Shaphan, the royal scribe, read aloud to King Josiah the promises and threats, which can hardly be other than those contained in Levit. XXVI, 3-45, and Deut. XXVII, 11-XXVIII, 68; and from II Kings XXIII, 21-23, it is clear that King Josiah celebrated the feast of Passover with the same solemnities and rites that had been prescribed in the book of the covenant found by Hilkiah. At II Kings XXIII, 25, it is said that none was comparable to King Josiah, king of Judah, who turned to Jehovah with all his mind, all his soul, and all his strength, as the entire law of Moses requires.

That the book of the law found by Hilkiah in the temple was not some briefer code of laws which the Jews had used since the age of David or Solomon, but the entire Pentateuch, Leonhard Bertholdt has demonstrated at length in his Dissertation on that which was most memorable in the purification of Jewish sacred things carried out by King Josiah (Erlangen, 1817, p. 47 ff.). 'The celebration,' he says, 'of the Passover by King Josiah with the greatest possible solemnity, as he had learned from the book found by Hilkiah, suggests that we should believe this book to have been not Deuteronomy, or a smaller book later incorporated into it, but the entire Pentateuch. Fewer are the things which Deuteronomy presents about the Passover, ch. XVI, 1-8, nor do those concern the entire matter; the principal law about celebrating the feast of unleavened bread is found at Exod. XII, 1-20, and Num. XXVIII, 16-25, which King Josiah must have had before his eyes in arranging the solemnities.'

After a portion of the Jews had returned from the lands of Babylon to their homeland, Joshua the priest and his kinsmen the priests, and Zerubbabel and his kinsmen, undertook to build an altar of the God of Israel, to offer burnt offerings upon it, as it is prescribed in the law of Moses, the man of God, Ezra III, 2. At the dedication of the second temple, Ezra VI, 18, the Levites and priests are said to have ministered, distributed into their classes and divisions, according to the prescription of the book of Moses, namely Num. III, 6, VIII, 11, 14. Nehemiah in the prayer which is read in chapter I of the book named after him, confesses that the Israelites had not kept the precepts, statutes, and laws which Jehovah had commanded Moses his servant, and prays to God that He remember what He had promised Moses in these words: When you have transgressed my precepts, and I have scattered you among the nations, nevertheless if you return to me and keep my precepts, even if you have been dispersed to the ends of heaven, I will gather you from there and bring you to the place which I have chosen to establish my name -- which words agree entirely with what we read in Levit. XXVI, 41; Deut. IV, 26, 27, XXVIII, 64, XXX, 1. Although these passages are found in books that were written many centuries after Moses -- partly shortly before the destruction of the kingdom of Judah by the Chaldeans, partly after the restoration of Hebrew affairs under the favor of Cyrus -- they nevertheless prove that in that age, that is, in the sixth century before Christ, there existed books from which the laws, promises, and threats mentioned above are cited, and that by the common judgment of the Hebrew nation these were regarded as having been written by Moses.

Now if we consider that such a judgment is not formed by the opinion of a few individuals, but must rest on what had been received from ancestors and handed down through many generations, those passages cited by us from the remaining books of the Old Testament provide by no means a slight argument for the Mosaic origin of the Pentateuch. Moreover, this argument gains no little strength from the fact that we find that the sacred and civil affairs of the Hebrews were at all times, from the age of Joshua onward, conformed to the standard of the laws handed down in these books. For even if some of those laws were sometimes neglected for some period of time, shortly afterward everything was nevertheless restored to what had been prescribed in the books of the Pentateuch. In the historical books of the Old Testament, things consistent with those books are continually praised, and things contrary to them are censured. The Prophets are entirely engaged in exhorting their countrymen to obey what is prescribed in the Pentateuch, setting forth the same promises of good things and threats of evils that we read in it. Moreover, they commemorate many things done by or happening to their ancestors in exactly the same manner as we read them narrated in the Pentateuch, so that there can scarcely be any doubt that they drew from this source. This therefore -- the so frequent reference by the prophets, indeed by the more ancient ones whose writings survive, Hosea, Joel, Amos, Micah, Isaiah -- to what is prescribed and narrated in the Pentateuch, is taken as an argument that it existed in the times in which they lived, and had as much authority and credibility among them as was deserved by a work which they believed to have come from the founder of their national institutions.

* Another argument, not dissimilar to the one just given, is offered by William Meignan, Les Propheties Messianiques de l'Anc. Test. pp. 44-60. The sum of his argument is: The entire history of the Hebrews attests the authenticity of the Pentateuch; moreover it consists of five propositions, each of which the author develops with great skill and erudition. To support the authenticity of the Pentateuch he adduces, first, the order of events, so intimately connected and linked together by chronology and genealogies, that nothing can be removed from them without the entire series being broken. Second, the perpetual belief among the Jews in the miracles narrated in the Pentateuch. Third, the religious, civil, and political institutions of the Jews considered in every century or state of the republic. Fourth, the dissensions among the various tribes of Israel, arising from the earliest times and never extinguished. Fifth, the conflict between both the other desires of the Israelite people and their zeal for idolatry on the one hand, and the Mosaic law on the other.

Those arguments, however (which we may call external), from which it is inferred that Moses is the author of the Pentateuch, are confirmed by the character, subject matter, and structure of the entire work. For the historical, religious, political, and geographical matters of the most remote antiquity contained in it -- especially those pertaining to the history and natural features of Egypt and Arabia -- are of such a nature that it is credible they were recorded by a man who had been liberally educated and trained in Egypt in ancient times, such as Moses was. Then what we read in the Pentateuch about the departure of the Hebrews from Egypt under Moses' leadership, and their journey through the desert of Arabia, is entirely consistent with the conditions of the places and times in which the events are narrated. The same holds true of the laws and institutions which Moses is said in these books to have handed down to the people he led. In the last of these books we hear the same man who governed his people for many years and promulgated the laws contained in the three preceding books, speaking to the same people; but now advanced in age, as is proved by the broader and more diffuse style which everyone observes in Deuteronomy. We hear in this book a leader near death, recalling to his people all that had happened to them under his governance, gravely exhorting them to diligently keep the laws he had handed down to them by divine authority, and also revising and supplementing certain laws. Deuteronomy indeed presupposes the three preceding books as written, frequently appeals to their subject matter, urges the observance of the laws read in them, and draws from the deeds narrated in them reasons for obeying the laws, so that without those books readers could not even adequately understand Deuteronomy. The order of the discourse is not everywhere convenient, and the discourse itself often proceeds through disconnected fragments without connection, not infrequently ending with a distinctive formula; which things indicate an author who did not write continuously, but was frequently interrupted -- such as we know Moses to have been, overwhelmed by so many and so great affairs, and distracted by continual migrations. Finally, the arrangement of the entire work is such as would be expected from a man who had resolved to give laws and rights to an uncivilized people and to establish a well-ordered and well-constituted society. For in the first book and the earlier part of the second book, having set forth as it were a preface to the laws to be delivered, he places before them those things which were either necessary for the sequence and order of events, or which could serve to form the minds of the Israelites toward the religion and worship of the one God, creator and governor of all things, about which we shall say more in the Prooemium to Genesis. Among the laws themselves he intersperses almost only those deeds which either provided occasion for them, or pertain to commending the observance of the laws; and he presents the laws almost all in the order in which they were promulgated. Since this is so, nothing seems to stand in the way of our believing that these books were written by him to whom both the common opinion of all ages and the remaining monuments of the Old Testament assign them as their author.

(b) 'Palestine,' says Renan, 'has this in common with Greece, Tuscany, and all countries that have seen the birth of original civilizations: that it offers, within the space of a few leagues, the most striking differences of character. Each of the valleys of Greece had its civilization, its myths, its art, its intellectual and moral character. An attentive critic would perhaps find no less marked differences between each of the cantons of Palestine. The late preponderance of the tribe of Judah did not efface these local characters. Ephraim, with its mountain of Gerizim, rival of Zion, its holy city of Bethel, its many memories of the patriarchal age, was, without question, the most considerable of the individualities that struggled against the absorbing action of Jerusalem. The rivalry of these two families in Israel dates from the most remote epochs of their history.'

III. What Opinions Seem to Be Opposed to the Pentateuch Having Been Written by Moses

Yet to that ancient and common opinion of Jews and Christians, that the Pentateuch as we now read it was written by Moses, the more recent age has opposed several things found in the work itself which seem by no means to admit that view. Some are drawn from the character of its language, others from the form and character of the whole book, still others from particular passages in it. As regards the language, they say that the language in which the Pentateuch is written differs much less from that in which the most recent books of the sacred library of the Hebrews were composed than one would rightly expect in books separated from each other by so great a span of time. For we find in those books written in the times of the Babylonian exile the same word-forms, inflections, and meanings as in the books attributed to Moses. But who, they ask, would believe that the Hebrew language alone of all languages possessed such stability that in the course of a thousand years it experienced no change? As for the form and character of the whole work, they say there are several things which show that it is not a work written in one continuous composition by one person, but composed from several documents of various periods and authors. To this point belong first the frequently recurring headings (see Gen. II, 4; V, 1; VI, 9; XXV, 12, 19; XXXVI, 1; Exod. VI, 14; Num. XXXIII, 1) and concluding formulas (e.g. Lev. VII, 37, 38; XXVI, 46; XXVII, 34; Deut. IV, 45) of the individual parts of the work, separated from each other by their subject matter, and the repeated narratives of the same events, now more prolix, now briefer, which nevertheless not infrequently disagree with each other in particulars, and which, by their variety of style and the use of different expressions for the same things, betray various writers. There is added to this the alternating use of the two names by which the Hebrews are accustomed to address the supreme deity, Elohim and Jehovah, the pattern of which is such that in some longer chapters of the same subject God is consistently called Elohim, in others Jehovah, and in those sections in which the name Elohim is employed, certain forms of speech and expressions are distinctive and peculiar which are not found in those sections that tend to use the name Jehovah. Furthermore, against the commonly received opinion that the Pentateuch was written by Moses, they cite several passages in it which betray a later age and cannot have been recorded by him. For cities are mentioned by names that were only given to them after Moses; explanatory notes are added here and there which it is scarcely credible that Moses would have added; and finally, several things are mentioned in a manner that indicates a writer who lived long after Moses.

* It scarcely needs to be pointed out that nothing can be inferred against the authenticity of the Pentateuch from these passages. The first indeed, namely Gen. XII, 6 and XIII, 7, Munk explains thus: 'The text of Genesis reads: The Canaanite was then in the land. The word "az" (then) can be explained at will as "already" or as "still." Already Aben-Ezra has remarked that it is better to adopt the sense of "already," and to suppose that the Canaanites had taken Palestine from other, more ancient inhabitants.' On the second passage, namely Gen. XXXVI, 31, see our annotations on that passage. On the third, finally, Clericus not badly opposes those who would contend that this passage could not have been written by Moses with these words: 'It must be recalled that Moses wrote not only for his own age, to whom his father and grandfather were well known, but also for following centuries, which he as it were addresses in this passage, not contemporaries who knew these things well enough.'

IV. The Various Opinions About the Origin of the Pentateuch Held by Those Who Deny It to Moses

Those who, led by the arguments set forth above, deny that the Pentateuch was written by Moses, diverge into various opinions about its origin, which we shall briefly review according to the plan of our work.

Thomas Hobbes, in his Leviathan (London 1651) Part III, ch. XXXIII, considers the Pentateuch to be named after Moses not because it was written by him, but because it deals with him; although he concedes that it is possible that Moses wrote those things which these books narrate as having been written by him, such as the code of laws which, as it seems to him, is contained from chapter XI of Deuteronomy to the end of chapter XXVII.

The opinion of Isaac Peyrere was that Moses had written daily records of all the memorable things that had been done by himself and others in his time, to which he prefixed the chronicles of the Jews from Adam down to his own times; but that those books had long since perished, and that now only certain excerpts from them survive. He thinks that from these Mosaic records was compiled the Book of the Wars of the Lord, mentioned in Num. XXI, 14, from which in turn the book of Numbers was excerpted.

Benedict Spinoza, in his Tractatus Theologico-politicus (Amsterdam 1670), ch. VIII, IX, p. 103, maintains that all the historical books of the Old Testament which narrate events before the age of Ezra have one author, whom he suspects to have been Ezra. He conjectures that Deuteronomy was the first of the books composed by Ezra, partly because it contains the ancestral laws which the people most needed, partly because that book is not connected to any preceding one by any conjunction, but begins with prose, "these are the words of Moses." Next, he believes that Ezra applied himself to describing the complete history of the Hebrew nation, from the creation of the world to the destruction of the city by the Chaldeans, into which he inserted the book of Deuteronomy in its proper place. Spinoza adds that perhaps Ezra called the first five of the historical books composed by him by the name of Moses, because in them the deeds performed by Moses were chiefly recorded. Yet he supposes that Ezra did not put the finishing touch on this work, and that he did nothing else than collect histories from various writers, and sometimes merely transcribe them, so that he left them to posterity neither examined nor arranged. Finally, he maintains that these records of Ezra, such as they were, have come down to us only in a mutilated, truncated state, disfigured with many errors, so that of ancient history we have nothing complete which does not need much correction.

That Spinoza said Aben-Ezra agreed with him, he did not do with sufficient honesty; for there is no doubt that Aben-Ezra only means that the common and widespread opinion among the Jews, which holds that all words from the first to the end of these five books refer to Moses as author, is without reason. Here are Aben-Ezra's words on Deut. I, 2: 'If you attend to the mystery -- in the twelve (that is, the last twelve verses of Deuteronomy, which describe the death of Moses), then these passages: Deut. XXXI, 9; Gen. XII, 6; XXII, 14; and Deut. III, 10, 11, you will know the truth,' namely, that not all and every part of the Pentateuch can have been written by Moses, but that some things were added after him by others for the purpose of explanation.

Richard Simon, in his Critical History of the Old Testament written in French (which first appeared in Paris, 1678, in Latin 1681, then augmented at Rotterdam 1685), book I, ch. II, considers that the difficult knots which entangle the interpreter of the Pentateuch -- both the frequent repetitions of the same events in it, not infrequently conflicting with each other in particulars, and the passages which indicate an age much later than Moses -- can most easily be resolved if we assume that the Pentateuch was composed from the various records and loose, unorganized memoirs of public scribes whom Moses had appointed after the Egyptian custom.

While calling Simon's opinion into review, Jean Le Clerc agreed with him indeed in denying the authorship of the Pentateuch to Moses; but as regards its origin, he proposed this conjecture: that it was composed at a time when the old Hebrew letters, in which it was first written, were better known than the new ones which they had adopted during the Babylonian exile, and by an author who was suspect neither to the Samaritans nor to the Jews, and from whose hand they would not unwillingly receive it. All these conditions, he argued, fit that Israelite priest sent from Babylon to teach the new inhabitants of Palestine the manner in which the God of that region desired to be worshipped, II Kings XVII, 27, 28.

Clericus himself later abandoned this opinion, as appears from Dissertation II De Scriptore Pentateuchi, prefixed to his Commentary on the Pentateuch.

Johann Gottfried Hasse, in his book Aussichten zu kunftigen Aufklarungen uber das alte Testament, in Briefen (Jena, 1785), conjectured that the Pentateuch was composed during the time of the Babylonian exile from ancient documents, with Mosaic materials that then survived being used but much augmented and variously altered. However, he later changed his opinion and vindicated the Pentateuch for Moses.

Friedrich Karl Fulda maintains that the Pentateuch as we now read it could not have been composed before the times of David, and that while few things in it are truly Mosaic, many others -- some more ancient than Moses, some more recent -- are so interwoven, and the old materials so altered and interpolated, that what is truly Mosaic can now scarcely be distinguished. He does not doubt that from Moses's own hand come the Decalogue, or the book of the covenant, the victory song after crossing the Red Sea (Exod. XV), and the list of encampments of the Israelites from the departure from Egypt to the plains of Moab, Num. XXXIII. Other materials recorded by Moses he thinks were interwoven into more recent writings, but that no more than ten truly Mosaic documents survive.

He who concealed himself under the name Otmar, Johann Christian Nachtigal, after having adduced further arguments for a period later than Moses found in the Pentateuch, and other arguments by which he endeavored to prove that among the Hebrews before the times of Samuel there existed, besides genealogical tables and tribal pedigrees, either no literary monuments at all or certainly very few, and that the memory of deeds performed by ancestors had been transmitted only by oral tradition and preserved by songs or stone monuments, maintains that it was only in the time of Samuel that in the schools of the prophets first historical songs were committed to writing, then that in the course of time several books were composed by wise and learned men which contained songs, narratives of ancient events, and collected laws, and that from books of this kind, by the combined efforts of several learned men, with the prophet Jeremiah perhaps directing the work, the Pentateuch was composed during the time of the Babylonian exile.

Johann Severin Vater, although he maintains that the Pentateuch as we now read it certainly did not exist before the time of the Babylonian exile, nevertheless considers that the greater part of Deuteronomy, and individual sections of the remaining books, were gradually composed from the age of David and Solomon onward.

Wilhelm Martin Leberecht de Wette, in his Beitrage zur Einleitung in das A. T. Part I, having examined those passages of the Old Testament books other than the Pentateuch in which mention of the latter is commonly believed to be made, maintains that from them it follows that the first traces of our Pentateuch appear not long before the destruction of the Hebrew commonwealth by the Chaldeans; that what is narrated as having been found by the priest Hilkiah in the temple during the reign of Josiah, II Kings XXII, 13 ff., the scroll of the law, was merely Deuteronomy.

Leonhard Bertholdt, in his Historisch-Kritische Einleitung in das A. und N. Testament, vol. III, p. 821 ff., in investigating the age and origin of the Pentateuch as it now exists, considers that one need not go back beyond the beginnings of the introduction of the royal dignity among the Hebrews. It seems probable to him that the Pentateuch was reduced to its present form by Samuel and deposited beside the Ark of the Covenant.

Charles-Francois Volney, in the work he entitled Recherches nouvelles sur l'histoire ancienne, Part I, chapters VI-VIII, p. 59 ff., although he by no means denies that the Pentateuch contains some writings that are truly Mosaic, nevertheless does not doubt that it, as we now have it, was produced by the combined efforts of Hilkiah the high priest, the prophet Jeremiah, Shaphan the royal scribe, and Achbor, with King Josiah not unaware, with the purpose that in those doubtful and perilous times the spirits of the Jews might be inflamed with love of country through the restoration of the ancient Mosaic institution.

V. What Weight Do the Arguments by Which the Pentateuch Is Denied to Moses Carry?

Those various opinions about a more recent origin of the Pentateuch, since they rest chiefly on a threefold class of arguments -- first, from the agreement of language and style with later books; second, from the structure of the entire work; third and finally, from passages found in the Pentateuch that could not possibly have been written by Moses (see Section III) -- let us now examine what force they have for denying the Pentateuch to Moses.

(a) Meignan aptly opposes to those attacking the authenticity of the Pentateuch legitimate presumptions which, although they do not directly refute their arguments, nevertheless greatly shake and weaken them. First, that German scholars, of a rather bold cast of mind and caring little for common sense, constantly pursue new and unheard-of hypotheses. Hear de Remusat: 'The systematic spirit is, in the most comprehensive sense of the word, the Germanic spirit. It is difficult to march with so proud a foot upon the head of common sense.' Second, that they disagree among themselves no less than they do with us, indeed contend fiercely. Third, that they by no means weigh the matter with a free mind: for, offended by miracles and prophecies, they rely on a tacit syllogism: 'If Moses had written this history of miracles, the miracles narrated therein would be true and divine; but miracles cannot happen: therefore Moses did not write this history.' What should be demonstrated is thus taken for granted.

And first indeed, as to what they say -- that a book whose language differs so little from that in which books published many centuries after Moses are written could not have been written by Moses -- we deny entirely that the very thing on which this argument rests is true. For although grammatical and orthographic archaisms found in the Pentateuch today are exceedingly few -- the reason for which doubtless lies in the fact that, as time went on, many of them were gradually removed by those who copied and revised the book at various times, lest more recent readers be troubled by them -- yet some survive. Furthermore, the number of words and phrases that are so peculiar to the Pentateuch that they never or very rarely occur in other books of the Old Testament is not small, just as conversely several others that are frequent in more recent books are nowhere or more rarely found in the Pentateuch. Nor should it be passed over that no foreign words are found in the Pentateuch except ancient Egyptian ones. Finally, in this whole question about the character of the Hebrew language in the Pentateuch, it must be considered that those languages to which Hebrew belongs, since in writing only the consonants of words are customarily set down, vary much less in written form than our own, which also express the pronunciation of vowels with letters. Hence it has happened that the Arabic of the Quran and of the most ancient poems appears to differ little from the Arabic in which more recent books are written. The same holds true for the Syriac language. For how slight the difference is between the Syriac in which that ancient New Testament translation called the Peshitta is written, and that in which Abulpharagius wrote many centuries later, is known by all who are not ignorant of that language.

(a) The adversaries have brought forward another argument drawn from language. Not a few expressions, they say, are found in the Pentateuch which, though entirely unused in the intermediate books, reappear in books written during or after the captivity. Reply first: I deny the consequence. For that this inference of the adversaries is defective and invalid, the words of Horace show: 'Many words will be reborn that have now fallen out of use, and those now in honor will fall, if usage so wills, in whose hands lies the judgment, the law, and the standard of speech.' Reply second: These expressions are very few, and not of such a kind as to furnish a reasonable man who examines them carefully with an indication that the Pentateuch was written in the time of the captivity. Hartmann found in the entire Deuteronomy only eleven post-Mosaic expressions!

Let us pass on to the other objection raised against the Mosaic origin of the Pentateuch, the structure of the entire work; for they argue that it was not written by one man, but was composed from various written records of different men and times, on the grounds of plainly unequal style -- now concise and compressed, now broad and diffuse -- then repeated and yet mutually contradictory narratives about the same events, and finally frequent headings and endings of individual sections. But as regards the inequality of style, it is of very little avail for proving diversity of authors, since it is well established that even the best writers vary their style in works written over intervals of many years, so as to seem different from themselves. Moreover, in the writings of a man who over a long series of years bore alone the role of commander, legislator, and judge, everyone would naturally expect a varied and less uniform style. Add to this that style must necessarily vary according to the variety of subject matter and purpose of the writer: all of which we observe in the Pentateuch. Nor do repeated narratives about the same events that are not always entirely consistent with each other prove multiple authors any better, for they can by no means surprise anyone in a work composed gradually over intervals of many years, by a man occupied with the greatest affairs.

* In the Pentateuch, even though composed in fragments, everything nonetheless coheres toward the end at which it aims, namely that by teachings, laws, histories, promises, and threats it may lead to religion and religious moral uprightness. Add that the ancients, indeed even much more recent Orientals, did not possess that art of writing which knows how to connect all disparate elements aptly.

Finally, of the least weight for proving that the Pentateuch was composed from various writings of different men, the frequent headings and endings of its individual parts carry the least weight, since it is clear that they were appended for the sake of clarity, so that sections of different subject matter might more easily be distinguished from each other. Furthermore, it is evident that the poorly connected and disparate parts of the four latter books were written down just as the circumstances of places and times dictated, whence they constitute a varied and miscellaneous work, in which we see scarcely any order maintained except that of time. Certainly if Moses left behind any written records at all, it is credible that they would have had just the character that we observe in the Pentateuch as it is structured. For if someone in more recent times had wished to compose either a law code or a history of Moses' deeds, it is probable that he would have arranged the matter very differently.

There remains the third class of arguments by which they attack the Mosaic origin of the Pentateuch: passages found in it that plainly cannot have been written by Moses, because they indicate a writer of a more recent age. Of these, however, if we set aside those that are doubtful and cannot be regarded as certain arguments for an age later than Moses, few will remain that we may determine were certainly written by a more recent hand. But that such things are found in so ancient and not inconsiderable a book, no one should rightly wonder at, and they are as little an obstacle to our calling these books the work of Moses as spurious verses inserted in the Aeneid or the Georgics are an obstacle to our believing those poems to be Virgil's. That these books have remained immune from the common fate of ancient books and have not been variously interpolated is not to be expected. For although the Hebrews esteemed them so highly that they would have considered it a matter of the greatest scruple to change anything in them in bad faith, this nevertheless did not prevent historical supplements and explanations of more ancient matters and words from being added here and there.

We have seen that the arguments by which the Mosaic origin of the Pentateuch is attacked do not have enough strength to overthrow those on which rest those who consider these books to be the work of Moses. Therefore we do not hesitate to say that the Pentateuch is by far and for the greatest part the work of Moses, but that we have it variously revised and reworked, with clauses of words and sentences interspersed throughout, as if patched together and rendered more explicitly, and arranged so as to continue the series of the history. As for these additions, since most of them betray their own character, distinguishing them from the genuine parts does not seem a matter of very great difficulty.

Note 4th. The Pentateuch was not recomposed. Arguments that stand against any recomposition, to whatever age it be assigned: If the work of Moses had been recomposed, the character of the language and style would betray a more recent age; words peculiar to the Pentateuch and archaisms would have been eliminated by the recomposer. No one could have impressed this character of style upon these books, and especially upon Deuteronomy; no one could have so completely assumed the person of Moses. In a work that had been recomposed under David or in a more recent age, some honorable mention of the ancestors of the royal house would be expected; something about building a temple; laws against drunkenness. Just as conversely many things that are read there would have been omitted, especially those that conflict with each other, such as the jubilee year, the release of slaves in the seventh year.

Let them say, then, in what age they think the writings of Moses were recomposed. Neither Ezra nor anyone of his age could have written this language and this style. The Samaritans would never have accepted a work recomposed by Ezra. The priest sent by the king of Assyria to Samaria was an exile and a priest of the calf, who much less could have written this language and style. Hilkiah perhaps, under the pretext of a codex found in the temple, imposed a recomposed book: but the king was terrified only upon hearing the ominous imprecations whose fulfillment through the Chaldeans was already imminent; and the Samaritans would have rejected a book recomposed by Hilkiah, a Jewish priest.

Note 5th. What in the eighteenth century our Encyclopedists confidently asserted -- that Moses could not have written the Pentateuch because the Hebrews at that time still did not know the art of writing -- is scarcely worth refuting. For all the adversaries of authenticity themselves either expressly teach or take for granted that the art of writing was known to Moses, since they are compelled to admit that certain parts of the Pentateuch, e.g. the laws and songs, are truly Mosaic.

VI. On the Samaritan Pentateuch

The Samaritans dwelling in Palestine have their own Pentateuch written in Hebrew, just as the rest of the Jews, but inscribed in the ancient Phoenician characters. About its age and origin -- whence it was first drawn, and from which codices it was copied -- there has been much and varied debate. There are two opinions in particular, diametrically opposed to each other on this matter. For some hold that codex to be most ancient, and believe that it existed in the same form in which it survives today already before Jeroboam among the citizens of the ten tribes; others on the contrary, who consider the Pentateuch not the work of Moses but composed only in the times of the exile, make the Samaritan copy no older than the temple on Gerizim, and hold that the Hebrew-Jewish codex of the Pentateuch was transferred to the Samaritans by Manasseh, a priest who had defected from the Jews, and was thence transcribed into the old Phoenician script to which the Samaritans were accustomed. The defenders of each opinion have sufficiently weighty arguments, which it would be tedious to examine and compare here, nor does the plan of our work require it.

What moves us to incline toward the former opinion is this: that throughout the entire span of time that elapsed from the revolt of the ten tribes to the destruction of the Samaritan kingdom, the Israelite prophets no less than the Jewish ones constantly and earnestly exhorted the people to worship the Lord alone, as enjoined in the Pentateuch, and to observe the entire body of laws prescribed therein: the same remarkable zeal for defending the Mosaic institution shines forth in the true prophets both Jewish and Israelite. This supreme agreement of the prophets of both kingdoms in recommending and inculcating the Mosaic institutions argues that there was some law code common to all. Hence to the arguments for the antiquity of the Pentateuch that we presented above (Section II) this new one is added: that long before the Jewish commonwealth was overthrown by the Chaldeans, the citizens of the Israelite kingdom possessed that law code.

As for the character of the Hebrew-Samaritan codex, it is so far from being comparable in purity to the Jewish, or Masoretic, text that it is rather much inferior to it, since it teems with errors and defects that owe their origin partly to the carelessness of scribes, partly to the desire of half-educated men to emend and supplement -- a fact that Gesenius, in his Dissertation on the subject, demonstrated so thoroughly that no room is left for doubt.
