Title

Chapter 1, verse 1. The most excellent song, which is Solomon's.

I. Chapter I, 2-6: The Bride

2. Let him kiss me! May the kisses of his mouth be granted to me! For your loves are sweeter than wine. 3. Your ointments are pleasing in fragrance. Your name is ointment poured out, Therefore the maidens love you. 4. Draw me after you, let us run: The king has brought me into his chambers. Let us exult and rejoice in you. Let us celebrate your loves more than wine: Rightly do they love you. 5. I am dark, but beautiful, O maidens of Jerusalem, Like the tents of the Kedarites, like the curtains of Solomon. 6. Do not look at me, because I am somewhat dark, Because the sun has gazed upon me. The sons of my mother burned with anger against me; They set me as keeper of the vineyards: My own vineyard I did not keep.

Notes. Verse 2. Others render: Let him kiss me with one or another of the kisses of his mouth. Your loves. The Alexandrian and Vulgate translators read daddaiq, your breasts. -- Verse 3. Maurer says: As for the scent of your ointments, it is good, so that tobim refers to the genitive shemanim, and the scent is that which the thing emits, not the sense of smell. Poured out, properly that which is being poured out; others: as ointment is poured out, so is your name, that is, your fame is widely spread. -- Verse 4. By the king has brought me in, the bride wishes to indicate the magnificence of the inner chambers into which she has been brought. -- Verse 5. Dark, namely from the heat of the sun. Kedar is the name of a nomadic tribe of the Arabian Desert, descended from Kedar, the second son of Ishmael (Gen. xxv, 13); it is used for Arab nomads in general, whose tents are made of black goatskins, or become blackened by constant exposure to the sun's heat. The Hebrews rightly observe that the individual similes correspond to the individual parts of the first hemistich in this manner: I am indeed dark like the tents of the Kedarites, but beautiful like the precious and splendid curtains of Solomon. -- Verse 6. The sons of my mother, my brothers, not full brothers, born of the same mother only as me, in whose guardianship and power Shulamith was placed after her father's death, and was treated harshly and inhumanely.

II. Chapter I, 7-17: The Bride and the Bridegroom

The Bride.

7. Tell me, O you whom my soul loves, where you pasture your flock? Where you make it lie down at noon? Why should I be as one veiled beside the flocks of your companions?

The Bridegroom.

8. If you do not know, O most beautiful among women, Go forth by the footsteps of the sheep, And pasture your young goats beside the shepherds' tents. 9. To the mares in Pharaoh's chariots I compare you, my beloved. 10. Your cheeks are beautiful among rows of pearls, Your neck is beautiful among necklaces. 11. We will make golden ornaments for you, With points of silver.

The Bride.

12. While the king was at his couch at table, My nard gave forth its fragrance, delighting the bridegroom. 13. A bundle of myrrh is my beloved to me, He rests between my breasts. 14. A cluster of henna is my beloved to me, Which grows among the vineyards of En-gedi.

The Bridegroom.

15. Behold, you are beautiful, my beloved! Behold, you are beautiful! Your eyes are like the eyes of doves.

The Bride.

16. Behold, you are handsome, my beloved! You are also delightful! Our couch too is green. 17. The beams of our houses are cedars, Our rafters are cypresses.

Notes. Verse 7. In hot regions, shepherds are accustomed to lead their flocks to shade and water during the noon hours, lest the milk be spoiled by heat and the animals fall into dangerous diseases. Veiled is a modest expression for harlot; Maurer says: unknown, that is, lest I seek you wandering as an unknown and foreign woman among the flocks of the shepherds. -- Verse 9. In Hebrew the word is mare in a collective sense. Horses and chariots from Egypt were brought to Solomon, as is established in 1 Kings x, 28, 29; 2 Chronicles i, 16. -- Verse 10. Eastern women wear such rows or lines of pearls on their cheeks and chin. -- Verse 12. The bride says this to her companions, explaining how she strives to please and win over the bridegroom. Nard is an Indian plant with a spike similar to a green wheat spike, from which the most fragrant and precious oil of nard was prepared. -- Verse 13. My beloved, properly my love. -- Verse 14. Henna is a type of shrub or small tree, with fragrant, whitish flowers growing in the manner of clusters. En-gedi, a town of southern Palestine in the desert of Judah by the Dead Sea, fertile in vines and palms. -- Verse 15. Behold, that is, how. Your eyes are doves, that is, as charming and cheerful as doves are. -- Verse 17. The bride also praises their common dwelling, because it is made of precious, solid, and durable material.

III. Chapter II, 1-7: The Bride and the Bridegroom

The Bride.

1. I am a narcissus of Sharon, A lily of the valleys.

The Bridegroom.

2. As a lily excels among thorns, So is my beloved among the maidens.

The Bride.

3. As an apple tree among the trees of the forest, So is my beloved among the young men. In his shade I gladly sit, And his fruit is sweet to my palate. 4. He brought me to the house of wine, And his banner over me is love. 5. Sustain me with raisin cakes, Support me with apples: For I am sick with love. 6. Let his left hand be under my head; And let his right hand embrace me.

The Bridegroom.

7. I adjure you, O maidens of Jerusalem, By the gazelles or by the does of the field, That you do not stir up nor awaken this love, Until she pleases.

Notes. Verse 1. For narcissus, others with Gesenius substitute the autumn crocus. Sharon is a proper name of two level regions, one of which extends from Joppa to Caesarea, while the other lies in northern Palestine between Mount Tabor and the Sea of Tiberias. -- Verse 4. The sense is: My beloved made me experience how sweet he is; he holds before me love as a banner, by which he draws me to himself. -- Verse 5. Here quince apples are meant, which possess the power of refreshing. -- Verse 7. That the bride, having obtained her wish, fell asleep, can be gathered from the words that follow; the bridegroom adjures the women of Jerusalem not to interrupt his beloved's rest and sleep.

IV. Chapter II, 8-17: The Bride

8. The voice of my beloved! Behold, here he is! He comes, Leaping upon the mountains, Bounding upon the hills. 9. My beloved is like a gazelle Or a young stag. Behold! Here he already stands behind our wall; He peers through the windows; He flashes forth through the lattice. 10. My beloved spoke and said to me: "Arise, my beloved, my beautiful one, come! 11. For behold, the winter has passed, The rain has gone away, has ceased. 12. Flowers appear in this land, The time of birdsong has come, And the voice of the turtledove is heard in our land. 13. The fig tree reddens its early figs, And the vines in blossom give forth fragrance: Arise, my beloved, my beautiful one, and come! 14. My dove hiding in the clefts of the rock, In the hiding places of the cliff, Show me your face, Let me hear your voice: For your voice is sweet, and your face is lovely. 15. Catch for us the foxes, The little foxes that ruin vineyards, Since our vineyards are blooming. 16. My beloved is mine, and I am his, Who pastures his flock among the lilies. 17. Until the day breathes, and the shadows flee, Return, be like, my beloved, a gazelle or a young stag, Upon the rugged mountains.

Notes. Verse 9. Wall, namely of the house or bedroom. -- Verse 11. The reason is added by which the beloved urges the bride to come out: the arrival of spring. -- Verse 12. The voice of the turtledove has something pleasant about it, insofar as it indicates that spring has come, because in winter turtledoves do not appear. -- Verse 14. Shulamith, who, led by fear and modesty, does not dare to go out of the house, is compared to a wild dove which, fleeing birds of prey, nests in the clefts of a rock. -- Verse 15. Since Shulamith does not come out of the vineyard, the beloved, by singing a fragment of a vintners' song, tries to entice her to come out. -- Verse 16. In Judea and Syria, lilies grow spontaneously in the fields: hence by Christ, Matt. vi, 28, they are called lilies of the field. -- Verse 17. The sense is: Before night draws near, turn yourself here, my beloved, as swiftly as young stags leap upon the mountains.

V. Chapter III, 1-5: The Bride and the Bridegroom

The Bride.

1. Upon my bed, at night, I sought him whom my soul loves: I sought, but did not find him. 2. I will arise and go about the city, In the streets and squares I will seek him whom my soul loves; I sought, but did not find him. 3. The watchmen going about the city found me. "Have you seen," I said, "him whom my soul loves?" 4. Scarcely had I passed from them, when I found him whom my soul loves; I took hold of him and said I would not let him go, Until I bring him into the house of my mother, And into the chamber of her who bore me.

The Bridegroom.

5. I adjure you, O daughters of Jerusalem, By the gazelles or by the does of the field, Do not stir up, nor awaken the beloved, Until she pleases.

Notes. Verse 1. The bride narrates to her companions what happened to her at night, how she sought the beloved in vain upon her couch. -- Verse 2. The city par excellence, just as for the Romans it was Rome, for the Hebrews it was called Jerusalem. -- Verse 4. Into the house of my mother. That a modest maiden among the ancient Hebrews would truly have wished to do such a thing is by no means probable, and from this it is sufficiently clear that these things must be understood allegorically. -- Verse 5. The bridegroom adjures the women of Jerusalem not to interrupt the bride's rest or sleep, since she was now exhausted from seeking the bridegroom everywhere throughout the night.

VI. Chapter III, 6-11: Chorus of Women

6. Who is this that comes up from the desert, Like columns of smoke, Enveloped in the fragrance of myrrh and frankincense, And of every aromatic powder? 7. Behold, it is Solomon's litter, Sixty mighty men surround it, From the mightiest of Israel: 8. All grasping swords And most skilled in war: Each one's sword upon his thigh Because of the terrors of the night. 9. King Solomon made himself a palanquin Of the wood of Lebanon. 10. Its pillars he made of silver, Its back of gold, Its seat of purple; Its interior he paved with love By the daughters of Jerusalem. 11. Go forth, daughters of Zion, And see King Solomon, In the crown with which his mother crowned him, On the day of his espousals, On the day of the gladness of his heart.

Notes. A solemn procession is described, in which Solomon's bride is escorted to his palace. The women of Jerusalem, who had been looking on from a distance, now see her approach and exclaim in wonder. -- Verse 6. Maurer translates: what is this that comes up, so as to describe the first and distant impression of the approaching procession. -- Verse 10. He paved with love, that is, the interior of the palanquin was adorned by the daughters of Jerusalem with the most exquisite art, prompted by love and devotion. -- Verse 11. In the crown: it was the custom among the Hebrews for the bridegroom to wear a crown on his wedding day.

VII. Chapter IV, 1 — V, 1: The Bridegroom and the Bride

The Bridegroom.

1. How beautiful you are, my beloved, how beautiful you are! Your eyes are like doves from behind your veil. Your hair is like a flock of goats That stream down from Mount Gilead. 2. Your teeth are like a flock of shorn ewes That come up from the washing, All of them bearing twins, And none among them is barren. 3. Your lips are like a scarlet thread, And your mouth is lovely. Your cheeks are like a piece of pomegranate From behind your veil. 4. Your neck is like the tower of David, Built in courses; A thousand shields hang from it, All the armor of the mighty. 5. Your two breasts are like two young fawns, Twins of a gazelle, That feed among the lilies. 6. Until the day breathes and the shadows flee, I will go to the mountain of myrrh, And to the hill of frankincense. 7. You are all beautiful, my beloved, And there is no blemish in you. 8. Come with me from Lebanon, my bride, Come with me from Lebanon: Come, you shall be crowned from the top of Amana, From the summit of Senir and Hermon, From the dens of lions, From the mountains of leopards. 9. You have ravished my heart, my sister, my bride, You have ravished my heart with one glance of your eyes, With one chain of your neck. 10. How beautiful are your loves, my sister, my bride! How much better are your loves than wine, And the fragrance of your ointments than all spices! 11. Your lips, my bride, drip as the honeycomb; Honey and milk are under your tongue; And the fragrance of your garments is like the fragrance of Lebanon. 12. A garden enclosed is my sister, my bride; A spring enclosed, a fountain sealed. 13. Your plants are a paradise of pomegranates With choice fruits: Henna with nard; 14. Nard and saffron, Aromatic cane and cinnamon With all the trees of frankincense, Myrrh and aloe-wood With every chief kind of spice. 15. A fountain of gardens, A well of living waters, And streams flowing from Lebanon.

The Bride.

16. Awake, O north wind; come, O south wind, Blow upon my garden, Let its fragrances flow forth. Let my beloved come into his garden, And eat its choice fruits.

The Bridegroom.

Chapter V, 1. I come into my garden, my sister, bride; I gather my myrrh with my balsam, I eat my honeycomb with my honey, I drink my wine with my milk. Eat, friends, drink and be inebriated, beloved ones.

Notes. Verse 1. From, properly from within, or behind. Tsammah, from tsemam, which in Chaldean means to cover, to conceal, signifies a veil. -- Verse 2. Bearing twins: all teeth correspond in equal pairs, one on the right and one on the left, so that they neatly fit together. -- Verse 4. Built in courses, that is, built with rows of stones, one upon the other. -- Verse 8. These are all mountains in the northern part of Palestine, abounding in wild beasts. The bridegroom invites the bride to leave these wild places and come to him. -- Chapter V, verse 1. Into the garden, to you namely, who are a garden planted with every kind of excellent plant.

VIII. Chapter V, 2 — VI, 3: The Bride and the Women of Jerusalem

The Bride.

2. I was sleeping, but my heart was awake. The voice of my beloved knocking at the door: "Open to me, my sister, My beloved, my dove, my perfect one; For my head is covered with dew, My locks with the drops of the night." 3. I have put off my garment, how shall I put it on? I have washed my feet, how shall I soil them? 4. My beloved put his hand through the opening, And my heart was moved for him. 5. I arose to open to my beloved, And my hands dripped with myrrh, And my fingers with flowing myrrh, Upon the handles of the bolt. 6. I opened to my beloved, But my beloved had turned away and gone. My soul went forth when he spoke. I sought him, but did not find him; I called him, but he did not answer me. 7. The watchmen who go about the city found me, They struck me, they wounded me; The keepers of the walls took away my cloak from me. 8. I adjure you, O daughters of Jerusalem, If you find my beloved, What will you tell him? That I am sick with love.

Women of Jerusalem.

9. What is your beloved more than another beloved, O most beautiful among women? What is your beloved more than another, That you so adjure us?

The Bride.

10. My beloved is radiant and ruddy, Distinguished among ten thousand. 11. His head is like pure gold, His locks are bushy, Black as a raven. 12. His eyes are like doves Beside streams of water, Bathed in milk, Sitting beside abundant waters. 13. His cheeks are like raised beds of aromatic herbs; His lips are lilies dripping with flowing myrrh. 14. His hands are golden rings, set with chrysolite. His belly is polished ivory, covered with sapphires. 15. His legs are marble columns, set upon bases of fine gold. His appearance is like Lebanon, choice as the cedars. 16. His mouth is most sweet, and he is wholly desirable. This is my beloved, and this is my friend, O daughters of Jerusalem.

Maidens of Jerusalem.

Chapter VI, 1. Where has your beloved gone, O most beautiful among women? Where has your beloved turned? And we will seek him with you.

The Bride.

2. My beloved has gone down to his garden, To the beds of balsam, To pasture in the gardens, To gather lilies. 3. I belong to my beloved, and my beloved belongs to me, He who pastures his flock among the lilies.

Notes. Chapter V, verse 2. These are the words of the bride narrating what happened to her at night. -- Verse 10. Radiant and ruddy: the first term denotes the whiteness of his complexion, the second the ruddy glow of health and beauty. -- Verse 11. His head is like pure gold: this refers either to the golden color of his hair, or to the crown upon his head. -- Verse 14. The Tartessian stone is the chrysolite of the ancients, the topaz of more recent writers. -- Chapter VI, verse 1. The maidens, moved by the bride's glowing description, offer to help her seek the beloved.

IX. Chapter VI, 4-8: The Bridegroom

4. You are beautiful, my beloved, as Tirzah, Lovely as Jerusalem, Terrible as an army with banners. 5. Turn your eyes away from me, For they have overcome me. Your hair is like a flock of goats That stream down from Gilead. 6. Your teeth are like a flock of ewes That come up from the washing, All of them bearing twins, And none among them is barren. 7. Your cheeks are like a piece of pomegranate Behind your veil. 8. There are sixty queens, And eighty concubines, And of maidens there is no number.

Notes. Verse 4. The bridegroom, meeting his beloved, praises her beauty. Tirzah was a city of Canaan, the royal seat of the kings of Israel before Samaria was built; it was renowned for its beauty. -- Verse 8. The number of queens and concubines given here is not the same as that assigned in 1 Kings xi, 3; hence interpreters draw various conclusions.

X. Chapter VI, 10 — VII, 11: The Bridegroom and the Bride

The Bridegroom.

10. Who is this that shines forth like the dawn, Beautiful as the moon, pure as the sun, Terrible as an army with banners? 11. I went down to the garden of nut trees, To see the blossoms of the valley, To see whether the vine had budded, Whether the pomegranates were in bloom. 12. I did not know: my soul set me upon the chariots of Aminadab.

Return, return, O Shulamite; Return, return, that we may look upon you.

The Shulamite.

What would you see in the Shulamite As in the dance of the two camps?

The Bridegroom.

Chapter VII, 2. How beautiful are your feet in sandals, O daughter of a prince! The curves of your thighs Are like jewels, The work of a craftsman's hands. 3. Your navel is a rounded goblet, Which never lacks mixed wine. Your belly is a heap of wheat, Set about with lilies. 4. Your two breasts are like two fawns, Twins of a gazelle. 5. Your neck is like a tower of ivory; Your eyes are the pools of Heshbon, By the gate of Bath-rabbim; Your nose is like the tower of Lebanon, Which looks toward Damascus. 6. Your head upon you is like Carmel, And the hair of your head is like purple; A king is held captive in its tresses. 7. How beautiful and how pleasant you are, O love, among delights! 8. This your stature is like a palm tree, And your breasts like its clusters. 9. I said: I will climb the palm tree, I will take hold of its branches; Let your breasts, I pray, be like clusters of the vine, And the scent of your nose like apples, 10. And your palate like the best wine.

The Bride.

Which flows most smoothly down the throat for my beloved, Gliding sweetly over the lips of those who sleep. 11. I belong to my beloved, And his desire is toward me.

Notes. Chapter VI, verse 10. The bridegroom speaks, gazing at the bride's beauty with wonder. -- Verse 12. My soul troubled me on account of the chariots of Aminadab: this is one of the most difficult verses of the Song; various interpretations are given. -- Chapter VII, verse 2. The bridegroom continues his praise of the bride's beauty, beginning from her feet. -- Verse 5. Heshbon was a city of Moab, later of the Ammonites, famous for its pools. -- Verse 6. Carmel is here used for its beauty and fruitfulness, not for the mountain. -- Verse 10. The word means: that which goes, descends through the throat, straight down; the bride interrupts and applies the bridegroom's metaphor to herself.

XI. Chapter VII, 12 — VIII, 4: The Bride and the Bridegroom

The Bride.

12. Come, my beloved, let us go out to the countryside, Let us spend the night in the villages. 13. Let us go early to the vineyards; Let us see if the vine has budded, If its blossom has opened, If the pomegranates have flowered: There I will give you my love. 14. The mandrakes give forth fragrance, And at our gates are all manner of choice fruits, New and old, Which I have laid up for you, my beloved.

Chapter VIII, 1. Would that you were my brother, Who had nursed at my mother's breasts, Then if I found you outside I could kiss you, And no one would despise me. 2. I would lead you, I would bring you Into my mother's house (there you would teach me): I would give you spiced wine to drink, And the must of my pomegranates. 3. Let his left hand be under my head, And let his right hand embrace me.

The Bridegroom.

4. I adjure you, O maidens of Jerusalem, Do not stir up, nor awaken this beloved, Until she pleases.

Notes. Verse 13. The signs of spring are described, similar to those found in chapter II, verses 12 and 13. -- Verse 14. On the mandrakes: they are a plant of the solanaceae family, with a heavy, stupefying scent. -- Chapter VIII, verse 1. The bride wishes the bridegroom were her brother, so she could show him affection publicly without censure. -- Verse 2. Spiced wine: wine mixed with aromatic spices, a luxury of the East.

XII. Chapter VIII, 5-7: The Maidens of Jerusalem and the Bride

Maidens of Jerusalem.

5. Who is this that comes up from the desert, Leaning upon her beloved?

The Bride.

Under the apple tree there I awakened you, There your mother was in labor with you, There she labored who bore you. 6. Set me as a seal upon your heart, As a seal upon your arm: For love is strong as death, Jealousy is hard as hell; Its lamps are lamps of fire and flames. 7. Many waters cannot extinguish love, Neither can rivers overwhelm it. If a man were to give all the substance of his house for love, He would despise it as nothing.

Notes. Verse 5. The final scene is opened, as the lovers, having attained their desires, enjoy intimate fellowship with each other. -- Verse 6. Set me as a seal: the ancients wore seal-rings hung by a cord about the neck, so that they rested upon the heart. Love is strong as death: a proverbial saying expressing the irresistible power of love. -- Verse 7. Many waters cannot extinguish love: the image of love as a fire that cannot be quenched even by floods.

XIII. Chapter VIII, 8-14: The Bride's Brothers and the Bride

The Bride's Brothers.

8. We have a little sister, And her breasts are not yet grown. What shall we do for our sister On the day when she shall be spoken for? 9. If she is a wall, we will build upon her a battlement of silver; If she is a door, we will barricade her with boards of cedar.

The Bride.

10. I am a wall, and my breasts are like towers. Then I shall be in his eyes as one who finds peace.

11. Solomon had a vineyard in Baal-hamon; He entrusted the vineyard to keepers; Each was to bring for its fruit a thousand pieces of silver. 12. My vineyard, which is mine, is before me: The thousand are yours, O Solomon, And two hundred for those who tend its fruit.

The Bridegroom.

13. O you who dwell in the gardens, Companions listen for your voice, Let me hear it.

The Bride.

14. Make haste, my beloved, be like a gazelle or a young stag, Upon the mountains of spices.

Notes. Verse 8. Who is speaking and about whom, interpreters disagree. For many, the sister is the bride herself. -- Verse 10. I am a wall: the bride declares that she is firm and constant in virtue. -- Verse 11. Baal-hamon: some take this as a proper name, others interpret it as "lord of a multitude." -- Verse 14. Make haste: the bride's final words, calling the bridegroom to come swiftly. The mountains of spices represent the delights of paradise.
