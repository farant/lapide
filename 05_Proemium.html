Giovanni Battista Pianciani, S.J.

Prooemium

(Preface to the Commentary on the Mosaic History of Creation)

Concerning the work of the six days, the ancient theologians who followed the method of Peter Lombard or St. Thomas Aquinas were accustomed to treat quite extensively, pursuing — not without praise for their talent and diligence — those questions which arise, as it were, of their own accord from the Mosaic history (Genesis chapters I and II). But they could not treat these matters otherwise than as those times allowed, when the state of the natural sciences was still very imperfect. More recent theologians, however, who have produced some compendium or curriculum of sacred doctrine, either pass over these questions entirely — being concerned only with establishing dogma — or, when speaking of God as Creator, discuss creation and the Mosaic history of creation only in general terms, and not, as those ancients did, each of its individual parts.

Since, however, this treatment seems to me to be of no small importance, and since the objections of adversaries and the advances of the natural sciences have made it possible for us — perhaps without the greatest difficulty — to expound the divine history of creation less imperfectly, and to defend it more strongly from the attacks of opponents in the present age, I thought it would be useful if a brief treatise on the work of the six days were composed, which could serve as a kind of appendix to theological lectures or courses.

This I ventured to write for the use of a small number of theology students, while I was living in Georgetown in America, a city belonging to the Archdiocese of Baltimore, situated in that part of Maryland which, being as it were separated from the rest of the region, is commonly called the District of Columbia. This same work I now commit to the press, confident that it will not be useless to young students of theology. And if God grants me life and strength, it is my intention to publish a more ample treatment of the work of the six days, in order to show that the natural sciences, the profane traditions of certain peoples, and the divine Scriptures all lead us to the same cosmogonic conclusions.

I ask pardon for these writings, whose cause was not glory for us, but usefulness and duty. From a man not accustomed to Latin composition, no one, I think, expects a more exquisite elegance of style. No one will be surprised if I have said not only many things less ornately, but even some things less than correctly in Latin. Such praises may rightly be demanded of those who both can and wish to attain them: but that I neither could, I myself frankly confess, nor wished to, my very speech will make clear.
