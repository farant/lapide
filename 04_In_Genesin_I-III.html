PROLEGOMENA TO GENESIS

I. The Subject of the Book

The first book of the Pentateuch, called by the Hebrews bereshith ("in the beginning") from its first word, and by the Greeks Genesis from its subject matter -- since it sets forth the origins of all things and of the human race -- constitutes, as St. Athanasius says in his Synopsis of Scripture (Works, vol. II, p. 66), a kind of preface to the four books that follow. For it contains those things which readers need to know in order to understand what is narrated in the subsequent books, or which serve to teach that the one God, creator and governor of all things, is to be worshiped, and to prepare the minds of readers for the body of laws contained in the remaining books. Beginning from the origin of things and the beginnings of the human race, the author carries forward a continuous series of genealogies and the history woven into them, from the first man all the way to the founder of the Hebrew nation, Abraham, so that the origins of that people might be traced back to the most remote beginnings of the world and of the human race -- for the antiquity of their origin was the glory of many ancient peoples. After recounting the dispersion of mankind across the earth, he narrows the history to the single family of Heber, and turning his pen to Abraham, he is engaged almost exclusively in narrating the affairs of this man and his descendants, selecting especially those things that pertain to the magnificent and repeated promises made by God to those ancestors of the Hebrew nation, and from those deeds of theirs in which their piety toward God and their faith in him shine forth. He indicates the origin of customs that prevail among the Hebrews (such as abstaining from eating the sinew of the thigh, xxxii, 32 ff.); he also traces the occasions and histories of sacrifices and rites, such as circumcision. He is more expansive in setting forth the life of Joseph, with whose death the book closes, so that Hebrew readers might learn how it came about that their ancestors arrived in Egypt and established their dwelling in that land.

II. That the Book of Genesis Was Written by Moses

If the things contained in the four later books of the Pentateuch were recorded by Moses -- which we have attempted to demonstrate above -- then the first book too must have been written by the same man, since it coheres with the beginning of the second book in the closest connection and contains things that are absolutely necessary for rightly understanding what is conveyed in the remaining books. If you remove Genesis from those books, you will have an incomplete and headless (akephalon) work. With this part of the Pentateuch removed, the Hebrews would not have known who the God of their ancestors was, whom they worshiped as their king, from whom they received their laws, in whose honor they celebrated sabbaths and festivals, to whom they offered sacrifices and first-fruits; they would not have known what those promises so often mentioned in the remaining books were -- the promises given to their ancestors by God their guardian (see Exod. vi, 4; xiii, 5; xxxiii, 1; Deut. i, 8; ix, 5; xxix, 13; xxx, 20; xxxiv, 4 -- all of which passages manifestly refer back to Gen. xii, 7; xiii, 15; xv, 18; xvii, 8; xxiv, 7; xxvi, 3, 4; l, 24). When Moses impressed upon them the primary law about observing the sabbath, Exod. xx, 11; xxxi, 17, he derived its rationale from the fact that in six days God had created the heaven and earth, and on the seventh day had done nothing and, as it were, rested -- which no one would doubt refers to those things that are expressly recounted at the beginning of Genesis (ii, 2, 3) on this matter. Furthermore, in the other books too there is observed a consistency between the first book and the rest, such as one would expect in the work of one and the same author (1).

That the book of Genesis is the work of the same author who wrote the remaining books of the Pentateuch was proved with several arguments by Hasse in Entdeckungen der altesten Erd- und Menschengeschichte, Part II, p. 198 ff.

III. On the Sources from Which Moses Drew What He Conveyed in Genesis, and How He Made Use of Them

Since in Genesis those things are set forth which were done and accomplished before Moses, the question arises whether he was the first to commit them to writing, or whether what he contains was drawn from older documents that subsequently perished. If Moses was the first to write these things, never previously committed to writing, then what he contains must either have been revealed to him by God, or narrated to him by people who held them in memory; for they could not have become known to him in any other way. But nowhere does he say that these things were disclosed to him by God, nor would anyone, given Moses' silence, venture to assert it as established fact (a). That so many names and so many sums of years, such as are read especially in the first ten chapters, could have been preserved by memory alone is scarcely credible. But if any written documents existed among the Hebrews before the time of Moses, it is likely that they were very few indeed. For their ancestors, who led a pastoral and nomadic life in Canaan, were if not entirely unskilled in the art of writing, at least little practiced in it; nor did they, after migrating to Egypt, oppressed by harsh servitude, cultivate it to such an extent that they devoted effort to committing the memory of ancient events to writing. This being so, it is reasonable to believe that Moses drew what he reports about the ancestors of the Hebrew people partly from ancient narratives and songs transmitted orally among the Hebrews, and partly from written tribal genealogies and family pedigrees (b). But as for those things that are read in his work concerning the origin of the world, the beginnings of the human race, and the progress and fortunes of the earliest ages, he seems to have adapted what the peoples then more cultivated than the Hebrews and neighboring to them -- the Phoenicians and Egyptians -- had handed down about those matters, converting them to his own purposes in such a way as to accommodate them to what he himself taught about the worship of the one God, creator and governor of all things, and to endeavor to alienate the minds of the Hebrews from the fables of their neighbors, and indeed from the religions that often rested upon those fables (2).

What and how many ancient documents committed to writing Moses may have used in composing the book of Genesis -- although scarcely anyone would expect this to be determinable after an interval of more than thirty centuries -- there have been and still are learned and sagacious men who have undertaken to investigate the matter. In this enterprise they hoped to be aided, beyond the repeated narratives of the same events, by the headings of individual parts of the book (3), and by the different designations of the supreme deity, since in some continuous parts of the book it is called by the name Jehovah, in others Elohim, and they observed that in each individual patch in which one or the other of those names is used, there are certain fixed phrases and expressions proper and peculiar to it. They maintain, moreover, that Moses joined together those ancient writings preserved completely intact, without even one or another word changed or added from his own, so that by carefully observing those distinguishing marks of the various documents, it can be determined to which of them each individual section of the book, indeed each individual verse, belongs. But as to how many ancient treatises there were from which the book of Genesis was compiled, the learned men who hold this view disagree among themselves. For Astruc (1), who thought himself the first to have discovered the manner in which the book was composed, set out to show that Moses had made use of twelve writings, both major and minor; Eichhorn (2) holds that it was drawn chiefly from two, into which however other material was here and there inserted; while Ilgen (3) judges that the book of Genesis was woven together from three older documents. This very diversity of opinions on the matter seems to argue that the distinguishing marks by which those learned men think the individual documents can be identified are by no means sufficiently certain and reliable (a). As for the repetitions, which they say are indications of the various sources from which the book was drawn, these by no means prove what they claim, since some are mere variations of style and expression, others are to be attributed to the excessive zeal for clarity of a writer not yet sufficiently practiced in his craft (4), others were necessary to aid the reader's memory after the thread of the narrative had been broken by the insertion of some unrelated matter (5), and still others are not even rightly considered to be repeated accounts of the same events (6). The headings, moreover, that are found prefixed to various parts of the book (such as ch. ii, 4; v, 1; vi, 9) by no means prove that these are patches from different writers; rather, it is clear that they were placed there by the one who wrote the complete book, with the purpose of indicating the subjects of its individual chapters. Nor, finally, does the alternating use of the names Jehovah and Elohim provide a more certain means of identifying and distinguishing the diverse fragments from which the book was supposedly composed, since this alternation is observed in all the other books of the Old Testament as well, and yet no one would conclude from this that those books too are stitched together from various patches by various writers (7), or would maintain that by means of those names these various parts can be distinguished from one another and each restored to its respective author. How uncertain an argument for diverse fragments is the alternation of those names of God is clear from the fact that it is not even consistently observed. Thus in that which runs from chapter II, verse 3, to the end of chapter III, in which the compound name Jehovah-Elohim is customarily used for God, yet three times (III, 2-5) the name Elohim alone appears. In another continuous narrative, concerning Jacob's dream of the heavenly ladder and his vow (XXVIII, 10 ff.), the names Jehovah and Elohim are used so interchangeably that the individual verses in which one or the other of these names occurs can only be torn apart by force and assigned to two different sources from which they are supposedly drawn (a). For it would scarcely occur to a prudent writer to stitch together the patches and patchworks of several books into a single historical work in so laborious a manner as those learned men suppose, without even removing the repetitions, or the contradictions between different narratives that they claim to have detected. Moreover, it is scarcely credible that before the book of Genesis was composed, several writings on the same subject existed, in each of which those names of God were respectively employed (1).

(a) No one attacked the theory of Astruc and Ilgen about distinguishing the authors of Genesis by the alternation of the names Jehovah and Elohim more sharply and copiously than Hartmann, in his Historisch-kritische Forschungen, etc. pp. 116-169. According to Hartmann, those names are used indiscriminately and are varied for no other reason than perhaps occasionally to guard against the appearance of polytheism which the name Elohim conveys, by using the name Jehovah. But most defenders of authenticity trace this alternating use of these names to the different signification of each name, so that Moses employs now Elohim, now Jehovah, not indiscriminately and at whim, but as the subject matter and his intended purpose require. Others, however, explain it differently. a) Sack, in a special treatise De usu nominum Elohim et Jehova in libro Geneseos, Bonn 1821, says that by the name Elohim, as its plural form indicates, the fullness of divinity in general is designated, that is, an infinite, omnipotent, incomprehensible being from which all finite and visible entities derive their origin, and, to put it briefly, God's unrevealed but quasi-visible relation with nature; whereas Jehovah designates God insofar as He reveals Himself to men as the one, true, and omnipotent being; or more briefly, the name Elohim designates divinity in general, the name Jehovah more strictly signifies God insofar as He manifests Himself to certain men through apparitions and conversations. The same author, in Christliche Apologetik, Hamburg, 1829, p. 157: "Elohim designates the general operation of divinity through nature, Jehovah His being and will as revealed to men. This distinction has its foundation in the religious sense of mankind, and indeed is found in more recent languages: Gott der Herr, Dieu le Seigneur, God the Lord; nor should it be attributed to a diversity of writers. For since the name Jehovah was already known before the invention of the art of writing, who would believe that an appellation so dear to the Israelites was used so sparingly by an Israelite writer?" b) Welte, in Nachmosaisches, etc. pp. 85 ff., holds that not only in Genesis but also in the other books of the Old Testament, according to Hebrew usage, God is called by the name Elohim when He is intended in a general sense, with no regard or with disregard to the theocracy, but is otherwise called Jehovah. c) Delitzsch derives the word Elohim from the Hebrew root alah, Arabic aliha, that is, "he feared," so that Elohim means "the Terrible One"; Jehovah, however, from the root hawah, which means to become, to be, and thus expresses the essential attribute of the thing, namely, "being." As for our own opinion, we agree with those most learned men in this, that there is a true difference in meaning between the names Elohim and Jehovah, and that a different attribute of God is designated by each, so that one is used in preference to the other as the subject matter and the writer's intended purpose require. This is also confirmed by the fact that in the Psalms too a deliberate and prudent use of those same names, by no means accidental, appears to have been made, so that modern interpreters have been able to classify the Psalms into certain categories based on this single indicator of the divine names. What, however, the proper notion of each divine name may be, from which its usage should be derived, is a matter not yet sufficiently explored nor settled among all scholars.

Bertholdt, in his Historisch-Krit. Einleit. in das A. und N. Test. P. III, section 239, supposes that originally only that part of this first book which is contained in chapters V-XXXIII existed, and that it was composed by Moses from several older documents with the purpose of demonstrating, by means of the promises made to the ancestors of the Hebrews recorded therein, that the Hebrews had occupied the land of Canaan by perfect right, and that Moses placed this book of his beside the sacred ark in which the tablets of the law were kept. The remaining portions, both those in the first four chapters of the book and those from chapter XXXIV to the end, were added to that first Mosaic book after Moses but before the time of Samuel, except for Jacob's prophetic poem, chapter XLIX, which was added only by those who arranged the Pentateuch in order during the reign of Saul. But in truth, even those portions which the most learned man attributes to Moses necessarily had to be prefixed by Moses to what is transmitted in the four subsequent books, as we observed above in section II.

That the book of Genesis, as we now have it, arose in such a way that several additions and imitations, produced by priests of the family of Ithamar in the schools of the Prophets during the time of Samuel, were joined to and inserted into the Mosaic writings, is maintained by Kelle (2), who also expended the greatest effort and study in separating what had been stitched onto and inserted into those ancient Mosaic documents, and thus restoring the genuine part of the book to its original integrity. But just as everything which the most learned man tells us about the rivalry between the two priestly families, the Eleazaric and the Ithamaric -- the former of which supposedly preserved the genuine Mosaic writings in its possession, while the latter, which had its seat at Shiloh, spurred by jealousy, fabricated writings similar to the Mosaic ones in the schools of the Prophets -- rests on no solid argument (1); so too the other thing he assumes, namely that in our book of Genesis the genuine Mosaic writings were joined, by David's command, with those things which had been fabricated by the priests of the Ithamaric family in imitation of them, and which not infrequently contradicted them, with the purpose of removing that rivalry and reconciling the two families with each other -- this is such that it could seem plausible only to the one who devised it. Furthermore, the arguments by which he claims some portions for Moses and endeavors to prove that others were added later are weak and assumed at will (2). Finally, when those portions which in the most learned man's opinion are of later date are separated from those which he judges to be truly Mosaic, everything appears so disjointed and torn that it is astonishing such things could seem probable to their author *.

* To which opinion, however, Tuch and de Bohlen more recently added their support. According to them, Genesis (like the Pentateuch in general) originally consisted of the documents of the Elohist author, as they call him; then a certain Jehovist compiler reworked it and augmented it with Jehovist fragments. But the opinion on which these aforementioned authors rely is false, namely that the chapters of the Elohist author, with the Jehovist chapters excluded, form a well-ordered work put together without a crack, while the fragments of the Jehovist author, with the Elohist's excluded, do not. For if from Genesis as it now stands you cut away the first sin of the First Parents and what followed from it, written by the Jehovist author, as a later addition, the remaining part of the book will easily appear disjointed and not artfully connected. For the primary author of Genesis, the Elohist (on this hypothesis), intended to transmit the historical preparation for the theocracy; and yet he would have omitted to mention the event which was the cause of the theocracy's establishment, and without which neither the origin, nor the purpose, nor the effects of the theocracy can be understood! And indeed, would it not seem strange to everyone that, after the Elohist author had described man as recently created, good and upright, the earth is immediately and as if abruptly described as defiled by crimes and corruption of every kind?
